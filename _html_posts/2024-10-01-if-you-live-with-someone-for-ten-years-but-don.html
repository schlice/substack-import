The answer, as with any legal question, is "it depends."

In a very small handful of states, there is what is known as "common law
marriage." In the few states that allow it, common law marriage is a
legally recognized marriage that occurs when a couple agrees to be
married and lives together without having a formal ceremony or obtaining
a marriage license.

Just living together is not enough. You have to live *as if you are
married.* You agree to be married. You tell everyone (friends, family,
acquaintances, etc) that you are married.

marriage:

In order for there to be property rights in a common law marriage, your
state has to BOTH recognize your common law marriage, AND agree that
common law marriages are recognized for the purposes of dissolution of
that marriage and property distribution.

For couples who have just been "living together," you're going to have
to buck up and divide community property as you see fit. There is no
standard for people who are just living together. You probably haven't
combined financial accounts, so you get the stuff you brought into the
common household (as they do), and then for the stuff you acquired while
you were together, you see who actually bought it, and they get it.

I suppose that's one of the downsides of not being married.
