But the research hasn't gone far enough to suggest that chimerism is the
cause of MS.

And then I read this:

[https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/\...(https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=55b643&url=https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17531842/){.external_link
rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank"

And the abstract is telling (emphasis mine):

> Scientific advances have demonstrated that autoreactive cells are a
> component of the healthy immune repertoire. If we define autoimmunity
> as an active induction of autoreaction, the solution should be an
> active induction of self-tolerance, and may indicate the direction to
> explore the future therapies. Microchimerism (MC) refers to the
> presence of a limited number of nonhost cells in the body of an
> individual. These cells can enter via blood transfusion and organ
> transplantation or naturally through pregnancy. **Chimeric cells
> engraft in the host body, develop, proliferate, and are accepted by
> the immune system as self.** These include stem cells that enter the
> maternal body during fetal stages. These stem cells are also
> postulated to be helpful reservoirs in protecting the host body. **MC
> has been considered a risk factor in autoimmune disease induction.
> However, today we know it is a natural phenomenon.** MC can be
> considered a natural model of successful transplantation, the earliest
> engrafting cells being fetal mesenchymal stem cells (MSCs). MSCs have
> two notable features. They have an immunosuppressive quality when
> encountering the adoptive immune system and they display
> repair-inducing potential within damaged tissues.

So the first thing we learned:

**Chimeric cells engraft in the host body, develop, proliferate, and are
accepted by the immune system as self.**

Therefore, your theory of microchimeric cells as viewed by the immune
system as invaders and thus as a trigger for disease process is
incorrect.

The next thing we learned:

**MC has been considered a risk factor in autoimmune disease induction.
However, today we know it is a natural phenomenon.**

It's even considered to be a "natural model" of successful
transplantation.

So microchimeric cells are indeed being studied, but not exactly in the
way you thought. They are definitely being studied in conjunction with
mesenchymal stem cells to investigate mostly if treatments like HSCT
might be beneficial for people with MS.
