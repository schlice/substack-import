The Court did a great job of breaking down the concept of "equity
jurisprudence." They basically said that, in essence, only the person
who sues can seek equity. Equity cannot be extended to people who did
not sue, even if they may be harmed by the action in question.

> The Judiciary Act of 1789 endowed federal courts with jurisdiction
> over "all suits . . . in equity," *ยง11, 1 Stat. 78*, and still today,
> this statute "is what authorizes the federal courts to issue equitable
> remedies," *S. Bray & E. Sherwin, Remedies 442 (4th ed. 2024).* Though
> flexible, this equitable authority is not freewheeling.
>
> \...\
>
> The Court's early refusals to grant relief to nonparties are
> consistent with the party-specific principles that permeate the
> Court's understanding of equity. "\N\either declaratory nor
> injunctive relief," the Court has said, "can directly interfere with
> enforcement of contested statutes or ordinances except with respect to
> the particular federal plaintiffs." *Doran v. Salem Inn, Inc., 422 U.
> S. 922, 931.*
>
> from TRUMP, PRESIDENT OF THE UNITED STATES, ET AL. v. CASA, INC., ET
> AL.

This puts to rest the practice of district courts granting nationwide
injunctions against Executive action. Crucially, it does not mean that
people cannot sue if they believe that the way the Executive action
affects them is unconstitutional. It also does not prevent **class
action** lawsuits from proceeding, to the extent that they can. The
order merely prevents district courts from overstepping their bounds and
imposing nationwide injunctions, as the court found that such power
likely oversteps the authority granted to district courts by Congress.

\

To the next part of your question, this decision is separate from the
question about birthright citizenship. This decision actually had
nothing to do with the question of birthright citizenship. It only
addressed the issue of nationwide injunctions. The Constitutionality of
Trump's Executive Order is still undecided.
