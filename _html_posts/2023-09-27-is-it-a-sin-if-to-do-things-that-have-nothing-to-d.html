--- title: '"Is it a sin if to do things that have nothing to do with God like playing games, chopping trees, etc.?"' date: '2023-09-27' tags: - theology layout: post --- Quora Answer 





Answer









Content: 

No, it is not a sin. But we are not instructed to do *only* things that
have something to do with God. **We are instructed to do all things for
the glory of God.**

In everything we do, do it for the glory of God.

There is an amazing writeup by Jon Bloom over at
[desiringGod.org(https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=0a3b06&url=http://desiringGod.org){.external_link
rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank"
onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);"
qt-tooltip="desiringgod.org"}]{.qlink_container}, and I want to
reproduce that here. It's totally readable, so take the time to read it.

> Explain this verse in your own words: "So, whether you eat or drink,
> or whatever you do, do all to the glory of God." If someone walked up
> to you right now and asked you how [[1 Corinthians
> 10:31](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=a808a1&url=https://biblia.com/bible/esv/1%2520Cor%252010.31){.external_link
> rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank"
> onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);"
> qt-tooltip="biblia.com"}]{.qlink_container} worked --- in eating, in
> drinking, in everything --- how would you respond? Do you know what
> Paul really meant?
>
> The verse is so familiar, we can easily assume we understand it, even
> if we don't. By itself the verse's meaning seems patently obvious:
> *glorify God in everything you do*. Well, of course that's true at the
> highest level. But what does Paul specifically mean by *glorify God*,
> and what does he mean by *everything*?
>
> If our primary application of this verse is thanking God for the tasty
> pizza we're eating, we haven't understood Paul --- even though he
> certainly would want us to thank God for the tasty pizza we're eating
> ([[1 Corinthians
> 10:30](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=f88af3&url=https://biblia.com/bible/esv/1%2520Cor%252010.30){.external_link
> rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank"
> onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);"
> qt-tooltip="biblia.com"}]{.qlink_container}). Paul has something quite
> specific in mind --- something quite relevant to us. When we look at
> the verse in its wider context, we see that Paul's command to do all
> to the glory of God relates to cultural idols, the Christian
> conscience, and how we live before an unbelieving world.
>
> A Wonderful, New Freedom
>
> Paul begins his point in chapter 8. There we discover that food was a
> major issue of Christian liberty in the Corinthian church ---
> specifically, "food offered to idols" ([[1 Corinthians
> 8:1](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=fb783d&url=https://biblia.com/bible/esv/1%2520Cor%25208.1){.external_link
> rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank"
> onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);"
> qt-tooltip="biblia.com"}]{.qlink_container}). All the Corinthian
> Christians (except perhaps the Jewish ones) would have had backgrounds
> in pagan idol worship. When they became Christians, they renounced
> these idols and all the expressions of worship associated with them.
>
> The problem was that idol worship was woven into the very fabric of
> Corinthian civic, trade, and social life --- it was culturally
> pervasive. Idol temples were social centers, and could function
> similarly to public restaurants ([[1 Corinthians
> 8:10](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=8c63e1&url=https://biblia.com/bible/esv/1%2520Cor%25208.10){.external_link
> rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank"
> onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);"
> qt-tooltip="biblia.com"}]{.qlink_container}). And much of the meat
> sold in the markets and served in homes had been ritually offered to
> idols ([[1 Corinthians
> 10:25](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=74f325&url=https://biblia.com/bible/esv/1%2520Cor%252010.25){.external_link
> rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank"
> onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);"
> qt-tooltip="biblia.com"}]{.qlink_container},
> [27(https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=636482&url=https://biblia.com/bible/esv/1%2520Corinthians%252010.27){.external_link
> rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank"
> onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);"
> qt-tooltip="biblia.com"}]{.qlink_container}). That meant eating meat
> could be interpreted as an act of idolatry, a betrayal of Christian
> beliefs ([[1 Corinthians
> 8:10](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=8c63e1&url=https://biblia.com/bible/esv/1%2520Cor%25208.10){.external_link
> rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank"
> onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);"
> qt-tooltip="biblia.com"}]{.qlink_container}).
>
> Wonderfully, however, some Corinthian Christians were discovering that
> "an idol has no real existence, and that there is no God but one" ([[1
> Corinthians
> 8:4](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=cffccf&url=https://biblia.com/bible/esv/1%2520Cor%25208.4){.external_link
> rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank"
> onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);"
> qt-tooltip="biblia.com"}]{.qlink_container}). Since idols were *no
> real things*, they realized that meat sacrificed to idols was meat
> sacrificed to *nothing* ([[1 Corinthians
> 10:19--20](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=b5abdb&url=https://biblia.com/bible/esv/1%2520Cor%252010.19%25E2%2580%259320){.external_link
> rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank"
> onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);"
> qt-tooltip="biblia.com"}]{.qlink_container}). Therefore, eating meat
> sacrificed to idols could not be idolatry if the people eating knew
> that idols weren't real. They were free to eat this meat with a clear
> conscience! Paul agreed with them ([[1 Corinthians
> 10:26](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=9154eb&url=https://biblia.com/bible/esv/1%2520Cor%252010.26){.external_link
> rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank"
> onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);"
> qt-tooltip="biblia.com"}]{.qlink_container},
> [29(https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=9f9d1b&url=https://biblia.com/bible/esv/1%2520Corinthians%252010.29){.external_link
> rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank"
> onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);"
> qt-tooltip="biblia.com"}]{.qlink_container}).
>
> Freedom's Power to Destroy
>
> Paul did not agree, however, with how some of them were exercising
> this newfound Christian freedom. In effect, some of the Corinthians
> had placed a higher value on enjoying this freedom than on the
> spiritual good of other souls. First, not all the Corinthian
> Christians "possessed this knowledge" ([[1 Corinthians
> 8:7](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=dde9a3&url=https://biblia.com/bible/esv/1%2520Cor%25208.7){.external_link
> rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank"
> onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);"
> qt-tooltip="biblia.com"}]{.qlink_container}). Some of them, perhaps
> newer converts or those who, for whatever reason, had tender
> consciences, still felt like eating meat sacrificed to an idol was a
> form of idolatrous worship. For them, to eat sacrificial meat was to
> deny Christ.
>
> Second, others, who may have even believed idols were nonentities,
> would face temptation to a different kind of idolatry by eating such
> meat. Many Corinthian converts likely paid a high price to become
> Christians. Renouncing the false pagan religion(s) meant renouncing
> social customs, family traditions, and friendship networks. Some, no
> doubt, lost their jobs. You can imagine the temptation some
> experienced to give at least an appearance of homage to the prevailing
> religion in order to avoid losing employment, social status, and
> family disapproval.
>
> Third, there was the issue of gospel witness among non-Christians who
> were watching the Christians. What would pagans think of Christians
> who knowingly ate meat sacrificed to idols? They would likely assume
> that the Christians venerated the idols just like they did, and
> therefore there was no real reason to give heed to Christians' odd
> claims. And what would Jews think of this behavior? That Christians
> were pagans and that Christianity was demonic.
>
> So, Paul strongly reminded the Corinthians that far more was at stake
> than enjoying sacrificial steaks. If Christians whose consciences were
> free to eat meat sacrificed to idols weren't very careful, the
> exercise of their freedom could destroy the faith of another Christian
> ([[1 Corinthians
> 8:9--11](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=ac02cd&url=https://biblia.com/bible/esv/1%2520Cor%25208.9%25E2%2580%259311){.external_link
> rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank"
> onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);"
> qt-tooltip="biblia.com"}]{.qlink_container}) or ruin Jesus's
> reputation among non-Christians ([[1 Corinthians
> 10:27--29](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=ed6887&url=https://biblia.com/bible/esv/1%2520Cor%252010.27%25E2%2580%259329){.external_link
> rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank"
> onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);"
> qt-tooltip="biblia.com"}]{.qlink_container}).
>
> True Christian Freedom
>
> This is why Paul said, "If food makes my brother stumble, I will never
> eat meat, lest I make my brother stumble" ([[1 Corinthians
> 8:13](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=c2ce05&url=https://biblia.com/bible/esv/1%2520Cor%25208.13){.external_link
> rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank"
> onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);"
> qt-tooltip="biblia.com"}]{.qlink_container}). And then he went on to
> describe throughout chapter 9 many ways he voluntarily abstained from
> things he was free to enjoy as a Christian --- not to mention an
> apostle --- like various kinds of food and drink, marriage, and a
> full-time ministry salary ([[1 Corinthians
> 9:4--7](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=59ac18&url=https://biblia.com/bible/esv/1%2520Cor%25209.4%25E2%2580%25937){.external_link
> rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank"
> onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);"
> qt-tooltip="biblia.com"}]{.qlink_container}).
>
> Paul's whole orientation in life was to win as many people to the
> gospel as possible ([[1 Corinthians
> 9:22--23](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=a08396&url=https://biblia.com/bible/esv/1%2520Cor%25209.22%25E2%2580%259323){.external_link
> rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank"
> onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);"
> qt-tooltip="biblia.com"}]{.qlink_container}), so he sought to remove
> as many obstacles to the gospel as possible ([[1 Corinthians
> 9:12](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=27132f&url=https://biblia.com/bible/esv/1%2520Cor%25209.12){.external_link
> rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank"
> onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);"
> qt-tooltip="biblia.com"}]{.qlink_container}). For Paul, this was
> Christian freedom: "For though I am free from all, I have made myself
> a servant to all, that I might win more of them" ([[1 Corinthians
> 9:19](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=bcc7e7&url=https://biblia.com/bible/esv/1%2520Cor%25209.19){.external_link
> rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank"
> onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);"
> qt-tooltip="biblia.com"}]{.qlink_container}). So, when Paul heard that
> the Corinthian Christians were arguing over whether or not they were
> free to eat sacrificial meat, he essentially told them they were
> missing the point:
>
> "All things are lawful," but not all things are helpful. "All things
> are lawful," but not all things build up. Let no one seek his own
> good, but the good of his neighbor. ([[1 Corinthians
> 10:23--24](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=4ba623&url=https://biblia.com/bible/esv/1%2520Cor%252010.23%25E2%2580%259324){.external_link
> rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank"
> onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);"
> qt-tooltip="biblia.com"}]{.qlink_container})
>
> To Paul, this was true Christian freedom: to do whatever it takes to
> love one's neighbor for the sake of Jesus.
>
> Do All to the Glory of God
>
> This is what Paul had in mind when he wrote, "So, whether you eat or
> drink, or whatever you do, do all to the glory of God" ([[1
> Corinthians
> 10:31](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=a808a1&url=https://biblia.com/bible/esv/1%2520Cor%252010.31){.external_link
> rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank"
> onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);"
> qt-tooltip="biblia.com"}]{.qlink_container}). We glorify God when, out
> of love for him, we lay down our rights, our freedoms, in eating or
> drinking or *whatever* in order to do what is most loving toward
> others, either for the "progress and joy \of their\ faith"
> ([[Philippians
> 1:25](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=d33830&url=https://biblia.com/bible/esv/Phil%25201.25){.external_link
> rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank"
> onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);"
> qt-tooltip="biblia.com"}]{.qlink_container}), or that they may be
> saved ([[1 Corinthians
> 9:22](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=e7797d&url=https://biblia.com/bible/esv/1%2520Cor%25209.22){.external_link
> rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank"
> onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);"
> qt-tooltip="biblia.com"}]{.qlink_container}). Paul's very next
> sentence says, "Give no offense to Jews or to Greeks or to the church
> of God" ([[1 Corinthians
> 10:32](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=592180&url=https://biblia.com/bible/esv/1%2520Cor%252010.32){.external_link
> rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank"
> onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);"
> qt-tooltip="biblia.com"}]{.qlink_container}).
>
> Now, back to our tasty pizza. God is certainly glorified when we
> wholeheartedly enjoy the fullness of the earth he created for our
> enjoyment ([[1 Corinthians
> 10:26](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=9154eb&url=https://biblia.com/bible/esv/1%2520Cor%252010.26){.external_link
> rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank"
> onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);"
> qt-tooltip="biblia.com"}]{.qlink_container}). Paul was a great
> advocate for our freedom from all false, legalistic abstinence from
> food or anything else ([[1 Timothy
> 4:1--3](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=2633de&url=https://biblia.com/bible/esv/1%2520Tim%25204.1%25E2%2580%25933){.external_link
> rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank"
> onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);"
> qt-tooltip="biblia.com"}]{.qlink_container}). He stated it clearly:
> "Food will not commend us to God" ([[1 Corinthians
> 8:8](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=3ab02b&url=https://biblia.com/bible/esv/1%2520Cor%25208.8){.external_link
> rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank"
> onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);"
> qt-tooltip="biblia.com"}]{.qlink_container}). And "everything created
> by God is good, and nothing is to be rejected if it is received with
> thanksgiving, for it is made holy by the word of God and prayer" ([[1
> Timothy
> 4:4--5](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=1ae847&url=https://biblia.com/bible/esv/1%2520Tim%25204.4%25E2%2580%25935){.external_link
> rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank"
> onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);"
> qt-tooltip="biblia.com"}]{.qlink_container}). So, Paul would take no
> offense by our applying [[1 Corinthians
> 10:31](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=a808a1&url=https://biblia.com/bible/esv/1%2520Cor%252010.31){.external_link
> rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank"
> onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);"
> qt-tooltip="biblia.com"}]{.qlink_container} to savoring our pizza ---
> provided that we have not lost sight of the more excellent way of
> glorifying God: sacrificial love.
>
> And this kind of sacrificial love is still needed, maybe especially
> needed, when it comes to Christian freedoms. For we, too, have our
> cultural idols, our saints with tender consciences, and our watching
> unbelievers. So, in "whatever you do," do not use your freedom to
> merely pursue what you feel free to enjoy, but use your freedom to
> pursue the ultimate spiritual good of your neighbor. As a Christian,
> you are free from all constraints: the external constraints of false
> religion and the internal constraints of your selfishness. You are
> free to enjoy all God has provided, and free to abstain for the sake
> of love. Do all you do to the glory of God.\[1\](#lJkOE){#cite-lJkOE
> .citation_link}

So if by you playing games or chopping trees you give the impression
that you honor other gods besides Him, you shouldn't do those things.
Like Jon says, "\D\o not use your freedom to merely pursue what you
feel free to enjoy, but use your freedom to pursue the ultimate
spiritual good of your neighbor." So while you may be free to do it, you
must make sure that you don't unintentionally hurt others' faith walks
in so doing.

Do all you do to the glory of God.
