Answer









Content: [I\'m going to take the opposite stance and say that it\'s
unlikely a court case would give you PTSD. The
[definition(https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=75868b&url=http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Posttraumatic_stress_disorder){.external_link
rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank"
onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);"
qt-tooltip="wikipedia.org"}]{.qlink_container} of PTSD:\
\
]

> Posttraumatic stress disorder (PTSD) may develop after a
> person is exposed to one or more traumatic events, such as sexual
> assault, warfare, serious injury, or threats of imminent death. The
> diagnosis may be given when a group of symptoms, such as disturbing
> recurring flashbacks, avoidance or numbing of memories of the event,
> and hyperarousal, continue for more than a month after the occurrence
> of a traumatic event\

\
It goes on to say that people who experience assault-based trauma are
likely to develop PTSD than those who experience non-assault-based
trauma (the difference between being assaulted vs witnessing an
assault). The individual in question must have witnessed or have been a
victim of an extreme stressor, such as the ones listed in the
definition. The individual would have felt intense fear or horror, or a
feeling of powerlessness to control such an event.\
\
While I absolutely agree that the court cases cited by
[Natalie(https://www.quora.com/profile/Natalie-Smolenski)]{.qlink_container}
would be traumatic, they would not rise to the level of PTSD.
