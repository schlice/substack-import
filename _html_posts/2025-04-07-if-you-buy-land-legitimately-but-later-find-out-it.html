--- title: '"If you buy land legitimately but later find out it was illegally sold religious property, can you get your money back?"' date: '2025-04-07' tags: - policy-analysis - law layout: post --- Quora Answer

Content: 

The answer, as with all legal answers, is "it depends."

The situation here likely involves what is known as a *title defect*,
meaning that the title to the land, when you legally bought it, was
either incorrect or was fraudulently produced. The seller may have
fraudulently produced a title, or the title was real but the seller
didn't have the authority to sell the property. In general, when you buy
titled land, your lender requires that you buy title insurance to
protect their investment against problems just like this. In this case,
the title insurance company would likely be on the hook to repay you for
your loss, but not all title insurance policies are the same and title
insurance may not cover every situation. It's important to check the
title insurance policy for specifics.

But if the title was legally transferred (as the question is asking) and
your name is now on the title to the property, and that deed was filed
with the appropriate governing body, **the previous owner cannot just
take the land back unless you sign a new deed granting them ownership,
either by gift or by sale. I**f that deed is real and is signed and
recorded with the registered deeds in that jurisdiction, the land is
legally yours. There\'s no way the previous owner could just call and
take it back. You would have to execute a new deed to transfer the land
back to the person who gave it to you, either by gift or by sale.

The rightful owner could, of course, reclaim the property through a
lawsuit to rescind the sale. If the sale is rescinded, then the rightful
owner gets the property back, and you get your money back. Your rights
may be limited depending on whether you knew the sale was fraudulent or
if you acted in good faith.

This is why if you're a property owner, properly recording the deed with
the Recorder of Deeds can help prevent fraudulent sales and establish
clear ownership. In some jurisdictions, whoever records the deed first
legally owns the property, regardless of whether the buyer knew the sale
was fraudulent. In other jurisdictions, the buyer\'s good faith purchase
and lack of knowledge of the fraudulent sale can protect them.

The seller who illegally sold the property may face legal consequences,
including criminal charges and civil lawsuits.
