Content: 

[[Arthur
Radford](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=ac78be&url=https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthur_W._Radford){.external_link
rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank"
onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);"
qt-tooltip="wikipedia.org"}]{.qlink_container} penned the following in
1954:

> Today, air power is the dominant factor in war. It may not win a war
> by itself, but without it, no major war could be won.

63 years later and the message is the same. The army doesn't matter,
whether it's the US Army or the British Army. If you're going to win a
war, you must have control of the air. Period. No army is "too"
dependent on air power. *Every* army depends on air power. If they
don't, it's because they don't really want to win.

So the message is clear: you must control the air to win the war; no
major war can be won without controlling the air.
