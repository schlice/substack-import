You have only cited one half of the Constitutional issue, the War Powers
Clause, which states that Congress has the power to declare war.

The other side of the Constitutional issue is Article I, Section 2,
Clause 1: "The President is Commander-in-Chief of the Army and Navy, of
the United States, and of the Militia of the several States, when called
into the actual Service of the United States ." This means that the
President is in charge of the military, not Congress, and the President
can issue orders to the military, including orders to strike another
nation. Presidents have used this authority with impunity for some time,
committing troops without Congressional approval.

Congress, after the Korean conflict, drew up the [[War Powers
Resolution](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=c9fc07&url=https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/War_Powers_Resolution){.external_link
rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank"
onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);"
qt-tooltip="wikipedia.org"}]{.qlink_container} (also called the "War
Powers Act").

> The War Powers Resolution requires the President to notify Congress
> within 48 hours of committing armed forces to military action and
> forbids armed forces from remaining for more than 60 days, with a
> further 30-day withdrawal period, without a Congressional
> authorization for use of military force (AUMF) or a declaration of war
> by the United States. The resolution was passed by two-thirds of
> Congress, overriding the veto of the bill from President
> Nixon.\[1\](#qphKC){#cite-qphKC .citation_link}

This means, dear questioner, that the President *can* commit troops,
but he has to get authorization from Congress in order to *keep them
there.* Congress has drafted *Authorizations of the Use of Military
Force (AUMF)* in the past (most recently for the Global War on Terror)
which the Supreme Court has ruled are a valid substitute for a
declaration of war, and fulfill the requirements of the War Powers
Resolution as "approval" by Congress.
