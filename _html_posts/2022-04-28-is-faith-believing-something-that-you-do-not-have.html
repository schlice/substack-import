This is a common misconception about faith, usually promulgated by
people who are not believers themselves, but sometimes from believers
who believe without *knowledge of* the good reasons behind their beliefs
(not that those reasons don't exist).

**Faith is not belief without evidence. Faith is believing what you
already know to be true, *even though there may be unanswered
questions remaining*.**

Let's give an example to aid in our understanding. Let's say I see a
chair. I would like to sit in the chair. *But how do I know that the
chair is going to hold my (greater than I'd prefer) weight?* I can look
at the chair, which appears sturdy. I can check to see if all the
fastening hardware is there. I can look at its design, and see if the
materials, in my experience, can support me. I can look around and see
if anyone else is sitting in a similar chair. *But I will never have a
100% guarantee that the chair will support my weight*. At a certain
point, I have no choice but to *have faith* in what I know, based on
the evidence I already have, and sit in the chair.

What does this example teach us? My faith in the ability of the chair to
support me is based on good reasons to believe it. It's based on
empirical and observational evidence, *but without direct evidence of
this chair's construction or ability to actually do what I need it to
do.* But I have faith that what I know about chairs in general, and what
I can determine about this chair in specific, will appropriately guide
me into making the correct choice. And so, I can proceed with faith that
what I know sufficiently helps me understand what I don't.

We can apply the same logic to religion as well. My religious faith is
also based on lots of empirical and observational evidence, but without
direct evidence of God's existence. At a certain point, the amount of
evidence I have must be enough to allow me to have faith that what I
believe is true.

Is faith believing something that you do not have good reasons to
