Good on you for stretching your evangelical legs.

I think first you need to define terms. What will they accept as
"proof?" Is the only proof they will accept discovered through the
scientific method? When they look for "proof," does that mean only
something that they can see, hear, taste, touch or smell?

That certainly wouldn't make sense. If God exists, then He is
*necessarily* immaterial (He exists outside of the material universe
which He created). So if God is not material, why would you use material
tests to try to find Him? It's like using an X-ray machine to find a
plastic cup. It's the wrong tools.

So if you're going to prove that God exists, you have to use tools of
inquiry which are *not* based on the material. Those tools of inquiry
are collectively part of the discipline of *philosophy*. Philosophy
allows us to probe truths that are beyond what science is capable of
understanding.

But let's not fail to realize that philosophy is not divorced from
science, as if you can rely on science alone, and philosophy is just
pseudo-babble for PhDs to discuss while smoking pipes in dimly-lit
rooms. Philosophy is the *foundation* of science. Science is dependent
on philosophy. In fact, *science is a slave to philosophy.* Bad
philosophy results in bad science, and good science requires good
philosophy.

Why? Because:

> 1.  Science cannot be done without philosophy. Science is really
>     nothing more than a search for causes. Scientists try to figure
>     out what causes what. Philosophical assumptions are utilized in
>     the search for causes, and, therefore, cannot be the result of
>     them. For example, scientists assume (by faith) that reason and
>     the scientific method allow us to accurately understand the world
>     around us. That cannot be proven by science itself. You can't
>     prove the tools of science---the laws of logic, the Law of
>     Causality, the Principle of Uniformity, or the reliability of
>     observation---by running some kind of experiment. You have to
>     assume those things are true in order to do the experiment. So
>     science is built on philosophy. Unfortunately, many so-called
>     scientists are very poor philosophers.
> 2.  **Philosophical assumptions can dramatically impact scientific
>     conclusions**. If a scientist assumes beforehand that only natural
>     causes are possible, then probably no amount of evidence will
>     convince him that intelligence created the first one-celled amoeba
>     or any other designed entity. When Darwinists presuppose that
>     intelligent causes are impossible, then natural laws are the only
>     game in town. Likewise, if a creationist rules out natural causes
>     beforehand (and we don't know of any who do), then he also risks
>     missing the right answer. But a scientist who is open-minded to
>     both natural and intelligent causes can follow the evidence
>     wherever it leads.
> 3.  Science doesn't really say anything---scientists do. Data are
>     always interpreted by scientists. When those scientists let their
>     personal preferences or unproven philosophical assumptions dictate
>     their interpretation of the evidence, they do exactly what they
>     accuse religious people of doing---they let their ideology dictate
>     their conclusions. When that's the case, their conclusions should
>     be questioned, because they may be nothing more than philosophical
>     presuppositions passed off as scientific
>     facts.\[1\](#VxqpY){#cite-VxqpY .citation_link}

Once we have determined that we have to use philosophy to discover the
truth of God's existence, we next need to learn the *arguments* for the
existence of God.

If you really want to learn this stuff, there is a great book you should
absolutely read. It is by Dr. Norm Geisler and Dr. Frank Turek, and it
is called *I Don't Have Enough Faith To Be an Atheist*. It is not a
difficult read (so it's not like you're learning about Kierkegaard or
Freud in school), and it lays out the philosophical and scientific
truths which act as the "signposts" that point us to the existence of
God. These signposts alone don't prove that God exists, but they lead us
to the knowledge that we can be certain, *beyond a reasonable doubt,*
*but not beyond all doubt**, that a creator God exists.*
