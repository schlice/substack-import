Answer









Content: 

It's difficult to say, because success is dictated by lots of factors,
and varies by the disease being treated. Additionally, adult stem cells
have been very successful, while embryonic stem cells have yet to find a
single success in treatment. Stem cell therapy is not an approved
therapy for every disease or condition, so there is no "overall success
rate" for stem cell therapy. However, in general, we can say that adult
stem cells induced into pluripotency have been treating diseases
effectively for many years now, treating dozens of diseases and
disorders.

Specifically in my case, stem cells have shown great promise in a
procedure called Hematopoietic Stem Cell Therapy (HSCT). You've probably
heard of it referred to as a "bone marrow transplant." Here's how it
works:

> HSCT (Hematopoietic Stem Cell Transplantation) attempts to
> "reboot" the immune system, which is responsible for damaging the
> brain and spinal cord in MS. In HSCT for MS, hematopoietic (blood
> cell-producing) stem cells, which are derived from a person's own
> (scientifically referred to as "autologous") bone marrow or blood, are
> collected and stored, prior to depleting much of the immune system
> using chemotherapy drugs. Then the stored hematopoietic stem cells are
> reintroduced to the body. The new stem cells migrate to the bone
> marrow and over time reconstitute the immune
> system.\[1\](#CLzOj){#cite-CLzOj .citation_link}

HSCT has shown some success in treating MS, but not 100% success. We are
finding that HSCT is very effective against certain forms of MS, and is
less effective against others.
