How do doctors tell what form of multiple sclerosis you have?

Doctors will use a variety of techniques to diagnose a patient.
Ultimately, diagnosis of MS is a diagnosis of exclusion --- we exclude
everything else that can cause the signs and symptoms reported and
discovered. When we've done that, the only thing remaining is MS.

The [[McDonald
Criteria](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=9cdf98&url=https://mstrust.org.uk/a-z/mcdonald-criteria){.external_link
rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank"
onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);"
qt-tooltip="mstrust.org.uk"}]{.qlink_container} specifies what signs and
symptoms constitute a finding of MS, but much of what constitutes the
different forms of MS comes from differing symptoms, and how the patient
experiences those symptoms, so a complete medical history will be taken.

The gold standard of diagnostic tools in multiple sclerosis is the MRI.
It can see the lesions caused by MS disease activity. Contrast material
can also indicate active disease activity. So the MRI can be used to add
to the body of evidence which shows MS.

But there are lots of diseases and conditions which can cause white
spots on an MRI. Doctors will also run a series of blood tests
(colloquially called the "little white spot series") to check for all of
the diseases and conditions which could cause white spots on an MRI.

Doctors have more things they can look for:

- A lumbar puncture (also known as a "spinal tap") can look for
  "oligoclonal bands" in the cerebrospinal fluid (CSF). This is another
  indication of MS disease activity,
- Evoked Potential (EP) tests can show the degradation of nerve signals
  between the time they leave the brain and the time they arrive at the
  muscle.
