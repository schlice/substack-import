> *Question Details:*
>
> *I just read that the crime rate in the Netherlands is falling at such
> a high rate that they announced in 2013 they were closing 19 jails and
> in 2014 announced they are renting out space in their jails to Norway
> to house Norwegian criminals. What is the secret of Dutch society that
> has led to this situation? Can it be applied to other countries with
> similar results?*

I don\'t think that the Netherlands is unique in this regard. Crime has
been falling steadily since the 90s all across the world. Here in the
US, violent crime rates have fallen almost every year since the 80s.
Policing has gotten better and more sophisticated. Crime has become less
profitable than it used to be, so crime goes down (which is the only way
to reduce real crime: make it less profitable).

The United States, however, while seeing the same downward trend of
crime, has not seen the same downward population change in our prisons.
Why? What\'s the largest population of our jails? The answer is that the
largest population of America\'s prisons are not criminals. The
largest population of America\'s jails are *drug addicts.*

So is the answer to make all drugs legal? Not at all; some drugs [[can
and should be made
legal](https://www.quora.com/What-do-police-officers-think-about-legalizing-drugs-Would-it-make-their-jobs-harder-or-easier)]{.qlink_container},
because the decision to make them illegal was a poor one (case in point:
marijuana). Other drugs are the scourge of the earth and should be
obliterated by any means available (cases in point: meth and alcohol).
But drug addicts are in jail because of one reason: the medical
community.

Despite overwhelming evidence in recent years detailing new discoveries
about the brain showing that addiction is a disease of the system that
handles choice, the medical community has not yet declared that drug
addiction is a disease and that addicts should be patients. Instead,
they still hold the opinion that drug addiction is a choice and that
addicts are simply bad actors. This leaves the problem of drug addicts
to the criminal justice system and the courts, when it should be handled
by doctors and hospitals. Thus, the overcrowding and unnecessary
incarceration of drug addicts.

If we can get on the medical community and get them to recognize that
drug addicts should be patients and not inmates, we will instantly
reduce the prison population by half, and we too will have the problem
of too many jails and not enough inmates.

That\'s a great problem to have, by the way.
