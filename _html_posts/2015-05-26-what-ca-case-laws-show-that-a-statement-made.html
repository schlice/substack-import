Content: [People v Sesslin (68 Cal.2d 421)\
\
This is the only case law in California I can find talking about
information and belief, and it is in relation to issuance of warrants.\
\
The case: Defendant and Frank Harper were charged with seven counts of
forgery and two counts of grand theft. Defendant and Harper waived trial
by jury and stipulated that the trial judge decide the case on the
transcript of the preliminary hearing. The trial court found defendant
guilty of one count of forgery and dismissed the other eight counts
against defendant. Defendant appealed.\
\
The CA Supreme Court held, among other things, that an arrest warrant
issued solely upon the complainant\'s \"information and belief\" cannot
stand if the complaint or an accompanying affidavit does not allege
underlying facts upon which the magistrate can independently find
probable cause to arrest the accused, and sections 806, 813, and 952 of
the Penal Code do not authorize the issuance of warrants of arrest based
solely upon complaints couched in the language of the charged offense
and therefore do not violate the Fourth Amendment.\
\
The only other item I might be able to offer (at a Federal level) is the
Private Securities Litigation Reform Act of 1995 which contains strict
requirements for pleading allegations on information and belief. It
states that plaintiffs must \"state with particularity all facts on
which that belief is formed.\" The most recent example of a case citing
this law was *in re Silicon Graphics* (*In re* Silicon Graphics, Inc.
Sec. Litig., 183 F.3d 970, 985 (9th Cir. 1999)). In that case, the court
explained "that a plaintiff must provide, in great detail, all the
relevant facts forming the basis of her belief. It is not sufficient for
plaintiff\'s pleadings to set forth a belief that certain unspecified
sources will reveal, after appropriate discovery, facts that will
validate her claim."\
\
So under both California and Federal law, claims based on \"information
and belief\" don\'t hold much weight.]
