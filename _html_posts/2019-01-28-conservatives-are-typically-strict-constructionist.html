**Conservatives are typically strict constructionists, so why do they
support Bible literacy classes in public schools?**

Let me phrase your question more neutrally:

**Does a Constitutional strict constructionist support Bible literacy
classes in public schools?**

The answer to this question is yes, strict constructionists do
support Bible literacy classes in public schools, since there is nothing
in the Constitution that prohibits it.

Note that there is a vast difference between *teaching* ***a
subject* and *advancing a particular religious belief.***

Remember this fact: **it is constitutional for a school to teach the
Bible; it is unconstitutional for a school to advance any particular
religious belief.**

In 1999, a guide endorsed by both religious and secular groups was
written entitled *The Bible in Public Schools: A First Amendment Guide.*
This guide has a number of important statements for any school wishing
to teach about any particular religious text and stay constitutional:

> The school\'s approach to religion is *academic*, not *devotional*.
>
> The school may strive for student *awareness* of religions, but should
> not press for student *acceptance* of any religion.
>
> The school may sponsor *study* about religion, but may not sponsor the
> *practice* of religion.
>
> The school may *educate* about all religions, but may not *promote or
> denigrate* any religion.\[1\](#jualZ){#cite-jualZ .citation_link}
