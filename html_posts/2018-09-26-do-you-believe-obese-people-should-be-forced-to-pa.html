**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **Do you believe obese people should be forced to pay for an extra seat when flying?** The problem with obesity is that it sometimes is uncontrollable, and it is sometimes behavioral but controllable. So it's difficult to talk about what obese people must do, because it seems to be intrinsically unfair to the obese people who have no control over their weight. So let's change the terminology a little and see if we can understand what needs to be done. What do *extremely tall people* do to fly? Your height is completely out of your control, so it's less of a controversial topic than your weight. And tall people have just as many issues these days as obese people, with rapidly declining seat pitch (the distance between seats) and seemingly no way to stop it from continuing without government intervention. So what do extremely tall people do? They either buy seat upgrades to seats with more legroom, avoid flying altogether (some will take the train or a bus), or wedge themselves into a standard seat. But we don't hear calls to *force* them to purchase a premium seat, because in general, a tall person, while personally uncomfortable, doesn't impact the comfort of his surrounding passengers. We only hear calls to force obese passengers to purchase an upgrade because it impacts the comfort of surrounding passengers. I can vouch for that discomfort: I was squished between two obese passengers in the last row of a 737. It was the most claustrophobic, worst flight I have ever taken, and I've flown in aircraft which couldn't land because the turbulence was so bad (and we tried three times). And these two gentlemen felt bad for me. They were very accommodating; they allowed me a few moments of reprieve to get out and air my head during the flight, but getting into and out of those seats was a chore for them too. However, this doesn't change my opinion that *forcing* an obese customer to purchase two seats is not right. If you can fit into the seat and buckle your seatbelt (even with an extender), you can sit there. If you are personally inconvenienced by an obese passenger next to you, talk to a flight attendant. If there is room, the flight attendants have far more flexibility to re-seat you once the doors close than the gate agents. If there isn't room, be certain to express your inconvenience. They'll do what they can to either offer you vouchers or whatever they have the ability to do to make up for your inconvenience. Despite their sometimes uncaring fa√ßade, they know that they have to do everything they can to retain passenger loyalty. Margins in the airline industry are razor thin; I saw a report that said that airlines make around \$4/passenger in profit. The only way they can sustain that sort of business is volume, and for that, you need passengers. They simply can't afford to lose passengers to poor customer service. Now, back to the obese customer. *If they can't fit in the seat,* they will either need to buy an upgraded seat in a section where they can fit into the seats, buy two seats next to each other, or avoid flying. We can always inform the flight crew if we are inconvenienced by another passenger, but we certainly should not be demanding that *others* pay more to aid in *our* comfort.
