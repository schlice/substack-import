**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} I'm a Christian, and yes, that's what it says. And yes, **Jesus did not condemn slavery, because He didn't need to.** As with any historical document, it needs to be read from the context of the community it was addressing. Slave ownership was a common practice long before the time the Mosaic Law was given. The law neither instituted slavery nor ended it. Instead, the law *regulated* it. It gave instructions on how slaves should be treated, but did not outlaw slavery altogether. Some people will indeed choose to criticize the Bible because it did not demand an end to slavery or an immediate overthrow of every deeply-rooted sinful custom of the day. But, as Warren W. Wiersbe pointed out, "The Lord chooses to change people and society gradually, through the ministry of the Holy Spirit and the proclamation of the truth of the Word of God"[\[1\]](#Ubqsl){#cite-Ubqsl .citation_link} Alexander MacLaren wrote that the gospel "meddles directly with no political or social arrangements, but lays down principles which will profoundly affect these, and leaves them to soak into the general mind."[\[2\]](#uyTWh){#cite-uyTWh .citation_link} Christianity was never intended to be a movement for political change. Instead, it works in the hearts and minds of individual Christ followers, who eventually enact social change and affect political policy. Indeed, in the places where Christianity spread and took firm hold, those Christians eventually brought slavery to an end. The fundamentals of the movement which resulted in the eventual end of slavery are indeed in the Bible, which teaches that all men are created by God and made in His image (Genesis 1:27), which condemns those who kidnap and sell a person (Exodus 21:16; cf. 1 Timothy 1:8--10), and which shows that a slave can truly be "a brother in the Lord" (Philemon 1:16). Clearly, following the Bible will naturally lead one to abandon slavery altogether.
