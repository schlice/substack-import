**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} > *How should I quit smoking without triggering a multiple sclerosis > attack?* ***Slowly, cautiously, and under the supervision of your medical team.*** It's well known that smoking not only makes people who were already predisposed to developing MS even more likely to develop it, but it is also well known that quitting smoking will improve your overall MS prognosis. You will progress to disability at a slower rate after you quit, and your relapses will not be nearly as bad nor last as long. The disease itself will also progress at a slower rate. I would highly suggest that you not try to rush the process. Don't quit "cold turkey." Doing so is stressful, and stress brings on relapses. What I **do** recommend is talking to your medical team. Advise them that you want to quit, but relapses often accompany your attempts. They can work with you to lessen the effects of quitting so as to alleviate some of the stress in the process. There are definitely products out there that can help make quitting less painful: antidepressants (bupropion), varenicline, nicotine patches and gum all are available. [\[1\]](#cZuEZ){#cite-cZuEZ .citation_link}Working with your healthcare team (primarily your GP and your neurologist) will be crucial in making sure you can control the side effects from smoking cessation so that you can work through the MS issues. And it may just be possible that in order to quit, you may have to endure a relapse. Remember that a relapse does not indicate disease progression; in other words, you are not making your disease worse by enduring a relapse. However, in the grand scheme of things, it's better to quit than to fear quitting because of a relapse. In other words, **the benefits of quitting far outweigh the risks involved in doing so.** Talk to your medical team, and see if there are supportive therapies that can help you quit, and be successful.
