**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} False equivalence. The assumption underlying your question (which isn't really a question, but instead is a statement that atheists possess logic while theists do not) is that all theists are dispossessed of any ability to think logically or critically and act on that assumption. You presuppose that a theist, in a life-and-death situation, would sit idly by and wait for God to intervene. Of course, this is illogical. God gave us **brains**. He gave us *reason* and *logic*. He expects us to use those. So in a life-and-death situation, he would expect that we would be able to use our brains and figure out the best method to get out, just like the atheist. The truth is that the one who is better in a life-or-death situation is the one who has had leadership training and has been trained to stay cool under pressure. Their religion has no bearing on the situation at that particular moment. The ***difference*** between the two would come after the situation was over. The atheist would shout, "Thank you, Mother Nature, for allowing this brain to spontaneously, accidentally evolve over thousands of years such that I could figure out this life-or-death situation all by my very lonesome! Ha, who am I kidding? Why am I thanking Mother Nature? That's just a child's myth. I just fell together out of spare parts and electricity, so there's no one to thank. If I have to thank something, it will be this purposeless, amoral, and meaningless universe which my logical brain is telling me gave me logic and reason and morals, because chaos creates order, randomness creates fine-tuning, and non-life creates life, and that's perfectly logical! Oh well, off to another personally meaningful but ultimately pointless endeavor. I'll learn and grow from this experience, and I'll teach others about it, but what's the point? We all die anyway, and then there's nothingness for all of eternity." In contrast, the theist would say, "Thank you God for setting the laws of nature in motion such that this brain that allowed me to think my way out of the situation was able to develop. Thank you for giving me this life that you clearly are saying isn't over yet. You still have more work for me to do, and I'll do my best. I'll learn and grow from this experience and I'll teach others about it, and about you, and especially that this life is not the end, and we have more to look forward to for all of eternity." \ Original question: Which person is a better leader in a life and death scenario?
