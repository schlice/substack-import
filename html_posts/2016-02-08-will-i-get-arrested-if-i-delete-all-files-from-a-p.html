**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} A2A Depending on the jurisdiction, [[Christopher Hawk](https://www.quora.com/profile/Christopher-Hawk)]{.qlink_container} is correct for the US and [[Ralph](https://www.quora.com/profile/Ralph-1517)]{.qlink_container} for the UK. The issue here is that a \"public\" computer is not \"owned\" by the \"public.\" A public computer is available for public use, but is still owned, operated, and maintained by some organization. It\'s like calling a restaurant a \"public place.\" It is not. It is \"open to the public,\" but it is still privately owned, and your \"privilege\" to be there can most definitely be revoked by the owner. Therefore, you can be charged with \"Damaging Private Property\" if there is no specific computer-related charge that fits. Most files on a public computer are not personal files, not sensitive in any way, but if you deleted them, the system would fail to function. This causes increased maintenance for the entity which maintains the system, and possibly increased costs if they have to seek outside assistance to get the machine operational again. Also, the machine is out of service while it is being maintained, which degrades the overall service the entity can provide to its customers. These are all reasons that an organization might seek charges against you if you intentionally damage files on their system. At the very least, failing to follow the user agreement would get you barred from future access to those systems; at worst, could get you charged with a crime.
