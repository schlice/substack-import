Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} Let's start with morality and then move to justice. You can answer the morality question all on your own, and you can do it by answering just one question: **from where do we get our morality?** For example, everyone just naturally knows that it's wrong to kill an innocent person. We didn't have to be taught this, we just know it. University of Texas at Austin professor J. Budziszewski writes, "Everyone knows certain principles. There is no land where murder is virtue and gratitude vice." C. S. Lewis, who has written profoundly on this topic in his classic work *Mere Christianity* put it this way: "Think of a country where people were admired for running away in battle, or where a man felt proud of double-crossing all the people who had been kindest to him. You might just as well try to imagine a country where two and two made five." In other words, a "shared morality" exists between people. So I ask again: **from where do we get this shared morality?** If you think we just naturally evolved, then this shared morality must have come from evolution, the universe. But I fail to see how a meaningless, purposeless, amoral universe could produce creatures (us) who are full of shared morality and who strive for meaning and purpose in their lives. That is a principle which makes no sense, and it violates the Law of Causality, which says (among other things) that an effect must resemble its cause. If the universe (clearly) did not provide us with this shared morality, the only other option is that we were created by Something other than the universe, and that Something imbued us with a shared morality. There are moral absolutes. And if you don't believe me, maybe we can start treating *you* unfairly and we'll see how you like it. Your reaction will reveal the Moral Law written on your heart. \ Once you come to realize that a Moral Law exists, you understand that the Moral Law is the unchanging basis for justice. Let me go back to C. S. Lewis, who writes: "\[As an atheist\] my argument against God was that the universe seemed so cruel and unjust. But how had I got this idea of just and unjust? A man does not call a line crooked unless he has some idea of a straight line. What was I comparing this universe with when I called it unjust?" In other words, we can only detect *injustice* because we have an unwavering, unchanging understanding of what *justice* is. You can't know what is evil unless you know what is good. And you can't know what is good unless there is an unchanging standard of goodness which exists outside of yourself. Without that objective standard, any objection to evil is nothing more than your personal opinion. \ Therefore, morality and justice are neither natural phenomenon, nor are they human creations. They are gifts from our Creator who is the one unchanging standard of both morality and justice. He is the standard we emulate, even if we don't recognize that the standard even exists.
