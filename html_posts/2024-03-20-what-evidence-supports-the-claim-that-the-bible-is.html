**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} While we don't have the original copies of the New Testament, we do have *manuscripts*. Think of a manuscript as a photocopy before photocopiers were invented. Someone literally rewrites a paper, by hand, to make a copy. Certainly, that process could introduce errors, but you can determine if those exist by looking at *multiple* manuscripts. Therefore, the more manuscripts we have, the more sure we can be that we have the original text. Here's the cool part: **The New Testament documents have more manuscripts, earlier manuscripts, and more abundantly supported manuscripts than the best ten pieces of classical literature** ***combined.*** :::: ui_qtext_image_outer ::: qtext_image_wrapper ![](images/qimg-fe74ced8ab2f1eb09b19a6b6b12dc1a3){.portrait .qtext_image .zoomable_in_feed master_src="images/qimg-fe74ced8ab2f1eb09b19a6b6b12dc1a3" master_w="749" master_h="1001"} ::: :::: This chart shows us the number of manuscript copies for the New Testament as well as other ancient documents, and it shows the timespan between the original and the earliest surviving copies of that document. You can see that of all of these early documents, the New Testament has more and better manuscript support than anything else from that era. So is it likely that there is mistranslation? No. We have reliable copies of the originals, so we know what they said. Modern technology helps us as well. In the age of digitization, we have all of this stuff online (of course). [[https://biblehub.com/](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=3bdeb0&url=https://biblehub.com/){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);"}]{.qlink_container} \ Part of what you may be thinking of when you think of "mistranslation" is actually related to the differences between languages. There is not a "one-to-one" correlation between languages. For example, Hebrew has fewer words than English. Therefore, many words in Hebrew have multiple meanings. It's up to the translator to pick the correct word in English to express what the original author was trying to say in Hebrew. Similarly, there are no fewer than six different words for "love" in Greek (familial love, platonic love, love between dear friends, erotic love, etc.), but in English, we only have "love." Therefore, when creating a Bible translation, you can either stick very closely to the original words, but you may have a hard time understanding the meaning of what the original author was trying to say, or you can reproduce what the author was trying to say, and stick less closely to the original words. Translations which stick very closely to the words are called "word for word" translations. They will very closely retain fidelity to the original words used, but some of the original meaning may be "lost in translation." To try to account for this, some translations will use a "thought for thought" method of translating. The translator will read a sentence and then re-write it in the target language, trying to retain the original words as often as possible, but will more frequently try to retain the original thought that a person reading it in the source language would have understood. Finally, translators may take a "paraphrase" method of translation, where translators will read an entire passage and re-state it in the target language, without concern to the original words, solely trying to "paraphrase" the original idea. As you move from "word for word" translations towards "paraphrase" translations, the translations become more easily understandable and easier to follow along (in fact, some of the paraphrase translations, like *The Message*, read like a novel), but retain less fidelity to the original words used.
