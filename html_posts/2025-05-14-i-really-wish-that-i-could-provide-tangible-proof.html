**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} Why do you feel the need to provide tangible proof of God's existence? Why can't you believe in God without tangible evidence? I posit that there are plenty of things that *even atheists* believe in without tangible evidence that they exist. In fact, there are plenty of things that we are very rational to believe in despite the fact that there is no scientific evidence to prove them: 1. Math and logic. Science can't prove them because science *presupposes them.* 2. Metaphysical truths, such as there are minds which exist outside of my own. 3. Ethical judgements. There is no scientific test you can do to prove that the Nazis were evil. 4. Aesthetic judgements. The beautiful, like the good, cannot be proven through science. 5. and ironically...Science itself. The belief that the scientific method accurately produces truth cannot be proven using the scientific method itself. You can't prove the tools of science (the laws of logic, the Law of Causality, the Principle of Uniformity, or the reliability of observation) by running some kind of experiment. You have to assume those things are true in order to do the experiment! So the need for "tangible proof" of God's existence is a bit ironic, since there are plenty of things we are very rational to believe in even without "tangible proof." We are very rational to believe that sunsets are beautiful and that the Nazis were evil, despite the fact that we can't provide tangible proof that "beautiful" and "evil" exist. \ Secondly, "tangible proof" is a bit silly, wouldn't you say? If God does exist, He is necessarily intangible. He's not made of stuff we can detect, because He *created* the stuff so can't be *made* of the stuff He *created*, because then He would have to exist before He existed. So you're never going to find one of God's hairs, or see one of God's footprints. Those things do not exist. But just because we don't have "tangible" proof that God exists doesn't mean that we can't prove that God exists, at least prove beyond a reasonable doubt. It just means that we can't use science to produce that proof. The correct tools to use are not scientific, but philosophical. Let's not forget, philosophy is not divorced from science, as if you can rely on science alone, and philosophy is just pseudo-babble for PhDs to discuss while smoking pipes in dimly-lit rooms. Philosophy is the *foundation* of science. Science is dependent on philosophy. In fact, ***science is a slave to philosophy.*** Bad philosophy results in bad science, and good science requires good philosophy. Why? Because: 1. **Science cannot be done without philosophy.** Philosophical assumptions are employed in the quest for causes and thus cannot be their consequence. For instance, scientists presuppose that reason and the scientific method enable us to comprehend the world accurately---a belief which is not provable by science. The foundational tools of science, such as the laws of logic, the Law of Causality, the Principle of Uniformity, and the reliability of observation, cannot be validated through scientific experimentation; they must be presumed to be true to conduct any experiment. Hence, science is fundamentally rooted in philosophy. Regrettably, numerous self-proclaimed scientists are really poor philosophers. 2. **Philosophical assumptions can dramatically impact scientific conclusions.** If a scientist presupposes that only natural causes exist, then likely no evidence will persuade them that an intelligent source created the first single-celled amoeba or any other entity with apparent design. When proponents of Darwinism assume that intelligent causes are not feasible, they default to natural laws as the sole explanation. Conversely, if a creationist dismisses natural causes *a priori* (though I'm not aware of any who do), they too risk overlooking the correct explanation. However, a scientist open to both natural and intelligent causes is able to pursue the evidence to its logical conclusion. 3. **Science doesn't really say anything---scientists do.** Data interpretation is always a task for scientists. However, when scientists allow their personal biases or unproven philosophical beliefs to influence their interpretation of data, they fall into the same trap they often criticize in religious individuals---allowing ideology to shape conclusions. Such instances warrant skepticism, as the resulting conclusions might merely be philosophical assumptions masquerading as scientific truths. In philosophy, evidence is often presented in the form of *arguments*. Over the years, thinkers have identified at least twenty arguments for the existence of God, which vary in complexity. Some of these arguments are based on the universe or history, while others are based on the existence of reason and beauty. So just like we know that reason and beauty exist, we can use the same tools to prove reason and beauty to prove the existence of God. So perhaps you can discuss the Cosmological Argument or the Kalam Argument, which discuss the beginning of the universe, which demands proof of a cause. We can discuss the Moral Law, and the fact that the existence of the Moral Law implies the existence of a Moral Law Giver. Those are just a few avenues we can use to discuss proof that God exists. \ Not knowing how to have a discussion with atheists doesn't make you a bad person. It simply makes you uninformed. To be honest, Christians get a mandate to spread the Gospel but never get any instruction on how to do so. They never get to hear common arguments against Christianity and how best to respond. This is evangelism in the form of *apologetics*. Christians are rarely taught how to have a conversation with an atheist.
