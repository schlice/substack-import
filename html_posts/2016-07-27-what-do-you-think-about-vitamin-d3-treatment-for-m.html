**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} Specifically, the evidence on Vitamin D is mixed. These two contradictory studies were just recently released: [[High Doses of Vitamin D Unlikely to Help MS Patients, UK Group Says](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=77af82&url=https://multiplesclerosisnewstoday.com/High%2BDoses%2Bof%2BVitamin%2BD%2BUnlikely%2Bto%2BHelp%2BMS%2BPatients%2Breport%2BUK%2Bstudy%2B){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="multiplesclerosisnewstoday.com"}]{.qlink_container} [[Vitamin D Could Repair Nerve Damage in Multiple Sclerosis, Study Suggests](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=11512a&url=http://wwwmsviewsandrelatednews.blogspot.com/2016/07/vitamin-d-could-repair-nerve-damage-in.html?spref%3Dfb){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="blogspot.com"}]{.qlink_container} I tend to do what my neurologist tells me to do, and he wants me to maintain a specific level of vitamin D in my blood. We test my vitamin D level quarterly, and we found that 5000 IU daily maintains that appropriate level in my blood. Your results **will vary.** Talk to your neurologist if you think adding a vitamin D supplement will help you, but I strongly urge you to advise your friend to ***not*** forego the traditional therapy. Proper diet, exercise, and treatment with one of the approved MS therapy drugs gives you the best chance for slowing down the disease progression and limiting its effects.
