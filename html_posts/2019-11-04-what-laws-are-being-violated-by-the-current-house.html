**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **Which laws are being violated by the current House impeachment proceedings? Can you cite them?** No laws are being violated. The House is free to conduct investigations as it sees fit. They don't need any investigations *at all*; they can simply bring Articles of Impeachment to the floor and vote on them and pass them on a party-line vote. The Constitution imposes no restrictions on how the House conducts impeachment inquiries, or even whether or not an inquiry needs to take place *at all*. That said, actions have consequences. If we were to compare how this House is conducing this impeachment inquiry as opposed to how other Houses have conducted impeachment inquiries, we would find some interesting contrasts. The investigation prior to Nixon's investigation was undertaken by the Senate Watergate Committee, which was bipartisan, and all members had equal authority. The Clinton impeachment was undertaken after Independent Counsel Ken Starr released his report which was originally surrounding the Whitewater scandal. In this case, there was no special investigative committee formed. There was no Independent Counsel (unless you count Special Counsel Robert Mueller's investigation into the Russia collusion hoax). In this case, House subcommittes are using their oversight power to attempt to run an impeachment inquiry, which is of questionable legal status anyway, despite the fact that a lower court has upheld some of the subpoenas already (currently subject to appeal). The functioning of a subcommittee investigation is not the same as a special committee investigation. It does not allow bipartisan participation in the investigative process, even though members from both parties are seated on the committee. In this way, Speaker Pelosi can claim that the investigation was "bipartisan" without stating the full truth that the investigation was actually quite partisan in its function. The rules for which the House voted last week are surprisingly different than any other impeachment inquiry that has ever been undertaken by a House. Among other procedural irregularities, they allow evidence to be suppressed by the Committee chair. Republicans wanted to see some semblance of a "fair and equitable process" laid out, and they believe there wasn't one. Besides, since only a simple majority is needed to pass both the procedural vote and Articles of Impeachment, Republicans instead used the occasion to vote *en masse* and signal their disapproval of impeachment in general, and this motion in specific. The only consequence to impeachment: each Member must go on record as to whether or not they approve of Articles of Impeachment and they will be judged by that vote. And there are several Democrats who won in 2018 in districts carried by the President during the general election in 2016. These Democrats are going to have to do some soul searching before they vote to authorize an impeachment inquiry, and they're going to have to think how that vote will play in a majority Trump voting district back home. Voters have shown that they will hold Members accountable for their actions while in Congress, and many Americans are upset that other legislative priorities are being ignored, such as voting on the replacement for NAFTA (yet another one that those same Democrats are going to pay for if they vote against).
