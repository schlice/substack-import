Dershowitz went on to clarify that U.S. residents don\'t have a right to infect others, and that if they wish to opt-out of vaccination, they could be given an alternative in which people rejecting the state mandate could remain in their homes permanently so as not to risk infecting others.

\"That\'s what a democracy is about,\" he said. \"If the majority of the people agree and support that, for public health measures, you have to be vaccinated, you have to be vaccinated. They should give you an alternative. The alternative is to live in your home, don\'t get vaccinated, but never ever leave your home or live in a bubble. But if you want to interact with other people, you cannot become Typhoid Mary. The Constitution doesn\'t give you the right to spread your illness to other people.\"

Basically, if a vaccine is developed, and you want to participate in society, the government has the right to demand you be vaccinated. You can refuse to be vaccinated, but if a majority of Americans agree, they can demand that you not participate in society. Basically, like he said, you don't have a right to spread illness to others. You are under no obligation to get a vaccine to protect yourself from disease, but you are indeed obligated to get a vaccine to prevent others from getting a disease.

The protection of public health permits reasonable actions to prevent the spread of communicable disease. The case that comes to mind is [Jacobson v. Massachusetts{.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="wikipedia.org"}]{.qlink_container}, and it was decided in the early 20th century during the smallpox crisis (Dershowitz said tuberculosis during his interview). Jacobson upheld the authority of states to enforce compulsory vaccination laws. The Court\'s decision articulated the view that individual liberty is not absolute and is subject to the police power of the state. The court reaffirmed its position in [Zucht v. King{.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="wikipedia.org"}]{.qlink_container}, in which a school was permitted to deny entrance to a student who had failed to be vaccinated.

If a vaccine is developed, and if the vaccine prevents transmission of a virus from one person to another, the state can compel your compliance with the vaccination, or it can exclude you from participation in society in any way it chooses in order to reasonably protect the public health.
