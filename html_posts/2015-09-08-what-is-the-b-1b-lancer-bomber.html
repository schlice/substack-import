**Content:** [On April 29th and April 30th, 2010, the time-to-climb records [[were set](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=80f432&url=http://www.dyess.af.mil/news/story.asp?id%3D123202603){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="af.mil"}]{.qlink_container} at Dyess Air Force Base, Texas, carrying payloads of 5,000 kg to heights of 9,000 and 6,000 meters in 4 minutes 47 seconds and 3 minutes 28 seconds respectively; records previously held by C-17s. The higher speed, service ceiling, and time-to-climb means that the B-1 is more survivable than the B-52 which it was intended to replace. It also holds 61 FAI world records for speed, payload, distance, and time-to-climb in different aircraft weight classes, so it is no slouch even when not being compared to its predecessor. In November 1983, three B-1Bs set a long distance record for the aircraft, which demonstrated its ability to conduct extended mission lengths to strike anywhere in the world and return to base without any stops. This is a big deal, as it contributes to the \"force projection\" mission of the military, and anything that allows us to project force farther and faster than anyone else is a big win for the United States.\ \ (Source: [[Wikipedia](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=2021c3&url=https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rockwell_B-1_Lancer){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="wikipedia.org"}]{.qlink_container})]{.rendered_qtext}
