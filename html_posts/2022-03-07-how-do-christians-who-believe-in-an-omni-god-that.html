**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} I was going to write a long diatribe about how justice and mercy work together, how Christians should temper justice with mercy, but then I found this piece over at [[GotQuestions.org](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=336f59&url=https://www.gotquestions.org/mercy-justice.html){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="gotquestions.org"}]{.qlink_container}, and I just had to share. > God's > [[justice](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=82be14&url=https://www.gotquestions.org/God-of-justice.html){.external_link > rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" > onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" > qt-tooltip="gotquestions.org"}]{.qlink_container} and > [[mercy](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=7b9d77&url=https://www.gotquestions.org/God-is-merciful.html){.external_link > rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" > onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" > qt-tooltip="gotquestions.org"}]{.qlink_container} are seemingly > incompatible. After all, justice involves the dispensing of deserved > punishment for wrongdoing, and mercy is all about pardon and > compassion for an offender. However, these two attributes of God do in > fact form a unity within His character. > > The Bible contains many references to God's mercy. Over 290 verses in > the Old Testament and 70 in the New Testament contain direct > statements of the mercy of God toward His people. > > God was merciful to the Ninevites who repented at the preaching of > Jonah, who described God as "a gracious and compassionate God, slow to > anger and abounding in love, a God who relents from sending calamity" > ([[Jonah > 4:2](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=042b08&url=https://biblia.com/bible/esv/Jonah%25204.2){.external_link > rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" > onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" > qt-tooltip="biblia.com"}]{.qlink_container}). David said God is > "gracious and merciful; Slow to anger and great in loving-kindness. > The LORD is good to all, and His mercies are over all His works" > ([[Psalm 145:8--9, > NASB](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=88806f&url=https://biblia.com/bible/nasb95/Ps%2520145.8%25E2%2580%25939){.external_link > rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" > onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" > qt-tooltip="biblia.com"}]{.qlink_container}). > > But the Bible also speaks of God's justice and His wrath over sin. In > fact, God's perfect justice is a defining characteristic: "There is no > God apart from me, a righteous \[just\] God and a Savior; there is > none but me" ([[Isaiah > 45:21](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=4eca45&url=https://biblia.com/bible/esv/Isa%252045.21){.external_link > rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" > onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" > qt-tooltip="biblia.com"}]{.qlink_container}). "He is the Rock, his > works are perfect, and all his ways are just. A faithful God who does > no wrong, upright and just is he" ([[Deuteronomy > 32:4](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=a39ca5&url=https://biblia.com/bible/esv/Deut%252032.4){.external_link > rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" > onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" > qt-tooltip="biblia.com"}]{.qlink_container}). > > In the New Testament, Paul details why God's judgment is coming: "Put > to death, therefore, whatever belongs to your earthly nature: sexual > immorality, impurity, lust, evil desires and greed, which is idolatry. > Because of these, the wrath of God is coming" ([[Colossians > 3:5--6](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=33b794&url=https://biblia.com/bible/esv/Col%25203.5%25E2%2580%25936){.external_link > rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" > onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" > qt-tooltip="biblia.com"}]{.qlink_container}). > > So the Bible showcases the fact that God is merciful, but it also > reveals that He is just and will one day dispense justice on the sin > of the world. > > In every other religion in the world that holds to the idea of a > supreme deity, that deity's mercy is always exercised *at the expense > of* justice. For example, in Islam, Allah may grant mercy to an > individual, but it's done by dismissing the penalties of whatever law > has been broken. In other words, the offender's punishment that was > properly due him is brushed aside so that mercy can be extended. > Islam's Allah and every other deity in the non-Christian religions set > aside the requirements of moral law in order to be merciful. Mercy is > seen as at odds with justice. In a sense, in those religions, crime > can indeed pay. > > If any human judge acted in such a fashion, most people would lodge a > major complaint. It is a judge's responsibility to see that the law is > followed and that justice is provided. A judge who ignores the law is > betraying his office. > > Christianity is unique in that God's mercy is shown *through* His > justice. There is no setting aside of justice to make room for mercy. > The Christian doctrine of penal substitution states that sin and > injustice were punished at the [[cross of > Christ](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=11b53a&url=https://www.gotquestions.org/meaning-of-the-cross.html){.external_link > rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" > onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" > qt-tooltip="gotquestions.org"}]{.qlink_container} and it's only > because the penalty of sin was satisfied through Christ's sacrifice > that God extends His mercy to undeserving sinners who look to Him for > salvation. > > As Christ died for sinners, He also demonstrated God's righteousness; > His death on the cross showcased God's justice. This is exactly what > the apostle Paul says: "All are justified freely by his grace through > the redemption that came by Christ Jesus. God presented Christ as a > sacrifice of atonement, through the shedding of his blood---to be > received by faith. He did this **to demonstrate his righteousness**, > because in his > [[forbearance](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=bcebd3&url=https://www.gotquestions.org/Bible-forbearance.html){.external_link > rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" > onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" > qt-tooltip="gotquestions.org"}]{.qlink_container} he had left the sins > committed beforehand unpunished---he did it **to demonstrate his > righteousness** at the present time, so as to be just and the one who > justifies those who have faith in Jesus ([[Romans > 3:24--26](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=a84e5e&url=https://biblia.com/bible/esv/Rom%25203.24%25E2%2580%259326){.external_link > rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" > onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" > qt-tooltip="biblia.com"}]{.qlink_container}, emphasis added). > > In other words, all the sin from Adam to the time of Christ was under > the forbearance and mercy of God. God in His mercy chose not to punish > sin, which would require an eternity in hell for all sinners, although > He would have been perfectly just in doing so. Adam and Eve were not > immediately destroyed when they ate the forbidden fruit. Instead, God > planned a Redeemer ([[Genesis > 3:15](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=761a99&url=https://biblia.com/bible/esv/Gen%25203.15){.external_link > rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" > onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" > qt-tooltip="biblia.com"}]{.qlink_container}). In His love God sent His > own Son ([[John > 3:16](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=486117&url=https://biblia.com/bible/esv/John%25203.16){.external_link > rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" > onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" > qt-tooltip="biblia.com"}]{.qlink_container}). Christ paid for every > single sin ever committed; thus, God was just in punishing sin, and He > can also justify sinners who receive Christ by faith ([[Romans > 3:26](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=3b3804&url=https://biblia.com/bible/esv/Rom%25203.26){.external_link > rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" > onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" > qt-tooltip="biblia.com"}]{.qlink_container}). God's justice *and* His > mercy were demonstrated by Christ's death on the cross. At the cross, > God's justice was meted out in full (upon Christ), and God's mercy was > extended in full (to all who believe). So God's perfect mercy was > exercised through His perfect justice. > > The end result is that everyone who trusts in the Lord Jesus is saved > from God's wrath and instead experiences His grace and mercy ([[Romans > 8:1](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=a8d51d&url=https://biblia.com/bible/esv/Rom%25208.1){.external_link > rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" > onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" > qt-tooltip="biblia.com"}]{.qlink_container}). As Paul says, "Since we > have now been justified by his blood, how much more shall we be saved > from God's wrath through him!" ([[Romans > 5:9](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=3548b3&url=https://biblia.com/bible/esv/Rom%25205.9){.external_link > rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" > onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" > qt-tooltip="biblia.com"}]{.qlink_container}).[\[1\]](#Gqywx){#cite-Gqywx > .citation_link} This post highlights that **Christianity is unique in world religions in that God shows mercy** ***through*** **justice.** In all other world religions, justice is *set aside* in favor of mercy. Christianity teaches that God is just and does not set aside justice to be merciful. *Justice was served at the cross. Jesus took the punishment for our sins, so there was no setting aside of justice. God shows us mercy when he accepts Jesus' punishment on our behalf.* And this is all accomplished because of God's *grace*, which allowed us to get something we did not deserve: *salvation.* Footnotes [\[1\]](#cite-Gqywx){.citation_index} [[How do God's mercy and justice work together in salvation?](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=336f59&url=https://www.gotquestions.org/mercy-justice.html){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="gotquestions.org"}]{.qlink_container}
