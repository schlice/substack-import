**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **Is the Senate constitutionally mandated to either amend or consent to appropriations (not allowed to ignore such appropriations)?** The correct answer is **no**, there is no Constitutional requirement for Congress to do its job. This is why, in quite a number of years, Congress has not passed a budget. There is no penalty for Congress to fail to pass a budget, either because the budget could not be resolved, or because of inaction in one chamber. Appropriations bills are controlled by law, not by Constitutional mandate. The law governing appropriations is called the Congressional Budget and Impoundment Control Act of 1974 , or just abbreviated as the Congressional Budget Act (CBA). Under CBA, the process works this way: - The President submits a budget. He generally has until the first Monday in February to submit his budget to Congress. It's important for the President to submit a budget to Congress, because it lets Congress know what the President's fiscal priorities are for his agencies, as well as what he will agree to, or veto if it is not present in the final bill (i.e. border funding in FY2019). - The Appropriations Committees in both Houses of Congress create **budget resolutions** based on the President's budget. These resolutions do not go to the President, but instead go to their respective chambers for consideration by the entire chamber. - The entire chamber considers, and passes, each one of their budget resolutions. In current practice, the Senate typically passes the House bill with the Senate version attached as a single substitute amendment, which means that the House generally passes their budget bill first, and the Senate amends their bill to the House bill. - The House and Senate Appropriations Committees will meet to iron out the differences between their two bills. - The committee produces a **conference substitute for the bill** which goes back to the full chambers for review. Usually, like the original appropriations bill, the House considers conference reports on appropriations measures first. The first chamber to consider the conference report may vote to adopt it, reject it, or recommit it to the conference for further consideration. After the first house adopts the conference report, the conference is automatically disbanded; therefore, the second house has only two options---to adopt or reject the conference report. The measure cannot be sent to the President until both houses have agreed to the entire text of the bill. - Once both Houses have agreed to the bill, it is sent to the President for signature. The President can either sign it, veto it, or do nothing. If he does nothing, the bill automatically becomes law when it has been on the President's desk for 10 days **when Congress is in session**. If he takes no action, and Congress is not in session in 10 days, the bill does not become law. This is known as a [[*pocket veto*](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=3aad86&url=https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pocket_veto){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="wikipedia.org"}]{.qlink_container}*.* Additionally, specific to your question, there is no penalty for Congress failing to pass a budget. Congress under the CBA has until April 15th to present a budget to the President for signature. Since 1977, Congress has frequently not met this target date. In many instances in recent years (1999, 2003, 2005, 2007, 2011 through 2015, and 2017), Congress did not adopt a budget resolution at all. There is also no penalty for Congress missing its deadline.
