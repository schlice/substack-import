--- title: "Can the continued use of leaded gasoline in airplanes be a contributor to the increase in the number of mass shootings?" date: "2016-06-28" tags: ["law-enforcement"] layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} Short answer: no. Longer answer: Lead has been phased out of most aircraft fuels. Some aviation fuels still contain lead, as no suitable substitute has been found to replace it yet. Aircraft engines have to be designed to run on specific fuel types. While commercial jet engines have been modified to run on "JET A," which is basically a kerosene-like fuel which does not contain lead, piston-based engines still use "avgas" which does contain low lead. Lead levels in the atmosphere have been falling since leaded gas was prohibited for cars. It was almost completely phased out by 1986. The highest concentrations of airborne lead are now found in the areas around airports where avgas is used. [[Scientific American](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=2c7296&url=http://www.scientificamerican.com/article/lead-in-aviation-fuel/){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="scientificamerican.com"}]{.qlink_container} reports, "Some of the health effects of repeated exposure to lead include damage to the central nervous system, kidneys and red blood cells, and decreased function in the cardiovascular and immune systems. Lower IQ levels and learning disabilities can also result from lead exposure, especially in children, whose young bodies are more sensitive than those of adults. And scientists at the National Toxicology Program have concluded that lead and lead compounds are 'reasonably anticipated to be human carcinogens.'"[\[1\]](#dhEOW){#cite-dhEOW .citation_link} So while lead is most definitely a carcinogen and harmful to health, lead exposure is not directly linked to antisocial or criminal behavior. The most likely cause of the decrease in crime rates through the 90s was the newly-developed practice of policing which actually began with the NYPD in 1994. The bosses there got around the then-radical idea that police could actually stop crime before it started instead of merely responding to crime. They analyzed crime statistics on a daily (and later, hourly) basis, assigning officers to known hot-spots to prevent crimes from taking place. As these efforts started paying off, they spread nationwide. No other government program has had the levels of success that data-driven policing has had. Over the last two years, however, movements like Black Lives Matter have caused officers to back off of data-driven policing. Sure, they still respond to 911 calls where a victim has been identified, but the number of discretionary traffic stops and preventative policing calls has sharply fallen. And in its wake is the first sustained spike in violent crime the United States has seen in 20 years. The "Ferguson Effect," as it has been coined, has seen murders rise nearly 17 percent in the nation's 15 largest cities last year alone, and cities with large black populations have seen crime rise the most.[\[2\]](#WmhWJ){#cite-WmhWJ .citation_link} So is aviation fuel responsible for more violent crime? Not likely. The Ferguson Effect is a far more likely cause.
