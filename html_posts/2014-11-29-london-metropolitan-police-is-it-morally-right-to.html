**Content:** [The anonymous answerer assumed that the asker was asking about police officers breaking the law in order to stop someone else from breaking the law. It\'s really hard to answer on behalf of police officers because I don\'t see many situations where a police officer would have to break the law to do his job (stop someone else from breaking the law). When a police 0fficer lies to a suspect to gain his/her trust or to convince him/her that telling the truth is the best alternative, this is perfectly legal and is all part of the game. Is it morally right? Maybe not. But it\'s also not illegal, so it doesn\'t answer the question. Police cars are not subject to vehicle and traffic law because they are \"emergency vehicles.\" Traffic laws are applicable to \"motor vehicles\" which police cars are not.\ \ (ED) I would also add that if a police officer had to break the law in order to enforce the law, this would be what is called an \"affirmative defense\" against the charge of the officer breaking the law. This would only be applicable if there was no other way for the officer to stop the perpetrator.\ \ I think it applies more to civilians breaking the law to stop other laws from being broken. For example (and here\'s a biggie): let\'s say I\'m acting as a civilian (not as a deputy \-- I straddle the line). As a civilian, I cannot conceal a weapon in a school, even if I have a concealed weapons permit (which I do). As an officer, I can, but not as a civilian. Let\'s say I know that there is a student with a gun in the school. Now the dilemma: should I illegally conceal a handgun on my person and enter the school in order to be the armed resistance should that student start shooting?\ \ I\'d be breaking the law, but I\'d meet the higher moral imperative of saving lives at any cost. In that circumstance, I would be in clear violation of the law, but breaking that law would be morally right.]{.rendered_qtext}
