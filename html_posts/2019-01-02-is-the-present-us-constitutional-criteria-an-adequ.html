**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **Is the present US constitutional criteria an adequate basis for removing a US president? If not, how should it be improved?** "Adequate?" As in, "covers all conditions in which we might want to remove a President?" If that's the question, then the answer is an emphatic yes. The impeachment power in Congress, while it is only supposed to be used for "high crimes and misdemeanors," can really be used for any offense a President commits. There isn't a list of offenses which can be called a "high crime or misdemeanor." Once a president is impeached, the Senate has a trial to determine whether or not that charge for which he was impeached is of a sufficient nature as to impede his ability to perform the duties of the office of president. If so, the Senate votes to remove him from office. If the president becomes physically incapable of carrying out the duties of the office, the 25th Amendment contains a process to allow the Vice President to assume the command of the office without any action required on the part of the President. Finally, if the President is acting in a way you don't like, or if he's a big mean orange bully and it hurts your feelings, you can simply not re-elect him, and he'll go be orange somewhere else. If the President can do too much damage in four years that you think you'd need to remove him before that, then Congress has given the President too much authority and needs to dial it back. So it would seem that the Constitution has all the bases covered, so to speak.
