**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **How is Delta offering a non-stop flight from LAX to Tokyo NRT in a Boeing 737?** Answer: it doesn't. Delta does not flay a LAX to NRT route, and does not use the 737. However, **a 737 is capable of flying a LAX-NRT route.** There is a great video you should watch so that you can understand why this is now possible. It's about 9 minutes long. [[https://youtu.be/HSxSgbNQi-g](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=24b6fc&url=https://youtu.be/HSxSgbNQi-g){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);"}]{.qlink_container} It has to do with changes to flight regulations which previously stated that two-engine planes could only fly no more than 60 minutes away from any airport. However, as time went on and engines became increasingly more reliable, these rules were severely outdated. In 1985, ETOPS (Extended-range Twin engine Operational Performance Standards, or more colloquially, Engines Turn Or Passengers Swim) rules were put in place (begrudgingly) for TWA (Trans World Airlines) to fly their twin-engine 767 between Boston and Paris. ETOPS certifications for aircraft mean that they are built with appropriate redundancies, fire suppression and communications systems to fly safely if one engine fails. The 767 previously mentioned has an ETOPS type rating of 180 minutes, which means it can fly anywhere as long as it is within 180 minutes of a diversion airfield. An ETOPS 180 rating means that 95% of the world's surface becomes available for aircraft to fly over. The granting of ETOPS type ratings allowed carriers to start serving the "long and thin" routes that are inefficient to fly with larger aircraft. The LAX-NRT route is, indeed, one of these "long and thin" routes. If you check the [[Great Circle Mapper](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=fa006c&url=http://www.gcmap.com/mapui?P%3Dlax-nrt%26MS%3Dwls%26DU%3Dmi%26EV%3D397%26EU%3Dkts){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="gcmap.com"}]{.qlink_container} for the LAX-NRT route, you see that it is well within the ETOPS 180 and over-water rating for which the aircraft has been certified. The Boeing 737--700ER (Extended Range), with its up to 9 auxilliary fuel tanks, can fly up to 5,510nm. Again according to GCMap, the LAX-NRT ideal route is 4,736 nm (5,451 mi), something the 737--700ER can handle easily.
