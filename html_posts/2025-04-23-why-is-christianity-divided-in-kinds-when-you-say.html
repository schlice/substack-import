**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} Think of this quote which was (supposedly) said by St. Augustine: > *In essentials, unity; in non-essentials, liberty; in all things, > charity* What this means in the context in which you asked is that while all Christian denominations agree on the essentials (the nature of God, the nature of the Trinity, the nature of Christ, the means for salvation, etc), the Bible allows us to disagree on the non-essentials. And sometimes, those disagreements are severe enough that people self-sort into groups who believe in a like-minded way. Those groups form *denominations*. Because the Bible allows us to have liberty on the non-essentials, we have many denominations. We all share common beliefs about the essentials, but we have sometimes very different views of the non-essentials. **This is not universally bad**. \ What *becomes* bad is when we start to deviate on the ***essentials***. And if you're looking from the outside in, you never know if you're looking at a disagreement on **non-essentials** or **essentials.** For example, here's some areas of essential disagreement: 1\. Salvation and Good Works: - Most denominations, like Protestantism, emphasize the importance of faith alone as the basis for salvation, with good works being a result of that faith. - Some, like Catholicism, believe that salvation requires both faith and good works, with good works being a necessary component of living out a Christian life. 2\. The Trinity and the Nature of God: - Unitarianism denies the Trinity, believing that God is a single entity rather than three persons. - Mainline Christian denominations, like Catholicism, Orthodoxy, and most Protestant groups, affirm the Trinity, believing in one God existing in three persons: Father, Son (Jesus), and Holy Spirit. 3\. Scripture and Authority: - Different denominations have varying approaches to interpreting scripture and the authority of tradition. - Most, like most Protestant groups, emphasize the Bible as the primary source of authority, while others, like Catholicism and Orthodoxy, give more weight to church tradition and the authority of the early Church Fathers. 4\. The Role of the Church: - Some denominations, like Presbyterianism, emphasize the importance of the church as a community of believers, while others, like Catholicism, emphasize the hierarchical structure of the church with a clear line of authority. - Different denominations also have varying views on the role of the church in society and its relationship with the secular world. 5\. Sacraments and Rituals: - Denominations have many different understandings of which sacraments are considered essential and the nature of these sacraments. - Some denominations, like Catholicism and Orthodoxy, recognize a greater number of sacraments than others. - Even worse, *within the same denomination*, there can be varying practices and interpretations of sacraments. Differences between denominations on essentials is problematic. But Christianity itself is not a religion, and there is no central governing authority. This sort of division is a natural outgrowth of that feature. That said, we Christians can continually pray for and work towards agreement at least on the essentials.
