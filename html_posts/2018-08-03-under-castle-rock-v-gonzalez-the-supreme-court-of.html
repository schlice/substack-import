**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **Under Castle Rock v. Gonzalez, the Supreme Court of the United States ruled police have no duty to enforce. Does that mean that police owe citizens no duty to protect and serve?** That's almost exactly correct. The ruling extends other rulings beginning with the watershed case *Warren v. District of Columbia*, where it was shown that the police do not have a duty to protect any one individual, but instead have a *general duty* to protect the population *at large.* "To Protect And Serve" is a *motto*, not a law or an oath. The precedent in *Warren*, and extended in *Castle Rock*, states that officers do not have an individual responsibility to protect any one person unless a special relationship exists (i.e. a person is in custody or specifically under police protection). The complainant in *Castle Rock* argued that her restraining order created a special relationship and that the police were required to protect her. SCOTUS ruled that there was no legal requirement under Colorado law to enforce a restraining order, and even if there was, it wouldn't create an individual right to enforcement that could be considered a protected entitlement, AND EVEN IF THERE WAS, such an entitlement would have no monetary value, and would thus not be counted as eligible property for due process purposes. You are partially correct when you say that *Castle Rock v. Gonzalez* states that the police have "no duty to enforce." Colorado has no law which places the burden on law enforcement to enforce restraining orders. There is nothing in state law which *requires* police to enforce them, or subjecting the department to penalties if they fail to do so. Therefore, the previous precedent is continued. The precedent flows from the Due Process clause itself, and basically says that while the *government* may not deprive an individual of life without due process, this does not place a burden on the government to protect your life against *someone else* taking it. In 1989, SCOTUS stated, \"Nothing in the language of the Due Process Clause itself requires the State to protect the life, liberty, and property of its citizens against invasion by private actors.\" Generally, the Due Process Clause does not provide an affirmative right to government aid, \"even where such aid may be necessary to secure life, liberty, or property interests of which the government itself may not deprive the individual.\"
