**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **If the court fails to honor and enforce Congressional subpoenas does this set precedence for their own to be dishonored?** As with all legal questions, the answer is "it depends." And what it depends on is *why* the court fails to honor Congressional subpoenas. So *if* the court finds that a subpoena is not enforceable, you have to determine the court's rationale. Does the subpoena follow the rules the body itself has set out, including the body's own precedents? And if it is changing that precedent, has it gone through the appropriate body process to do that? Is the subpoena authorized by the charter of the body requesting it? For example, can the Ways and Means committee do a criminal investigation? Unlikely, since it it outside of their charter. If the legitimacy of a subpoena is appealed to the Supreme Court, and they decide to hear the case, then they will be right. Remember, SCOTUS is not the last word because they're always right; SCOTUS is always right because they're the last word.
