--- title: '"How should I have responded when I was told during a job interview in NYC that my salary history was a requirement, which is illegal to ask?"' date: '2017-04-20' tags: - policy-analysis layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} Tell them that you will give them your salary history when they give you the salary histories of all the people who previously held that position. Don't worry, they will never do that. It's confidential information. But so is your salary history. That's personal financial information. It's not a "perfectly reasonable" question to ask someone in a job interview. It's a "highly personal" question to ask someone, and it's exceedingly impolite to ask someone you just met about highly personal things. If you were selling your car, and someone asked you, "What other offers have you gotten on your car, and what were you offered?" you'd probably tell them to get lost, right? That information is *your bargaining chip.* You're the one offering your services. The company is not in the position of power just because they have a job opening. You are on equal footing with the company. They have a job, you have the talent. They need you just as much as you need them, or they wouldn't be bothering to interview you! You must maintain that mindset. To be fair, the more important issue is that **if a recruiter can't figure out how much you are worth without knowing what someone else paid you, they're a really bad recruiter.** Once someone has your salary history, you lose all of your negotiating leverage. Some employers have a policy that they won't pay you more than 10% more than what you were previously making, because they feel that a 10% pay bump is sufficient when moving from one company to another. If they simply refuse to move you forward in the hiring process until you provide that information, *leave*. There is clearly too much distrust and improper employment practices (or apparently in New York, *illegal* hiring practice) to be worth your efforts. Move on. Understand your worth, and know when it is not being respected.
