**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} I'll echo what [[Paul Harding](https://www.quora.com/profile/Paul-Harding-14)]{.qlink_container} said, but add a little bit of the law behind it so you understand why this is. - There are **no** provisions in the law which allow you to disobey the lawful orders of a peace officer in the course of his duty. - Whether or not the police officer was acting lawfully or whether or not the police officer was correctly enforcing the law are matters of **fact** to be determined by a judge or a jury. No one on the street has the authority to determine whether or not a police officer is acting lawfully. Knowing these two facts (you never have the right to disobey a peace officer who is acting lawfully, and whether or not the officer is acting lawfully is up to a judge to decide), on the street, ***you must presume the officer is acting lawfully and you must obey the police officer's lawful orders. How do you know the orders are lawful? They come from a lawfully-appointed police officer doing his job.*** Now some of you are going to cite the times that people disobeyed the police, were arrested, and were later found to have been innocent. You can certainly go this route, but as the OP asked, ***you can be arrested for failing to follow a lawful directive***. You may be found to be completely innocent, but the onus now falls on you to prove that the officer was not acting lawfully, and you are doing so from a respondent's position instead of from a complainant's position. Remember, the directive is not lawful because the officer is correctly interpreting the law, **the directive is lawful because it was given by a lawfully-appointed police officer**. The officer can even be legally wrong, and you still have to follow the order or face charges for failing to follow a lawful directive. Now, on to the service dog. The ADA allows law enforcement and providers of public accommodation to ask two questions of any owner of a service animal in a public place: 1. Is this a service animal trained to assist with a disability as recognized by the ADA? 2. What specifically is this service animal trained to do? Service animals in-training are usually given the same deference as actual fully-trained service animals, but they do not need to be. The general standard is that if a service animal is going to be in a place where people are, it must act with the same decorum as you would expect of a person. Therefore, it cannot be a nuisance, it cannot publicly urinate or defecate in that space, it cannot bother or annoy other patrons, and it cannot bark incessantly. Service dogs do not do any of these things unless it is part of their training and they are responding to an emergency situation. For example, I know of a diabetic service dog which alerts if its owner is going into a diabetic attack, and will bark without stopping if its owner loses consciousness until its owner sits up again. I know of one incident in which this happened, and the dog responded exactly as it was trained to do, and it saved the woman's life.
