**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **Do the Presidential alerts really have access to the E911 chip in your phone?** While there may be some problems with privacy concerns and E911, Presidential Alerts are not the problem. John McAfee got it wrong. :::: ui_qtext_image_outer ::: qtext_image_wrapper ![](images/qimg-a687e5907d3294f9e9531ba0ec73a5a7){.landscape .qtext_image .zoomable_in_feed master_src="images/qimg-a687e5907d3294f9e9531ba0ec73a5a7" master_w="640" master_h="470"} ::: :::: Sorry John, but this is simply not accurate. The Wireless Emergency Alert (WEA) system is the same one that sends you severe weather alerts and AMBER alerts. The Presidential Alert has never been used, and has never been tested until just recently. It basically functions like an advanced text message. It can get through when other applications are using your phone. But to be sure, it's a one-way message. It sends nothing back. Additionally, your phone doesn't have an "E911" chip. You do have a GPS chip in your phone, and in the future, E911 systems will be able to see your GPS location when you call 911. Most 911 centers don't have this functionality, and most carriers haven't enabled the 911 interface to allow dispatchers to see the GPS data on your phone when you call 911 yet. It's in the works, but not rolled out yet. And as I mentioned, while there are indeed privacy concerns about your GPS chip and its utilization by law enforcement, they have absolutely zero to do with Presidential Alerts. When WEA is activated, it sends a text message to your phone. That's literally it.
