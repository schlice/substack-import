**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} I started out my faith journey as a baby, when my parents baptized me as a Roman Catholic. I was "sprinkle baptized," which isn't Biblical. No one was "sprinkle baptized" in the Bible. And no one was a baby, either. Catholics believe that baptism is required to impart God's grace, so parents get their babies baptized as quickly as possible. Protestants do not believe that baptism does anything special to you. **Protestants do not believe that baptism saves you. Protestants believe that baptism is what saved people do.** Later on, I was baptized by immersion of my own choosing and through the urgings of the Spirit, to show that I was "all in" on my faith. We were commanded to be baptized, to prove to the world and to ourselves that we stand for something greater than ourselves, not to gain salvation. Ephesians 2:8--9 says that it is by grace through faith that you are saved, not by anything you can do yourself. Baptism (as a thing you do) no more saves you than putting on a Tampa Bay Lightning jersey makes you an NHL hockey player. You become an NHL hockey player when you sign your contract, just as you are saved when you repent and believe. Much like the hockey jersey represents your NHL contract, baptism is an outward sign of an inward salvation. But anyone can put on a hockey jersey; it doesn't make them any more a hockey player than dunking yourself in a tub of water makes you saved. TL;DR: **Repentance and belief save you. Baptism does not save you.** And once you've been baptized (through immersion baptism), you don't need to be baptized again. You can certainly choose to be baptized again, but it's not necessary, and one might question your motives if you were already saved and you've already been baptized.
