I can't say actually zero because I'm unaware of the Court's complete docket, but I have not heard of any cases challenging the Constitutionality of the *Obergefell* ruling. So before you flip out over *Roe* triggering some massive overturning of all of your favorite Court decisions*,* remember that in order for SCOTUS to reverse a previous ruling, it has to be hearing a case which makes a valid Constitution argument against a previous ruling. In this case, there is a very solid Constitutional argument in favor of overturning *Roe*, namely that the basis for *Roe* was completely unconstitutional. *Roe* used a previous ruling in a marital privacy case, *Griswold v. Connecticut. Griswold* was a case out of Connecticut wherein at the time in the 1950s, it was illegal to use "any drug, medicinal article, or instrument for the purpose of preventing conception\...\". Violators could be \"\... fined not less than fifty dollars or imprisoned not less than sixty days nor more than one year or be both fined and imprisoned.\" The Court overturned the Connecticut law by claiming that the law violated the right to marital privacy, which it claimed existed in the Constitution. To be clear, there is no amendment to the Constitution which recognizes the right of the people to have privacy, in their marriage or anywhere else. What the Court did was to look at the characteristics of existing amendments, and decide that what the Framers *intended* was that the basis of all of the other rights was a right to privacy. The Court reasoned that the provisions of the Bill of Rights created \"emanations\" of protection, and these emanations created \"penumbras\" within which rights could still exist, even if they were not explicitly enumerated in the Constitution. The Court concluded that the right to marital privacy fell within this protection. *Roe* then used this right to privacy (which the decision in *Griswold* created) to argue that a woman has a right to privacy in her person, and that abortion is covered by that right to privacy. So there is good Constitutional basis to reverse *Roe*, because it was a bad decision based on a nonexistent right.
