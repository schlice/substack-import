**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} This is a good question, and I don't take any personal offense by you asking it. It's a common misconception, and it has a lot to do with what the name "disabled" means. Disabled, implying the root "disability," is a legal term, not a medical term. Legally speaking, a disability is a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities. By this alone, naming your group after its legal definition isn't probably the most efficacious name. However, it goes deeper than that. It strikes at the heart of the issue: **how do persons with disabilities define themselves?** Is a person with a disability *defined by* that disability? In other words, does the disability make the person, or are they simply a person who happens to also have a disability? I probably don't need to answer that question, but I will for clarity: *persons with disabilities are not defined by their disabilities.* They are people. Plain and simple. The fact that they also have a disability is meaningless. People don't want to be artificially sorted into boxes. People want to feel included. People want to feel appreciated for the skills they do bring to the table, they don't want to feel segregated by a disability. A name like the "Disabled Sailing Club" would probably segregate more than it would include. Something others fail to recognize as well: naming your group the "Disabled Sailing Club" artificially limits the membership of the group! You probably never intended to *restrict membership to only disabled persons.* You probably thought, "Well, anyone can join, but we cater specifically to the disabled." That said, how does your name tell people that anyone can join? From the looks of your name, only people with disabilities can join. Doesn't make your club sound very inclusive, does it. Your club isn't about **disabilities**. Your club is about **sailing**. Your name should reflect that reality, not create additional boxes where none are necessary.
