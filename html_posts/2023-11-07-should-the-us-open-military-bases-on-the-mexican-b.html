Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} I like the way you think. But there are a few notable problems with your idea. 1. Military base locations are picked to assist the **military** with its core function. Military base locations are not picked based on helping the **local community**. 2. It is not the military's job to "secure the border." That's the job of the Department of Homeland Security, not the Department of Defense. I know it sounds like splitting hairs, but the federal government has already taken states to court over this issue (and won), claiming that it is solely within their purview to determine how best to secure (or not) the border. The federal government has designated the authority to secure the border to DHS, not to DOD. 3. If it were a task assigned to the Department of Defense by the President and the Joint Chiefs of Staff, the military can absolutely go teach border security. But the construction of a base is not necessary to accomplish that task. Ultimately, the problem here is that the military is the political arm of the government, it is not an entity of its own. Clausewitz said, "Politics is war without bloodshed. War is politics with bloodshed." He's right. War is politics. The military is just a physical manifestation of US foreign policy. It's the global "teeth" behind US foreign policy. Even though they can do it, and it might be a good idea, it's never going to happen. The government would rather continue using the southern border as a wedge issue in elections rather than doing what's best for the country and its people.
