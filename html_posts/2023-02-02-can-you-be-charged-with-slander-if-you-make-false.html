**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} Slander (like its printed cousin libel) is part of a family of offenses called *defamation*. There are four elements which are required to exist in order to prove *prima facie* defamation: 1. **a false statement** purporting to be fact 2. **publication** (libel) or **communication** (slander) of that statement to a third person 3. **fault** amounting to at least negligence, and 4. **damages**, or some harm caused to the reputation of the person or entity who is the subject of the statement. Some other notes about defamation: - Fault is shown when, at least, negligence in not taking reasonable care to prove or disprove the truth of the statement before it was communicated occurred. - Public officials are held to a higher standard when claiming defamation. Public officials must show that the false, defaming statements were said with \"actual malice.\" This was outlined in the famous defamation case *New York Times v. Sullivan* (376 US 254, 1964). The reason why public officials are held to a higher standard is because of the threat relaxed defamatory statements could pose to first amendment freedom of speech. The *Sullivan* court stated that \"actual malice\" means that the defendant said the defamatory statement \"with knowledge that it was false or with reckless disregard of whether it was false or not.\" The plaintiff must prove actual malice by \"clear and convincing\" evidence, rather than the preponderance of the evidence standard normally required in a civil case. - Truth is considered to be an *absolute defense* to defamation. In other words, if you make a statement which harms someone's reputation, but the statement is actually true, a defamation claim will fail.
