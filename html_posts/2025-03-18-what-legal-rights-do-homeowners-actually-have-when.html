--- title: '"What legal rights do homeowners actually have when someone threatens their pets?"' date: '2025-03-18' tags: - law-enforcement - law layout: post ---

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} Legally, pets are property. You can't threaten my property legally. It'd be like talking to my car, saying, "Car, I'm gonna hurt you." So legally, property can't be threatened. However, pets-as-property are not regarded as *fungible*. In other words, if someone damages your car, it can be replaced with original parts or with a totally new car. Pets, not so much. Since pets are legally property, you *legally* can't defend property with deadly force. But what you *can* do is **interpose yourself between your property and the attacker**. Then it becomes a self-defense situation, in which case you may defend ***yourself*** with whatever force is necessary, up to and including deadly force. But doing so would be highly legally fraught. You *inserted* yourself into the situation which caused the situation to become a deadly force situation. The situation was not a deadly force situation until you inserted yourself in the middle of it, some might say, in order to *cause* the situation to become one which would result in deadly force where it wouldn't have otherwise. Additionally, if your "property" is small and movable (like most pets), creating a scenario which would require deadly force would not be looked upon favorably. While you might escape jail time, you probably won't escape the wrongful death lawsuit which would inevitably follow. \ So that's all well and good, but what can you do? Well, you can't use ***deadly*** force to protect your pets, but you can use ***reasonable*** force. Remember, force is a spectrum. There are lots of things you can do **before** you reach the handgun to defend your pets from someone physically attacking them. You shouldn't discount the use of pepper spray, or even a pepper ball gun. Pepper ball guns are not lethal force and in nearly every state do not need permits or licenses to carry (your mileage may vary, check your state laws, etc). And pepper spray works equally as well on people as it does on other dogs who might attack your dog. Pepper spray is very effective against dogs. As always, check your state laws before you carry anything to be used for self-defense. If you ever have questions, the Internet is not the place to ask them.
