**Content:** [One of the most impactful pieces of legislation in our time is the Americans with Disabilities Act. This massive law affected many different aspects of our society, including the availability of handicapped parking spaces, accessible bathroom facilities, ramps and elevators, automatic doors, and related items.\ \ It also changed the way businesses hire employees, to allow for more fairness in hiring the disabled. It changed what businesses are allowed to ask when hiring an individual; they can only list the requirements of the job and can ask the prospective employee whether or not they can perform those essential job functions \"with or without reasonable accommodations.\" If they answer yes, the prospective employee is affirming that they physically and mentally meet the requirements of the job.\ \ An employee does not have to disclose to his/her employer that he/she is disabled. That information stays between the doctor, the patient, and the insurance company. The employee will have to disclose if they seek accommodations, but otherwise, they can keep their disability to themselves. There is a presumption, of course, that the disability does not preclude the individual from actually performing the job. The requirements of the job should list what is required in order to ensure that the disabled person can actually accomplish the requirements.\ \ This was one of the biggest advances for the disabled in recent years, and has led to many more people with disabilities being valued in the workplace not because of their disability, but in spite of it; for their **ability**.\ \ Of course, with all of the upsides, there are downsides. The presumption that disabled people are telling the truth about their disability leads to some abuse of the system. If you claim to have a disability, you can\'t be asked to provide proof that you actually have that disability. If you bring a dog into a restaurant and claim that it is a service dog, you don\'t have to show proof that the service dog is actually trained to perform any specific function; you have to take the disabled person\'s word for it. And there is very little that can be done if someone abuses the system, claiming a disability and receiving accommodations when they don\'t actually have a disability. One big exception is in parking spaces and claiming to have a disability when you don\'t. This can result in fines and suspension/revocation of your license. But since in most business cases the accommodations provided are not onerous, there\'s not much that the business can do to redress those grievances against someone who lies about having a disability.]{.rendered_qtext}
