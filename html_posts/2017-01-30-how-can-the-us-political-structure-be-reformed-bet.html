**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} TL;DR: Good thing we don't have a pure democracy and we don't elect presidents based on popular vote. We don't need reform: we have the Electoral College, and we have two houses of Congress, which balance state-based representation with population-based representation. *The Electoral College*. The Electoral College was put in place for this very reason. Smaller states were upset because they would have less representation when it came to the election. They were concerned about the **tyranny of the majority**, where demagogues enthrall large populations of people, and groupthink pervades. The demagogue rides a wave of popularity directly to power, and the people not only support his dangerous agenda, they actively encourage it. PRO TIP: if there is dissension between government and groups of people, you're doing it right. The Electoral College uses the same distribution of Electors as we give that state for the House of Representatives (we'll get there in a second) and the Senate. The formula uses the population of the state along with the size of the landmass of the state to determine how many Representatives (or Electors) are granted to each state. This gives smaller states a more equitable say in Presidential elections than larger, or simply more populous, states. Now, instead of populations electing a President, we have *states* which elect a president. Each state holds its own election, and the winner of the election in each state gets the votes of that state's Electors. There are 538 Electors, which equals the 435 members of the House of Representatives, 100 members of the Senate, and three representatives from the District of Columbia, as provided for by the 12th Amendment. *Congress*. The same debates which took place later regarding elections took place earlier regarding Congress. Smaller states wanted more representation, and feared the tyranny of the majority. Centralized authority advocates feared having their state's will overridden by smaller states or more distant states that had no relation to them. The framers came up with, I think, a rather brilliant plan. It took pieces from England's parliamentary system and improved on it. They created two Houses of Congress, each with a unique configuration. The layout, by the way, is all detailed in Article I of the Constitution. It is a true combination of population-based representation, and state-based representation, as is much of the Constitution. - The House of Representatives is made up of a certain number of Representatives from each state. That number is determined by the population of that state (which is determined every ten years as part of the US Census). The state population provides for congressional districts, which each get one member of the House of Representatives. This is population-based representation. - To balance this population/landmass representation, we also have a Senate, which is composed of two Senators from every state. In this body, all states are equal, and no state has any more or less influence than any other state. This is state-based representation. Congress functions (when it actually does) because it has a mix of both state-based and population-based representation.
