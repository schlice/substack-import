**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} So let's be clear. I'm not an atheist. But your characterization of what will happen to them is not exactly correct. First, the Bible does not say eternal **torture**. The Bible says eternal **torment**. Those two words are different. What you described is eternal **torture**, and the Bible does not say that. It does say that they will suffer eternal torment, but "unimaginable pain" is incorrect. The punishment of the wicked dead in hell is described throughout Scripture as "eternal fire" (Matthew 25:41), "unquenchable fire" (Matthew 3:12), "shame and everlasting contempt" (Daniel 12:2), a place where "the fire is not quenched" (Mark 9:44-49), a place of "torment" and "fire" (Luke 16:23-24), "everlasting destruction" (2 Thessalonians 1:9), a place where "the smoke of torment rises forever and ever" (Revelation 14:10-11), and a "lake of burning sulfur" where the wicked are "tormented day and night forever and ever" (Revelation 20:10). But "fire" in the Bible is often used as a symbol of God's **wrath** or **judgement.** God's "unquenchable fire" is His holy wrath, poured out on the unrepentant, for all of eternity. It is **punishment**, not **burning in unimaginable pain**. It is torment to fully know God and to be fully separated from His love. **That** is true punishment, the unquenchable fire of God's wrath. The punishment of those who actively reject God in hell is as never ending as the bliss of the righteous in heaven. Jesus Himself indicates that punishment in hell is just as everlasting as life in heaven (Matthew 25:46). The wicked are forever subject to the fury and the wrath of God. How? By being burned in pain forever? Not necessarily. Those in hell will acknowledge the perfect justice of God (Psalm 76:10). Those who are in hell will know that their punishment is just and that they alone are to blame (Deuteronomy 32:3-5). In short, people in hell will fully know the glory of God. They will fully know that they are eternally separated from that glory. And they will fully know that it was their own choices which put them there. Having that **full knowledge** of their circumstances, and the eternal separation from the love of God, is the one thing which will torment the unrepentant, day and night, for all of eternity. \ Now that we got that little detail out of the way, atheists are "willing to risk it" because they don't believe the evidence for the existence of God is convincing enough. In fact, they believe that accepting the truth of Christianity would require them to change their thinking, friends, priorities, lifestyle, or morals, and they are not quite willing to give up control over their lives in order to make those changes. They believe that life would be easier and more fun without such changes. Perhaps they realize that while Christianity is all about forgiveness, it's also about denying yourself and carrying your cross. Indeed, Christianity is free, but it can cost you your life. Atheists are simply unwilling to make those changes. But that's okay. They are free to make that choice, as God has given all of us **free will**. > In fact, that's the beauty of God's creation: if you're not willing to > accept Christianity, then you're free to reject it. This freedom to > make choices---even the freedom to reject truth---is what makes us > moral creatures and enables each of us to choose our ultimate destiny. > This really hits at the heart of why we exist at all, and why God > might not be as overt in revealing Himself to us as some would like. > For if the Bible is true, then God has provided each of us with the > opportunity to make an eternal choice to either accept Him or reject > Him. And in order to ensure that our choice is truly free, He puts us > in an environment that is filled with evidence of His existence, *but > without His direct presence*---a presence so powerful that it could > overwhelm our freedom and thus negate our ability to reject Him. > > In other words, God has provided enough evidence in this life to > convince anyone *willing* to believe, yet He has also left some > ambiguity so as not to compel the *unwilling*. > > In this way, God gives us the opportunity either to love Him or to > reject Him without violating our freedom. In fact, the purpose of this > life is to make that choice freely and without coercion. For love, by > definition, must be freely given.
