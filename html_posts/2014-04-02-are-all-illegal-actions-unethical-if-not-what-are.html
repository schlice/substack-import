--- title: '"Are all illegal actions unethical? If not, what are the boundaries between illegal actions that are ethical and those that are not? What are the criteria that can be used to decide this? Who gets to decide this?"' date: '2014-04-02' tags: - law layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** [Laws do not dictate morals or ethics. Morals can dictate law, but laws based on morality often make bad law. Morality is borne of the whole of society, so what is morally or ethically acceptable to one may not be ethically acceptable to another.\ \ For some, all illegal actions are unethical. Some people may never break the law even if the situation dictated that a law may need to be broken in order to fulfill an ethical requirement. Their own moral code tells them that it is wrong to break the law in any situation, so therefore, for them, their ethical code is violated every time the law is broken. Others will break the law if it is morally right to do so in that situation, because following one\'s moral and ethical code is more important than following the law at that particular moment. In order for this to happen, your personal moral code has to tell you that the law is not all-encompassing; the law is breakable if it meets a moral imperative.\ \ The example that was given was speeding (or safely bypassing a red light after coming to a full and complete stop and clearing for cross-traffic, which I may have done once or twice in my life) when racing to the emergency room with a critically ill or injured person (I have transported one of each in my lifetime). Can you be cited for breaking the law? Yes, absolutely. The law does not provide exceptions for speeding to a hospital. But ethically, it\'s the right thing to do to save someone\'s life, so when you break the law, you accept the consequences of doing so.\ \ If ethically, you can accept the consequences of breaking the law in order to meet a moral imperative, then the morality wins. For those who are guided by a solid moral compass, these decisions, while painful to make, can be made easily.\ \ Concealing a handgun on school property is one that would probably evoke more emotional responses from people. It is currently illegal for anyone with a concealed handgun permit to conceal their weapon in a school. But that\'s exactly what the \"active shooter\" wants: a place where no one is armed and they can kill as many people as possible before they are killed or kill themselves (active shooters are mentally unstable and have severely impaired judgement). If I carried my concealed handgun into a school and was able to stop an active shooter bent on killing as many people as he could, would I be wrong? Legally, I would be absolutely wrong for concealing my handgun on school property. But morally, I would have saved potentially many lives by breaking the law. I would have to accept the consequences of breaking the law in order to meet a moral imperative. Some would tell me that the moral imperative doesn\'t matter; breaking the law is unethical, period. Others would say that you do what you must to meet the moral imperative and you accept the consequences. It would be up to a judge to determine whether or not the moral imperative was enough of a mitigating circumstance to overcome the violation of the law. It may not, it may simply provide for a lesser consequence. But I would be responsible for my own actions and the consequences of those actions.\ \ This is the quandary we face when trying to balance following the law (which we know is important for a properly-functioning society) and following our moral and ethical code.]{.rendered_qtext}
