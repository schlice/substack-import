--- title: '"If federal agents are protected legally from following orders that are illegal, are there any such protections for refusing to follow orders that are immoral, unjust and possibly violate ethical codes of conduct?"' date: '2018-06-20' tags: - policy-analysis layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **If federal agents are protected legally from following orders that are illegal, are there any such protections for refusing to follow orders that are immoral, unjust and possibly violate ethical codes of conduct?** No. The reason is simple: **there is no standard for morality or ethics that is universal amongst all people.** My ethical and moral code is most likely different from yours. So there is no basis upon which we can set a baseline for behavior. If everyone is free to ignore orders based on their own moral code, then anyone can claim that their own moral code prevents them from following orders, and no one would follow orders, and no one could be prosecuted for failing to follow orders. Agents can refuse to follow *unlawful* orders. Agents cannot lawfully refuse to follow *unethical* or *immoral* orders. They can certainly do it and hope for the best in their disciplinary hearings, but there is no rule that allows an agent to refuse to follow an order because they personally find it immoral or unjust. An "ethical code of conduct" sounds like department policy. If an agency is issuing orders that violate its own internal policies, that should be addressed separately.
