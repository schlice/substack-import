**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} A **loophole** is defined as a *perfectly legal implementation of a law or a rule*, but which does not achieve the goal of the law or the rule. Therefore, by definition, if you found a true loophole (and you were not using any deception or illegal practices in order to execute it), it would not be illegal. Now, if your "loophole" was a *software glitch* which made the ticket machine spit out a free ticket when you hit a specific key combination, that's not a loophole, that's *theft* and you can indeed be charged with that. Here's an example of a loophole: The bus company gives free bus rides to students. You are a regular working shlub just like the rest of us, but in your spare time from home, you are enrolled in an online class wherein you are learning how to use a particular piece of software. The course is 12 weeks long, and it is free to join. *But your status as a "student" is recognized by the bus company* and you are registered as a "student" and thus you get free bus rides. This would be a "loophole." The idea of the free rides for students plan is to allow students to get to and from school, but you are not using it that way. However, since you are technically a "student," you do technically qualify for student rate bus passes. (EDIT) Since people still seem to be confused about what a loophole is, let me give you an example of what a loophole *is not.* When you ride the train, you are supposed to buy a ticket. The ticket shows your origin and your destination, and charges you a fee appropriately. If your origin station is rural, your origin station may not have turnstiles or any other form of barrier before boarding. No matter, you are still required to buy a ticket at your station of origin. **Failing to buy a ticket at your station of origin** ***is not a loophole***, because you are not legally using the system. This is actually called [[fare evasion](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=5a34fc&url=https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fare_evasion){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="wikipedia.org"}]{.qlink_container} and is illegal. It is not a loophole. \ Original question: I live in the UK and I found a loophole. By using that loophole, I don't pay for any bus tickets. Is it a crime or not? If it is a crime, what would be the result of it?
