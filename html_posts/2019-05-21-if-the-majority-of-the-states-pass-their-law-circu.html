Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **If the majority of the states pass their law circumventing the electoral college would it be upheld by the Supreme Court when it is challenged?** Almost certainly not. Let's look at the issue another way. The Supreme Court has stated that states have the absolute authority to decide how their electoral votes are divided. What if they decided to hold a lottery and sell their electoral votes to the candidate who paid them the most money? Do you think the Supreme Court would allow such a scheme? If you said no, then you already understand why the National Popular Vote (NPV) scheme is unconstitutional. There are several portions of the NPV which violate the Constitution, but the biggest one is related to the actual power of the state to designate Electors who participate in presidential elections. This power is a "delegated" power, unlike some other powers which are "reserved powers." Madison said that reserved powers are "numerous and indefinite," while delegated powers are "few and defined." For example, the power of a state to enter into compacts with other states is a reserved power. It generally involves the use of some other reserved power, such as taxes or water rights. NPV's advocates believe that because the power to enter into compacts is a reserved power, NPV is Constitutional. Delegated powers are specifically defined by the Constitution, and must be used in accordance with the reason why they were delegated in the first place (or in lawyer-speak, in accordance with the power's underlying purpose, and its historical context). Courts can intervene if a state tries to use a delegated power in a way in which it was not intended. The power of a state to appoint electors to participate in a presidential election is a delegated power. It must be used in the way it was intended: to represent the interests of *that state*. The Electoral College and its system of electors was designed to balance a number of competing interests, not just the popularity of the candidate. To that end, the system of Electors was designed so that they act in the best interests of their own state, in order to maintain the balance of all of those competing interests. NPV would have Electors acting on behalf of other states, and in just the same way that a state would not be allowed to sell Electors to the highest bidder, a state cannot declare Electors vote as agents of other states. This would be a breach of the public trust, and would therefore be unconstitutional. Additionally, because NPV would have a majority of electoral votes, it would functionally repeal the Constitutional provision requiring the House of Representatives to hold a run-off election in the event that no candidate reached the requisite 270 electoral votes. This action requires a Constitutional amendment to implement.
