--- title: "Besides multiple sclerosis, what other neurological disorders would cause brain lesions that continue to grow in size and number over the years?" date: "2016-09-04" tags: ["health-autoimmune"] layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} There are many disorders which can cause the symptoms of MS, but there are a scant few that cause white spots on an MRI, and there are none others that I know of which cause lesions which grow in size and number over the years. As a matter of fact, there is one disease that is specifically ruled out by checking to see if there are both old and new lesions. **Acute disseminated encephalomyelitis (ADEM)** is a disease which causes brief but intense inflammation in the brain and spinal cord, and sometimes the optic nerve, as well as causing white-spot-producing damage to myelin. Neurologists will look for evidence of old and new lesions, which would indicate a progression in disease more similar to MS than ADEM. At a first attack with no old lesions, it is difficult to differentiate MS from ADEM. Doctors will closely monitor patients who experience such attacks to see if there is reoccurrence to suggest that the disease is MS. **Lyme Disease** can sometimes produce positive MRI results, especially in Lyme which is undiagnosed for long periods of time. Again, though, progression of disease usually indicates MS and not Lyme. Early Lyme disease responds well to antibiotics, whereas MS does not. Finally, **leukodystrophies** are a group of diseases which affect myelin, or the white matter of the brain. Myelin is made up of about ten different chemicals. These leukodystrophies affect how the body metabolizes or produces these chemicals. Each of the leukodystrophies is a defect in a gene which controls the formation and metabolization of one of these chemicals. Specifically, they are metachromatic leukodystrophy, Krabb√© disease, adrenoleukodystrophy, Pelizaeus-Merzbacher disease, Canavan disease, Childhood Ataxia with Central Nervous System Hypomyelination or CACH (also known as Vanishing White Matter Disease), Alexander disease, Refsum disease, and cerebrotendinous xanthomatosis. There are likely others, but this is what I came up with off the top of my head. Hopefully some of my esteemed colleagues can fill in the blanks.
