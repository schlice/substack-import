--- title: '"How do the findings of the study on infant deaths in Texas following the anti-abortion law compare to national trends?"' date: '2024-06-24' tags: - law layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} So to answer your question, [[the study](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=f10313&url=https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamapediatrics/fullarticle/2819785?guestAccessKey%3D7d568a16-aea5-41e6-bb65-f9c586b2ecb0%26utm_source%3DFor_The_Media%26utm_medium%3Dreferral%26utm_campaign%3Dftm_links%26utm_content%3Dtfl%26utm_term%3D062424){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="jamanetwork.com"}]{.qlink_container} found that between 2021 and 2022, infant deaths in Texas rose from 1,985 to 2,240, a year-over-year increase of 255 deaths. This corresponds to a 12.9 percent increase in infant deaths in Texas versus a 1.8 percent increase in infant deaths in the rest of the U.S. during the same period. The study defines infants as under 12 months old. So there's the answer to the question. Now, what do you do with this information? Well, in order to draw a *correct* understanding, you need more information. For example, one might ask how "infant deaths" is related to abortion. Ah, there's the rub. The study goes on to try to interpret the data for you (which is where the study turns from science to propaganda) and explains that the reason you see this increase in "infant death" is because babies with terminal abnormalities are being allowed to be born and then are dying before 12 months of age, which is classified as an "infant death." *Whoa, wait a second. So the only reason it is classified as an "infant death" is because those babies were not allowed to be* ***aborted?*** Yep, that's right. So let's look at the difference between Texas and, say, California, when it comes to the same baby, so we can see the difference: - California: baby with fetal abnormalities is aborted: **dead baby.** - Texas: baby with fetal abnormalities cannot be aborted, is born, and dies of these fetal abnormalities: **dead baby.** So the result is exactly the same: a dead baby. But on one hand, it's called an abortion, and on the other hand, it's called an "infant death." Therefore, **the study means absolutely nothing.** Literally, the only thing that the study shows is that when you disallow abortion because of fetal abnormalities, some babies will be born and will die before they turn a year old. That makes the "infant mortality" rate arbitrarily climb, because those babies are not aborted before they are born. *This is expected behavior in Texas, this should not be a surprise.* However, it's the analysis of the study that is more telling. *The study's authors find that to be a worse crime than abortion, because of the hardship the family must endure.* The authors posit (without evidence) that it is easier on the families to simply abort the baby rather than to allow it to be born and die, so the more moral position is abortion. \ But this view is incorrect, because the study is looking at the wrong data. In order to have answered the correct question, the study's authors should have investigated the following: ***How many babies with fetal abnormalities were born and DID NOT DIE?*** The study failed to even address that point. In other words: in Texas, it's possible that some of those babies may have survived, whereas in California, that same baby *would never have been given the chance to try to survive.* Of course, we don't know how many babies with pre-existing fetal abnormalities survived, because the study authors did not address that issue. Anecdotal reports of babies diagnosed with fetal abnormalities being born and surviving (even thriving) are abundant on the Internet. I'm not going to speculate why that is, because those are topics for other Spaces, and in any case, it is not materially related to this question. But what we *can* say is that, at least in Texas, we give the baby a fighting chance instead of killing it before it even has the chance to fight.
