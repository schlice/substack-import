**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} > *Question Details:* > > *My family and I have been recently burglarized and I thought it was > surprising they did not steal any technology (laptops, television set) > nor did they steal any jewelry. They did manage to get away with some > cash and a briefcase filled with important documents such as birth > certificates, social security cards, blank checks etc. A couple of > questions I have are: What can they do with that information? How can > I retrieve these important documents back?* I answer from experience; I was the victim of credit card fraud, so I am thoroughly intimate with this process, more so than I ever wanted to be. [[Andy McGee](https://www.quora.com/profile/Andy-McGee)]{.qlink_container} hit the nail on the head, thanks for your contribution Andy. They don\'t actually want your things, they want money for your things, so they have to convert your things into money. And your identity is much much more valuable than the paper it is printed on, and much easier to convert to money than physical items. Identity theft is the fastest growing crime in the world. It is immensely profitable and is very hard to catch an identity thief. It is the Internet that has made identity theft so popular, so easy, and so profitable. A good identity thief can steal your documents and make thousands of dollars on their sale to those who will use them to open credit cards, perpetrate bank takeover fraud, and do a hundred other things, as you, and you\'ll incur thousands of dollars worth of debt before you can blink an eye. Just look at all of the thefts from major retailers of credit card transaction information: Hobby Lobby, Target, and most recently The Home Depot are some of the largest thefts in recent months. These days, these things are FAR more valuable than physical items like TVs or laptops. Items have to be sold, and it\'s a lot harder to sell these things at a pawn shop than it used to be. Law enforcement works closely with the pawn shops and police looking for stolen items will know if they are pawned almost as soon as the sale is complete. ***This is important: You have to move quickly to protect your credit from damage that will take a very, very long time to correct.*** Therefore, if you haven\'t done so already: 1\. Contact all three major credit bureaus ([[Experian](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=a3cf12&url=http://www.experian.com){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="experian.com"}]{.qlink_container}, [[Equifax](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=72c490&url=http://www.equifax.com){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="equifax.com"}]{.qlink_container} , and [[Trans Union](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=e9ce70&url=http://www.transunion.com){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="transunion.com"}]{.qlink_container}). Complete the process of identifying on your credit that you are the victim of identity theft. They will put a ***fraud alert*** on your credit report and prospective creditors will contact you before anyone has the opportunity to open up accounts in your name, using your identity. **THIS IS VERY IMPORTANT! THIS NEEDS TO BE DONE RIGHT AWAY!** The reason you have to act quickly is that identity thieves can strike within **hours** of stealing your information, and it can take **years** to recover your credit. Do not hesitate, do this immediately! You can also put a ***credit freeze*** on your credit, which means that you can\'t use credit **at all**. This is a fairly drastic step, but it may be worth it until you can get your affairs in order. You can remove the credit freeze with a PIN that you establish when you place the freeze. In addition, you should obtain copies of your credit reports (you can get them for free if you were the victim of identity theft). Check them carefully and contact any entities with which you did not do business. 2\. You mentioned that blank checks were stolen. Contact that bank and work with them. If you can identify the range of check numbers that was taken, you can re-issue checks with a completely different range (for example, if checks 100-375 were stolen, you can get new checks with check numbers starting at 1000, and anything coming in with a check number lower than that will be bounced). Worst case, the bank can close your old account and move your assets to a completely new account number. 3\. Birth Certificates: You will have to get the state in which you were born to re-issue you copies of your birth certificates. Notarized copies are as valid as real birth certificates, but you will have to have them re-issued. Check the State website for the state in which you were born to find out how to get new copies of your birth certificates. 4\. Social Security Cards: These will need to be re-issued by the Social Security Administration. You will have to have the SSA re-issue your Social Security cards. 5\. Finally, enroll in a credit monitoring service. LifeLock is one, but there are others. The credit bureaus offer their own services. My employer offers one for its employees. This is part of the \"layered security\" you need to put in place on your credit, along with your fraud alerts you placed in step 1. That step will be the biggest deterrent, but this one is also important as you will know when the illicit activity is happening. The police may also want to know as part of their investigations, so along with the fraud alerts and the credit bureaus contacting you when someone is trying to use your credit, credit monitoring services will also be helpful when someone is simply \"looking you up\" to see what your credit looks like. Good luck.
