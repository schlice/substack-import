--- title: "Why do people give up their rights and choose to live in an HOA?" date: "2023-05-31" tags: ["homeowners-assn"] layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} What rights, exactly, am I giving up? The right to paint my house electric blue? The right to not mow my lawn for six months? The right to leave my front porch looking like a shithole? The right to have six cars in my front lawn, only three of them with wheels? The reason why I choose to live in an HOA is not to control *my* behavior. I already do that on my own; I don't need an HOA to tell me how to live. Everything that the HOA would ever tell me, I already do. I choose to live in a neighborhood with an HOA (and I serve on the Board of Directors) to make sure that ***my neighbors*** control their own behavior. *And why do I care what my neighbors do?* Because when everyone is forced to keep their houses and properties looking neat and orderly, ***home values remain as high as possible, even in a down market.*** So we choose to live in a community with an HOA to ***PROTECT OUR OWN INVESTMENTS***. It's really that simple. Plus, there are fringe benefits of living in a community with an HOA. The HOA coordinates the collection of money to pay for insurance for the common areas (so if one of my kids gets hurt playing there, someone can help pay for the medical bills). They also pay for the landscaping in the common areas and at the entrance monoliths, and the water to keep those areas pretty. They coordinate community events to foster a sense of community. They pay for amenities like playgrounds and dog parks, and even if you don't take advantage of those amenities, they make your community more attractive to prospective buyers. I totally understand that some people simply don't like anyone ever telling them what to do, especially neighbors. I get it. It's your house. You paid for it. You should be able to do anything you like to it, and you shouldn't need anyone's permission to do it. But even if you don't live in a community with an HOA, don't you sometimes have to get permission to do some things? If you want to put up a tower to create a HAM Radio base station, doesn't the government in which your house is located control that? If you want to start farming pigs on your property, doesn't your local government control that? So it's not that someone is telling you what you can and cannot do, and what you must get permission to do before you do it (because you always have that). It's the **level of control** you object to. Again, that's just fine. But because I don't live out in the middle of nowhere, because I choose to live in a neighborhood with 114 other homes, and because, if I ever decide to sell my house, I want to get the maximum value from it, I'm going to welcome that HOA ensuring that my neighbors toe the line, because people won't buy a pretty house in a shitty neighborhood. And if they do, they won't pay top dollar for it.
