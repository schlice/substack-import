**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **How does dismantling and/or defunding the police make any sense?** It doesn't. The people championing this position are either uninformed, or are willfully anti-authority, of any kind. Maybe even a little bit of both. The way we change the behavior of police officers is the same way we change the behavior of criminals. Remove the motive. For example, if I'm a purse snatcher, and I work in an area where I have a guaranteed force monopoly (i.e. people are not allowed to carry guns), I can make a good living snatching purses and there's little chance I'm going to face any resistance, armed or otherwise. Even if there's a 1 in 100 chance that I run into a woman who is a black belt in multiple forms and she beats the snot out of me, for a 1 in 100 chance, I'm still clearing a large, tax-free salary. But what if I allow firearms and now 50% of purse-carrying women are armed? Now there's a 50% chance that I'm going to grab a purse and **die**. Now, it's no longer profitable to be a purse snatcher, so I stop. So what is it that makes it "profitable" for a racist cop to exist? **Protection from prosecution**. We have to revamp the doctrine of *qualified immunity*. We have to make it so that we can disallow qualified immunity in specific cases where it is clear that misconduct took place, either willful or ignorant misconduct. > An ideologically diverse collection of organizations---including the > ACLU, the Cato Institute, and the Law Enforcement Action > Partnership---have submitted multiple amicus briefs to the Supreme > Court, describing a "cross-ideological consensus that this Court's > qualified immunity doctrine under 42 U.S.C. 1983 misunderstands that > statute and its common-law backdrop, denies justice to victims of > egregious constitutional violations, and fails to provide > accountability for official wrongdoing."[\[1\]](#ydpRL){#cite-ydpRL > .citation_link} This is the time for the court to either severely revamp, or perhaps eliminate, the doctrine of qualified immunity. The net effect of this for police officers is that civil rights violation suits (which seems to be the biggest issue today) would increase, and the focus of those investigations would get back to what it is supposed to be about: determining whether or not police officers exceeded their Constitutional authority. But it wouldn't change Constitutional *protections*, it would only allow courts to review these cases without being barred by qualified immunity. In essence, every action a police officer takes will have to be taken knowing that he could face a civil rights case, and his defense had better be spot on. Causing an officer to pause shouldn't have a chilling effect on law enforcement, because it doesn't change Constitutional protections at all. It only allows civil rights cases to move forward instead of simply failing at pre-trial motions. It allows courts to do what they are designed to do, and allows civil rights actions to be properly adjudicated.
