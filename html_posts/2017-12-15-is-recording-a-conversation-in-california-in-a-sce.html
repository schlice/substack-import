**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **Is recording a conversation in California in a scenario where the participants can\'t expect privacy legal?** So it appears you're asking specifically about situations which are **not** telephone recordings, since there is an expectation of privacy on a telephone call. Phone calls are covered by wiretap laws, and so California's "two party requirement" to record phone calls is in full effect on the phone. However, recording conversations that are **not** over the telephone is a whole different ballgame. According to Harvard's Digital Media Law Project: > If you are recording someone without their knowledge in a public or > semi-public place like a street or restaurant, the person whom you\'re > recording may or may not have \"an objectively reasonable expectation > that no one is listening in or overhearing the conversation,\" and the > reasonableness of the expectation would depend on the particular > factual circumstances. Therefore, you cannot necessarily assume that > you are in the clear simply because you are in a public place. > [\[1\]](#oUsnm){#cite-oUsnm .citation_link} I would say that this means that if you're a party to the conversation and you're recording it, you *might* be legally okay to use that recording as evidence, but if you're simply *eavesdropping* on a conversation, you would probably not be covered. California enacted some pretty tough anti-paparazzi laws, and they apply to video and audio recordings related to people who are not party to the conversation. As you can see, California recording privacy laws are really sketchy and generally favor the rights of the individuals involved over the admissibility of recording evidence. So if you are planning on making an evidentiary recording that would be admissible in court, it would probably be best in any setting to get the consent of everyone being recorded before you start.
