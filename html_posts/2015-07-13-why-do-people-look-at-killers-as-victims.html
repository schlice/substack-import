**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} > *Question details:* > > *I kind of understand the logic behind it, but not really. This girl > was trying to justify the actions of Eric Harris and Dylan Klebold by > saying they were victims and that their families lost a loved one. > Their families did lose someone they loved a lot but I feel like we > still shouldn\'t look at them as victims because of their actions. Why > do people try to justify this?* If that were the case (that suspects couldn\'t actually also be victims), then there would be no need for defense in our legal system. If the facts were present, you go straight to jail. Suspects can have mitigating circumstances that cause them to do what they do. Just because they are a suspect doesn\'t mean that they aren\'t a victim of another set of circumstances as well. If we want to focus only on **punishment**, then your sentiment is correct: they are criminals, they get punished, end of story. If, however, we also want to try to prevent **future crimes**, we have to try to figure out why they did what they did. What we discovered was a \"bullying\" culture in some schools that led to the creation of killers like Klebold and Harris. As a society, that we allowed this to happen is ***our*** failure. Since then, schools have adopted zero-tolerance policies on bullying and bullying behavior. Teachers have been trained to recognize and put an end to bullying behavior. It\'s much much better than it was 15 years ago in Littleton. It\'s certainly not perfect, but it\'s much much better.
