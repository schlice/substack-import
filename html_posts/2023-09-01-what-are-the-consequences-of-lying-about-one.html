**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} Well, since you don't put your income on a W-4, you don't really need to worry about it. What you ***do*** put on it is the number of *exemptions* you have, so that your employer can calculate how much of your salary to withhold for taxes. Your company then pays your *estimated* income tax on your behalf, right out of your paycheck. Then, at the end of the year, you do an IRS form 1040, wherein you calculate your *actual* taxes due, taking into account everything you earned, all of your exemptions, and all of your deductions and credits. - If the amount your company withheld is more than your actual taxes due, you get a refund. - If the amount your company withheld is less than your actual taxes due, you owe the difference. Putting exemptions on your W-4 to which you are not entitled simply means you end up owing tax at the end of the year rather than getting that sweet, sweet refund check. But if you withhold ***too much***, then you don't get enough of your paycheck throughout the year. Sure, you get a massive refund check, but you could have used that money throughout the year. So it's only to your benefit that you do your W-4 correctly, and set your withholdings right.
