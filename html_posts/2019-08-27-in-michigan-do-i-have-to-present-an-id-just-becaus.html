Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **In Michigan, do I have to present an ID just because an officer asks for it?** Only if you are driving a car or performing some other licensed activity. If you're driving a car, you must present a valid drivers license upon demand. Since there is no requirement for identification at any other time out in public, you cannot be compelled to produce identification in any other routine "walking down the street" situation. Whether or not you are required to identify yourself when a police officer asks is a different story. If you are just having a general "how's the weather" conversation with a police officer, you're under no obligation to tell them anything at all, including your name. Those are called "consensual interviews" because you are communicating by mutual consent (like, you know, normal people). If you ever are concerned whether or not you are being questioned or the conversation is a consensual interview, just ask if you're free to go. If you are, most likely your conversation was a consensual interview. If the police have conducted what is known as a *Terry stop* (named after the Supreme Court case in 1968 *Terry v. Ohio*), whether or not you are required to identify yourself is a matter of state law. Most states have established "stop and identify" laws that state that if the police are conducting a *Terry stop*, you are required to, at a minimum, provide your real name and address. Michigan does not have a "stop and identify" law, so you are not required to identify yourself even during a *Terry* stop. If you are engaged in a *Terry* stop, you simply need to say that you want to remain silent. Just saying, "I wish to remain silent" is enough.
