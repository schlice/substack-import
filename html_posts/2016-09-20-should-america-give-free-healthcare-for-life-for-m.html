Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} Our military does a job that few understand, and even fewer would volunteer to do. We owe more to our veterans than we could ever repay. And let's understand something else: their healthcare is not free. They paid for it in sweat equity for 20 years. Should they do it? Absolutely. For more reasons than you can count. That said, the process they have in place currently is exactly what they should have, no more, no less. The actual post-retirement benefits programs are maintained by the Veteran's Administration, not by the military service. The service only runs healthcare while the member is active. If you served honorably for 20 years, you and your dependents can remain in the military healthcare system for the remainder of your life. This is a **benefit** afforded to military members, and an incentive to complete a career as a military member. If you served for fewer than 20 years, you do not get healthcare for life, unless you were disabled during military service with a disability rating of at least 30%, with a condition directly related to your military service (also known as a *service-connected disability*). If this is the case, any healthcare needed to manage your disability is covered by the veteran's healthcare system so long as you maintain that rating (some conditions can improve, particularly mental health). All gave some, some gave all.
