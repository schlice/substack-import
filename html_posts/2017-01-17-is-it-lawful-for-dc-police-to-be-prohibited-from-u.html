--- title: "Is it lawful for DC police to be prohibited from using body cameras while on duty at protests on inauguration day?" date: "2017-01-17" tags: ["law-enforcement"] layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} The short answer is that it is lawful to prohibit police from activating their BWCs unless they are actively responding to a call for service, or have engaged in an incident. The civil rights organizations feel that it's dangerous for police to be able to record everything, because that feels too much like a "police state" and that the video can be used against honest citizens taking part in Constitutionally-protected activity. Those same civil rights organizations will have to reap what they sow when the police videos presented at trial do not contain any of the "lead-up" details before an incident, the very things that those same privacy advocates wanted police to have BWCs for in the first place. The BWC will only be activated when an officer engages in a call or is responding to a call. You can see an incident, but if you don't see what happened *before* the incident, you don't see the justification for the use of force recorded. This means that you must fall back to traditional jurisprudence in order to determine whether or not the use of force was lawful, and the BWC itself becomes less efficient. So depending on the local ordinances and the timing of the officer engagement, it is possible that some of the lead-up to the incident may not be recorded. A jurisdiction is well within its rights to limit police in this way. They must understand, however, that there are consequences to such laws.
