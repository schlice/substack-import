**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} The "Q Source" (or the Q Document or the Document of Q) is part of the "two-source hypothesis" which has been debated but never proven regarding the Gospels of Matthew and Luke. > The case for Q\'s existence follows from the argument that neither > Matthew nor Luke is directly dependent on the other in the double > tradition (defined by New Testament scholars as material that Matthew > and Luke share that does not appear in Mark). However, the verbal > agreement between Matthew and Luke is so close in some parts of the > double tradition that the most reasonable explanation for this > agreement is common dependence on a written source or > sources.[\[1\]](#AyQuY){#cite-AyQuY .citation_link} :::: ui_qtext_image_outer ::: qtext_image_wrapper ![](images/qimg-49aca61c9f67d73b88874dd0b4055bd9){.portrait .qtext_image .zoomable_in_feed master_src="images/qimg-49aca61c9f67d73b88874dd0b4055bd9" master_w="788" master_h="921"} ::: :::: *(By Alecmconroy - Own work, CC BY-SA 4.0, from* [[*Wikimedia Commons*](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=67181d&url=https://commons.wikimedia.org/w/index.php?curid%3D3158802){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="wikimedia.org"}]{.qlink_container}*)* You can see in the graphic how the Gospels of Matthew and Luke both use elements of the Q Source, along with two other sources, called the [[M Source](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=692cc0&url=https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M_source){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="wikipedia.org"}]{.qlink_container} and the [[L Source](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=02f050&url=https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L_source){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="wikipedia.org"}]{.qlink_container}, represented by green and teal respectively. But for as many arguments as exist for the existence of a Q Source, there exist just as many *against* the idea of a Q Source: > While the two-source hypothesis remains the most popular explanation > for the synoptic gospels\' origins, the existence of the \"minor > agreements\" has raised serious concerns. These minor agreements are > those points where Matthew and Luke agree against or beyond Mark > precisely within their Marcan verses (for example, the mocking > question at the beating of Jesus, \"Who is it that struck you?\", > found in both Matthew and Luke but not in Mark, although this \"minor > agreement\" falls outside the usually accepted range of Q.) The > \"minor agreements\" call into question the proposition that Matthew > and Luke knew Mark but not each other, e.g. Luke might have indeed > been following Matthew, or at least a Matthew-like source. Peabody and > McNicol argue that until a reasonable explanation is found the > two-source hypothesis is not viable.[\[2\]](#iLGjQ){#cite-iLGjQ > .citation_link} So at best, the Q Source is a *hypothesis* to explain how the two Gospels are so closely related at some points. At worst, it's just a misunderstanding of what came first and who knew whom. So to answer the question: *what happened to the Q Source?* Only God knows, since we aren't even entirely sure that it *exists at all.* \ Original question: What happened to Document Q, an early copy of the Christian scriptures? Like early Greek texts verify, embellishments such as the Johannine Comma (included to support the Trinity) would have become readily apparent.
