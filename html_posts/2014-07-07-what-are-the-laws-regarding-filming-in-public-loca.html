**Content:** [With little variation, if you are in a public place, anything you see is fair game. If someone doesn\'t want to be videoed, or have their picture taken, they shouldn\'t be out in public. There is no \"reasonable expectation of privacy\" in a public place. Someone can tell you to stop, but if you are in public and the subject you are recording is also in public, you\'re okay. The basic rule of thumb to follow in public is \"if you can see it, you can shoot it.\"\ \ If you are in a business that is open to the public, however, the rules change slightly. Unless it is clearly posted that you cannot take pictures within the business, you are also allowed to video whatever you like, but the owner of the business (or an employee) is allowed to tell you to stop and if you fail to comply, he can ask you to leave. As soon as he does, that public place is no longer \"public\" to you, and you must vacate immediately or risk being tossed out by law enforcement for trespassing.\ \ If someone has a \"reasonable expectation of privacy,\" they cannot be videoed without their consent. If you are on private property, taking photos is considered trespassing. While most government buildings are fine, courthouses are off-limits for photography, as well as military bases.\ \ Generally, when someone is shooting a film in a public place, the people on camera will sign forms authorizing the film studio to use their likeness in that film. This is something completely different: you can certainly video someone in public, but you cannot use anyone\'s recognizable likeness for commercial gain without their consent.
