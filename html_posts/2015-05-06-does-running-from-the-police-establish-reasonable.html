--- title: "Does running from the police establish reasonable suspicion or probable cause for a detention or an arrest?" date: "2015-05-06" tags: ["law-enforcement", "law"] layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** [For the most part, [[Jonathan Books](https://www.quora.com/profile/Jonathan-Books)]{.qlink_container} is correct. Running from the police alone is not probable cause. If you are allowed to walk away from the police, who cares how fast you walk?\ \ However, in some \"high crime\" jurisdictions, some special waivers have been enacted to help combat that crime which essentially say that running from the police provides \"reasonable suspicion,\" which is a lower level of proof required than probable cause, to stop said runner. If the person was present in a high crime area, exhibiting all the behavior of someone conducting criminal activity, and then they run when the police arrive, that creates reasonable suspicion to stop that person. A \"stop-and-frisk\" revealing evidence of the criminal activity that the person is suspected of doing would then provide the needed probable cause for arrest.\ \ Jonathan is not correct in saying that the person would then be required to stop when the police yell stop because they were running in the first place. Remember, if it\'s not illegal to run from the police, then if they tell you to stop after you already started running, they have no authority to do so (i.e. tell you to stop). If they didn\'t have the authority to detain you, they certainly don\'t have the authority to tell you to stop and wait for them (which is a form of detainment). In that case, as long as they say the magic words \"Stop, Police!\", then they can stop anyone from running, anytime, anywhere, perfectly legally. Therefore, that is not necessarily true. It would add an aggravating factor if the police already had a reasonable suspicion, but the police saying the magic words isn\'t enough to create reasonable suspicion.\ \ (EDIT) I want to be clear about something. The fact that an officer may not have the authority to tell you to stop ***is not an invitation to continue to run, citing the officer\'s lack of authority as your excuse!*** If an officer tells you to stop, STOP! The lack of authority I mentioned is ONLY useful to your lawyer, who will use that information to get all your charges thrown out, along with potentially providing grounds for false arrest charges against the department.\ \ So to reiterate what I just said, if an officer tells you to stop, you MUST stop. Let your lawyer determine if the officer had the authority to do what he did. That\'s what we pay our lawyers for.]{.rendered_qtext}
