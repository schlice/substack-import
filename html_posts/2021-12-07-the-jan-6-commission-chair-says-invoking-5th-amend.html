**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} No, totally wrong. Numerous Supreme Court decisions have upheld the validity of the right to 5th Amendment protections in Congressional inquiries. If it is valid to use the 5th Amendment in a Congressional hearing, then all the same caveats which apply to the invocation of that Amendment still apply even though it's not a criminal trial. And the most notable of those caveats was a SCOTUS case called *Slochower* v. *Board of Higher Ed. of New York City* from 1956, in which the Court made it exceedingly clear: invoking the 5th Amendment does not amount to an admission of guilt. The right extends to any answer that would "furnish a link in the chain of evidence," which SCOTUS has stated includes a refusal to answer or to testify. Therefore, **no one may make any implication of guilt** based on an invocation of the 5th Amendment.
