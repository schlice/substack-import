**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **Is it possible to keep voting in my home state when I can\'t attend jury duty?** > I just got a jury summons in a State that I don't live most of the > year. Despite that, I consider it my home state (my family is there, I > go back several weekends a year and every holiday). I'm working most > of the week in the state that I live most of the time because of an > assignment from my company (it'll be another two-three years before I > can move back). I have to explain my situation to the jury summons, > but I don't want to lose my ability to vote in my home state? Will > this affect my ability to vote? This is a question of state residency, not a voting question. Which state you can vote in is based on which state is your **state of residence**, while jury duty is based on which state provides your **driver's license**, which is a secondary function to which state is your state of residence. The voting question is the simple one. You vote in your state of residence, pure and simple. If you're not going to be there during the election, you contact your county clerk's office, you advise them you will be out of state during the election, and they send you an *absentee ballot* which will allow you to vote through the mail. The residency question is more complicated. It's interesting, because most states use the IRS' determination of state residency to determine whether or not you are a resident of a state. To that end, please allow me to quote from TurboTax's website about moving and state residency (emphasis mine). In your case, you'll want to follow as many of the bullets as you can to prove your permanent residency in your home state despite spending more time in your new state simply for work: > The truth is that it\'s often easier to become a permanent resident in > your new state than it is to stop being a permanent resident in your > old state. In most states, even though you are presumed to be a > resident after you\'ve lived there six months, you may have to be gone > from your old state for 18 months before you are considered by the > time test to be a nonresident. > > So, **it is often more important to show your intent to leave a state > than it is to show your intent to become a resident in a new one.** If > you end up with both states wanting to claim taxes on your income, > your evidence of intent will be crucial. > > If you intend for your move to be temporary, avoid doing the things we > just listed for proving permanent resident status, and do the > following instead: > > - Vote by mail in your home state instead of changing your voter > registration to the new state. > - Keep the registration for your car in your old state, but be sure to > call your insurer and find out if you need different coverage > because your car will be in the new state for a temporary period of > time. > - Keep your driver\'s license from your old state up-to-date. > - Live in rented property in your new state, and maintain your > household in your old state. > - If you purchase a house to live in while you\'re in your new state, > don\'t apply for any special property tax exemption that you would > be entitled to if it were your primary residence. If you\'re only > there temporarily, then your primary residence is still in your old > state. > - Don\'t move your family to the new state. > - Open a new bank account in your new state if you need it for your > convenience, but keep your main bank accounts in your old state. > - If you belong to certain social or business organizations, check > with the chapter, group or similar organizations in your new state > to see if you can have temporary visiting membership rights. The > fact that you made an inquiry about membership rights may be a > deciding factor if your residency is challenged. > > Most important, be sure to move back to your old state when your > temporary period of residence is over. So it's less important how long you're in the new state for work. It's more important *where you declare your state of residency*, and that you intend for your home state to *remain* your state of residency.
