**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} The answer, as with all legal answers, is "it depends." And note that this is an answer exclusive to labor laws in the United States; other countries may be different. In this case, it depends on whether or not you are an "at-will" employee, or a "contract" employee. A "contract employee" has signed an employment contract which specifies things like the term of employment, the reimbursement rate, and how employment will be terminated. If you are a contract employee, you must follow your contract's rules about how to sever your employment. If your contract requires a certain timeframe between notification of termination and actual work end date, you must follow that, or you could be in breach of contract. For everyone else, most states follow the "at-will employment" model. Both the employer and the employee can terminate the working relationship at any time for any reason, or no reason at all, with the exception of the fact that employers cannot terminate employees for legally-restricted reasons (age, sex, religion, country of national origin, color, etc). If you are an at-will employee, you technically do not legally need to inform your employer of your desire to terminate the employment relationship until you are walking out the door. All things considered, you probably should give them some heads-up. Traditionally, employees would provide "two week's notice" before leaving in order to give the employer an opportunity to have you train someone else in your job, or to find a replacement, but today that is just a formality. It is not legally required to give two week's notice before you leave.
