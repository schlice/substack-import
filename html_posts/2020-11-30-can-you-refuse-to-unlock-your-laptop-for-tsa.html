**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **Can you refuse to unlock your laptop for TSA?** For context, we are talking about United States domestic airline travel within the United States. We are **NOT** talking about a US citizen at a Customs and Border Patrol checkpoint while you are traveling back into the United States from abroad. Totally different set of rules at the border. \ I have been a frequent flier for many decades now. *At worst,* during the whole 9/11 airline scare and increased airport security, I was occasionally asked to open and power up my laptop, so that they could see it was a laptop that actually booted, and not a bomb. I have only had that experience maybe twice in my life. They saw the beginnings of the boot sequence and then let me go. Never have I been asked to power up my laptop and log in. **Because that's illegal.** In order for any US law enforcement official to examine the *contents* of your computer, they must have a **search warrant**. TSA is **not** law enforcement. TSA is exactly what it stands for: Transportation Security. They work for the Department of Homeland Security. They are not law enforcement. The **only** time you must enter your password and unlock your laptop is when you are presented with a **search warrant**. If you are presented with a search warrant and you refuse to comply, you can be arrested and charged with obstruction of justice, or whatever the appropriate crime is in your jurisdiction. However, *none of this* would happen in the TSA screening line.
