**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} As with all legal questions, the answer is "it depends." In this case, it depends on whether or not the movie is going to *make money.* For example, if the movie in question is a public service announcement, or a news story, you may not need to get permission, even though it is highly recommended. The question turns on whether or not the movie is using someone else\'s name, likeness, or other personal attributes without permission *for an exploitative purpose.* Don\'t get hung up on terminology here, "exploitative purpose\" essentially just means where somebody makes profit from the movie. This doesn\'t mean that if a nonprofit corporation makes money at all, the movie "made money." A non-profit corporation can make a movie that doesn\'t sell tickets. The non-profit corporation can still make money from their advertised nonprofit endeavors without making money from the movie. Nevertheless, as a general rule, if you use anyone\'s name or likeness in any sort of media production, it is always best practice to get their permission. If you have experienced a situation in which someone has used your name or likeness in a movie without your permission, and you think you may have a claim, it is imperative that you contact a licensed attorney in your area who specializes in copyright law, to assist you with determining whether or not you have a claim for invasion of privacy through misappropriation of name or likeness (\"misappropriation\"), or violation of the right of publicity. \ Original question: Can someone use your likeness in a movie without your permission?
