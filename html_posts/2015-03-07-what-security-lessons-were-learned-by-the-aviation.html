**Content:** [Hijacking aircraft, and the attitudes surrounding it, have been the biggest changes in the aviation industry.\ \ Before 9/11, a hijacker would \"take over\" an aircraft by holding the pilot hostage and demanding the plane be flown to a particular location. The plane would be met at the destination airport by police, a hostage situation would ensue, and the hijacker would either surrender to police or be killed. Easy peesey.\ \ 9/11 completely changed the attitude towards hijackers, and completely changed the security posture inside of an aircraft. During the 9/11 attacks, instead of taking the pilots hostage and demanding they fly to some location, the hijackers *killed* the pilots and took over control of flying the aircraft, to turn the aircraft into human-guided missiles. This was a quantum shift in the hijacking game, one which changed the face of in-aircraft security, and changed the way we think about hijackers.\ \ Have you noticed that not too many planes have been hijacked since 9/11? The incidence of hijackers on aircraft has dropped substantially, and 9/11 is the proximate cause of that change. Cockpit security has been dramatically increased, and any attempts at incursion into the cockpit have been met with violent and swift response, binding any attackers until the aircraft can make its first emergency landing to remove the unruly passenger. Even drunkards on aircraft are dealt with more harshly now. There is very little tolerance these days for un-gentlemanlike or un-ladylike behavior aboard an actively flying aircraft.]{.rendered_qtext}
