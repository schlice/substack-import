**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} No, nothing like that. 13 states composed "trigger laws," which are laws that would only go into effect if *Roe* was overturned (that\'s the *trigger*). That\'s literally the only unique thing about the laws. The laws vary in their implementation. Some of the laws allow exceptions for rape and incest, and some do not. Some of these trigger laws have been on the books for years, simply waiting for the day that *Roe* would be overturned. Some have just very recently been passed. But make no mistake, **there is no law that would prevent a doctor from saving a mother\'s life by taking the baby if it was medically necessary.** None. Even if the law has no explicit exception for abortion to save the life of the mother, a doctor can still do it. This was never a question, even before *Roe* was decided. Abortion activists try to muddy the water and claim that banning elective abortions would somehow prevent a doctor from saving a woman\'s life. This has never been the case in the United States. Yes, there are some obscure examples from history in other countries where this may have happened, but not here. \ Original question: Are R v Wade so-called "trigger laws" outright bans on abortion, bans that don't include exemptions for things like rape, incest, or even to save the mother\'s life?
