Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} The Court doesn't issue "rulings" that states must follow. The Court simply decides whether a law, or the way the law is implemented, violates the Constitution. For example, the draft opinion from the court regarding *Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health* says that the law is Constitutional because the grounds upon which the case was appealed (a violation of the finding in *Roe v. Wade*) are unconstitutional. In doing this, the Court is stating that the finding in *Griswold v. Connecticut* (that a right to privacy exists in the Constitution) was incorrectly decided and is reversed, and that the finding which flowed from it in *Roe v. Wade* (that the right to privacy found in *Griswold* extended to abortion rights) is also reversed. Therefore, since none of these things are actually Constitutionally-guaranteed rights, the question of whether or not those things are *legal* falls back on the states to decide. States are actually free to do as they please when it comes to abortion. Some states will ban it, most states will apply some level of restriction to it, and some states will allow it completely. However, if the Supreme Court finds that a law is constitutional, any state "going against" that finding (and enforcing the unconstitutional law anyway) will find that the aggrieved party will file suit in district court alleging that the law is unconstitutional based on the Supreme Court's findings, and the law in that state will be ruled unconstitutional and will be unenforceable. At best, the state could re-write the law to achieve the same goal, while doing so in a Constitutional way. \ Original question: If enough states were to go against a particular ruling from the Supreme Court, could they possibly overrule it?
