Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** [Sadly, that\'s a political question, and not a military one. If you want to judge the question in a completely military way, you have to look at what the military is charged with doing, and make every budgetary decision based on what their actual job description is.\ \ The question goes deeper than just job description, and it gets down into details.\ \ ]{.rendered_qtext} - Is the military able to conduct wars on multiple fronts simultaneously? As military budgets grow and shrink depending on the prevailing political climate, this ability changes. In our current military configuration, the military cannot successfully conduct wars on multiple fronts; there simply is not funding to accomplish it. - How technologically advanced should are Armed Forces be? We had one of the most technologically advanced armies going in to Afghanistan, and it was the simple IED that cost us many soldiers\' lives. The technology to overcome the IED was developed through necessity during Afghanistan, but that was through necessity, and political posturing during the process cost several years in development, and cost many more lives as a result. - Eventually there will not be \"people\" on the front lines at all; it will all be robots and UAVs. These things cost money, and how quickly we start replacing troops with robots depends on the size of the budget. - Our military equipment is aging. We have B-52s in service that former Air Force aviators and their grown children have both flown during their respective careers. This isn\'t just because the venerable BUFF is a great bomber, but because we simply don\'t have the money to replace the entire B-52 fleet with B-1s. Our forces will need to modernize in order to keep up with the security needs of a growing world. \ So the question of \"how big should the budget be\" is one that is driven out of necessity, but dictated by politics. This is the nature of our country, and I suspect that it will always be this way.
