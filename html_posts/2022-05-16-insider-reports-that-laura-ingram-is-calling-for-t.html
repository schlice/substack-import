**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} The Marshal of the Supreme Court is already investigating. The Marshal, if he finds evidence of criminal wrongdoing, will turn his evidence over to Federal prosecutors. If the Marshal's office needs assistance, they can absolutely call in the FBI as they see fit. But calling on someone else to do the investigation which is already taking place is probably unnecessary. But I am noticing that plenty of people are on Quora answering this question and saying that there was no violation of law. That answer is, simply, wrong. Here is just a potential list of crimes which the leaker could be charged with: - Disclosure of confidential information (18 USC § 1905) is most certainly still on the table, as it doesn't require the documents to be classified to be "confidential." The unfinished work product of Supreme Court justices is most certainly not eligible for public release. - A law which came on the books as part of the Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002, Corruptly Influencing an Official Proceeding (18 USC § 1512(c)(2)) is on the table if the leaker intended to sway the Court's opinions using public pressure brought on by the leak. - And while it has only been tried once and the case never concluded, a determined prosecutor could make the case that Conspiracy to Defraud the US (18 USC § 371) also applies. In 1919, Ashton Embry, a clerk to Supreme Court Justice Joseph McKenna, sent an opinion to some Wall Street executives ahead of a judgment involving a railroad company. He was indicted for having violated § 371. The prosecution's theory was that, by releasing the opinion early, the clerk and his "co-conspirators deprived the Court of the right to announce its decisions at the customary time." At the very least, the Embry case puts to rest the argument that law clerks have never been prosecuted for leaking Court documents. There are others, but they are less likely to be able to stick. For example, Theft of Government Documents (18 USC § 1691) may not apply if the clerk didn't actually "steal" the documents because they already had legal access to them. So that one may not stick. However, this should at least tell you that when people say that "there is no criminal statute" that makes leaking draft court opinions "illegal," they are simply incorrect.
