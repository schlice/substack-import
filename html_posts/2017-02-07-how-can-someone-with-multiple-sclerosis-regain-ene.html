**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} MS fatigue, also known as MS Lassitude, is one of the most common symptoms of MS. It's estimated that 80% of MS patients have experienced MS fatigue at one time or another. Fatigue in people with MS can have many different causes. For example, if your MS includes bladder dysfunction and you have to get up in the middle of the night to use the bathroom, the lack of sleep can be a cause of fatigue. Depression can cause fatigue. Certain medications can induce fatigue as a side effect. Finally, there are other diseases such as thyroid issues and anemia which can also lead to fatigue. People who have MS and get stressed by heat also experience fatigue. Over-exposure to heat, or over-exertion, can also bring on MS fatigue. Because there are so many issues which could potentially be causing your fatigue, **if your fatigue is interfering with your life, you should first seek the advice of your doctor.** He should review your medications with you, see if anything needs to be modified, and if all other avenues are exhausted, perhaps prescribe a medication which has demonstrated some benefit to some people with MS fatigue. The National MS Society recommends the following interventions for someone who experiences fatigue: > - Occupational therapy to simplify tasks at work and home. > - Physical therapy to learn energy-saving ways of walking (with or > without assistive devices) and performing other daily tasks, and to > develop a regular exercise program. > - Sleep regulation, which might involve treating other MS symptoms > that interfere with sleep (e.g., spasticity, urinary problems) and > using sleep medications on a short-term basis. > - Psychological interventions, such as stress management, relaxation > training, membership in a support group, or psychotherapy. > - Heat management --- strategies to avoid overheating and to cool > down. > - Medications --- [[amantadine hydrochloride > (Symmetrel®)](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=e69066&url=http://www.nationalmssociety.org/Treating-MS/Medications/Amantadine){.external_link > rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" > onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" > qt-tooltip="nationalmssociety.org"}]{.qlink_container} and > [[modafinil > (Provigil®)](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=e437cd&url=http://www.nationalmssociety.org/Treating-MS/Medications/Provigil){.external_link > rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" > onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" > qt-tooltip="nationalmssociety.org"}]{.qlink_container} --- are the > most commonly prescribed. While neither is approved specifically by > the U.S Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for the treatment of > MS-related fatigue, each has demonstrated some benefit in clinical > trials. The most recent trial of modafinil, however, reported no > difference between modafinil and placebo in relieving > fatigue.[\[1\]](#fgcCO){#cite-fgcCO .citation_link} So my advice? First see your doctor, then think about some of these other interventions to assist with regaining some energy. Will you ever have enough energy to run again? It's hard to say. Fatigue, like MS, is different for everyone who experiences it. It's important to continue some exercise regimen while you can, so keep moving, even if it's just walking and not running. Good luck!
