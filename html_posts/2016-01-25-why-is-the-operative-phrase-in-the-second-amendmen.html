**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} The operative phrase is the one that recognizes the right. The prefatory phrase describes why the right needs to be recognized in the first place. We can identify the operative phrase when we see the wording which determines to whom the right belongs: \"the right of the people.\" The court in *DC v Heller* explained all of this. Reading the *Heller Law Brief* will give you very good insight into what the court was thinking. The court stated that the first part of the Second Amendment is called the **prefatory clause**. This is the clause which explains the purpose of the Amendment, the \"why\" this amendment is necessary. The prefatory clause is \"A well-regulated militia, being necessary to the security of a free state\". This explains why we have a Second Amendment, but does not limit or expand the scope of the second part. The second part of the Second Amendment is called the **operative clause:** \"the right of the people to keep and bear arms shall not be infringed.\" The *Brief* states it most succinctly: *\"Reading the Second Amendment as protecting only the right to \'keep and bear Arms\' in an organized militia therefore fits poorly with the operative clause's description of the holder of that right as \'the people\'\"*. The phrase \"the right of the people\" was used three times in the Bill of Rights, and each time it was used to connote an **individual** right, not a right gained by membership in a group. The rights are guaranteed to each individual. That said, the framers saw \"all of the people\" as \"the militia.\" They didn\'t see \"the militia\" and \"the citizens\" as two distinct groups. They saw \"the militia\" as every able-bodied citizen who could point a gun. Therefore, since any person could be called to defend their country at any time, the right to keep and bear arms should be included along with that. So today, since not every person is a member of the militia (in practice, if not in law), does that mean that the Second Amendment has no relevance? Not at all. The Second Amendment listed only one prefatory clause, but there were many that the framers were aware of at the time. One of them was the \"natural right\" of self-defense, as defined by Blackstone from England. The framers relied heavily on Blackstone\'s definitions of \"natural rights\" when writing the Constitution. They understood that we had a right to life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness, not because the government granted those rights, but because we were \"endowed by our Creator\" with those rights. The militia prefatory clause was listed because it was the most forefront on the minds of the colonists. In other words, they were saying \"Since it is understood that we won this time, but you can be called at any time to defend this new country, no one will ever abridge your rights to keep and bear arms.\" There were plenty of other things that were also understood, but none of those things needed to be listed. The necessity of a militia was one of many things that precipitated the recognition of that right. Additionally, one of the other big contentions during the development of the Constitution was the creation and authorization of a standing army. People were (rightly) fearful of a standing army, because of the way they were abused by the King in England. People really only wanted armies stood up during wartime. The opposite view held that attempting to stand up an army at the brink of war was not only foolish, but it forced you to begin your war with untrained people who lacked the proper technique and training to properly wage a war. The compromise was to allow a standing army, but to give the people the right to keep and bear arms to counter that standing army should the need arise. Additionally, they passed the *Posse Comitatus Act*, one of the very first laws passed by the first Congress. That act basically says that the military cannot enforce civilian law. The military has no jurisdiction over civilians. Together (the Second Amendment and the Posse Comitatus Act), these two provisions were enacted to protect the citizens from the army. Today, the weaponry of the army has far outstripped the weaponry available to average citizens, but the military lives by the law, and you\'d be hard pressed to find a Commander-in-Chief who would order the military to attack civilians because of it. While we don\'t need to repel our own army, there are plenty of other natural rights for which keeping arms are nearly a requirement. The Bill of Rights, therefore, is designed to enumerate the rights that you already have as a human that the government may not take away. Your right to self-defense and, by extension, your right to keep and bear arms, is a natural right which the government did not give you, so the government cannot take it away.
