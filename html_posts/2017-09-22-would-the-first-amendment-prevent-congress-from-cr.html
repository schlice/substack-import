**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} This is a great question, and very prescient, I might add. Because of the proliferation of "fake news," and the attention it has brought, some have questioned whether or not such a thing should be legal. So the question is: **Does the First Amendment protect fake news?** The short answer is yes. We know that it does, but the real question should be under what circumstances would fake news *not* be protected? There are already some classes of speech which can run you into legal trouble, and clearly the biggest one is libel. Defamation is closely related. But knowingly publishing something you know not to be true is not a crime, regardless of how untrue it really is. But what if it's so compelling, and social media picks up on it, that it spreads like wildfire and people start to believe it? > I call this the Loser's Argument: If that other side would stop being > so persuasive, people would agree with me. Typically, people deploying > the Loser's Argument are losing, and they know it. Arguers are looking > for some external leverage to control the debate. Juries are known as > fact-finders, not because they are clairvoyant truth machines, but > because eventually facts are needed to move cases forward. Searching > for some source of authority, in the case of prosecutions for fake > news appealing to the courts and eventually the police, doubles down > on the Loser's position.[\[1\]](#qjgIL){#cite-qjgIL .citation_link} So what then do we do? How do you counter free speech? Do you do so by *restricting* that speech? To that, I can say most definitely **no**. **You counter incorrect but free speech with more, correct, free speech.** You win hearts and minds in the arena of ideas. You have to learn to present better, more cogent arguments, and counter ideology with fact. Don't try to *silence* your opponent. Instead, *debunk* your opponent. There are some other ways around fake news distribution, and that is with better editorial oversight. Facebook is building and refining tools to do this automatically. Google is "demonetizing" content that does not meet its "quality standards." Editorial discretion is actually also protected free speech, so this fits quite nicely with the tenet of using free speech to counter fake news. **TL;DR: Criminalizing fake news is not the way to go. Countering fake free speech with louder, correct free speech is the correct way to go.** Footnotes [\[1\]](#cite-qjgIL){.citation_index} [[Should Publishing Fake News Be a Crime?](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=f0894f&url=http://www.ozy.com/pov/should-publishing-fake-news-be-a-crime/80180){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="ozy.com"}]{.qlink_container}
