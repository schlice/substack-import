--- title: '"Is it acceptable for public schools to display religious symbols, such as crosses, on their property if they do not require students to participate in religious activities while on school grounds?"' date: '2025-01-05' tags: - law layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} *(US Answer Only)* While these types of answers are highly dependent on state law, in general, the answer in the United States is **no**. Public schools in the US can only display religious symbols when they are used as part of a **temporary** educational lesson, discussing the history or cultural significance of religion, and not as a permanent display that could be seen as endorsing a specific faith; essentially, they can be used as teaching aids to illustrate religious concepts within a broader curriculum, but not as standalone symbols of religious devotion. Religious symbols can be displayed when they are used to **teach** about religion in a historical or cultural context, like discussing the symbolism of a cross in a world religions class. The display should be **temporary** and only used during the relevant lesson, not as a permanent fixture in the school. Schools must also present religious symbols in a **neutral** way, avoiding any appearance of promoting one religion over another. **Examples of acceptable use:** - Showing a menorah during a lesson on Hanukkah - Displaying a picture of the Star of David while discussing Judaism - Including a nativity scene as part of a broader holiday display with diverse cultural elements **Examples of unacceptable use:** - Permanently displaying religious symbols like the Ten Commandments on a school wall - Using religious symbols as a form of decoration without educational context - Forcing students to participate in religious activities or displays
