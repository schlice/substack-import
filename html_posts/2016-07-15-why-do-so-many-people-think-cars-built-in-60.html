**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} People make a ***value judgment*** and compare cars to any other product commercially available on the market, like toasters, blenders, and refrigerators. They say, "these products are made cheaply, with components which wear out faster, and are not designed to last." People then incorrectly assign these product characteristics to cars. This is incorrect for many reasons, but primarily because auto manufacturers are under some of the strictest government guidelines controlling their products, far stricter than any other product manufacturer. For example, you may have noticed a strong trend in the auto industry towards smaller engines and the addition of turbochargers to make up for the difference in BHP (brake horsepower). This was not caused by any trend in buying habits (as the rest of the manufactured goods markets are), it was caused by government clean-air regulations. Fuel efficiency can only be increased so far, as far as physics allows, so in order to increase fuel efficiency further than physics (which is normally impossible), the only thing which can be done is to reduce the size of the engine. Smaller engines burn less fuel and therefore, by default, run more "efficiently" in terms of miles per gallon. But that reduction in engine size reduces the power output of the engine, which is why turbochargers were added.
