**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} A2A If she was just diagnosed, she will be beginning her journey through the 5 stages of grief: denial, anger, depression, bargaining, and acceptance. Remember, everyone grieves in their own way. Not everyone experiences all five stages, and not everyone experiences them in the same order. Grieving is a personal process. There is no time limit. There is no "right" way to grieve. **Denial** **** is only temporary. We do it to protect ourselves from that first big wave of pain caused from the realization that your life will never be the same, your life will never work out the way you originally thought or dreamed it would, and that you have an incurable disease that will cause you grief and become a new and totally unwanted facet of your daily life, but could also land you in a wheelchair (or worse) when you're older. Denial may only last a few days, and along with bargaining, may be one of the quickest phases to go through. **Anger** is what happens when the denial wears off. The reality of the situation hits us, and we're wholly unprepared to deal with it. We deflect the pain away from ourselves, and it is manifested as anger. The anger is caused by the helplessness and the vulnerability we feel when we learn we have MS. Be aware, *you* may be the target of that anger. Please, please know it is not personal. She is not attacking you. She is releasing her pain because she is protecting herself from it. When we find that medications may slow down the disease progression, but nothing will ever cure us of the disease, **depression** sets in. Almost everyone with MS has experienced some level of depression throughout the course of the disease. Some research claimed that injectable therapy carried a risk of depression, but I truly believe that the association is anecdotal. I believe that in the course of progressing through the grieving process, we come to the inevitable realization that *we will have MS until the day we die, and potentially, MS could make that happen sooner.* There is simply no way that depression would not follow that realization. Sometimes, depression is caused by fear of the unknown, or fear of the practical complications of the disease. "Will I be able to walk my daughter down the aisle?" "Will I be able to throw a baseball with my son ever again?" (That one was mine, since my first symptom was optic neuritis, and suffice it to say that for six months anything moving toward or away from me at any reasonable speed gave me double vision. I couldn't catch a baseball). I actually started letters to my children titled "Read this in case of my untimely death" but I couldn't get through them because the grief was so strong, and the tears fell so freely. There is a second type of depression that more often occurs with terminal diseases (of which MS is not), but still occurs with MS with some regularity, and that is the realization of our mortality and the preparation for death. We silently and subconsciously push others away from us to insulate them from our pain. We protect them because we love them and we don't want to see them hurt. My wife would say things like, "Are you deliberately trying to push me away?" I wasn't *deliberately* doing it, I was *subconsciously* doing it. Don't feel bad if you start to feel that your partner is pushing you away. She doesn't know she's doing it. Perhaps she just needs a hug. Perhaps she just needs to know that despite her pain, you're not going away. **Bargaining** follows the helplessness and vulnerability, as we seek a way to control the uncontrollable. Lots of "if only" statements are made. "If only we hadn't gone to Bermuda and gotten those horrible sunburns, I wouldn't have skin cancer now." "If only I would have found out sooner, I could have gotten ahead of this." In the case of MS, "If only my vitamin D levels were straightened out before I turned 15, I may have prevented this" (fwiw, the prophylactic effects of vitamin D in early childhood are still being investigated). Deals with God often happen during this phase, but are just another line of defense against the pain. While bargaining or making deals, what we are actually doing is *experimenting* with strategies to cope with the disease. "Maybe I can exercise more, to make myself healthier and potentially stave off some of the muscle effects caused by the disease." This bargaining/experimenting phase is what starts lifting us out of depression. Be supportive of your partner during this phase. She may have some crazy-sounding ideas, but she's toying with the ways to cope with the disease, and starting into the next stage: **Acceptance.** This is the point we reach when we realize that we *have* MS, we *are not* MS. We are not our disease. With proper disease management, we can live as full and active a life as someone without the disease. With modern medicine, mortality rates among MS patients nearly match those of people without the disease. And no one can ever predict the sudden turns the disease may take, but they are no more or less sudden and unexpected in their onset than being involved in a car accident. Worrying about them will not make the disease progress more slowly, so worry is a wasted emotion. For me, one of my great tools of acceptance is Quora (and other Q&A and medical community sites). I get to help others who are recently diagnosed, or who are struggling to come to grips with a disease that is eminently beyond understanding or prediction. Being a patient advocate means I know what it's like to be a patient, because I am one (I'm not only the Hair Club president, but I'm also a client) (If you don't know that one, look it up. I may have just shown my age). Make sure you give your partner space to grieve, but not so much space that she feels like you're pulling away from her because she has a disease. Be there for her, but don't smother her. She needs reassurance that you're not going away, but she needs time alone to work through her grief. *Keep being her best friend*. Don't let MS change that. Ever.
