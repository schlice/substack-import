**Content:** [Anywhere from 50-75% of MS patients will get lesions on their spinal cord at one time during the cours of their disease. In about 20% of already-diagnosed MS patients, lesions occur on the spinal cord only, and not on the brain. This is what is commonly referred to as \"spinal MS.\" It simply means that lesions occur, either alone or along with others, on the spinal cord.\ \ The lesions on the spinal cord are more likely than any others to cause some sort of acute physical symptom, according to [[a report](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=6a355c&url=http://www.urmc.rochester.edu/smd/Rad/neurocases/Neurocase108.htm){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="rochester.edu"}]{.qlink_container} from the University of Rochester Medical Center. Spinal cord lesions [[may be more difficult to see](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=720fcd&url=http://www.healthcentral.com/multiple-sclerosis/c/6639/25072/cord-ms){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="healthcentral.com"}]{.qlink_container} on MRI scans than brain lesions, so unless there are physical indications of spinal involvement through neurological exam, spinal cord lesions are easy to miss.]{.rendered_qtext} ***Disclaimer:*** ***This answer is not professional medical advice****, nor is it a substitute for professional medical advice.
