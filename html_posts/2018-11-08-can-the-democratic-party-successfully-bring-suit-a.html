**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **Can the Democratic party successfully bring suit against the Republican party for gerrymandering thus denying Democrats their right of association?** Either party can sue for gerrymandering. The problem: *it's very difficult to tell actual gerrymandering (illegal) from genuine redistricting (legal).* Essentially, it's really easy to say what districts *should not* look like. Some of the worst gerrymandering is easy to spot. > \[I\]t's much more difficult to say what districts *should* look like, > because reformers can disagree on what priorities should govern our > political cartography. Should districts be drawn to be more compact? > More conducive to competitive elections? More inclusive of > underrepresented racial groups? Should they yield a mix of Democratic > and Republican representatives that better matches the political > makeup of a state? Could they even be drawn at random? These concepts > can be difficult to define and often stand in tension with one > another.[\[1\]](#Fbapm){#cite-Fbapm .citation_link} Some states attempt to do this by taking redistricting out of the hands of politicians. Colorado *literally did this Tuesday.* We voted for amendments to our state Constitution which takes redistricting out of the hands of politicians and gives it to an independent commission which is made up of 12 members: four from the state's largest political party, four from the state's second largest political party, and four independent members. They will draw boundaries for both Congressional districts and State Congress districts (two separate Amendments were passed for that: Amendment Y and Amendment Z). According to our new Constitutional amendment, "\[t\]he redistricting commission should set district lines by ensuring Constitutionally guaranteed voting rights, including the protection of minority group voting, as well as fair and effective representation of constituents using politically neutral criteria." But lawsuits about gerrymandering rarely yield results, since it is very difficult to spot actual gerrymandering except in the worst cases. Gerrymandering is difficult to separate from legal and necessary redistricting without resorting to simply removing the political motivation altogether.
