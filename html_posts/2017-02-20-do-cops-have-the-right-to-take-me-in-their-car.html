--- title: "Do cops have the right to take me in their car?" date: "2017-02-20" tags: ["law-enforcement", "law"] layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} Do they have the right to take you to the police station? As part of fulfilling their duties, indeed they do. They have the right to ask you to do anything they like. For example, they can ask you to do the chicken dance on the sidewalk. Or they could ask you to take your hat off. There are a million things they could ask you to do. But that's not the question you should be asking. The question you ***should*** be asking is whether or not you should do what they ask. The answer to *that* question is a little more complex. You must determine whether or not you **must** go with them, or if it's just a suggestion. That's harder for someone your age to do, especially without a parent or guardian's assistance. Let's say you ask them, "Are you detaining me?" or "Are you arresting me?" If they say that you are not under arrest, you then have to determine whether or not you feel comfortable going with them, or whether or not you should just go home and talk to your parents about it. The bottom line: **if you are not being detained, you do not have to stay! You can say "no thank you" and leave. You could also run, although I would suggest walking.** If it's legal for you to leave, then the speed at which you leave is irrelevant. If they are not detaining you, you can ask for a card from the officer and you can bring it home to your parents. They can then call the police station and find out why the police thought it would be a good idea to take a minor for a ride who was not being detained and who was without parental supervision.
