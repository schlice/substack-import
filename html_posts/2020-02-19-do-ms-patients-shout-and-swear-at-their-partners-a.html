**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **Do MS patients shout and swear at their partners and children every day?** I'm going to take a different tack than my colleagues here and suggest (possibly incorrectly) that this is happening to you, questioner. And your question here is to ask if this is normal behavior. So let's start with the facts. Many MS patients will indeed experience cognitive issues. In fact, about 65% of people with MS will experience some [[changes in cognitive functioning](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=3512f2&url=http://www.nationalmssociety.org/Symptoms-Diagnosis/MS-Symptoms/Cognitive-Changes){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="nationalmssociety.org"}]{.qlink_container}, most commonly related to speed of information processing, memory and attention. In MS, we talk about **cognitive reserve**. Cognitive reserve is the way which our brains can protect against cognitive decline caused by anything, from MS to aging. > Research has shown that a lifetime of intellectual enrichment from > education and stimulating work and leisure activities helps to lessen > or slow the impact of disease- or aging-related changes in the brain. > In other words, people with cognitive reserve have more protection > from cognitive decline. Cognitive reserve is like savings in the > bank.[\[1\]](#hwEDP){#cite-hwEDP .citation_link} The good news is that anyone can take steps today to improve cognitive reserve, and research shows that it does add cognitive reserve, at any age. Some activities you can participate in in order to improve cognitive reserve: - Reading - Creative writing - Journaling - Handiwork - Board or card games - Drawing or painting - Cross word or jigsaw puzzles - Learning a new language - Learning to play an instrument In addition, engaging with others -- in a support group, book club, fellowship meetings at your church or synagogue, or through [[advocacy activities](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=9a1503&url=http://www.nationalmssociety.org/Get-Involved/Advocate-for-Change){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="nationalmssociety.org"}]{.qlink_container} -- is another way to stay cognitively active and enjoy the companionship of others at the same time.[\[2\]](#skJPb){#cite-skJPb .citation_link} The bottom line here: if signs exist of cognitive decline, talking to your neurologist is the first step in getting connected to the resources and extended care team which can improve overall brain health and maybe head off some of these symptoms.
