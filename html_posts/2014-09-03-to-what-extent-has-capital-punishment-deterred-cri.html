**Content:** [No, my belief about the death penalty remains unchanged: we should not have it.\ \ The risk of killing someone who might potentially be innocent is too great of a risk to take, in my opinion. And the death penalty, in its current incantation, does not deter anyone from committing crimes they were going to commit anyway (Dude, don\'t do that, you\'ll get the chair!). There are decades of legal challenges that have to be waded through before someone can be put to death, and the possibility of being released on a technicality is great, because the burden on the state is so high.\ \ Life without the possibility of parole is a better punishment. Remove the individual from society. If, in 20 years, some technology comes along that can prove someone\'s innocence (like the story you quoted), all the better.\ \ I understand this will increase the prison population, I get that. It\'s a small price to pay in my opinion.\ \ EDIT: If we can remove one of the largest sources of the prison population from prison (drug offenders), we can afford to keep \"real\" criminals in prison longer. See [[What do police officers think about legalizing drugs? Would it make their jobs harder or easier?](https://www.quora.com/What-do-police-officers-think-about-legalizing-drugs-Would-it-make-their-jobs-harder-or-easier)]{.qlink_container} for a great discussion on why it is so futile to keep these people in prison.\ \ The major reason we have drug addicts in prison is because the medical community doesn\'t treat drug addicts as patients. It doesn\'t yet fully recognize that addiction is a disease and that addicts are patients. It still contends that addiction is a choice and that addicts are bad actors, leaving the problem of addicts to the legal system instead of the medical system. Once that hurdle is overcome (we start treating our addicts as patients rather than inmates), we can alleviate a major burden on the criminal justice system, and keep the real criminals in prison.]{.rendered_qtext}
