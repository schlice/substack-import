**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **Is illegally obtained evidence (i.e. video of a murder) usable against a serial killer? Will he/she be free if there\'s no other evidence?** So, two questions here. Let's break them down: **Is illegally obtained evidence (i.e. video of a murder) usable against a serial killer?** It depends on who obtains it, and whether or not they will testify as to how they obtained it. Clearly, if an agent of the government (i.e. a police officer) illegally obtains evidence, that evidence is inadmissible. If a civilian illegally obtains evidence at the behest of a government agent, it is still inadmissible. But if a civilian illegally obtains evidence all on his own, and without government direction, the evidence is (in most cases) legal and admissible. Of course, this will usually require the person obtaining the evidence getting up on the stand and testifying as to how they came into possession of said evidence, which would clearly implicate them in criminal behavior, opening up a Pandora's Box of issues. But prosecutors have done this in the past, especially in high-profile cases; a serial killer would definitely count. **Will he/she be free if there\'s no other evidence?** Not necessarily. Circumstantial evidence can be so strong that even without illegally obtained evidence, a person can be guilty *beyond a reasonable doubt.* Remember that the standard to which the jury is held is the "reasonable doubt" standard. If the prosecutor can make the case using the circumstantial evidence, the defendant can still be found guilty.
