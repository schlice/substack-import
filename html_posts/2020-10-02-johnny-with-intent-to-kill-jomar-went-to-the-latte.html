**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **Johnny with intent to kill Jomar went to the latter\'s house and upon seeing him on the bed, shot him immediately. Later, Johnny realized that Jomar was already dead for a couple of hours. What is the crime committed? Why is it punishable by law?** Cool, law school questions! Anyway, you can't murder someone who is already dead. The elements of the crime of murder are not met, namely the very first one: the unlawful taking of a life. Even if you fully intended to murder someone, you cannot commit the crime if they are already dead. You could absolutely be charged with attempted murder. In attempted murder, a person must take a direct step towards the killing and must have the specific intent to kill that person. The crime of attempted murder has an *action* and an *intention*. The action would be to going to Jomar's house and entering it. The intention is to kill Jomar. In the vast majority of US states, Johnny could be charged with attempted murder. The *defense* to the attempted murder charge would be one we call *impossibility*. Johnny doesn't deny going to Jomar's house with the specific intent of killing him. However, he could not commit the murder because Jomar was already dead. You can't have an attempted murder if you can't actually commit the crime of murder. Some states do not allow the impossibility defense, but if Johnny's state does, he could make that claim. He could also be charged with unlawful discharge of a firearm, and mutilating a corpse.
