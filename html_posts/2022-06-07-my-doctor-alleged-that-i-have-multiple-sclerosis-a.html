**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} If your doctor has offered a preliminary diagnosis (not an allegation), it will need to be confirmed through testing. The main piece of diagnostic equipment we use to diagnose MS is the MRI. A positive MRI along with symptoms is enough to meet the [[McDonald Criteria](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=9cdf98&url=https://mstrust.org.uk/a-z/mcdonald-criteria){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="mstrust.org.uk"}]{.qlink_container} to diagnose you with MS. The McDonald Criteria is important. This is the standard your neurologist will use to guide you through the diagnosis of MS, and to order the appropriate tests. The criteria was published in 2001 by Professor Ian McDonald, and it has been extensively used and revised several times. It was most recently updated in 2017 to reflect our growing confidence in the MRI as a descriptive and predictive diagnostic tool. Your doctor will use other testing to add to the body of evidence: - Potentially, a lumbar puncture (spinal tap) can be ordered to look for fragments of the damaged nerve material. The LP is not necessary in all situations, but if other tests come back inconclusive, the LP can be ordered. - Blood tests will be ordered to rule out the other possible causes for little white spots on an MRI (generally called the "little white spot panel") - Evoked Potential tests, particularly Visual Evoked Potential, in order to assess the damage to the optic nerve which can be caused by MS (if vision issues are one of your symptoms, like mine were) - Plus, of course, a full neurological exam and a patient history will also be taken One more note about your last question: > Do I have a say on the type of testing I should be performed? You always have a say on what happens to you. Your neurologist should be telling you the types of tests you need to diagnose MS, and why you need them. Where options are available, you should be given those options. But healthcare is a *cooperation* between you and your doctor. It is not "one-sided." But trust your neurologist to order you the correct tests to properly diagnose what's going on with you. Some tests (like the MRI) will be crucial for your diagnosis. Other tests are not absolutely necessary, but may be needed to aid your diagnosis. If you are ever unsure about a test, ask. Ask your neurologist why you are being ordered to take that test, and what they hope to see in the results of that test. MS is a difficult, life-changing diagnosis. As such, you should always feel free to seek second opinions if you are unsure about your diagnosis. We're all in this together. If it's MS, don't worry, it's not the end of the world. Your world will change, but it will not end. Plus, there are whole communities of MS patients and caregivers all over the world who can help you. Even right here on Quora, the [[Multiple Sclerosis Forum](https://multiplesclerosisforum.quora.com/)]{.qlink_container} exists for exactly this reason. Good luck to you.
