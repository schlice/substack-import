**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} It's the same reason why people are claiming that the Justices "lied" in their confirmation hearings. **People truly believed that** ***Roe v. Wade*** **was "settled law" and could never be overturned.** Indeed, they believed that when the justices talked about "precedent" and *stare decisis*, this was an indication that if a case ever came before the Court questioning the very foundation of *Roe*, the Justices would vote to pass on the case, because somehow *stare decisis* makes the case untouchable. The logic doesn't pass muster. And you only need to look at *Plessey v. Ferguson* to understand why. *Plessey* was the case in the late 19th century which decided that racial segregation was Constitutional. It was the "separate but equal" decision. So long as the facilities offered were equal, racial segregation was Constitutional. That decision was "precedent" and was treated as *stare decisis* for over 60 years before the Court, in *Brown v. Board* determined that racial segregation in schools was, in fact, unconstitutional. That was the beginning of the end for the *Plessey* finding, and over the next few years, more decisions were made which functionally overturned the decision. But why did they keep it so long? People may have understood that the decision was not Constitutionally accurate, and that *Plessey* actually did violate the equal protection clause of the 14th Amendment, but ultimately, it came down to the fact that **people liked the outcome, and so the decision wasn't reviewed.** This case is absolutely no different. The Justices are looking to fix an error in a Constitutional decision. Legal scholars have long agreed that the *foundation* of the *Roe* decision was flawed, Constitutionally speaking. But, exactly as in the case of *Plessey*, people liked the outcome, and so the decision wasn't reviewed. However, the draft opinion in *Dobbs* says that the basis on which they wrote the *Roe* decision, as well as the decision on *Planned Parenthood v. Casey*, was without merit (the right to privacy in the Constitution). Therefore, because there is no right to privacy in the Constitution, there is no right to an abortion in the Constitution, either. \ Now that all of this is established, why do people believe that interracial marriage and same-sex marriage are next? Well, their limited logic goes, if a *sacrosanct ruling* like *Roe* can be overturned, can't the other decisions as well? The short answer is yes, they can. However, it is *extremely unlikely* that they ever would. But why not? What makes those cases different from this one? The first reason is because **the basis upon which those cases were decided is Constitutionally sound.** Both of those cases found that the right is protected under the Equal Protection clause of the 14th Amendment. Knowing this, it would be exceedingly challenging to claim that somehow, the right to interracial marriage or the right to same-sex marriage are somehow unconstitutional. The other reason is that there is difference between interracial marriage/same-sex marriage and abortion, and it is the idea of ***victimless rights***. Professor Alan Dershowitz coined this term a few weeks ago. In the case of interracial marriage or same-sex marriage, does the exercise of the right impact the exercise of anyone else's rights? The answer is no. Therefore, these are ***victimless rights.*** In contrast, does the exercise of the right to abortion impact the exercise of anyone else's rights? The answer is *potentially*, because at least potentially, the exercise of the right to an abortion impacts the right of the fetus to life. The *Roe* court *passed* on the opportunity to declare whether or not the fetus has such a right, because, at the time, the medical and philosophical communities could not answer the question as to whether or not the fetus is alive, and thus possessing the right to life, and thus to not have that life taken without due process. It appears that this Court is at least willing to state that the fetus *may* have a right to life, and therefore the Court should proceed assuming that it *does*. If that is the case, therefore, the right to an abortion is **not** a victimless right, and the right to have an abortion must be weighed against the right of the fetus to life. \ TL;DR: People fail to realize that the basis upon which interracial marriage/same-sex marriage are based is Constitutionally sound, unlike the basis upon which *Roe* was built. \ Original question: Why do people think that if the Supreme Court overturns abortion that they would go after same sex marriage next, despite the lack of any substantial movement to do so in the last seven years?
