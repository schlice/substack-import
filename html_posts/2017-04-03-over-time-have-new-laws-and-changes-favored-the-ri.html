--- title: '"Over time have new laws and changes favored the rich and powerful?"' date: '2017-04-03' tags: - veteran-benefits - law layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} A2A I think you need to qualify the question somewhat. *Criminal laws* do not benefit or hurt anyone within the text of the law, in the context of the law's applicability. Laws are required by the 14th Amendment to apply equally to all people, rich or poor. So criminal laws themselves do not apply unfairly to any one group in particular. However, it is well known that people who are wealthy have an *easier time* working with the law. Lawyers are expensive; good lawyers even more so. The best, most expensive lawyers are better at protecting their clients; judgments for rich people tend to be lighter because their lawyers are better at negotiating settlements. Rich people also have it easier when it comes to things like restitution. A \$10,000 judgement against a rich person may be a drop in the bucket, whereas the same judgement against a middle-class working family may be the death knell in their finances, forcing them into bankruptcy. Despite the universal applicability of criminal law, ***tax*** ***law*** most definitely favors the wealthy. The richer you are, the more tax avoidance strategies you are able to implement; while the same tax avoidance strategies exist for everyone, you generally have to be rich to take advantage of them. As an example, you can deduct medical costs from your taxes, but only after your medical costs exceed 10% of your Adjusted Gross Income (AGI). If your healthcare insurance is paying the majority of your healthcare expenses, 10% of your AGI out of your pocket for healthcare is a massive chunk of change. In some years, and with some big out-of-pocket expenses, you might be able to deduct some of it, but for most people, for most normal years, you'll never spend that much out of your pocket. Unless you're rich, and you pay most of your healthcare expenses without insurance. Then, you could easily spend 10% of your AGI on healthcare, meaning you'd get a substantial tax break. Another example is donations. The more money you have, the more money you can donate, and every donation shaves off significant dollars from your taxable income. Tax law, therefore, disproportionately benefits the rich.
