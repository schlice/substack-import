**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **When Bloomberg says "What I have asked for... are background checks, we shouldn't be selling guns to minors, to people with psychiatric problems, or people with criminal records", do they not realize these are laws already ?** For the most part, yes. The problem is they equivocate on the details. Let's review: **Minors**: Um, we don't sell guns to minors. My guess is the equivocation here is what you consider a *minor*. You're no longer a minor at 18 in the United States. However, we do restrict handgun sales to 21 or older. Perhaps they want to universally impose a 21 year old age limit for all gun sales. Some states say this is a Second Amendment violation, and other states are already doing this. These cases will soon be coming to the SCOTUS, with more and more cities and states restricting all firearms sales to those 21 and older. **Background checks**: You do know that there are many places you can buy guns without a background check, right? It's called *private sales*. Now, obviously this is legal and it is how the system was designed, but they don't like it. They feel like it's an *end run* around the background check system. When they say they want "universal background checks," what they are really asking for is the elimination of the private sale exception. However, to do that means to expand the NICS system and allow access to anyone. But that opens up a whole new can of worms, not the least of which is how to pay for such a massive NICS upgrade. But typically of those who espouse this particular gun control measure, they're relatively unconcerned with where the money to upgrade NICS will come from. The other significant NICS-for-all speedbump is the privacy issue, which gets even worse when we get to the next point: **People with psychiatric problems:** I think we can all agree that we don't want the criminally insane to be able to purchase firearms, and currently they can't. However, they contend, most of the mass shooters did not violate any current laws and, they argue, probably had mental issues. Their issue is that we're not doing *enough*. But that, of course, poses immense new problems, the elephant in the room being *what constitutes a psychiatric problem*? I was diagnosed with depression about 10 years back. I'm better now. Do I lose my gun rights? What about veterans who require financial assistance? [[They've already been denied gun rights](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=333dc6&url=https://thehill.com/blogs/pundits-blog/defense/295484-va-is-restricting-veterans-gun-rights-without-due-process){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="thehill.com"}]{.qlink_container}! That's right, if you requested fiduciary assistance from the VA, they are similarly claiming that you are not mentally capable of owning a gun. The arbitrary and capricious application of labels is a major concern, and without a major overhaul of the mental health evaluation system in this country, will be amazingly difficult to implement fairly. Also of note is the privacy concerns mentioned in the first bullet. Health information is protected by HIPAA. You can't publish that kind of thing in a database which is open to the public, but we want people to be able to use NICS before they engage in a private sale. This will require a complete overhaul of NICS, from the ground up. Illinois' FOID card system is not a bad way to go. You are investigated once, and if you are allowed to own a firearm, you receive a card, with a number. Any time a potential firearms sale is to occur, the card is validated. A simple website where you enter the FOID card number and you receive a YES or NO response based on your NICS eligibility is all you need to confirm the purchaser is still valid. That transaction against that FOID card would be logged, and you'd get a confirmation number. Keeping the confirmation number along with the bill of sale would be all that is necessary for record keeping (i.e. I saw the FOID card, I confirmed it was valid, so I made the sale). Now you have universal background checks, and they are divorced from the actual transaction of the firearm. It will cost money to revamp the system and issue everyone eligible a FOID card, but it can be done. Perhaps we could leverage the Social Security Administration or at a local level, the DMV, to assist with getting those cards issued. **People with criminal records:** Today, violent felons lose their right to firearms, but I guess that doesn't go far enough either. The question, of course, is what exactly does this mean? Is it only violent felonies? We have that today. Is it domestic violence? We also have that today. Is it shoplifting? Is it jaywalking? What crimes, exactly, should we restrict Constitutional rights once you commit them?
