**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **Aside from the impossible "coward" excuse - what possible reason would an armed cop have for standing around watching Cruz shoot up the school for 6 minutes?** The simple fact is we don't know. The way most police departments around the country train in "code red" scenarios (active shooter) is that a solo person waits for two to three other officers before entering a live-fire scenario. A solo officer never enters a live fire situation on his own. Two people is the bare minimum to enter an active shooter environment, and that's only during extreme emergencies, such as when there is literally no other backup; ideally 3--4 officers in a military diamond formation should enter. Yes, we all want to believe that a solo officer could have run in there and killed the shooter armed with a rifle, just using his sidearm. But the odds are terribly stacked against him. He is way outgunned in such a scenario. Two things are certain: 1. There is nothing in the law that requires that a police officer go in solo and risk dying to try to stop an active shooter. We all may want to "Monday-morning quarterback" the situation and say we would have run in to confront the shooter (I know I feel that way), but until you're actually facing live gunfire ***coming at you***, you don't know exactly how you'll react. But the fact of the matter is that a police officer's job description does not say "Gets killed when necessary." And many years of case law backs this up: police have a general duty to protect the public at large; they have no specific duty to protect any one person or any group of people. So legally, he did not abdicate his duty or anything like that. He contained the situation, which is legally permissible. 2. We also don't know the code red rules in that department. Was the deputy required to hole up and wait for backup? Was he directed to not enter the school until backup arrived? In other words, did he follow his department's rules? We don't know any of that either. But we do know that a dead officer won't do anyone any good. He certainly contained the situation even if he didn't go in, and that meets the legal standard of "doing his job." The department hasn't come out and said yet whether or not the officer was following orders, or following department operating procedure for code red scenarios. Interesting why they haven't said anything. Probably has something to do with the fact that the answer may be tough for people to hear.
