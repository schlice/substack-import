**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **Is intentionally giving someone the Coronavirus attempted murder?** While cases are currently rare concerning COVID-19, we can use intentional spread of existing viruses and infections in order to determine what might happen to you if you know you are infected and you intentionally put others at risk. For example, here in Florida, if you have been diagnosed with an STI (sexually transmitted infection) and you knowingly engage in sexual relations with someone else without informing them of your infection, you could be convicted of a crime (criminal transmission of an STI) if that person becomes infected. It's not a stretch to see that the same legal principles could be applied to someone who knows they are COVID-19 positive and attempts to spread (or succeeds in spreading) the infection with intent to infect others. In Texas, an 18 year old woman was arrested and charged with making a terroristic threat when she claimed on Snapchat that she was COVID-19 positive and was spreading it on purpose. This just occurred the week of April 6 of this year. So, to the question, is it possible that intentionally spreading COVID-19 could be considered attempted murder? **If someone knowingly and purposefully infected someone else with the knowledge and intention to cause death, then charges of attempted murder are not outside the realm of possibility.** In most cases under most state's laws, COVID-19 would likely fall into the same category as STIs other than HIV, since it is not a lifelong illness. Experts suspect that if criminal charges were to be imposed for knowingly, purposefully or recklessly exposing people to coronavirus, those charges would likely be categorized as a first-degree misdemeanor.[\[1\]](#UOEwp){#cite-UOEwp .citation_link} Here in Florida, conviction of a first-degree misdemeanor brings punishments of up to one year in jail, and fines up to \$1,000.
