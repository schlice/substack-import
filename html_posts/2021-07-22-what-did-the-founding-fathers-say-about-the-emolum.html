**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} Since I'm assuming you are not talking about what they "said" in the Constitution, let's look at everything that is *not* the Constitution, such as the Federalist Papers, the Articles of Confederation, state constitutions, and usage in the early republic. I would refer you to [[this paper](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=0140f0&url=https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id%3D3487898){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="ssrn.com"}]{.qlink_container} which is pretty comprehensive on the topic. In a nutshell, when the framers talked about "emoluments," they were looking specifically at the salary and benefits derived from holding a government office. **The term was specifically not applied to profits related to non-government business or investments.** It would be challenging to think that non-government business or investments should be included in that definition, because that would mean that any president who ever invested in any company which did business with the government would be in violation of the clause. Additionally, the emoluments clause doesn't just apply to the president, but to every member of Congress, and indeed, every Federal "office of profit or trust." That means that every one of them who invests or does business with any company which also does business with a foreign government would be in violation of the clause. \ Original question: What did the Founding Fathers say about the Emoluments Clause?
