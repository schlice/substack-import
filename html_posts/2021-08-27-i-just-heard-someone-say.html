**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} In just the same way that you shouldn't take medical advice from politicians, don't take legal advice from people who are not your lawyer. Don't take legal advice from me, either. But I've got one or two years experience under my belt; I can keep you out of jail better than what you've apparently been told about a repo. Maybe you forgot that you don't own the car yet. It doesn't belong to you. It belongs to the lender or leasing agency (depending on whether you took out a loan to buy or you are leasing), and you are working on buying it, but you haven't made all the payments yet. You don't hold the title to the car, the lender or the leasing agency does. About the only part they got right is the part in which they can't tow it with a person inside of it. However, if you jump in your car and refuse to get out, the tow truck driver will call the police. The police officer will order you out of the car, at which point you have one of two options: you get out of the car on your own and maybe you get to leave on your own, or you are dragged out of the car in which case you're going to jail. In many states, it is a *crime* to get in a car which is legally in the possession of a tow operator (once the tow hook is on the car, it is legally in the possession of the tow company), and you can go to jail for that act alone, let alone refusing to follow the lawful orders of a police officer. Repossession is the natural consequence of a contract you signed. If you fail to make the payments on your car, it gets repossessed. Your complaint is not with the tow truck driver; your complaint is with the lender or leasing agency. And if the repo driver has your car on his list, he's bringing your car in, plain and simple. Neither the repo driver nor the police officer really care that you have problem X that is preventing you from paying, or that the leasing agency is screwing you personally over, or anything else you choose to tell them.
