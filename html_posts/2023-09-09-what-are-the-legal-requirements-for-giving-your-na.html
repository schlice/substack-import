**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} Florida is considered a "stop and ID" state. This means that if you are stopped by law enforcement on suspicion of a crime or traffic violation, you are expected to identify yourself. This also means that if the officer does not have reasonable suspicion that you are involved in a crime or a traffic violation, you do not have to identify yourself, but that is the only time you are not obligated to positively identify yourself. The "stop and ID" law means you do have to identify yourself correctly (about this, you cannot lie; lying about your identity would be a separate offense). Usually your name and address are sufficient for an officer to identify you. The officer can ask you to *confirm* your identity. This means you must present a valid form of identification if you have one on you. If you are not driving a car, you do not have to have one on you. You don't have to agree that the suspicion is reasonable before you confirm your identity. Reasonableness is a matter of law to be decided by a judge. Some other related rights you retain (especially important for naturalized citizens or lawful permanent residents): - You do not have to answer questions about where you were born, whether you're a U.S. citizen, or how you entered the country. (Separate rules apply at international borders and airports.) - You are only expected to identify yourself to Florida law enforcement officers (police officers and Sheriff's deputies, not immigration or FBI agents) when you are stopped on suspicion of a crime or a traffic violation.
