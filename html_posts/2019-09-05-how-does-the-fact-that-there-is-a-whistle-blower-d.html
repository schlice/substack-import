**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **How does the fact that there is a whistle blower describing evidence that the Trump administration is interfering with mandatory audits for all Presidents\' and Vice Presidents\' taxes strengthen the case for Congress to see President Trump\'s taxes?** 1. It doesn't pass the smell test. We also have very public knowledge that the President has placed his assets in trust and that he donates his salary (or its equivalent) to charity. Why exactly would he want to conceal his returns from audit after he has become President? It makes very little sense. 2. It's not physically possible to prevent the IRS, the very agency with which you file your taxes, from then subsequently auditing them. They already possess the returns; the President (or his staff) cannot stop the audit process. If this is actually occurring, it is a problem within the IRS, not with the President. 3. The IRS has claimed that they have audited the President's tax returns. So who is lying? The IRS, or the whistleblower? Has the IG's office been engaged to look into this whistleblower's claims? 4. EVEN IF ALL THIS WERE TRUE, this *still* doesn't strengthen Congress' case to see the tax returns. 1. Congress is known to leak like a sieve, which will cause more damage to the President than Congress will gain by seeing his returns. Democrats in Congress have a vested interest in actually leaking those returns so they can use them as political ammunition. They have already visually demonstrated the depths to which they will sink in order to harm their political enemies. This situation does not seem as if it would be any different. 2. If Congress needs to see how laws are being implemented, they only need to subpoena the IRS agents and directors. If those people refuse to testify, they should be held in contempt of Congress. There's been a lot of bloviating about holding individuals in contempt of Congress, but for all of their tough talk, I don't see the courts actually holding any of those contempt charges up for scrutiny. 3. Congress should have turned those contempt charges over to the US Attorney's Office for continued investigation. Why has this not been done? Could it be because Congress' agenda isn't really determining how effectively the law is being followed, but actually trying to dig up dirt on the President? Who can say, really... 4. Congress is only authorized to see aggregate tax data, not tax data on identifiable individuals. Congress is not the FBI, they are not the Inspector General, and they are not the IRS. They need to stay in their lane.
