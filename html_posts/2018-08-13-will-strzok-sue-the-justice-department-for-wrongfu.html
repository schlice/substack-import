**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **Will Strzok sue the justice department for wrongful termination? Will he win or lose if he does?** He can try. But his *conduct*, as is clearly spelled out in the Department of Justice's Inspector General report, does not present him in the best light. We knew since the DOJ filed its IG report on the Hillary investigation that Strzok had some problems, saying that his text messages between himself and his mistress, Lisa Page, "potentially indicated or created the appearance that investigative decisions were impacted by bias or improper considerations," and added that Strzok's behavior was "not only indicative of a biased state of mind but, even more seriously, implies a willingness to take official action to impact the presidential candidate's electoral prospects." The report found that Strzok's decision to put the Russia probe ahead of the Hillary investigation could have been tainted by the same bias. The report said, "Under these circumstances, we did not have confidence that Strzok's decision to prioritize the Russia investigation over following up on the Midyear-related investigative lead discovered on the Weiner laptop was free from bias." Finally, the investigation determined that **Strzok's activities were "antithetical to the core values of the FBI and the Department of Justice."** It is statements like these which would lead any reasonable person to believe that a termination was not only not wrongful, but nearly required, especially from an agency which is supposed to engender public trust. The only thing that could work against the FBI is what is known as "implied promises." According to the Washington Compost, the FBI's disciplinary office had stated that Strzok was to receive a demotion and a 60-day suspension. This could be perceived as an "implied promise" that he was not to be terminated because of this incident. Following this determination, the Deputy Director terminated him anyway. Additionally, Strzok had been a longtime agent and did not have many disciplinary actions against him, which could also have produced what is known as an "implied employment contract." Therefore, there may be a case for a wrongful termination suit, but that IG report is so damning and speaks volumes against what a seasoned agent *should be* doing, that it appears that Strzok violated any implied employment contracts that may have been present, and because of this, it's unlikely that a wrongful termination suit would be successful.
