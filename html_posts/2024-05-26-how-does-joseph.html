**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} So it's easier for us to understand in a more modern translation. So let's look at the passage in ESV: > As for you, you meant evil against me, but God meant it for good, to > bring it about that many people should be kept alive, as they are > today. This verse is at the end of the story of Joseph, when he revealed himself to his brothers at the Pharaoh's temple. After his brothers sold him into slavery, and after a very long and tumultuous journey up the proverbial chain of command, Joseph had been appointed the Pharaoh's viceroy, readying the nation for the upcoming famine. Joseph's brothers travelled from Canaan to see the viceroy to get some grain, not realizing that Joseph (whom they had sold into slavery years before) was actually the viceroy in question. Just the verse before, Joseph told them that he was not going to seek any revenge on them for selling him into slavery, and that he had turned all vengeance over to God. He then speaks the target verse, proclaiming that even what they meant for evil, God had turned to good. He doesn't discount what they did, and that in fact, they meant it for evil. But as he already said in Genesis 45:5--7, Joseph insists that God's ability to turn any situation for His good is more powerful than any scheme that any evil can devise, or any evil that man can foist upon man. What does this mean for us today? God asks Christians to view our lives this way. > And we know that for those who love God all things work together for > good, for those who are called according to his purpose. --- Romans > 8:28 ESV Those "all thing" include those things which were meant for evil against us. God still uses them for good. Now, God does not promise that evil will never happen to us. But He does promise that He will always care for us (cf Romans 8:31) and that nothing can ever separate us from His love (cf Romans 8:34--38).
