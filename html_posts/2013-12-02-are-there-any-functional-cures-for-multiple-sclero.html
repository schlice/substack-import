**Content:** [EDIT: I have been thinking more about this and I wanted to append (prepend?) my answer. You asked if there was a \"functional cure\" for MS. This is not a term generally used for MS, it is more commonly associated with HIV. In that field of study, the definition of \"functional cure\" means no further viral evidence present without ART (anti-retroviral therapy), no disease progression, and no disease transmission. To compare this to MS, one would be \"functionally cured\" if there was no further disease progression, no further disease activity (i.e. no new/active lesions), and no further relapses, all without taking any of the approved disease modification therapies available. So based on that definition, as of this writing, there are no functional cures for MS. The current batch of immunomodulators does not halt disease progression, they merely slow it down. They do not eliminate exacerbations, they reduce their severity and duration. And all evidence points to the fact that ceasing immunomodulator therapy leads to increased disease activity. We can treat the disease, not just the symptoms, and this is a huge step from where we were just 20 years ago. We can make peoples\' lifespans nearly match their non-MS counterparts, and we can improve the quality of life for people with the disease. However, we need a much greater understanding of the disease itself and its origins before we can approach a functional cure.\ \ Your leaky gut theory holds water (no pun intended), as one of the most recent treatments for MS, natalizumab (marketed as Tysabri) is thought to work by preventing auto-immune cells from passing through the blood-brain barrier into the spinal fluid, and also into the intestinal cavity. Tysabri is used as a treatment for MS as well as Crohn\'s disease.\ \ At over 60%, Tysabri has a higher efficacy rate than any other approved MS therapy on the market today.\ \ Does this lend credence to your theory? I will let you decide.]{.rendered_qtext}
