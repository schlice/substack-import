**Content:** [There are differences between a tow because of an illegally parked vehicle, and a tow under police direction. ***If the police direct the car to be towed***, there is zero you can do about it, hooked, unhooked, nothing. You can\'t request the car be dropped, you can\'t stop the tow driver from doing his job, you have to take it up with the police. The tow driver is legally obligated to tow the car when directed by police, he can\'t be stopped other than by police.\ \ ***If the car is being towed because it is illegally parked***, generally (varies by state), once the tow hook is on the vehicle, it can\'t be touched; it\'s in the custody of the tow company. You can\'t get into it at that point (and in most states, doing so is a crime). If you\'re willing to leave before the car is hooked, the tow driver should let you leave. If the car is hooked, the tow driver can let you have it for a \"drop fee.\" In some cases in some states, drop fees are dependent on time of day. In others, it\'s a flat fee. In Colorado, the drop fee can be no more than \$70, and drivers must accept credit cards.\ \ ***If you try to drive off while the car is on the tow truck,*** not only is that not bright (it risks damages to your car and the tow truck), but it\'s illegal. You have to break the tow cable, and you would then be liable for leaving the scene of an accident. We had a case in 2011 in Colorado Springs where a woman got into her car that was attempting to be towed and drove off. The tow hook was on her car, and the tow cable became ensnared around the ankle of the tow truck driver. The cable snapped, and she dragged him for 1.4 miles before he slipped out of his boot. He was dead at the scene. She claimed she didn\'t see him. She was convicted on several counts, including manslaughter, vehicular homicide, leaving the scene of an accident, and some other lesser charges.\ \ [[Colorado Springs jury convicts woman in dragging death of tow-truck driver](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=ad33ba&url=http://www.denverpost.com/ci_20041489){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="denverpost.com"}]{.qlink_container}\ \ This case prompted the city to toughen the laws on tows. Tow drivers now place a big sticker (8.5 x 11) on the driver window explaining that the vehicle may not be touched because it is legally in the possession of the tow company, and getting in to the vehicle is a crime. Penalties for violating the new laws are now much tougher. The new law was named after the tow truck driver, Allen Lew Rose, and is now known as \"the Rose Law.\"]{.rendered_qtext}
