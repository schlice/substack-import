**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **As a 32-year-old with a moderate amount of lesions from multiple sclerosis, do you think there is hope within the next 10 to 15 years of some type of brain repair?** When I was first diagnosed, I had well over 20 lesions and only one of them wasn't a silent lesion. So I fully appreciate your question. I will answer with two points. Prolactin, when used with an interferon beta, [[has been shown](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=fedfd8&url=https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC4367923/){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="nih.gov"}]{.qlink_container} to have remyelinating properties. But it also is a pro-inflammatory agent, so less ideal for MS, in which the inflammation of the nerves precipitates most of the symptoms. Not to mention (and especially important for me), pure prolactin supplementation has been shown to produce male lactation. Um, thanks but no thanks? Anyway, some groundbreaking work is ongoing right now to understand prolactin's role in remyelination and to produce some results in terms of repairing some of the lesions in the brain. Secondly, I was on Tysabri for 18 months. It worked *so well*, that my brain was actually able to repair itself. For the only time in the history of my disease course, my lesions actually shrank while I was on Tysabri. That's not anything that Tysabri did other than to shut down disease process so thoroughly as to allow the brain the opportunity to heal. So not only are we very close to having a medication which could potentially aid in remyelination, we currently have approved MS therapy drugs that work so well that the body can, in fact, heal itself. So to answer your question: **yes**, I firmly believe that we will have some restorative treatments within the next 15 years.
