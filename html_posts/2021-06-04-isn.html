**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} What in the world are you talking about? Any baritone worth his salt can sing this in its traditional key of Ab. I sing it routinely. I was a singer for the Sky Sox a few seasons ago in Colorado Springs, and I sang Opening Day for the Sox. Tons of fun. I also sang for military events. Secondly, the other verses do not involve slavery. I wonder if you're worried about the first line in the fourth verse: "Oh thus be it ever, when freemen shall stand". "Freemen" was less about African slaves and was more about indentured servants who were released from their financial obligations. And even at the time, it had little to do with slavery; it was a condition to vote: > In the Massachusetts Bay Colony, a man had to be a member of the > Church to be a freeman; in neighboring Plymouth Colony a man did not > need to be a member of the Church, but he had to be elected to this > privilege by the General Court. Being a freeman carried with it the > right to vote, and in Plymouth only freemen could vote by > 1632.[\[1\]](#tmhOI){#cite-tmhOI .citation_link} Regardless, it expresses an idea about people, not a condoning of slavery. There is no need to change the word. The song is derived from a poem written by Francis Scott Key while he was on a ship watching the attack on Fort McHenry on September 14, 1814. Come the morning light, he was relieved to see that the American flag was still waving over the Fort: the British were unable to take the fort. His joy at seeing the flag still waving inspired him to pen the poem. The poem was printed in newspapers, and yes, it was eventually set to music, a popular English drinking tune called "To Anacreon in Heaven" by composer John Stafford Smith. Nevertheless, the song stuck, and people started calling it "The Star-Spangled Banner" and in 1916, President Woodrow Wilson declared that it would be performed at all official events. Congress adopted it as our national anthem on March 3, 1931. Many things we did as early colonists are holdovers from our time as British subjects, and many of our popular cultural ideas of the time were similarly holdovers from British times.
