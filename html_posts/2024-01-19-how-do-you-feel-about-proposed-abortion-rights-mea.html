**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} Viability clauses make for bad law, for a number of reasons. 1. Viability is a moving target. As medicine advances, babies are surviving at younger and younger gestational ages. For example, the current (2023) average minimum gestational age is about 28 weeks. [[But technology will change that, and soon.](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=b91327&url=https://abcnews.go.com/GMA/Wellness/artificial-wombs-tested-human-trials-new-technology/story?id%3D103356485){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="go.com"}]{.qlink_container} Babies will survive earlier and earlier as medical technology advances. Adding a viability clause makes bad law because no one has a definite minimum date of viability. 2. What does "viability" even mean? Does that mean survival without any assistance at all? Or does that mean survival with medical intervention? Does it mean survival in an artificial womb? We'd have to agree on a definition of viability. 3. What difference does viability make? Why is a baby worthy of death because it can't survive outside of the womb? If the thought is, "the baby can survive if we do nothing to help it," then the logic is incorrect, because "do nothing" means leave it exactly where it is until it naturally leaves the womb. "Do nothing" does not involve killing a living human being, regardless of its stage of development or its "viability" outside of the womb.
