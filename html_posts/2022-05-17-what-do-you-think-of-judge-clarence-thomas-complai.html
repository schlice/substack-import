**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} What he specifically said was the following: > As a society, \"we are becoming addicted to wanting particular > outcomes, not living with the outcomes we don\'t like,\" Thomas said. > > \"We can\'t be an institution that can be bullied into giving you just > the outcomes you want. The events from earlier this week are a symptom > of that.\"[\[1\]](#jCmbp){#cite-jCmbp .citation_link} The Supreme Court specifically is an organization which is designed to be *outside* of the influence of people. That's one of the reasons the Justices are appointed for life. They don't ever have to worry about appeasing the public in order to be re-elected. They can focus their attention on the Constitutionality of the laws, even if the decisions may be unpopular. What people need to realize is that while they are indeed allowed to freely express their opinion, there is no requirement for the government to respond, or to alter what they are doing because of it. In 1984, the court said, "Nothing in the First Amendment or in this Court\'s case law interpreting it suggests that the rights to speak, associate, and petition require government policymakers to listen or respond to communications of members of the public on public issues."[\[2\]](#jHjwQ){#cite-jHjwQ .citation_link} This is especially true of the courts, which are designed to be free from public pressure. Thomas is basically saying that people need to realize that these protests are more about expressing your opinion than they are pressuring a Justice to change his vote, as the latter encroaches dangerously close to some violations of federal law. \ Original question: What do you think of Judge Clarence Thomas complaining about the nationwide protests against the leaked draft overturning Roe v.
