**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **Should or could the impeachment charges against ex-President Donald John Trump be amended or expanded?** The impeachment is complete. It cannot be expanded or amended, as it has already been transmitted to the Senate, and to modify it now would require a new Bill of Impeachment. The House cannot pass a new one as the person is no longer a Federal official. You cannot impeach someone who is no longer a Federal official. Which poses an interesting Constitutional question for the trial: ***how can you try someone in Congress who is not a Federal official?*** You may have noticed that Chief Justice Roberts declined to serve as the judge for the trial. He's right to decline, as he can only serve when the President is on trial. The president is not on trial, a private citizen is on trial (which is also an issue, and we'll get there). The Vice President (the President of the Senate) would be the next logical choice, but she cannot preside, as she has a direct conflict of interest, which is why they chose Sen. Leahy. The framers of the Constitution never contemplated an ex-President being tried, as the punishment is moot (removal from office). The Constitution specifically forbids Congress trying private citizens in its prohibition against ***Bills of Attainder***. A Bill of Attainder is a legislative act that singles out an individual citizen or group of citizens for punishment without a trial, and while we call the Senate proceedings a "trial," it is actually not, because the Senate "trial" is only for Federal officials, not private citizens. The Constitution provides that \"\[n\]o **Bill of Attainder** or ex post facto Law will be passed\" (Article I, Section 9, Paragraph 3). Simply put, you cannot try a civilian in a political venue, especially when the result could be a punishment. But Trump is no ordinary citizen; he is a former President. So the Senate will have to vote to determine whether or not they should proceed with a trial. Of course, the Senate will vote that it does have standing to try an ex-President, but it will be a very close vote. It might even be 50--50, and the Vice President will have to break the tie. But this also means that there is no way in the Hot Place that you'll get 2/3 to convict, mostly because of what we just talked about: Congress does not have standing to punish a civilian, and Congress is Constitutionally prohibited from passing a Bill of Attainder, which is what any punishment against a civilian would amount to. So you will find that if a trial goes forward (unconstitutionally, of course), many of the people who vote against any punishment will do so because they recognize the lack of Constitutional authority, and the specific prohibition against Bills of Attainder.
