**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} Firstly, it\'s not a \"single\" contract to build a plane. There are multiple contracts involved, delivering the components of the aircraft, and then there are contracts to deliver the follow-on maintenance and follow-on replacement parts. Also, Lockheed recently won a \$84.4 million follow-on cost plus fixed fee contract to provide special tooling and special test equipment that will be important to maintain current production rates. Second, all government contracts are covered by the Federal Acquisition Regulations (FAR), which is an *exhaustive* set of rules which must be followed when writing, bidding, awarding, and fulfilling government contracts. The FAR is what keeps companies like Boeing, Lockheed, Grumman, and others alive and kicking. There are lots of different contract types; some place more risk on the contractor, some place more risk on the government. :::: ui_qtext_image_outer ::: qtext_image_wrapper ![](images/qimg-46281179d223d3c158556237e9f153f8){.landscape .qtext_image .zoomable_in_feed master_src="images/qimg-46281179d223d3c158556237e9f153f8" master_w="485" master_h="273"} ::: :::: (from [[Government Contracting - The Basics](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=f7250c&url=http://www.acq.osd.mil/osbp/docs/government_contracting_the_basics.pdf){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="osd.mil"}]{.qlink_container}) Fixed price contracts place more risk on the contractor, as cost overruns are paid for by the contractor. Cost Plus contracts place more risk on the government because deadline overruns are not penalized, and some cost overruns are paid for by the government, depending on contract type. So depending on how the contract is written, risk of cost overruns or risks due to subcontractors not delivering, or risks due to projected solutions not being produced can either be placed on the government, the contractor, or both. All of these things happened in the F-35 program: cost overruns, subcontractors not delivering, solutions required for proper functioning not being developed in the aggreed-upon timeframes, etc. Additionally, hackers and Edward Snowden caused some of the classified components of the project to be released, causing the government to have to trash those components and develop new ones, delaying the project further. It\'s easy to look at the total overruns and say \"Well, clearly someone screwed up in writing *that* contract\" or \"Well, that\'s just the government wasting our money again\" but it\'s far more complex than that. It\'s an integrated set of contracts that all work together, and there were failures across the board: the contractors did not properly report when they were going to slip, the government contracting agencies did not provide proper oversight and demand updated reports on subs, etc.
