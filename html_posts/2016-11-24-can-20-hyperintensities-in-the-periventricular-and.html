**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} *Can* it be nothing to worry about? Yes, it can. *Is* it nothing to worry about? Once you find out the cause of such hyperintensities, you won't have to worry about it. But I presume that you got an MRI in the first place because you had a symptom of something. Like me. I got an MRI when I had a bout of *Optic Neuritis*, which is a condition where the optic nerve behind the ocular orbit swells, causing decreased signal intensity through the area of swelling. The condition itself is not much to worry about, some discomfort when looking around, and some vision change during the period that ON is busy doing its thing. The condition resolves itself in the "few weeks to a few months" timeframe. *What causes it* is the more nefarious question. In half of the people who presented with ON at my age, there was no underlying cause. In the other half of the people, the cause was multiple sclerosis. I needed an MRI to confirm. My MRI showed all the signs of someone with MS. And by the way, I also had about 20 hyperintensities in my MRI. The majority of them caused no symptoms; what we call "silent" lesions. I still needed a neurologist to do a full neuro workup on me, including blood tests to rule out the most easily identified causes of hyperintensities on MRIs (what they referred to as the "little white spot" series). I did not need a lumbar puncture (also known as a spinal tap) since my symptoms along with the MRI were enough to confirm MS. So my suggestion to you: if you haven't seen a neurologist yet, now is the time. Make an appointment (or get a referral, depending on your insurance) and go see a neurologist. If you have an MS Center in your area, try to get in with one of the docs in there. They are specialists in neurological conditions, and you will be best served by the resources they can offer.
