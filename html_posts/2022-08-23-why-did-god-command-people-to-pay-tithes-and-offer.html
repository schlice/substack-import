**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} This confusion is common. The Bible separates out the two types of giving: giving to the government (which is always mandatory) and giving to the church (which is always voluntary). Except in the case of Israel, which at that time was a [[theocracy](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=f98387&url=https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theocracy){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="wikipedia.org"}]{.qlink_container}*.* In other words, Israel at the time was *both the government and the church.* So sometimes, modern day Christians confuse the giving which was required to run the government, and the giving of your heart to the church, *because they both went to the same place.* The Levitical priests acted as civil government, so the giving of the tithe (see Leviticus 27:30--33) was what we would consider today to be income tax. Deuteronomy 14:22--29 also details a second tithe to fund a national festival. This was more than what we would consider to be a religious tithe (10% of a person's income); it was actually well above 20%. But all of that money was paid to the "government" to run the country. Any giving *above that* was to the church and was totally voluntary (see Exodus 25:2 and 1 Chronicles 29:9). People would give whatever was in their heart to give. This giving was never specified by the church.
