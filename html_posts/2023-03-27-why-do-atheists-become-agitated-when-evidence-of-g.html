--- title: "Why do atheists become AGITATED when evidence of God is presented?" date: "2023-03-27" tags: ["theology"] layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} Because it's not the "right kind" of evidence. Evidence exists. Not everyone finds the evidence compelling or convincing --- that's by design. But that doesn't mean that the evidence is nonexistent. The problem is when atheists demand **proof** which is either unreasonable or irrelevant. They tailor the definition of "evidence" so narrowly that God's existence can never meet their unrealistic burden of proof. Clearly, you're not going to find *physical* evidence of a God who is not physical, and some atheists will claim that if God is not material, then God does not exist. However, even those very same atheists and devout skeptics agree that many things which are immaterial do exist. For example, the laws of logic are not visible or material, but they are indeed real and can be examined and used as proof. The same would hold true for other concepts like morality and love. In fact, thought and reason itself is immaterial. Yes, it involves chemicals in the brain, but chemicals do not reason, they do not think. So thought and reason are not purely material. There are several broad categories of evidence for the existence of God. None of them taken alone can prove that God exists, or even that the God of the Bible provides an accurate description of Him. But when you take them together, they provide a fairly compelling argument that God is real and He does exist. - **Humans have a natural "sense" of God.** Both anthropologists and historians agree that nearly all people who have ever lived have had some form of supernatural belief. There are vanishingly few people worldwide who totally reject all manifestations of a higher force or spirit. Even in extremely \"secular\" cultures, this is true. Secular fields of research like cognitive science of religion contend that such convictions are ingrained in the human mind\'s basic structure. This implies, at the very least, that there is something real to be perceived, just as the senses of sight and hearing focus on real phenomena. - **Logic points to the existence of God.** Several logic-based arguments support the existence of God. Though they are challenging to argue against, some, such as the ontological argument, are not thought to be particularly compelling. Some are thought to be considerably more reliable, such the kalam cosmological argument. Along the same lines, teleological arguments---such as intelligent design---draw logical conclusions from facts to support the presence of God. - **Observational science supports the existence of God.** Because so many components of reality seem to have been planned out, teleological arguments can be convincingly made. Such proof strongly suggests the existence of a Creator on its own. The Big Bang is a well-known illustration. Atheists initially opposed this theory because they felt it was too \"religious.\" Nonetheless, secular science\'s proof of a non-eternal cosmos lends substantial support to the assertions made in the first few chapters of the Bible. - **History, literature and archaeology support the existence of God.** Whether or not opponents agree with it, the Bible is a reliable source of proof for God\'s existence. Not just \"because the Bible says so,\" but also due to the Bible\'s track record of accuracy. It is illogical to dismiss it as prejudiced just because it asserts ideas that skeptics do not agree with. That would be just as illogical as denying the validity of every biography of Julius Caesar and then asserting that there are no records mentioning him. While debating the existence of God, it follows that we would point to the Bible\'s accuracy and its consistency with secular history and archaeology. - **Personal experiences support the existence of God.** These are undoubtedly compelling just for those specific people. But a lot of people have developed extremely personal relationships with and understandings of God. These experiences are legitimate to take into account as supporting evidence for the existence of God insofar as they align with other evidence. It would be virtually impossible to deny everyone in all of human history who has ever had any kind of spiritual experience. This includes Abraham, Moses, Isaiah, Kepler, Newton, Pascal, and Jesus Christ himself. Finally, it's important to note that it is literally the weakest response to evidence of God's existence to simply ignore it and claim "there is no evidence." This claim usually comes alongside a very fluid definition of what is considered proof. Just like what happened with the Big Bang (which virtually no one denies by this point), atheists will still ignore the (continually growing) mountain of evidence that the universe, and all time, space, and matter within it, began at a single point and nothing existed before that, and will still claim that humans are some cosmic accident arising uncaused, from random, nonliving chemicals. First, disprove Einstein's Theory of General Relativity and then we'll talk about the merits of "from Goo to You through the Zoo." But just as one person's belief that God exists doesn't make it true, one person's disbelief doesn't make God's existence untrue. Combining what we know from logic, science, personal experience, and other areas, there is more than enough evidence that God exists. In fact, there is more evidence which shows that God exists than evidence which shows that God does not exist. In fact, you atheists who are chomping at the bit to reply to me, **I challenge any one of you to provide me with proof that God does not exist.** Any proof will suffice. There is plentiful evidence of God's existence, but there is no proof that He does not exist. Therefore, more faith is required to be an atheist than to be a theist, because more evidence exists proving God's existence than not. **TL;DR:** When presented with the facts, and when presented with the truth that it takes more faith to be an atheist than it does to be a theist, I can understand why atheists become agitated. It's called *cognitive dissonance*, and yes it is very annoying. It can be really agitating to have to try to explain away all of the affirmative evidence for the existence of God. And the farther along we go, and the more science we learn, the more we find out that the universe is even more impossibly complex than we could ever have imagined, and natural processes become less and less likely to have been able to do what atheists say they did.
