**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} Proof. Overwhelming, convincing, literal proof in front of our eyes, that we can see and touch. Literal proof right in front of us that there is no God. That's what we'd need to even consider abandoning faith. There is precedent for those who needed proof. Thomas was one of Jesus' disciples, and he refused to believe that Jesus was raised from the dead until he had literal proof in front of his eyes, that he could see and touch. The Bible recorded what happened next. The disciples had gathered as they did every week, in a locked upper room (as they were being hunted by the authorities at the time). Jesus had appeared to them before, but Thomas was not with them. We pick up the story from there: > Now Thomas (also known as Didymus), one of the Twelve, was not with > the disciples when Jesus came. So the other disciples told him, "We > have seen the Lord!" > > But he said to them, "Unless I see the nail marks in his hands and put > my finger where the nails were, and put my hand into his side, I will > not believe." > > A week later his disciples were in the house again, and Thomas was > with them. Though the doors were locked, Jesus came and stood among > them and said, "Peace be with you!" Then he said to Thomas, "Put your > finger here; see my hands. Reach out your hand and put it into my > side. Stop doubting and believe." > > Thomas said to him, "My Lord and my God!" > > Then Jesus told him, "Because you have seen me, you have believed; > blessed are those who have not seen and yet have believed." --- John > 20:24--29 NIV (By the way, the phrase "doubting Thomas" came from here) Thomas needed to see Jesus to believe. And while that was fine for Jesus, he also says, "blessed are those who have not seen, and yet have believed." Jesus acknowledges that we will not be able to touch the nail holes in his hand, or put our hand in his side where he was pierced by the centurion's spear. But how wonderful is it for us, who didn't have the luxury of those things, to still believe that he was raised from the dead and takes away the sins of those who believe in him? Since the only "proof" so far that god does not exist is being promised by atheism, and atheism makes the claim that there is no god, let's turn the question on them: **When will atheism be able to present the proof that Christians need in order for them to believe there is no god?** They can't. Atheism is based on faith that their presumption about science is correct. Atheism says that because you can't see God in a microscope or a telescope means that he doesn't exist, and faith is silly at best. Of course, they fail to see that the scientific method simply isn't able to answer metaphysical questions very well. Nevertheless, atheism is indeed a religious worldview. Just because it doesn't have a higher power doesn't make it any less of a religion. The theist looks at the totality of the circumstances and says, "there is a god" while the atheist does the same and states, with exactly the same level of conviction, "there is no god." They both operate from a primary belief (and faith) that results in their own particular worldview. But you can't prove a negative. As legal scholar and philosopher Mortimer Adler says, "An affirmative existential proposition can be proved, but a negative existential proposition---one that denies the existence of something---cannot be proved." So atheism makes a claim that it can never prove. Therefore, Christians will continue to believe that God exists, and atheists will slide into eternity with their fingers crossed, hoping that they don't find out the unpleasant truth that eternity does indeed exist, and it's an awfully long time to be wrong. Adler continues on to say, "More consequences for life and action follow from the affirmation or denial of God than from any other basic question." And there's no penalty in life to living in Christian faith. There's nothing I can't do in this life that I could do if I were an atheist. I haven't lost anything by being a Christian. At worst, the time I've spent in fellowship has made me a better person, and that is certainly a laudable goal, if nothing else. Basically, I look at it using the old comparison: if I'm wrong, and there is no god, I've lost nothing. But if you're wrong, and there is a god, you've lost *everything*. \ Original question: What will it take for Christians to stop buying the nonsense that Christianity preaches?
