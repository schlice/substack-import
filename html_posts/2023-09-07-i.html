**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} *Homeowners* do not form HOAs. *Developers* implement HOAs in the neighborhoods they develop. They do this because they want to maintain the look of the neighborhood, as usually the pre-designed look of the neighborhood is one of its key selling points. Also, a high-value neighborhood allows the developer to sell other high-value neighborhoods by pointing back at the neighborhoods they've already developed. So if you build a 200-home neighborhood in a craftsman or farmhouse style, someone changing the fa√ßade of their house to beach coastal would definitely look out of place, and would cause nearby houses to lose value, causing the neighborhood as a whole to lose value. So developers will implement HOAs to keep the overall look and feel of the neighborhood that they designed so that they can use the neighborhood as a reference neighborhood when the build and sell other neighborhoods. \ But HOAs benefit homeowners as well, even though some people vehemently oppose HOAs "telling them what to do" (even though government zoning rules already tell them what to do, but whatever). Homeowners benefit from a neat, well-kept neighborhood and home values which remain as high as possible. And homeowners do not have to deal with "that neighbor" who paints their house electric blue, or who has six cars on their front lawn, only two of which have wheels, or the one who has trouble with the days of the week and puts their trash at the curb the day after trash collection, leaving it there all week until the next collection, and who thinks that cutting grass is for someone else, instead leaving their lawn to become a jungle of tall weeds. Those things reduce the values of neighboring homes (since no one wants to live next door to that). HOAs address that. Also, the neighborhood can benefit from *amenities* like pools and clubhouses, and HOA dues fund those amenities.
