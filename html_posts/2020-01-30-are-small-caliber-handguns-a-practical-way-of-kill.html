**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **Are small caliber handguns a practical way of killing mice and rats? How problematic can the damage from over penetration and noise be?** Handguns *might be* useful, but there are better choices. There are indeed some subsonic 22 ammunitions that produce a report much quieter than others. For example, the [[CCI Quiet-22](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=b44308&url=https://www.americanhunter.org/articles/2013/5/23/cci-quiet-22/){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="americanhunter.org"}]{.qlink_container} subsonic round fires a .22 caliber bullet at 710 feet per second but still only produces an astonishing **68 decibels** at the shooter's ear. For comparison, a loud conversation is 65 decibels. City traffic is 85 decibels. Standard gunshots (as other answers have noted) can be 140 decibels or above (that's in the hearing damage range). You could shoot a Quiet-22 in your apartment and your neighbors probably wouldn't even know you fired a gun (even though doing so is ***extremely unwise and most likely illegal***). Even better, one of the flavors of Quiet-22 offered is a *segmented hollow point* which is designed to expand slightly and then break into three segments on impact. This can potentially do more damage than a single round-nose bullet. The downside: you can't use this type of ammunition in a semi-auto handgun or gas-operated rifle. The ammunition is not powerful enough to cycle the action. Of course, you can manually cycle the action, but that kinda defeats the purpose of a semi-auto mechanism. It would be most useful in a single-action rifle or a revolver. But with a shorter barrel comes slower muzzle velocity. Testing with handguns produces much slower muzzle velocity (in the 500 ft/sec range), resulting in the segmented HP not even separating on impact. Nevertheless, the point of my (way too long) description of quiet .22 ammo is that you can indeed shoot it indoors, without hearing protection, without disturbing the neighbors, without the fear of over-penetration, and it might be effective for mice and rats. But my point was that there are better choices. Pellet guns, for example, can achieve much higher muzzle velocities than the Quiet-22, without the fear of over-penetration or ricochet, and they are virtually silent in operation. I keep a pellet gun for pest management and it is extremely effective. A pellet rifle firing .177 pellets will still kill pests quite effectively (literally drops them where they stand) and you won't run afoul of most city ordinances which state that you can't fire a "firearm" within city limits.
