**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} We need to dissect your question because it's actually two questions. 1. **Can the military disobey the president's orders?** As most folks have already answered here, the military can, and is taught how to, disobey direct orders which violate the Constitution or the law in general. 2. **Can they refuse to go to war if they know it is wrong?** You didn't clarify what "wrong" means. Do you mean "illegal?" Or do you mean "morally incongruent with their beliefs?" The military can only disobey ***illegal*** orders. If you receive a ***legal*** order that you don't like because you don't think the US should be involved in a war there, or you think war is bad, or you think the president is an immoral buffoon and so shouldn't be allowed to wage war anywhere, **you cannot disobey that order.** You do not get paid enough to make those kinds of decisions. Those are someone else's job to make, not you. If you do decide that you don't want to go to war because you believe that the US should not be involved in that war, or you think that war, in general, is morally wrong, you are called a *conscientious objector.* There is [[a very long process](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=424726&url=http://girightshotline.org/en/military-knowledge-base/topic/conscientious-objection-discharge%23topic-if-you-are-facing-deployment){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="girightshotline.org"}]{.qlink_container} that you go through when you wish to claim conscientious objector status, or a conscientious objector discharge, and it involves counseling, forms, questionnaires, and letters from friends and family describing how you live your beliefs in the rest of your life outside of military service. It's not just as easy as saying, "I don't wanna go." But it can be done.
