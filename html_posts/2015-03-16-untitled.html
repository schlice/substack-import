**Content:** [Actually, it doesn\'t. The Constitution is a document which places limits on the Federal government, not the states. The 10th Amendment says that any rights not expressly granted to the Federal government belong to the states, or to the people. It\'s clear that the Constitution and its Amendments were intended to bind the Federal government, not the states. Also, ratification by a state does not mean that the state is instantly bound by the Amendment; it simply means that the state agrees to make the Amendment part of the US Constitution. There is no effect on a State Constitution when a State ratifies an amendment to the US Constitution, unless that amendment invalidates part of that state\'s Constitution.\ \ The Supreme Court spent a great deal of time wrestling with this argument. People like Hugo Black wanted \"mechanical incorporation\" of all of the Bill of Rights (1 thru 8) into State law. Others, like Felix Frankfurter, thought that incorporation should be gradual, but only because of due process. He said, \"It is possible that some of the personal rights safeguarded by the first eight Amendments against National action may also be safeguarded against state action, because a denial of them would be a denial of due process of law. If this is so, it is not because those rights are enumerated in the first eight Amendments, but because they are of such a nature that they are included in the conception of due process of law.\" (Twining v New Jersey, 1908) What he was saying is that the rights are protected because of due process (which can change over time), not because they were listed in the Bill of Rights (which does not change). For example, in Palko v Connecticut (1937), the majority ruled that the 5th Amendment protection against double jeopardy did not violate due process, and so therefore didn\'t apply to the states. This was overturned on appeal. It went this way for several Amendments (didn\'t violate due process so didn\'t apply to the states, but then later overturned). So while the Bill of Rights was never \"mechanically incorporated\" to the States, over time and through case law, all 8 of the individual rights amendments were bound against the states. There are some exceptions, but for the most part, they are binding.]{.rendered_qtext}
