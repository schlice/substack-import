**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} Silly question by the silly Quora Prompt Generator. Silly silliness. *Nixon v. US* ruled that the courts have no jurisdiction over the impeachment process, primarily because impeachments are a *political* process, not a *legal* process. I can understand why the confusion sometimes happens. When a President is tried in the Senate, the Chief Justice of the Supreme Court presides over the trial. And it does give the appearance of being very *legal-esque,* what with a prosecutor and a defense, and the Constitution calling it a *trial*. But make no mistake, impeachment is nothing more than a political process to remove an elected official from office. And while impeachment is reserved for "high crimes and misdemeanors," which certainly reads like the accused violated laws (and up until Trump's impeachment in his first term, it was), the accused does not need to actually violate a law in order to be impeached. Just ask Trump. In fact, it was another former president, Gerald R. Ford, nearly a half century ago, while he was still in the House during his 1970 attempt to impeach U.S. Supreme Court Justice William O. Douglas, who said "\[a\]n impeachable offense is whatever a majority of the House of Representatives considers it to be at a given moment in history." Ford later became vice president and then president after President Richard Nixon resigned rather than face impeachment himself. So given that there is no clear delineation of what is impeachable and what is not, it becomes clear that the impeachment process is entirely political, and not judicial. Therefore, the courts have no jurisdiction over the impeachment process.
