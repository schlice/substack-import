--- title: '"A good friend of mine has just been diagnosed with multiple sclerosis. How can I be supportive?"' date: '2017-02-08' tags: - health-autoimmune layout: post ---

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} We've answered this question multiple times on Quora. Did you use Quora search? I'll admit it's not very good, but try it sometime. Your friend will be going through some very heavy emotions. It's the same set of emotions (the stages of grief) one goes through when someone close to you dies. There's rejection, anger, compromise, and eventually acceptance. Those emotions all happen, but they don't happen at any pre-defined pace. In other words, it's different for everyone. With MS, there are lots of "if only" mental exercises you go through. "If only I would have done X differently, I wouldn't have MS." "If only I hadn't smoked in my late teens, I wouldn't have MS" (that was mine, by the way). But then comes compromise. "Maybe if I do X, I can get through this." Such as, "Maybe if I walk a mile a few times a week, I can improve my overall health and it will all be manageable." "The MS pills aren't so bad, I can take a pill a day." Or for me, when I started an only injectables were available, "My seven-year old neighbor is diabetic and gets insulin shots three times a day; my medicine is only once a day. If he can do it, so can I." And then you accept that "you have MS, but MS does not have you." In other words, MS is just one of the things that make up who you are; it does not define you. You should be there for your friend while they go through this emotional roller-coaster. It's important to remember that while MS represents a significant change to life and how you plan on living it, *it doesn't represent a death sentence.* You cannot die from MS. You can die from complications from severe, long-term untreated MS, but MS itself is not fatal. Make sure that through the grief and anxiety, your friend makes an appointment with a neurologist if they haven't done so already. That diagnosis should be delivered or confirmed by a neurologist. While there is no cure for MS, your friend will need to start on one of the approved MS therapy drugs. There are plenty to choose from, but they all basically do the same thing: slow down the progression of MS, and reduce the severity and duration of relapses. It's important to start on one of the approved MS therapy drugs as soon as possible. This is the "conservative" approach: to "conserve" as much brain matter as possible, because once it's damaged, it doesn't come back. Depending on neural load, some reserve neurons can wire around the damaged areas, but that reserve load is a fixed quantity. The current theory is that once that reserve load is depleted, relapses gain more permanence and do not remit as easily (or fully). Therefore, we want to save as much brain matter as possible. In short, *just be a good friend*. Be the ear to listen, and be the shoulder to lean on, while your friend goes through the stages of grief which, while painful to watch (and even more painful to go through yourself), are necessary to the healing process.
