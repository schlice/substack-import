**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} All religions should have religious freedom protections, not just Christians. The Indiana anti-abortion law was written in such a way as to specify that life begins at conception. This is what caused them issue, since the plaintiffs in this case argued that there are other non-Christian religions which do not hold that life begins at conception, specifically Judaism and Islam, as well as other "independent spiritual belief systems." The non-Christian litigants are being represented by the ACLU. \"Although some religions believe that human life begins at conception, this is not an opinion shared by all religions or all religious people,\" the ACLU said in a statement. Therefore, the religious beliefs of these non-Christian religions are being substantially burdened by a law which has at its basis the claim that life begins at conception. Indiana legislators should take note: **how you word your laws matters.** [[**US News: Judge Blocks Indiana Abortion Ban on Religious Freedom Grounds**](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=4100ba&url=https://www.usnews.com/news/top-news/articles/2022-12-02/judge-blocks-indiana-abortion-ban-on-religious-freedom-grounds){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="usnews.com"}]{.qlink_container} (EDIT) I will add that I think Indiana's judges missed the point of these laws. A law which states that abortions are illegal does not unduly burden any religion. The Religious Freedom Restoration Act (both the Federal version and Indiana's version) prohibits the government from substantially burdening anyone's religious beliefs *except "in furtherance of a compelling governmental interest,"* and it requires politicians to use "the least restrictive means" possible. The government has a compelling interest in protecting innocent life, and no less restrictive means are available than prohibiting abortion. "Science, not religion, tells us that abortion kills a human being," a spokesperson for Attorney General Todd Rokita (R) said in a statement. "When a legislature outlaws abortion, it extends the same ethical judgment embodied in laws prohibiting homicide. Making ethical judgments about the treatment of human beings is a compelling function of government. Indeed, the very nature of law is to make ethical judgments about human actions and their consequences. Just because a legal rule effects an ethical judgment does not make it 'religious.'" In other words, even if there was a religion which expressly authorized murder, the state could still pass a law making murder illegal; and while it would substantially burden that religion, it is done in furtherance of a compelling governmental interest (the protection of innocent life), and it is done by the least restrictive means possible (there is no less restrictive means available other than to make it illegal). Abortion laws follow the same ethical standards, and are not religiously-based. But even if they are, the same philosophy applies.
