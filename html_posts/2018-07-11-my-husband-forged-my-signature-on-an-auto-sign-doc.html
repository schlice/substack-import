**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **My husband forged my signature on an auto sign document. Is it legal?** I'm going to assume that in your answer "auto sign" means some document you needed to sign for your "auto" or your car. You probably meant to say an "auto loan" document. [[Definition of forgery from FindLaw.](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=de2e90&url=http://criminal.findlaw.com/criminal-charges/forgery.html){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="findlaw.com"}]{.qlink_container} Notice the essential element of forgery as a crime (emphasis mine): > In most jurisdictions, the crime of forgery is not charged unless the > forgery is done **with intent to deceive or the attempt to commit > fraud or larceny**. For instance, works of arts can be copied or > replicated without any crime being committed unless someone attempted > to sell or represent the copies as originals. In these cases, the > copies would become illegal forgeries. Therefore, legally speaking, ***if he actually had your permission, and you consented to the signature***, the signature is legal. But the legality turns on *intent*, which is very difficult to prove. So if I was your husband, I'd have taken a picture of the document and sent it to you and you would have replied saying "go ahead and sign for me" so that I had a paper trail that I could prove that you consented to me signing your name. Now, you didn't say whether or not you consented to your signature being used. ***If you did not consent to your signature being used***, it is by definition a forgery, and the signature is void, and your husband could be facing fraud charges. If it's a loan, he could be facing bank fraud charges, and that's a Federal crime.
