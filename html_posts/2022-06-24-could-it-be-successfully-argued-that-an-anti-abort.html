**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} Unlikely. Those who argue for removing the "life of the mother" exceptions say that doctors can still intervene to save the life of a woman without being prosecuted. They make a distinction between the death of a fetus *as a result of lifesaving treatment*, and the intentional killing of a fetus by abortion. Additionally, they argue, some "life of the mother" exceptions are ambiguous. Exceptions span the range from "life of the mother" to "health of the mother," and there is no standard as to what procedures are medically necessary, and what procedures cross the line. In states with minimal explanation of what constitutes an exception, doctors fear crossing the line and being subject to penalties, including jail time and loss of their licenses. There is nothing in the Constitution which guarantees that a medical decision will not cost someone their life. There are laws against medical malpractice in place already. The only guarantee in the Constitution is that life cannot be taken without due process of law. There is no requirement to save any particular life in the Constitution. As far as the Constitution is concerned, all life has equal value. So it's unlikely that such an argument could be made successfully. \ Original question: Could it be successfully argued that an anti-abortion law which doesn't take into account the life of a mother is unconstitutional?
