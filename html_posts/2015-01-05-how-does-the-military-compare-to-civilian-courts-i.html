**Content:** [The problem here, of course, is that you are comparing apples to oranges.\ \ Civilian courts are adjudicating using the US legal system which follows Federal, State, or local laws. Military courts are adjudicating cases based on the Uniform Code of Military Justice, the UCMJ. UCMJ is far stricter than civilian law in most cases, as the military is expected to be held to a higher standard.\ \ In fact, punishment in the military is much harsher than civilian law. You can receive confinement (prison, which still includes hard labor; i.e. turning big rocks into little rocks with sledgehammers), garnishment of salary, and dishonorable discharge. For crimes like treason, the UCMJ still offers execution as a punishment.\ \ Also, this is the only area of law in the US where \"double-jeopardy\" doesn\'t apply. Double-jeopardy is the principle that states that if you\'ve already been tried and convicted for a crime, you can\'t be re-tried and re-convicted for the same crime (there was actually a really good movie titled \"Double Jeopardy\" which explored this legal principle). In situations where you have violated both a civilian law and a military law, the military can try you, convict you, and sentence you, and then at the completion of your sentence, you can be released to civilian authorities to be tried again, for the same crime.\ \ In most cases, where civilian law and military law coincide, civilian prosecutors defer to the military to try the case first. It\'s actually pretty rare that a crime would be first tried by the military and then tried by civilians, but it does happen. What doesn\'t usually happen is for civilian courts to try and convict someone and then have the military do the same. The military would consider the civilian court decision an administrative matter and proceed with military discipline (i.e. counseling, confinement, garnishment, or discharge).]{.rendered_qtext}
