**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **If Trump was found to be colluding with Russians, does that make his presidency illegitimate, and would all his doings be reversed?** In short, even if Trump was found to have ***illegally paid*** the Russians to swing the election in his favor, it would not make his presidency illegitimate, it would not automatically invalidate any Executive Orders he has signed, nor would it invalidate any bills he may have signed into law. First, we have already determined that no votes were changed, and no US citizens were prevented from voting. Therefore, ***we have already determined that the election was legal, and the election results are final***. Trump is a legitimate president, and the US presidential election process was not tampered with. In fact, there is nothing that *automatically* makes a president illegitimate. **If the people voted for the president, the presidency is legitimate.** Therefore, the only thing that the Russians could have "hacked" is the mind of the voter. To that end, we're talking about who is allowed to purchase advertising and who is allowed to talk to US citizens about US elections. And incidentally, we're also assuming that US voters are so dumb that they are easily swayed by advertising. The question, therefore, is *did Trump ask Russians to buy advertising to try to sway voters to vote for him, and if he did, did he pay them to do it?* If Trump ***paid*** Russians using campaign money, he may be guilty of violating campaign finance laws. Even this does not automatically make the president illegitimate. The president's presidency is still legal and legitimate. If he did not pay Russians, but instead ***asked*** them to swing the election in his favor (in other words, "collude" with them to try to influence Americans to vote for him instead of Clinton), and they did this without being paid, how is this any different from when [[Americans have interfered in other foreign elections](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=37fe5c&url=https://www.washingtonpost.com/news/worldviews/wp/2016/10/13/the-long-history-of-the-u-s-interfering-with-elections-elsewhere/?utm_term%3D.1558ccae67ba){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="washingtonpost.com"}]{.qlink_container}? Perhaps you believe this action is not in keeping with the office of the President. Your only recourse is to impeach the President, claiming that such antics constitute "conduct unbecoming of the office of President." 2/3 of the House must agree to impeach, and then 2/3 of the Senate must agree that the impeached offense is severe enough that you need to be removed from office.
