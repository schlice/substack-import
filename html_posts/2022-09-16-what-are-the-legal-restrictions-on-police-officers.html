**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} The legal restrictions on police officers detaining people without probable cause are the same in California as they are in the rest of the country, because these are Constitutional principles not limited by state law. All police officers can detain people without probable cause. No police officer can detain people without **reasonable suspicion.** That standard is lower than probable cause. Probable cause is required for *arrest*, which is different than *detention*. When you are detained, you are not under arrest. Investigative detentions were named so after the landmark Supreme Court case *Terry v. Ohio*, which is why they are often called "Terry stops." During an investigative detention, the suspect is not free to leave, may be handcuffed for officer safety, and may be frisked (briefly searched) for weapons. An officer may find evidence during the investigative detention which provides him with the requisite *probable cause* to effect an arrest. But an officer cannot *arrest* you without probable cause, and an officer cannot *detain* you without reasonable suspicion. Terry stops can last anywhere from a few seconds to a few hours. There really is no absolute limit on how long a Terry stop can take, but the stop "must be temporary and last no longer than is necessary to effectuate the purpose of the stop..." (*US v. Segoviano,* ND III, 2019). In other words, the length of the detention must be reasonably related to the reason for the investigation. Everyone has a 4th Amendment right against unreasonable searches and seizures. An arrest without probable cause would be a violation of that right. A detention without reasonable suspicion of criminal activity would be a violation of the civil right against unlawful detention.
