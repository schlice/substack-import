**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} What\'s more important is that regardless of jurisdiction (some jurisdictions like Nevada, as [[Tim Dees](https://www.quora.com/profile/Tim-Dees)]{.qlink_container} mentioned, exempt private property from DUI enforcement while others, like Illinois, as [[Christopher Hawk](https://www.quora.com/profile/Christopher-Hawk)]{.qlink_container} mentioned, do not), the *hot pursuit rule* applies. If an officer observes your behavior on a public street, he can then chase you anywhere to catch you: inside a home, inside a building, onto private property (even without the owner\'s permission), across state lines, etc. Some rules do apply depending on jurisdiction, such as when the officer actually began the pursuit. And there are inter-agency considerations when traversing agency borders, but in most cases, an officer can pursue you almost anywhere to stop you. Note that this law also notably applies to military police; if they begin a pursuit on a military facility, they can continue that pursuit off the base/post. Note that they will normally hand off primary control of the pursuit to a local law enforcement agency once they engage, but fleeing off the base is no guarantee of freedom from pursuit
