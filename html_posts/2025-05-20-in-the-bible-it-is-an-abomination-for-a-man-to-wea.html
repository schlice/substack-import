**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} Ah yes, another Levitical law question bring asked of Christians who are no longer under the old laws. Let\'s see...(thumbing through index of standard answers)...ah, here we are: Biblical Laws. The Bible contains three types of laws: ceremonial, civil, and moral. Let\'s elaborate: **Moral Laws**. These laws, such as the Ten Commandments, are seen as reflecting God\'s eternal character and are believed to be binding on all people, including Christians. **Ceremonial Laws**. These laws dealt with religious practices like sacrifices, purifications, and the observance of holy days within the Old Testament covenant. They are understood to have pointed to Christ and were fulfilled by his death and resurrection. Therefore, these laws are not binding on Christians. **Civil Laws**. These laws were specific to the nation of Israel and dealt with matters like social order, criminal justice, and property rights. They are considered to be temporary and not binding on Christians, if they were specifically related to the functioning of the nation of Israel, since God was creating a nation out of a group of people who were multi-generational slaves and did not know how to govern themselves. The laws about clothing were intended to separate the nation of Israel from all of the other nations, causing Israel to be set apart among the nations. Dictating customs of dress was one way that God separated the Israelites from the other nations around them. This is yet another example of laws which only applied to the Israelites of the day, and do not apply to Christians in the modern day. That doesn\'t mean that the laws are not descriptive of the time in which they were written. But just because they were *descriptive* does not mean that they are *prescriptive*. Something can exist in the Bible without it relating directly to us.
