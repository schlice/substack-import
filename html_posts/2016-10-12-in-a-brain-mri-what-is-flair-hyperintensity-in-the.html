**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} FLAIR stands for "Fluid-Attenuated Inversion Recovery" and is a pulsed sequence used in MRIs to produce an image. Using various Time to Echo (TE) and Response Time (TR) values, we can produce different images, as [[BR](https://www.quora.com/profile/BR-185)]{.qlink_container} described. The T2-weighted FLAIR image (high TR/high TE) can show us a great deal more detail compared to older spin-density sequences (high TR/low TE spin echo). A "hyperintensity" is basically a white spot. In a T2-weighted FLAIR image, a bright spot on the image is considered a hyperintensity. **What it means** is not for us to say here. Simply saying that there is a hyperintensity on a particular area of the brain (in your case, the anterior pole of the right temporal lobe) signifies nothing other than an MRI finding. It's up to the neurologist to tell you what that hyperintensity indicates. White matter hyperintensities in otherwise normal brains may be a cause for concern. For you, it may be expected after brain surgery.
