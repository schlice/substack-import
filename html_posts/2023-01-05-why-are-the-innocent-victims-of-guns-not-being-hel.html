--- title: "Why are the innocent victims of guns not being helped by smart lawyers? Lawyers should be parading researchers, gun control organisations, politicians and presidents in court as witnesses to testify on the power of guns to make people commit crimes?" date: "2023-01-05" tags: ["policy-analysis"] layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} Because despite all of the entities you mentioned, *the statistics simply do not support the notion that guns make people commit crime.* And the most telling examples are "all the other first-world or advanced economies around the world" which have little to no guns in public hands. ***They all still have crime***. And for those first-world/advanced economies *which have transitioned away from general public ownership of firearms,* the statistics show that the change induced negligible impact to overall crime rates. In Australia, for example, their big gun buyback (which only removed about 1/3 of the guns from public hands) had virtually no impact on the (already downward trending) rates of homicide. :::: ui_qtext_image_outer ::: qtext_image_wrapper ![](images/qimg-11e6c652242b6533a47fed92136abe20){.landscape .qtext_image .zoomable_in_feed master_src="images/qimg-11e6c652242b6533a47fed92136abe20" master_w="1073" master_h="770"} ::: :::: Innocent victims of violence using a gun do not change the fact that crime rates are not positively correlated to gun ownership. Those innocent victims of violence can be more helpful in making the case that **politicians at the state level need to get tougher on crime.** Gun crimes need to attach mandatory sentences, and DAs should be forced to charge gun crimes when guns are involved, and they should not be able to plead gun crimes away. But at the state level, *exactly the opposite* has been happening, and California has been leading the charge. California has been converting more high-level misdemeanors to low-level misdemeanors (so they can't even detain for those crimes any longer). They have also eliminated cash bail, meaning that an offender can walk free with no accountability, increasing the chance that they will not return for their appointed court dates. New York and Illinois are not far behind. And don't forget, those states have some of the highest rates of gun crimes out there, despite having some of the toughest laws on the books. Guns don't make people commit crime any more than forks make people fat and paint and brushes make people artists. *People commit crimes*. Guns have no magical powers to induce people to commit crimes. If your idea is that guns somehow entice people to "punch above their weight," so to speak, then you might want to take a closer look at "liquid courage," otherwise known as *alcohol*. Alcohol is the largest external factor in domestic violence, and yet guns are not even the number one or number two weapon used in those cases (first is knives and second is fist/feet). Controlling alcohol is a much more productive way to reduce crime. Plus, alcohol has no positive effects whatsoever, while guns *prevent orders of magnitude more crimes than they cause.* But you probably won't do that because you like alcohol and you don't like guns.
