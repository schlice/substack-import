**Content:** [To Bill Moyers\' point, a corporation is a legal construct; a corporation is not a person. A corporation is considered a legal entity separate from the people that make it up, but a corporation is not a person. In some cases, a corporation can be considered a person, but not for all things that people are considered people.\ \ Under common law, the definition of murder is \"the unlawful killing of a human ***by another human*** with malice and forethought.\"\ \ Since a corporation is not a human, it cannot technically be charged with murder. The officers of the corporation, on the other hand, can be charged individually (usually) with conspiracy to commit murder. Probably much safer legal footing than trying to charge a corporation with murder.\ \ On the other hand, it is possible (however unlikely) that a corporation could be charged with manslaughter. For example, an automobile manufacturer could be charged with manslaughter if it knew that implementing a change, or failing to implement a change, would cause death and, for the sake of the corporation, it chose not to do so, that corporation could potentially be charged with manslaughter.\ \ Jennifer Arlen, a professor at NYU Law School, says that while difficult, [[a corporation can be charged with manslaughter](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=5ef472&url=http://fortune.com/2014/06/05/could-gm-be-charged-with-murder/){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="fortune.com"}]{.qlink_container}:\ ]{.rendered_qtext} > For a company to be charged with manslaughter, Arlen said, a few > things have to happen. First, prosecutors would have to be able to > find a specific person whose actions led to people dying. Second, that > person would have to have taken those actions for the benefit of the > company and within the scope of their employment, and have been "in > the right mental state" when they took the actions. > For instance, Arlen said, if a postal employee walks into a post > office and shoots innocent bystanders, they are not acting "on behalf > of the company." So, even though it happened in a post office and was > committed by a postal employee, the post office would not be guilty of > homicide because the shooter was not acting on behalf of the company.\ So while it\'s unlikely but possible that you could charge a corporation with manslaughter, it\'s extremely unlikely, if not impossible, to charge a corporation with murder.
