**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **How Does \"Gabapentin\" affects for MS?** Gabapentin does not affect MS. It does not alter the disease process. It does not reduce the time between relapses, and it does not reduce the severity or duration of a relapse. What Gabapentin does is to manage a possible symptom of the disease, and no, it's not seizures. It's highly unlikely that an MS patient would be suffering seizures as part of his disease. Gabapentin is an *anticonvulsant*. It was originally used in epilepsy to control seizures. The downside to Gabapentin was that it was not long-acting. It is broken down in the body fairly quickly, and new anticonvulsants have been released (since Gabapentin was approved as an anticonvulsant) which work much better and control seizures for a much longer period of the day, so it isn't used much for its on-label primary use anymore. Gabapentin has shown some benefit for people who suffer from the "MS hug," as that is a nerve misfiring issue which sometimes responds positively to Gabapentin. However, Gabapentin has one big side effect: *drowsiness*. Gabapentin makes you tired. It makes you tired quickly, and it clears out of your body within about four hours. So in some cases, it is effective as a *sleep aid* when other sleep aids are contraindicated. Gabapentin also has some fairly complex dosing. Therefore, make sure you only use Gabapentin exactly as it was prescribed for you. Do not take more or less of it because you think it will do something else for you.
