**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} I just finished reading a book series by David Simpson called, collectively, the \"Post-Human Series.\" If you like sci-fi, dystopian future stories, limits of human intelligence, etc., you\'ll really like that series. One of the overarching themes in that series was the following: *\"Feelings are never wrong. Acting on those feelings may be, but feelings themselves are never wrong.\"* Likewise, *desire* to kill someone is not wrong. *Acting on that desire* would clearly be wrong, but simply desiring to kill someone is not immoral in and of itself. Since desire and feeling are nothing more than emotion stemming from brain activity (thought), desire is borne of thought. From the [[Encyclopedia.com](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=8ff46a&url=http://encyclopedia.com){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="encyclopedia.com"}]{.qlink_container} entry for "morality of thought:" > The act of thinking is, in itself, amoral, although it is a spiritual > activity proper only to a person. All moral activity involves > thinking, but it also involves volition. In speaking of the morality > of thoughts, therefore, thinking must be understood to include, or to > be associated with, some activity on the part of the > will.[\[1\]](#Sgloz){#cite-Sgloz .citation_link} So, in order for a thought to gain a moral component, there must be some overt action involved in it, which reinforces my quote from Simpson's book. To bring it back into context: thought is not immoral, because thoughts in and of themselves are amoral. Volition or will is required to turn thought or desire into overt action, which can then contain a moral component.
