**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} As [[Matthew Park Moore](https://www.quora.com/profile/Matthew-Park-Moore)]{.qlink_container} explained, in general, Good Samaritan laws are designed to indemnify you against lawsuits for what might happen if you cause more harm to someone when attempting to assist in a life-threatening situation. For example, if you pull someone from a burning car but they had a spinal injury and become paralyzed as a result, you would generally be protected from lawsuits seeking damages because you caused the paralysis. There are, of course, limitations to that protection. For example, if you work in a field in which you should know better (i.e. you're a doctor), you are not covered under many states' Good Samaritan laws (but hopefully you have medical malpractice insurance that can protect you). Also, you have to be making a *good faith attempt* to save the victim's life; the protection does not allow you to be reckless or place the person in more danger than you found them (also called gross negligence). Back to the burning car example, if you were to drag the person to safety and leave them on the pavement in the middle of a six-lane freeway, it could be argued that you were negligent in your actions, and it doesn't take a rocket surgeon to figure that out. If, however, you are making a good faith attempt to help, you're not a trained first responder, and something goes horribly wrong during your attempt, you should be indemnified under most states' Good Samaritan laws. Not all states are the same. Alabama only protects medically-trained personnel. Oklahoma only protects non-trained personnel if they are performing CPR or controlling bleeding; anything else, and you risk a liability lawsuit. However, before you stop to help at an accident scene, take the time to review the Good Samaritan statutes in your state. Also, bear in mind that there is never any liability involved if the only thing you do is to be a good witness and call 911 to let the professionals handle it.
