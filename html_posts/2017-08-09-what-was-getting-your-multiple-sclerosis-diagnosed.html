--- title: '"What was getting your multiple sclerosis diagnosed like?"' date: '2017-08-09' tags: - health-autoimmune layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} Well, it explained my symptoms. And it explained a few other things too, that were unexplained from at least a year or more earlier. Beyond that, I was numb for a while. I got my first MRI and got a call back from the doctor the same day (FYI, that's never a good sign to begin with). He said you need to find a good neurologist, because your MRI shows all the signs of someone with MS. I was numb all the way through that first appointment. I remember going home with stacks of swag and literature from all of the drug manufacturers and thinking to myself, "If they can afford this much swag for someone *who hasn't even started on their drug yet,* they're really fighting for my dollars because these drugs probably all do the same thing, but with different levels of effectiveness." That much was true. What was also true was the route of administration, as well as the side effects, were all different. MS medicines have lots of side effects. And the ones with the least amount of side effects have to be taken most frequently. I started on Copaxone within six months of my diagnosis. At that time, the Copaxone formulation was a daily injection. It was easy enough to add to my routine, but the inconvenience of needles was a bit much. Traveling with my medicine was a challenge too, and I was on a fairly full-scale travel schedule at that time. Once I got through the numb phase, I experienced all of the other emotions that anyone who has experienced a loss experiences: grief, fear, anger, compromise, and eventual acceptance. It probably took me about four months to go through the full range of emotions and come out on the other side. A few things that helped me: I know that MS is not fatal. It can produce side effects which can be life threatening, but the disease itself cannot kill you. And I have always held to the belief that *that which does not kill me only makes me stronger*. So MS must, therefore, make me stronger. It certainly doesn't make me physically stronger, but it did make me mentally tougher. I experience things that my non-MS friends and family will never experience, and I'm still standing and still fighting. I also know that everyone has *something* that they have to deal with. You can't always see those things that others deal with, but there is always something. I also learned that I have been given this disease for a reason. I still don't know what that reason is, but I'm doing what I can, in forums like this one and others, to help people who have been diagnosed, know what to expect and to be able to work through it. And that's something.
