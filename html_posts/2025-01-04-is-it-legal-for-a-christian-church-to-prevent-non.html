--- title: "Is it legal for a Christian church to prevent non-Christians from entering the building?" date: "2025-01-04" tags: ["theology", "law"] layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **Yes, the church can prevent non-Christians from entering the building.** While anti-discrimination laws apply to businesses, religious gatherings are specifically exempted from anti-discrimination laws which prevent discrimination based on "creed" or "religion". You wanted a separation between church and state, so great, the church won't influence the government. But that works both ways: the government cannot interfere with how a religious organization operates. Additionally, churches are private property, and can control who enters and who does not, so long as they do not bar entry for *any other non-religious* *protected reason* (race, color, country of origin, etc). **Even if the church is using public property**, like a public school on the weekend, during the time that the church is using the property, church rules apply, not government rules. Churches can keep non-Christians out if they so choose. \ *But why would they?* The whole point of church is to get more people to *become* Christian. Yes, the church also ministers to believers, but they similarly minister to *unbelievers* as well. Most churches of which I am aware actually *welcome* those who do not believe. Heck, come on in, have a free donut and some coffee, listen to some good live music, and hear a message which, even if you don't believe the Christianity of the message, probably still has some beneficial themes and points. We encourage non-believers to come in, ask questions (even the hard ones), and enjoy our hospitality. **TL;DR:** They *can*, but they *shouldn't.*
