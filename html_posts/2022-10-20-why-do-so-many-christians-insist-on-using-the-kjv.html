**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} First of all, let's address the elephant in the room. Despite what someone may have told you, no translation of the Bible has "added" or "subtracted" anything of substance to any book of the Bible. I challenge anyone to show me where brand new text, "out of whole cloth," as we say, has been either added or subtracted, from the Bible, from any version. Show me the original text, and then show me what was added or subtracted. That said, doing translations between languages can be challenging, and translating anything out of Greek is no exception. For example, there are *seven* Greek words for love, and there are at least two Greek words for rock. The different words describe different "contexts" of the word. In the case of love, words like *eros*, which means romantic love, *philos*, which means intimate friendship, *storge*, which means familial love, and *agape*, which means universal emphatic love, all exist. But we only have one word in English. So how do you translate it? If you just say "love" when you see one of the seven Greek words for love, you lose quite a bit of context. So since the very first translation, there has been a debate between a "word-for-word" translation, and a "sense-for-sense" translation. Both types of translations exist, and the debate over which kind is best continues today. \ Original question: Why do so many Christians insist on using the KJV, when it has multiple additions, changes, and subtractions in direct contradiction to Revelation 22:18,19?
