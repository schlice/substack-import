--- title: "Is it appropriate to tell someone who claims to believe in something without evidence that they do not truly believe in it?" date: "2024-11-06" tags: ["law"] layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} No, it's not appropriate, because they truly do believe in it. It's more appropriate to point out that they have *much more faith than you do*, because the gap between the facts and their belief (which is bridged by faith) is much wider for them than it is for you. Therefore, they must have more faith to believe in something which is supported by fewer facts. :::: ui_qtext_image_outer ::: qtext_image_wrapper ![](images/qimg-751b230ea612134b164f342ac7d224f2){.landscape .qtext_image .zoomable_in_feed master_src="images/qimg-751b230ea612134b164f342ac7d224f2" master_w="1097" master_h="485"} ::: :::: *(As you can see, when you have fewer facts, you need more faith to reach your belief)* For example, all of the best information we have tells us that the universe exploded into being out of nothing. The Law of Causality tells us that nothing begins to exist without a cause. Therefore, either **someone** caused something to come into existence out of nothing, or **no one** caused something to come into existence out of nothing. Which view is more reasonable? The view that **someone** created something out of nothing. Which view requires more faith to believe? The view that **no one** created something out of nothing.
