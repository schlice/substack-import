It has to do with the Colorado \"Safe Haven Law\" which states that any mother can leave any baby, up to 72 hours old, at any fire station or hospital, without fear of being prosecuted for abandonment, no questions asked. So long as the baby is unharmed, you\'re good to go, literally. [[Colorado Safe Haven Home Page](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=55d38a&url=http://www.coloradosafehaven.org/){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="coloradosafehaven.org"}]{.qlink_container} All 50 states have Safe Haven laws on the books. The rationale for the fire station is that fire department personnel are more heavily trained as paramedics and EMTs than police. In fact, 70% of calls that firefighters roll on are medical calls, not fires. Fire department rescue vehicles roll on all sorts of medical situations. In fact, some jurisdictions have ambulance services that have teamed up with fire departments and run joint ambulances. Most police departments do not have their own ambulance services, and while police are generally trained as medical first responders, they are not EMT or paramedic certified, while most firefighters are. (EDIT) Additionally, fire stations are a better choice for Safe Haven drop points because people feel much more comfortable giving their baby to a firefighter (who cannot arrest them) than to a police officer (who can, but shouldn't in this scenario). Making Safe Haven drop points be police stations might deter some people from using them, so the logical choice is the fire department.
