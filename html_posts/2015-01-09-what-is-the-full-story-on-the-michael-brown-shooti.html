Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** [Whether or not Officer Wilson was \"wrongfully\" acquitted is a matter of opinion. The full, and only, story is that a grand jury, in an abnormally-extended timeframe, very carefully considered the testimony of over 60 people involved with the case, as well as forensic evidence, and found that there was not enough evidence to indict him on murder charges. This is, technically, not a \"wrongful\" acquittal, since a grand jury, presumably doing its job to the best of its abilities, made a judgment. So to say that it was a \"wrongful\" acquittal is a matter of opinion, not of fact.\ \ This isn\'t to say that he didn\'t act in accordance with department policy or that he, perhaps, should have done something differently. That determination is up to the department, and they\'re not saying what they found, as they are under no obligation to release the results of any internal review into the incident. At this point, it makes no difference since Officer Wilson resigned from the department. What he did, and/or what he should have done, is now a matter best suited to in-service training for other officers.]{.rendered_qtext}
