**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} While it might be difficult to think about it, the answer is **yes**. This is something Christian believers understand as part of the doctrine of **predestination**. Predestination is the Christian doctrine that states that before the universe was created, God decided the eternal destiny of all rational creatures (angels and humans). God's choice to save certain people is called **election**. Predestination may not be the central theme of the Bible, but it is, in fact, biblical doctrine, not mere speculation. - [[Rev. 17:14](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=9cf28a&url=https://www.esv.org/verses/Rev.%252017%253A14/){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="esv.org"}]{.qlink_container} Predestination is a feature of the larger doctrine of *salvation by grace alone* (*sola gratia*), which states that it is God's power, wisdom, and righteousness that save us, not anything we do. That said, He predestines those He saves to salvation *through Christ* and His salvific work on the cross. Those who have been predestined, therefore, must still have faith in Christ (*sola fide, soli Christo*) in order to be saved. Therefore, you cannot simply say, "If God has predestined that I will be saved, then I don't have to believe or go to church." God determines both the **end** and the **means** by which you are saved. We might say that God is evil because He didn't choose to save everyone. But actually, considering the fact that we are all sinners, the correct question should be, "Why doesn't God send everyone to hell?" Unconditional election is the friend of sinners, not the enemy, because without it no one would be saved. I know it's a tough doctrine to wrestle with, but that doesn't make it unbiblical. It doesn't make it worthy of our ignorance, either. We should understand it and rejoice in it, because God has given us hope through Christ.
