Answer

**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} Police are not there to incriminate you, per se. They're nearly universally not there to falsely accuse you. But **police are there to find a person who fits the facts, arrest that person, and make it stick.** That's their job. Their job is not to believe you or not believe you. Their job is not to find the truth. Their job is to find a person who fits the facts they have and build a case against that person. If police are talking to you, it's because they are trying to find a person who fits the facts and build a case against them, and you might just be that person, despite what they tell you. To that end, ***police WILL use EVERYTHING you say against you, regardless of how innocuous you think it is.*** And the things you think are completely inconsequential could provide the police the reasonable suspicion they need to start looking in to you as a suspect. Even if you are completely innocent and you say nothing but the truth, you will always give the police something that they can use to help them convict you. Here's a little tidbit that may help you: you cannot give the police any information that would ***help*** you at trial. The Federal Rules of Evidence \[ยง801(d)(2)(a)\] explains what evidence can be used against you. It's interesting to note that your words can be used *against* you but never *for* you. In order to use what you said to the police *for* you, you'd have to put the police officer on the stand and have him relay the conversation (which benefits you), but he can't, because it's *hearsay*. It is for that reason alone that there is never benefit to you from talking to police. Even if you do tell the truth but the police have **bad evidence** or **unreliable evidence**, and it contradicts what you said (which happens to be the truth), they can use the contradiction to presume you are lying and incorrectly look at you as the suspect. Additionally, even if you are completely innocent, it is very difficult to tell the same story twice in exactly the same way, which may make you look like you are changing your story, and provide evidence to the police to look into you further. Professor James Duane was a former defense attorney turned law professor and is an outspoken voice on police interactions. When you ask about "don't ever talk to police," Professor Duane is always mentioned first. He has even written a book about it, titled [[*You Have the Right to Remain Innocent*](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=a75900&url=https://www.amazon.com/You-Have-Right-Remain-Innocent/dp/1503933393){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="amazon.com"}]{.qlink_container}. Here's a few videos of him if you want to learn more: [[https://youtu.be/-FENubmZGj8](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=1a433e&url=https://youtu.be/-FENubmZGj8){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);"}]{.qlink_container} [[https://youtu.be/d-7o9xYp7eE](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=d890f6&url=https://youtu.be/d-7o9xYp7eE){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);"}]{.qlink_container} One of the things Professor Duane points out in that second video (I know it's 45 minutes, but it's worth every second) is part of the opinion from *Ohio v. Reiner:* "\[O\]ne of the Fifth Amendment\'s basic functions is to protect innocent persons who might otherwise be ensnared by ambiguous circumstances. Truthful responses of an innocent witness, as well as those of a wrongdoer, **may provide the government with incriminating evidence from the speaker\'s own mouth**." **TL;DR:** The Fifth Amendment exists so that you can never be forced to be a witness against yourself. Don't be a willing witness against yourself. If the police ask you questions, don't talk to them.
