--- title: '"Given the current tensions between the NYPD and the citizens of NY, particularly the black community, would it make sense to have a referendum? Allow neighborhoods to decide if they want police on patrol?"' date: '2015-01-07' tags: - law-enforcement layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** [Police don\'t routinely just show up in neighborhoods with nothing to do. The days of random patrol are long since gone. Budget cuts mean fewer deputies on patrol, which means that we routinely have more calls for service than we have deputies to service those calls. There\'s not much we can do about that, it is what it is.\ \ What this means is that the number of police in a neighborhood is directly proportional to the number of calls for service those people place. If a neighborhood unilaterally decided they didn\'t want police presence in their neighborhood any longer, the process would be very simple: get everyone together and tell them never to call the police. Done. Police will never be back unless they are investigating something else, not responding to a call for service. Like I said, the days of random patrol and officers with long periods of downtime between calls is long gone.\ \ But they will then learn a very hard lesson: sometimes, the devil you know is better than the devil you don\'t. They think the police are bad, wait until the police are gone. The bad guys know the police are gone just as much as the good guys do. That neighborhood will degenerate into the worst hellhole of lawlessness ever. The police bring order; they don\'t cause chaos. That\'s putting the cart before the horse.\ \ Neighborhoods have another option: they can *incorporate* as a township or a city (if the size justifies it). Then they can hire their own police force, levy their own taxes, maintain their own roads, and essentially act autonomously (as long as they follow all laws for the community organizations above them: city, county, state, etc). That way, if they don\'t like the police force as it stands, they can make their own. They can\'t be discriminatory in hiring practices, but they can define the hiring practices for their own police force. If they could define some test to ensure someone isn\'t biased against black people, they could incorporate it into their hiring practices, assuming that they could do so in a completely non-discriminatory way. It\'s not likely that such a thing could happen, but I\'m throwing out the possibilities here.]{.rendered_qtext}
