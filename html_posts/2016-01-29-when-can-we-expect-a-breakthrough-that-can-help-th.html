**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} Thanks for the A2A. First, it\'s important to understand that we have made **major** progress in the treatment of this disease over the last roughly 40 years. Before then, we didn\'t have a way to slow down the disease at all. Medicine could only treat the symptoms of the disease, and not very effectively either. We\'ve known about MS and recognized symptoms of the disease for more than 300 years. Having the first real advances in treatment of the disease just happening within the last 30 years is astonishing. Starting about 25 years ago, some of the first MS treatments were introduced. They are a class of medications called ***immunomodulators***. Primarily, they modulate (or modify) the immune response using *interferons*. Interferons are naturally-occurring proteins secreted by cells in the immune system. These interferons are not well understood, but we understood them well enough to create medications which can *modulate* the immune system response. The early MS medicines (Avonex, Betaseron) are interferons that modulate how the immune system responds to nerve cells, slowing down their attacks on myelin. Other immunomodulators like Copaxone are synthetic proteins which mimic the protein structure of myelin. When immune cells bind to Copaxone\'s synthetic protein, they create anti-inflammatory cells rather than the inflammatory cells created when immune cells bind to myelin. A host of other MS medicines have been introduced, including the latest medicines, oral medications. Finally, something to treat MS, in a pill! While none of these are *cures*, we have progressed the medications such that a person diagnosed with MS today can expect roughly the same lifespan as someone without MS, and can also enjoy an acceptable quality of life, with some lifestyle modifications. Incidence of severe disability is low (around 10%), so most people with MS will continue being normal, active people. Now, on to specifically what you asked: what progress has been made towards a **cure**? Until we can identify a **cause**, we can\'t think about identifying a **cure**. We know epidemiologically that there is a hormonal component (as women get MS two to three times more frequently than men), there is a familial component (close first-degree relatives of someone with MS have a higher chance of developing the disease), there is a geographical component (living at higher than 40 degrees latitude poses a greater risk of developing the disease), and there is an ethnic component (whites of eastern European descent are most likely to get the disease). We know that vitamin D has prophylactic qualities, and studies have shown that many MS patients also have very low vitamin D levels, and generally cannot maintain normal vitamin D levels without supplementation. We also know that there is some infectious trigger that initiates the disease process in a person who is predisposed to getting the disease. Beyond that, we still do not know what defect causes the immune system to attack normal, healthy body cells. We have learned much more over the past 15 years about T cells, which are the primary aggressors against myelin. There are three broad categories of T cells: - Helper T cells which recognize foreign antigens and activate other T cells - Regulatory T cells which do the opposite, or \"turn off\" other immune functions - Cytotoxic or \"Killer\" T cells which attack and destroy antigenic cells. What we\'ve found over the last 15 years is interesting regarding the blood of MS patients when compared to healthy persons: - Decreased regulatory T cells in peripheral blood during an attack - Increased helper T cells in CSF (cerebrospinal fluid) - Increased activated T cells crossing the blood-brain barrier, which then attracts other immune cells - Increased T cell activity against myelin - T cells found in MS plaques (scars) on nerves So what does all of this mean for treatment and potentially a cure? The MS Society says the following (emphasis mine): > The abnormal immune process that is likely to be responsible for the > central nervous system demyelination in MS appears to involve > selective activation of helper T cells and killer T cells, with a > corresponding decrease in regulatory T cells. **These findings suggest > the rationale for therapies that target only specific T cells or T > cell receptors that are sensitized to myelin.** Some of these > approaches --- such as the use of monoclonal antibodies directed > against certain T cells --- have proven successful in treating animals > with experimental allergic encephalomyelitis (EAE), an animal model of > MS. Pilot clinical trials are in progress. **Future experimental > therapies may involve antibodies directed against the cytokines that > turn on the T cells or therapy designed to desensitize or inhibit T > cell activity.** > > While much more information is needed before the exact nature of the > immune response in MS is explained, it appears that T cells and their > cytokines are the keys to this process. **Ongoing research in these > areas may provide new, specific immunotherapies that will stop the > progression of MS, without harming any immune cells that are not > involved in the process of myelin destruction.** I am actually using one such drug \-- Gilenya. Gilenya works by trapping certain T cells within lymph nodes and not allowing them to leave and attack the nerve cells. Not all T cells are affected, but enough T cells are trapped in lymph nodes to slow down the progression of the disease. I think we are just a few years away from having therapies that directly target the progression of MS while leaving other immune cells untouched. We\'re very close today. So we are rapidly approaching a time where we can directly target the cause of the symptoms of the disease, even if we haven\'t yet found the actual cause of the immune-mediated response yet. I have said since the beginning, if I was to get MS at any time in history, this isn\'t the worst time to get it.
