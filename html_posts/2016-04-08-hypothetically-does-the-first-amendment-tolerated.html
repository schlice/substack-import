Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} The First Amendment says, as you said, \"Congress shall make no law respecting an establishment of religion, or prohibiting the free exercise thereof.\" This means the government cannot establish a state religion, nor can it prevent you from practicing whatever religion you choose. The First Amendment does **not** say that once you apply a uniform to your body, you can no longer practice a religion. The problem we run into is the unwritten \"separation of church and state\" that has become so popular over the last 60 years. The First Amendment simply says that the government can\'t endorse a religion, which means that the government itself can\'t write a law endorsing a religion. A military member (essentially an agent of the government) practicing a religion (essentially endorsing it) is not a violation of the First Amendment, because the very same statement which says that Congress can\'t make a state religion ***ALSO*** says that the government cannot prohibit the ***free exercise*** thereof. That means that if an agent of the government wants to practice a religion, the government is specifically enjoined from prohibiting that practice. That\'s why Mikey Weinstein\'s Freedom From Religion Foundation is so very dangerous. He claims that the First Amendment guarantees that you can be free ***from*** religion, not just be free to practice whatever religion you choose, or none at all. This implies that religion, and its free exercise, can actually be prohibited in certain places, namely anywhere where someone not of that religion is present. Therefore, the religious practice of \"proselytizing\" would be prohibited based on Weinstein\'s definition. However, the Constitution does not read this way, nor has it ever been interpreted in this way. The Constitution prohibits ***the government*** from making laws which endorse, or prohibit, religion, or its free exercise. Government agents, such as military members, may practice a religion, but they cannot write orders which \"order\" people to participate in a religion, or to practice any particular religion. Some commanders (military officers, not cadets) at the Academy would tell their cadets, \"I\'m participating in \ this Saturday. You are encouraged to go; you are not ordered to go.\" Weinstein\'s contention is that constitutes a *de facto* order to go because \"encouragement\" from a commander is tantamount to an unspoken order (despite the fact that the same commander, in the same sentence, said you are not ordered to go). His argument is that the military climate would lead itself to believe that the \"not ordered to go\" part would be ignored and the first part would be considered a de facto order. There is a religious culture at the Academy. And it is largely Protestant. Look at the Cadet Chapel (if there was ever an indication that military members could participate in religion without endorsing it, the very existence of the Cadet Chapel proves that). :::: ui_qtext_image_outer ::: qtext_image_wrapper ![](images/qimg-9941fea18b2ed8f4d29b86f5299fffce){.landscape .qtext_image .zoomable_in_feed master_src="images/qimg-9941fea18b2ed8f4d29b86f5299fffce" master_w="3264" master_h="2448"} ::: :::: The upstairs area, the grandest area in the Chapel, is the Protestant worship area, which holds 1200 people. Immediately below the upstairs doors (down the steps below ground level) is the Catholic Chapel, which holds 600 people. Behind the Catholic Chapel is the Jewish Chapel which can hold 100. We then have two All-Faiths Rooms which can be set up in any configuration. Currently, one of the rooms is set up for Buddhist worship. The desires of military members to practice their faith must be balanced with the desires of those who choose to not exercise any faith, as the Constitution allows. To that end, there can be no pressure to participate in any religion. There can be no reprisals for not participating in a religion. There can be no special treatment given to anyone of any particular faith, and there can be no negative consequences for failure to participate in any given faith. This leaves you free to talk about your religion. You can \"proselytize:\" tell people about the benefits of practicing your religion (none of which have anything to do with your military career). Most sane people can make this distinction in their minds quite easily. This is the rule, and is abundantly clear, at the Academy. And even when people try, wholeheartedly, to ensure this remains the order of the day, misinterpretations and the few who fail to follow orders cause the majority to look bad. *(Full disclosure: I was Catholic at the time I attended the Academy \[1990-1994\]. I am no longer Catholic; I am officially a non-denominational Christian. My roommate was Protestant. One of my roommates was Mormon. We\'re all still good friends.)*
