--- title: "How does the meaning of the life of a man differ from that of a fly?" date: "2015-04-06" tags: ["uncategorized"] layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** [The difference is consciousness.\ \ The fly exists fulfilling unthought primal urges. Eat. Avoid being eaten. Make baby flies. Repeat. The fly does not \"think,\" it just \"does.\" It therefore has no awareness of self. It is alive because of the definition of life. But it is not \"alive.\"\ \ The man has consciousness; he is aware of his existence. Because of this awareness, he now understands that he is \"alive,\" and that at some point in the future he will not be. Therefore, his life must derive some meaning beyond that of the fly, so he examines his life searching for meaning. He says, \"The unexamined life is not worth living.\" (Specifically, Socrates said that, but I digress). He therefore is the only creature that must define meaning from life. William Hazlitt (another man) said, \"Man is the only animal that laughs and weeps, for he is the only animal that is struck with the difference between what things are and what they ought to be.\" And George Eliot (yet another man) said, \"It is never too late to be who you might have been.\" Finally, John Ruskin (yep, another man) said, \"The highest reward for a man\'s toil is not what he gets for it, but what he becomes by it.\"\ \ These men sought to understand why they were alive, and somehow create, and thus derive, some meaning from their lives.]{.rendered_qtext}
