**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} The Supreme Court's power is already limited. The Constitution checks the Judicial branch: - The court does not have *original jurisdiction* in the vast majority of cases. It only has *appellate jurisdiction,* which means cases can be *appealed* to it, but cases cannot *originate* in it. There is one notable exception: cases involving disputes between two or more states, cases where a state is a party, and cases affecting ambassadors, other public ministers, and consuls. This means these cases are initially tried in the Supreme Court, not a lower court, and the Supreme Court\'s decision is final. In every other case, the case must be heard by a lower court and then appealed to the Supreme Court. The Legislative Branch checks the Judicial branch: - Justices on the Supreme Court are subject to impeachment, just as any other federal official is. The Legislative Branch can impeach a Justice. - Finally, the Legislative Branch can override a Supreme Court ruling by re-writing an offending law in such a way that it does not violate the Constitution. In that way, the law is still in effect, but it simply does not violate the Constitution. The Executive Branch also checks the Judicial branch: - The Executive Branch checks the Judicial Branch through its power to nominate judges, issue pardons, and enforce court decisions. The President nominates judges, including Supreme Court justices, but these nominations require confirmation by the Senate. The President also has the power to issue pardons for federal crimes, which can influence the outcome of judicial cases. Finally, the Executive Branch is responsible for enforcing court orders, which is essential for the judicial system to function effectively.
