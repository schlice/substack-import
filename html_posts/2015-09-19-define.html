**Content:** [I think it\'s a valid question. What exactly does it mean to infringe on a Constitutional right? Can you place reasonable restrictions on firearms without infringing one\'s right to keep and bear them?\ \ Let\'s look at another amendment to try to gain a better understanding of \"infringment.\" Can you shout \"Fire!\" in a crowded movie theater (assuming there is no fire)? Why not? If you possess freedom of speech, you should be able to say whatever you want, whenever you want. But you can\'t shout fire in a crowded movie theater. Interesting. Let\'s continue this example. Can you gather a few hundred people in the center of town and make a political speech? This is tailor-made for what the Amendment protects. But what if, by your speech, the crowd goes into a frenzy and destroys businesses and hurts people? You aren\'t rioting, you just exercised your First Amendment right to speak. Is this legal? Can the government legally prevent that speech without actually infringing on your right?\ \ I asked a lot of questions, but they\'re all rhetorical. You can\'t shout fire in a crowded theater. You can\'t whip a crowd into a frenzy with your speech. There are reasonable limits (such as public safety and preventing riots) that we can place on speech. The state has a vested interest in keeping people safe; indeed, the state is well authorized and remanded to do so. So reasonable restrictions on rights so that people remain safe are absolutely allowed.\ \ The question then falls to balancing the safety of the general public vs the free exercise of the right. Can you make a law that says if you carry a rifle in public, the chamber has to be empty? The answer is yes you can. Does this infringe on your right to keep and bear arms? Not appreciably. Does it keep people safe? Debatable. Nevertheless, because it accomplishes its goal without unduly infringing on your right to keep and bear arms, this is a reasonable law. Can you impose a 10,000% tax on ammunition? Does it keep people safe? Not really. Does it infringe on your right to keep and bear arms? Absolutely. The ammunition is considered part of the gun, and taxing at such a high rate is a clear attempt at circumventing the Amendment by using the taxation authority of the Congress. This, clearly, would be unconstitutional, and would infringe on your right to keep and bear arms.\ \ Each law must be evaluated individually looking at each of these criteria. The court has said that whatever laws are passed must further a compelling state interest, and must do so in the least intrusive way possible.]{.rendered_qtext}
