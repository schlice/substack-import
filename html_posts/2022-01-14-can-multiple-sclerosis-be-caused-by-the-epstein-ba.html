Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} EBV was investigated as a possible trigger for the MS disease process, as have many viruses, such as rabies, herpes simplex virus, measles, coronavirus, canine distemper virus, and HTLV-1, among others. None has yet been confirmed. Chlamydia pneumoniae, a bacteria which 90% of the population has been exposed to over their lifetime, has also been suggested but never proven. While we have yet to identify positively the virus, scientists still believe that some infectious trigger initiates the disease process. (EDIT) It's looking more and more like EBV is the infectious trigger that initiates the disease process. [[A study](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=d756f5&url=https://med.stanford.edu/news/all-news/2022/01/epstein-barr-virus-multiple-sclerosis.html){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="stanford.edu"}]{.qlink_container} done at Stanford University in 2022 proved that the Epstein-Barr virus triggers multiple sclerosis by priming the immune system to attack the body's own nervous system. > "This is the first time anyone has shown rather definitively that a > virus is the trigger for multiple sclerosis," \[co-author Lawrence\] > Steinman said. "And these exciting findings open up some new > directions for clinical trials in MS treatment." \ Original question: Can multiple sclerosis be caused by the Epstein-Barr virus (EBV)?
