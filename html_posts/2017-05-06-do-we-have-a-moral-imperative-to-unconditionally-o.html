**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} I do appreciate your question, because it discusses the morality of law. The question boils down to which came first: the morality or the law? To that end: what is morality? The [[book definition](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=e2c452&url=https://www.google.com/search?q%3Dmorality){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="google.com"}]{.qlink_container} is "principles concerning the distinction between right and wrong or good and bad behavior. A particular system of values and principles of conduct, especially one held by a specified person or society." From where do you get your morality? Depends on your frame of reference. If you believe in God, your morality probably stems from your beliefs. In fact, a recent report in *Psychology Today* concluded: \"The most significant predictor of a person\'s moral behavior may be religious commitment. People who consider themselves very religious were least likely to report deceiving their friends, having extramarital affairs, cheating on their expenses accounts, or even parking illegally.\" [\[1\]](#nUrje){#cite-nUrje .citation_link}Ultimately, what this means is that if you believe in God, it most likely has an effect on your thinking and your behavior. If you don't believe in a God, or in any form of higher power, your only moral code is the one your parents defined for you, or the one you create for yourself. In the United States, since we don't have defined or mandatory religious practices, you only have to follow the law, you are free to create whatever moral code you choose. However, since you are free to have your own moral code (and we don't have a dictatorship), whose moral code is right? Instead, we have laws. Laws, therefore, define a base level of morality (don't kill, don't steal, leave your neighbor alone and they will leave you alone). These are essentially "morals," but they are a minimum level of morality which we as a society have defined in order to live together. This was done over many hundreds of years; ours, in fact, come from old western European traditions: the Magna Carta, writings from Sir William Blackstone discussing "natural law," these things influenced the founders greatly when defining a basic set of laws founded on the morals of the time. Surprisingly, this set of basic morals codified into law has changed little across the more than 200 years in which it has been in place. By the way, this answers the optional question *what do you think are the values on which the modern western concept of justice is founded.* So now, with that framework, let's answer your questions. *Do we have a moral imperative to unconditionally observe certain fundamental human rights?* The short answer is no. In a free society, everyone is free to define their own moral code. ***You*** may have a moral imperative which tells you to observe some fundamental (to you) human rights, but do we as a society have an obligation to do the same? As a society, we are only obligated to follow the law (which, as we learned, is a function of morality, but is a base moral guideline for all citizens to follow). We are under no obligation to follow any other moral code other than our own. Your moral code may hold you to a higher standard, but even if your morality says killing is acceptable, the law does not. This is why the law defines a base level of morality that all citizens must follow in order to live peacefully together. Since I answered no, the next question is *what makes something acceptable vs. criminal?* As we have determined, our base morality was codified into law. Therefore, the only thing that makes something criminal is the law. Morality does not make criminality. Only law can make an act criminal. Criminality, therefore, is a legal definition, not a moral one. Hope this helps.
