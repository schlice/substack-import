**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} If a case came before the Court that posited a valid argument that *DC v. Heller* was incorrectly decided because the basis for its decision was unconstitutional, I would ***expect*** that it would be overturned. But that's unlikely, as we'll see here shortly. The problem, as I see it, is that **you expect bad rulings to stand,** ***because you like the outcome.*** You may not have liked the outcome of *Heller,* but it will stand because it is a Constitutional ruling. You probably wanted *Roe v. Wade* to stand, because you liked the outcome. But the problem with *Roe* was that it was a bad decision because it was unconstitutional. The foundation upon which *Roe* was built was not Constitutionally based. A right was found which, in actuality, does not exist in the Constitution. You can't "make up" a right that doesn't exist, and then use that as the foundation for a decision. A house built on sand will wash away. Not so with *DC v. Heller.* The foundations upon which that case were built are strong Constitutional ones. The reasons why the case was decided in the way that it was are clear, and are all found in the Constitution. The Court in *Heller* did not "make up" any rights and then build their decision on them. They used solid historical analysis to show that the Second Amendment protects an individual right to own firearms, unconnected with service in a militia. This interpretation was not *radical* or unsurprising, as it matches similar arms-bearing rights decided by the states, both before and after the passage of the Amendment. In order to overturn *Heller*, the Court would have to contravene many State Constitutions which also protect the right of an individual to keep and bear arms. Therefore, *Heller* is Constitutional. *Stare decisis* would say that because the decision was, in fact, Constitutional, it should stand. A house built on solid rock will withstand the storm.
