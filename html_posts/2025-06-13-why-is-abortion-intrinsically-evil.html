**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} Abortion remains one of the most contested moral and ethical dilemmas in modern society. To those of us who hold a pro-life stance, abortion is not simply a choice that a woman makes, it is the termination of an innocent human life. The act itself is inherently evil because it disregards the fundamental right to life of an unborn child. The moral argument against abortion is deeply rooted in the belief that life begins at conception. **Science confirms that a unique human being, with distinct DNA, is formed at fertilization.** This is well-known, and unsurprisingly, well-accepted scientifically, a fact that pro-choice advocates must ignore to make their case. From that moment, the fetus embarks on a journey of development, forming vital organs and systems necessary for survival. To terminate that process is to intentionally end a life that, given the chance, would develop into a fully realized human being. Moreover, the ethical consequences of abortion extend beyond the unborn child. Abortion undermines the sanctity of life, promoting a culture where convenience and personal autonomy overshadow responsibility and the protection of the most vulnerable members of society. *When life is treated as disposable, it sets a dangerous precedent for how society values human existence.* Religious and philosophical perspectives further reinforce the immorality of abortion. Many faith traditions uphold the belief that human life is sacred and should be preserved at all costs. While more religions than Christianity teach that abortion is immoral, they teach that every individual is created in the image of God, with purpose and value, and that the intentional destruction of life which God intentionally created. In addition to the ethical concerns, abortion carries significant emotional and psychological consequences for those involved, consequences that the pro-choice crowd also conveniently tries to gloss over, gaslight, and whitewash. Countless individuals experience regret, depression, and trauma after undergoing the procedure. Many organizations that support women post-abortion report cases of deep emotional suffering and lifelong struggles with guilt and grief. Ultimately, abortion presents a profound moral crisis. While arguments in favor of abortion often emphasize "bodily autonomy" and "reproductive rights," they fail to address the **fundamental injustice of terminating an innocent life.** If society is to champion human rights and dignity (and we argue that any truly moral society should), it must extend those protections to the unborn, recognizing abortion as an inherently evil act that strips the most vulnerable of their right to exist. Fundamentally, we believe that all humans have an equal right to life, and there is no calculus by which the unborn can be excluded from that right, because *it is our very humanity which gives us that right to life.* Society does not bestow a right to life. It is precisely because we are human that we possess an equal right to life. The earliest humans ***are still human***. They are also deserving of the protection of their right to life.
