--- title: "Is healthcare a fundamental human right? Should it be?" date: "2014-04-02" tags: ["law-enforcement"] layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** [We\'ve answered this in other similarly-worded questions, but the answer is the same:\ \ **Free** ***access*** **to healthcare is a basic human right; access to free** ***healthcare*** **is not.**\ \ If you can wrap your head around that statement, you can begin to understand the problem we face. Everyone has the right to ***access*** healthcare. Everyone does **not** have the right to ***free*** healthcare, because healthcare is not free. You may believe that it should be, but it is not. Healthcare costs money. It costs money to train doctors for many years with specialized training and skills before patients can be diagnosed. It costs money to protect doctors from lawsuits; a single malpractice lawsuit could cost a doctor millions of dollars in punitive damages, so insurance costs for doctors are very high, and that cost is passed on to patients. Creating safe, germ-free hospitals is not free. And doctors, who have received this specialized training and are licensed to practice it, can charge accordingly for their services, just as any other highly skilled professional. Ultimately, ***someone*** must pay for these services.\ \ You have to accept that healthcare costs money. You have to accept that doctors can charge money for their services. And you also must accept that public funds simply cannot cover the costs of **all** healthcare for **all** people.\ \ In the United States, this country was founded on freedom. The government was set up in such a way as to allow everyone the equal opportunity to achieve whatever they chose to achieve. It was not set up to provide for any more than just the basic needs: sanitation, safety services (police, fire), power, water, etc. All other needs and wants were left to the people. We should definitely add healthcare into that list, but to what extent should public money be used to cover healthcare?\ \ The answer is fairly simple; accepting the answer is not. We need to separate our healthcare system into a public system for basic healthcare needs and a private system for more advanced healthcare needs. We should provide basic healthcare using public money, and advanced healthcare using money from individuals/employers/insurance companies. But if healthcare is a for-profit business, how do we get doctors to participate in the public healthcare system? Subsidy. Subsidize doctors as they attend medical school. Once they are done, they are required to serve in a neighborhood healthcare clinic for a period of time before moving to private practice if they choose. Some may choose to stay in the public clinic, some may not.\ \ The difference is that the public neighborhood clinics only provide basic healthcare services to taxpayers at no additional cost. Advanced healthcare must still be paid for by the individual, their employer, and/or insurance companies, not by taxpayers.]{.rendered_qtext}
