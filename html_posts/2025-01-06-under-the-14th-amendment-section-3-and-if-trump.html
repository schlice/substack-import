**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} Hmm. 14th Amendment, Section 3: > *No person shall be a Senator or Representative in Congress, or > elector of President and Vice President, or hold any office, civil or > military, under the United States, or under any State, who, having > previously taken an oath, as a member of Congress, or as an officer of > the United States, or as a member of any State legislature, or as an > executive or judicial officer of any State, to support the > Constitution of the United States, shall have engaged in insurrection > or rebellion against the same, or given aid or comfort to the enemies > thereof. But Congress may, by a vote of two-thirds of each House, > remove such disability.* So let's take a look at how we got here (the point where we needed a 14th Amendment). First, we need to understand that the 13th, the 14th, and the 15th Amendments are what we call the ***Reconstruction Amendments***. They were passed after the Civil War to "reconstruct" the United States and to right some of the wrongs perpetrated by the Confederate states. > Soon after losing the Civil War in 1865, those states began to send > unrepentant former Confederates (such as the Confederacy\'s former > vice president, Alexander H. Stephens) to Washington as senators and > representatives. Congress refused to seat them and drafted Section 3 > (sometimes called the Insurrection Clause or Disqualification Clause) > to constitutionally bar from federal or state office anyone who, > having taken an oath as a public official to support the Constitution, > \"engaged in insurrection or rebellion\" against that document or lent > \"aid or comfort\" to its enemies. Congress can later remove a > disqualification by a two-thirds majority of each house. Southerners > strongly opposed the clause, arguing that it would hurt national > reunification. > > Section 3 does not specify how it is to be invoked, but Section 5 says > Congress has enforcement power. Accordingly, Congress enforced Section > 3 by enacting Sections 14 and 15 of the Enforcement Act of 1870, the > pertinent portion of which was repealed in 1948; there is still a > current federal statute (18 U.S.C. ยง 2383) that was initially part of > the Confiscation Act of 1862 (and revised in 1948), disqualifying > insurrectionists from any federal office. Based on precedent, Congress > would need only a simple majority from both houses to impose a > disqualification. Additionally, Congress can impeach a federal > officeholder, as it did to disqualify federal judge West Humphreys for > insurrection before the adoption of the Fourteenth Amendment. In Trump > v. Anderson (2024), the Supreme Court held that Congress determines > eligibility under Section 3 for federal officeholders and states may > only bar candidates from state office.[\[1\]](#qAdrW){#cite-qAdrW > .citation_link} So as you can see, it is Congress which determines whether or not someone is to be disqualified under Section 3, and a simple majority from both houses would be all you would need to disqualify a President as ineligible to serve. And the case has already been to the Supreme Court in *Trump v. Anderson* (2024), and it was specifically about Trump being removed from state ballots because he was being labeled as someone who was not qualified under Section 3. SCOTUS said that Congress determines eligibility for federal officeholders, and states may only bar candidates from state office. So therefore, if Congress moves to disqualify the President, then he is disqualified. If they do not, then he is not. It's really that simple. You are certainly free to hold the *opinion* that he is ineligible under Section 3, but until Congress disqualifies him, then he remains qualified. Your opinion does not make him ineligible or an "unconstitutional President." Your opinion is your own. Congress is the only authorized body which is able to make that determination, and since everyone (including the President) is **innocent until proven guilty**, he will have to actually be disqualified by Congress before he is actually ineligible to serve. \ But let's say you get your wish and Trump is disqualified under Section 3 of the 14th Amendment. What then what would happen? You'd have President JD Vance for the next four years, and he will be able to **appoint his own Vice President**. Is that the configuration you're hoping for? Because that's what you'll get. Be careful what you wish for.
