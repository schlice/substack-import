--- title: '"What are the limitations of a Homeowners Association (HOA) in terms of dictating who can live in a home and how many people can live there?"' date: '2024-05-27' tags: - law - homeowners-assn layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} Usually, the CC&R must follow Federal and state law when it comes to housing. Most "limitations" that an HOA could place in its CC&Rs run afoul of the Fair Housing Act, which prohibits discrimination based on "familial status." This status is limited to families with minor children. A numerical occupancy limitation, the effect of which would prohibit a husband and wife with three children from residing in a two bedroom home, would therefore violate the Fair Housing Act, and constitute discrimination based on familial status. However, it does not implicate "family" relations of individuals who are 18 or older, as is the case in age-qualified communities. That said, the Act doesn't really go into detail about how many people comprise a "single family" when they reside in "single family housing." If an HOA is going to have numerical limits in its CC&Rs, they must do so carefully to avoid butting up against Federal housing law. If your Association has a numerical limit you think violates Federal law, consult with a real estate attorney who specializes in HOA law.
