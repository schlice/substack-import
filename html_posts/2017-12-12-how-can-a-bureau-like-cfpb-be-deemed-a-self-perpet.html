--- title: "How can a bureau like CFPB be deemed a self perpetuating leadership by having its leader appoint his successor? Isn\" date: "2017-12-12" tags: ["law"] layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} The problem with CFPB comes with the interpretation of the Vacancies Reform Act, and its text which states that it is the \"exclusive means\" for authorizing the appointment of an acting head unless another statute expressly designates a particular officer to serve in that acting capacity. The legal battle is over the wording "exclusive." The administration argues that the authors of the law would not have said that the VRA is "exclusive" if it wasn't meant to be the overriding law, despite other statutes. A statute can't be both "exclusive" and "subservient to another law." The opposition states that where the VRA says "another statute expressly designating a particular officer" means that Dodd-Frank (which established CFPB and its chain of succession) is that "other statute," so VRA doesn't apply. The administration says that if other laws are to take precedent, then the VRA would have been worded to say, "Any other law that names a succession takes precedence. In the absence of such law, the VRA can be used." However, it does not say that; it says that it is the "exclusive means" for appointing an acting head. The word "exclusive" is the one that the administration claims makes it supersede any other law. So far, the courts have sided with the administration, refusing to institute an injunction against the president installing an interim head of the CFPB until a permanent head can be designated and make it through Senate confirmation hearings.
