We girls just can't catch a break, can > we?[\[1\]](#jHntf){#cite-jHntf .citation_link} For most of that history, we have not had any medications to treat MS. We could treat the symptoms as they occurred, but we could not effectively treat the disease itself. Then in the late 1980s, while researching interferons for use in cancer patients (which they are today), it was discovered that they also slowed the progression of MS. Thus, in 1993, interferon beta-1b was approved to treat MS (marketed in the US as Betaseron). It was quickly followed by interferon beta-1a (marketed as Avonex) and glatiramer acetate (a synthetic protein, marketed as Copaxone). These three drugs were universally known as the "ABC" drugs (Avonex, Betaseron, and Copaxone) for MS. These drugs were the first drugs to actually treat the underlying disease process. Since we don't know what causes it, we still don't have a cure for it, so these drugs do not **cure** MS. They do several things: - Slow the disease process so that it does not progress as rapidly. - Reduces the duration and severity of relapses - Elongates the amount of time between relapses The following drugs have been approved by the FDA and have been found through clinical trials to reduce the number of relapses, delay progression of disability, and limit new disease activity (as seen on MRI).
