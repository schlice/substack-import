**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **Is there any legal retribution or recourse for victims of over-zealous prosecutorial misconduct such as perjury traps, threats against family members and entrapment?** These things are not "prosecutorial misconduct." *Webster\'s New World Law Dictionary* defines prosecutorial misconduct as \"an illegal act or failing to act, on the part of a prosecutor, especially an attempt to sway the jury to wrongly convict a defendant or to impose a harsher than appropriate punishment.\"[\[1\]](#YXUpF){#cite-YXUpF .citation_link} Let's start with "perjury traps," the phenomenon where an unscrupulous lawyer tricks you into committing perjury. Here's an example of a perjury trap: Let's say a lawyer knows you committed a crime, but the statute of limitations expired on that crime, so you can't be prosecuted. Let's say the lawyer asks you under oath about that crime. If you lie about it, that's a new crime (perjury). Tricking someone into lying is still someone lying. No US federal court has ever dismissed a case because someone claimed a perjury trap. You weren't tricked into lying; you lied. Period. If the prosecutor told you that you can still be prosecuted for that crime when he knew that you cannot, that may be considered *entrapment by estoppel,* which we'll get into a little bit later in this answer. Threats against family members are exactly that: threats. If someone threatens your life or your safety, that's a threat. Call the police. It's also unlikely that a prosecutor can get someone to commit entrapment, which is enticing someone to commit a crime who would not otherwise have committed a crime. The *only* charge you may be able to make is *entrapment by estoppel*, which is a defense which \"applies when, acting with actual or apparent authority, a government official affirmatively assures the defendant that certain conduct is legal and the defendant reasonably believes that official\"[\[2\]](#VXJKK){#cite-VXJKK .citation_link}. This was first asserted in *Raley v. Ohio*, where a defendant was told they could reasonably plead the 5th. However, in Ohio, because they were already provided immunity deals, the 5th didn't apply. When they pleaded the 5th, they were then prosecuted for failing to answer questions. The convictions were overturned on appeal under the grounds of entrapment by estoppel. If you think you have a case for entrapment by estoppel, you must appeal your conviction. Prosecutors who engage in this sort of misconduct can be disbarred.
