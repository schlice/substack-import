--- title: '"An aircraft flying over land at its maximum altitude passes over a cliff where the land drops away several thousand feet, effectively increasing the altitude. Given the aircraft was at its maximum altitude, what would the effect be?"' date: '2014-11-24' tags: - uncategorized layout: post ---

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** [[[Katie Shackelford](https://www.quora.com/profile/Katie-Shackelford)]{.qlink_container} beat me to it.\ \ There are several altimeters in an aircraft, but they measure two different heights:\ \ AGL: Above Ground Level, the distance above the ground. When an aircraft is on the ground, this is zero.\ \ ASL: Above Sea Level: This is the standard that all aircraft use when flying, as there must be a consistent baseline for all aircraft to follow. Most aircraft use a \"pressure altimeter\" which uses barometric pressure to calculate altitude. When ascending through 18,000 feet ASL, aircraft set their altimeters to the universal standard 29.92 inHg. When descending through 18,000 feet, aircraft are advised of the local barometric pressure to which to set the altimeter. Aircraft will also use GPS as a source of altitude, but it is not the primary altimeter. The pressure altimeter is more accurate, as long as it is set based on local conditions. When an aircraft is on the ground, this altimeter will read the height of the aircraft above sea level.\ \ For instance, my favorite airfield (the airstrip at USAFA) is at 6565\' above sea level. When the altimeter is set correctly, it reads 6565 when on the ground. It\'s one of the settings you have to make when prepping your aircraft to fly.]{.rendered_qtext}
