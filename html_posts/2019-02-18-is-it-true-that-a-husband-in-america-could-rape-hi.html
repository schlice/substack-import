**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **Is it true that a husband in America could rape his wife without penalty from the law?** Absolutely not. [[Marital rape](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=80de82&url=https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marital_rape_(United_States_law)){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="wikipedia.org"}]{.qlink_container} (also known as spousal rape) is illegal in all 50 states. It does have a very long history, and for many parts of that history, it was *not* illegal in all 50 states. The idea stems back to 17th century England. Writings by Sir Matthew Hale indicate some of the foundation for this idea that husbands are insulated from rape charges by the "marital contract." He [[wrote](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=104071&url=https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Historia_Placitorum_Coron%25C3%25A6){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="wikipedia.org"}]{.qlink_container} that a \"husband cannot be guilty of a rape committed by himself upon his lawful wife, for by their mutual matrimonial consent and contract the wife hath given up herself in this kind to her husband which she cannot retract.\" The [[Model Penal Code](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=b93273&url=https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Model_Penal_Code){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="wikipedia.org"}]{.qlink_container} retained that idea, defining rape as a crime committed when "a male \[...\] has sexual intercourse with a female **not his wife**." Staring around the mid 1970s (following the womens' rights movements in the United States), the view of spousal rape began to change. In 1984, the New York State Court of Appeals heard the case of [[*People v. Liberta*](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=d0ee7a&url=https://www.law.cornell.edu/women-and-justice/resource/people_v_liberta){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="cornell.edu"}]{.qlink_container}, and in it, judge [[Sol Wachtler](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=00ba5b&url=https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sol_Wachtler){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="wikipedia.org"}]{.qlink_container} stated that \"a marriage license should not be viewed as a license for a husband to forcibly rape his wife with impunity. A married woman has the same right to control her own body as does an unmarried woman.\" Beginning in the mid 1970s, states started removing spouse exceptions from their rape laws, and by 1993, marital rape (or spousal rape) was a crime in all 50 states.
