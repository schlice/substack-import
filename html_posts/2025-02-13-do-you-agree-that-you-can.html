**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} As always, with every legal question, the answer is "it depends." In the United States, Title II of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 provides protection based on race, color, religion, or national origin, but it doesn\'t explicitly include sex, gender identity, or sexual orientation. Because there is no national standard with respect to sex discrimination in public accommodations, businesses must rely on state statutes in the majority of cases. State rules about sex-separated private venues vary depending upon what each state considers a "public accommodation." Many cities and states have laws which protect gender, as well as some states which protect gender identity, in a place of public accommodation (like libraries, movie theaters, shops, and other businesses which are open to the public). But those laws vary widely by state. And in most cases, private clubs, which are not open to the general public, are not considered places of public accommodation, and are usually exempt from public accommodation laws.
