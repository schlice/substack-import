**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} This is an excellent question. We can rephrase it to a more basic question: ***Is salvation a license to sin?*** The short answer is **no**. I think intuitively we probably knew that. To understand the longer answer, we need to understand what happens when a person is "saved." When a person is truly saved, two things must happen: they must **repent** of their sin, and they must **accept** Christ as their lord and savior. - **Repentance** means a "turning away" from your past life and your past sinful acts. Think of "turning your back on" your past life. The language about repentance is very figurative and descriptive, especially when it talks about "dying to your sins." Your old life is *over*. The old you **dies**. - **Acceptance** means recognizing you are a sinner, you will always be a sinner (human nature being what it is), and you have **zero hope of salvation** without Christ. Romans 6 (the whole chapter) discusses this concept. It is a *really great read*, and I would recommend you read the New International Version, as it is easy for the average English-speaking person today to understand: [[https://www.biblegateway.com/pas\...](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=a029b5&url=https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search%3DRomans%25206%26version%3DNIV){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="biblegateway.com"}]{.qlink_container} Just look at verse 1: "What shall we say, then? Shall we go on sinning so that grace may increase?" What a fascinating thought! I mean, if some grace in forgiving my sins is good, should I keep sinning so that I get *more grace*? Verse 2 gives you the answer: "By no means! We are those who have died to sin; how can we live in it any longer?" **By no means!** Because we *died to sin*. Our old life is **dead**. We are reborn as new people in Christ. And now, the big question: ***Does this mean that once we are saved, we automatically never sin again?*** **No.** We are still human. Salvation doesn't change our human nature. We are still sinful by nature. Salvation instead assures that Christ *paid for my sin with his death and resurrection, past, present, and future.* I certainly don't live in my old ways any more, but I still sin. I sin in thought and I sin in action. I still get angry with that stupid driver doing 15 miles an hour below the speed limit on the Interstate, or the oblivious self-obsessed shopper blocking the entire aisle with her cart. It happens. But I try to stop it. Instead of snapping to judgement, I try instead to pray for that person, that whatever is going on in their lives can be handed over to God, as I did, so that they can experience the fullness of God's grace, as I have. Instead of thinking "What a Karen," I try to actively think, "Does she even know how much God loves her?" It takes work, but you can improve on your sinful nature. Even so, I still go to God and ask forgiveness for the sins I committed in thought and deed. I am still human, and I recognize that I still, daily, need Christ's healing and power to transform lives. I'm being transformed a little bit at a time, in a process called *sanctification*. Salvation was a one-time, instantaneous action, while *sanctification* is the process by which we, through faithful study and prayer, are transformed into the person God wants us to be. It is normally a lifelong process, but the results get more and more beautiful every day. :::: ui_qtext_image_outer ::: qtext_image_wrapper ![](images/qimg-80f3adcec5721a2596f9ddc675a9190f){.landscape .qtext_image .zoomable_in_feed master_src="images/qimg-80f3adcec5721a2596f9ddc675a9190f" master_w="321" master_h="306"} ::: :::: I'm still a work in progress.
