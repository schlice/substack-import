**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} > *Question Details:* > > *I live in a subdivision that has a home owners association.\ > I found out after thousands of dollars on the pool and equipment, > excavator, and skid steer rental, sand delivery and about 80% > completion that I can not have an above ground pool in my own fenced > in back yard.\ > The president of the association stopped by on the day I started to > level my yard and was about 80% done with digging and leveling the > area to explain to me that it is written in the by-laws that home > owners are not allowed to have above ground pools in this > subdivision.* > > *He explained to me that it was written into the by-laws by the land > developer and had no clue of why it was not allowed, I then petitioned > the HOA and submitted a detailed plan of what my intentions were for > the pool. After 4 weeks I received am E-mail explaining that my plan > had been denied because of the written by-law. When asked again of > why? The answer was because the land builder had it written into the > by laws.* > > *I have only been here since August so I have not read the complete > list of the 320 page by-laws. So it is definitely my fault for not > looking into, and reviewing the by-laws but what can I do to over turn > this stupid rule?\ > Or what are they legally capable of doing to me if I throw the pool up > anyway?* > > *Thank you in advance for your serious feedback.* I hate to be the bearer of bad news... It appears that you're not in a very good position. It was your responsibility to know the HOA rules before you started work. You even could have asked the HOA for clarification. If you proceed with the installation, the HOA can fine you for every day you are not in compliance. If you don't pay the fines, the HOA will turn your account over to their attorney and they will continue to fine you, apply late fees, and now attorney's fees, to your balance. Eventually, they will place a lien on your house for the unpaid fines and fees. And eventually after that, if your account is delinquent for long enough, they can foreclose on your lien, selling your house to pay for the fines. You really have two options: 1. Restore the grass and landscape back to the way it was. And then move. 2. Retain a real estate attorney in your area who specializes in HOA law and challenge the legality of the Bylaws, or the clause in the Bylaws which you are in violation of. **NOTE:** There is one notable exception, and it is *very new and very hot off the presses.* If you happen to live in Florida, you just dodged a bullet. Florida Statute 720.3045 was just passed by the Legislature and signed by Governor DeSantis. It reads, in part: > Regardless of any covenants, restrictions, bylaws, rules, or > requirements of an association, and unless prohibited by general law > or local ordinance, an association may not restrict parcel owners or > their tenants from installing, displaying, or storing any items on a > parcel which are not visible from the parcel\'s frontage or an > adjacent parcel, including, but not limited to, artificial turf, > boats, flags, and recreational vehicles. So if it is not illegal to install a pool in your town or city, the HOA cannot prohibit you from installing a pool so long as it is not visible from the front of your house, or from a neighbor's property.
