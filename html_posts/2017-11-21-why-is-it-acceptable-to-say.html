**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **Why is it acceptable to say \"teach men not to rape\", but bigoted to say \"teach Muslims not to commit terrorism\"?** It's not acceptable to do either. I think it's clear why it is bigoted to say "teach Muslims not to commit terrorism." Terrorism is not unique to Muslims simply because there are some Muslim groups which espouse the use of terrorism. Timothy McVeigh was not a Muslim, and yet he committed the largest act of domestic terrorism the United States had seen until September 11th. But it's also not acceptable to say that we "teach men not to rape," for several reasons: 1. *Women can be rapists, too.* While original legal wording of rape crimes made it a crime that only a man could commit against a woman, more nuanced and modern understandings of the crime understand that male stimulation is not a sign of consent. Women can, without consent, stimulate and rape men, and they have. 2. Why is it appropriate to teach men not to rape, but not to teach them to not commit other crimes? The answer is that *we don't teach people to not commit crime. We teach people to understand the difference between right and wrong, and act accordingly*. That's how people are able to navigate the world, and not go around committing crimes just because they haven't been taught specifically not to commit that crime.
