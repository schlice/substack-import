**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **Are multiple sclerosis patients more vulnerable to progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy?** Let me see if I can provide an updated answer. As everyone has already said, MS by itself does not increase your risk of contracting PML. However, certain medications commonly prescribed for MS can increase your risk of developing PML. So there are two factors involved in a person with MS developing PML: 1. Taking certain MS medications which are immunosuppressive 2. Having been exposed to the [[JC Virus](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=6a2af9&url=https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/JC_virus){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="wikipedia.org"}]{.qlink_container} and thus being "JC Positive." Currently, [[Tysabri](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=c18f05&url=http://www.tysabri.com){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="tysabri.com"}]{.qlink_container} is the primary medication which has been correlated with an increased risk of contracting PML. In fact, Tysabri was pulled from the market when it was directly related to three deaths from PML. Tysabri was re-released with a [[black box warning](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=fa2f55&url=https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boxed_warning){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="wikipedia.org"}]{.qlink_container} about PML and Tysabri. Continued study found that there was a much higher risk of developing PML when you had multiple MS medications in your body at the same time. So if you are going on to, or leaving, Tysabri as a therapy, you will find that they will force you to be medication free for at least four weeks (and possibly more) before you can start Tysabri, or before you can start a new medication. However, you can only develop PML as a result of the JC Virus, so if you are JC negative, the Tysabri restrictions will not apply to you. You can be sure that if you start or while you're on Tysabri, your doctor will monitor you very closely for any signs of the JC virus. Interestingly, Biogen (the maker of Tysabri) universally pays for JC virus testing. In fact, they encourage it. If you're JC positive, you can still take Tysabri, but the therapy may be cut short since the risk of developing PML increases over time. This is something you'll want to work out with your neurologist, and determine the amount of risk that applies to you in your given situation. I'll tell you from personal experience that Tysabri worked better than anything I've ever been on. In fact, it worked so well that my body was able to heal some of my lesions. I've only had lesions shrink while I was on Tysabri. Indeed, the efficiacy of Tysabri is higher than any other MS medication out there. Risk of PML has also been shown with [[Gilenya](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=4825b8&url=http://www.gilenya.com){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="gilenya.com"}]{.qlink_container}, but not as tightly correlated as with Tysabri. In 2012, one case of a person taking Gilenya developing PML was reported, but this was classified as a "carryover" from previous Tysabri treatment. In 2015, three cases of Gilenya patients contracting PML were reported who were not previously treated with Tysabri.
