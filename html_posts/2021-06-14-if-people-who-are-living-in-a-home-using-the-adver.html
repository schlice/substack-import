**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} Side-step the entire process and file for eviction. So long as adverse possession hasn't kicked in yet (as [[Cliff G.](https://www.quora.com/profile/Cliff-G-13)]{.qlink_container} mentioned, it takes upwards of 7 years, and you indicated that someone is living in the house for only around 2 years), you or your fiancee are still on the deed, so you can begin eviction procedures. (EDIT) You commented on Cliff's answer and said something about Phoenix, so I'll presume you are in Arizona. Arizona has one of the shortest adverse possession claims periods, requiring the adverse possessor to continuously possess the property for two years before they can make a right of possession adverse possession claim. Also, while you have a cause of action for trespass, the statute of limitations for trespass is also two years. You must act quickly or adverse possession may kick in, and they may be able to file for right of possession by adverse possession. If you lose your adverse possession claim, investigate the money order fraud. If they fraudulently paid a government agency using fraudulently obtained money orders through misrepresentation, contact the police and investigate that. It's a long shot, but perhaps the tax payments can be invalidated along with the adverse possession. While I understand your issues, if possession has been continuous by the adverse possessor for two years or more, you may still be able to regain possession of the property before they file for right of possession, but you must seek the advice of an attorney. Contact the state bar association to help you find an attorney who will help you.
