**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} In the vast majority of cases in the United States, you can film anyone in public, including the police. The general rule of thumb is, "If you can see it, you can film it." Specific to the police, if they are in public and so are you, film away. But be respectful: stay back from active police activity. Never interfere with what an officer is doing while you record. Don't be one of "those people" who shove their phones in police officers' faces. That's just a douche-y move. Legislation has been introduced in some states to enforce sensible distance requirements for those filming, but it has yet to gain any real traction. Just be a human while you record, and you can fill up your phone's memory all you like. Also, obey police orders. Don't try to argue Constitutional protections with a police officer performing his or her duties. If an officer tells you to move, you move. If they tell you to back up to the other side of the street, you do it. If they tell you to stop recording, you do it. If you think you just recorded a police officer trampling on your rights, you should run, don't walk, to your nearest civil rights attorney or ACLU office and show them the video.
