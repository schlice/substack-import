Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} Let's answer each specific question. *Is this protected speech?* This is most definitely protected First Amendment speech. ***That said***, the department is allowed to regulate how officers appear in public. Most departments (I found to my surprise\*) allow visible tattoos while in uniform. Most departments do specify that the tattoos may not be offensive. While it's clear that this tattoo is not offensive, I can see how it may be misinterpreted. However, this is a problem of the department's own making. By allowing visible tattoos, it's clear that sooner or later they'd run into a problem like this. *Should be be fired?* Not only should he **not** be fired, but it would be stupid for them to do so. Since (as we established already) the tattoo is protected First Amendment speech, firing him or otherwise disciplining him over it would be extremely problematic because not only is it protected by 1A, but it *doesn't actually violate department policy.* *What if he had the tattoo removed?* Even *asking* him (as opposed to ordering him) to remove it would be problematic. As you may know, removing tattoos is a painful process requiring laser surgery. Since the department is requiring the surgery for continued employment, does that make the tattoo removal surgery payable under the department's health plan? If he has to take time off to recover, is that covered under Worker's Comp? \ \* When I was Active Duty in the US Air Force, uniform regulations required that tattoos be not visible while in uniform. Therefore, shoulder and torso tattoos were very popular. I hear that this regulation may also have changed as well...
