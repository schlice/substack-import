**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **How has treatment for Multiple sclerosis changed over the past 10 years?** Treatments for MS have changed more in the last 10 (and even 20) years than they have in the previous 200 years. The disease itself was officially identified and given a name in 1868, but its existence was documented long before that. St. Liduina of Schiedam is said to have had MS[\[1\]](#MMZbT){#cite-MMZbT .citation_link}, and this was in 1396. Her symptoms were documented in her biography, written after her death in 1433, and include relapses and remissions. Her bones were discovered in the 1970s and show signs of limited mobility in her right arm and her legs, but whether or not it was actually MS is debatable. From that point through the late 20th century, treatment of MS was limited to management of the symptoms. However, the treatment options literally exploded in the 1990s. The 90s are known as the "drug explosion" decade.[\[2\]](#fIfGf){#cite-fIfGf .citation_link} Interferon, an injectable drug to treat cancer, was approved for the treatment of MS in the early and mid 90s. For the first time since the 1800s, we could actually *alter the course* of the disease by actually addressing the disease itself, and not just its symptoms. We were no longer slaves to symptoms. This was **revolutionary** in the treatment of the disease. Since then, our treatments have become more specific, more targeted, and more effective. Our medicines have continued to improve efficacy, and routes of administration have improved from injection-only therapies to infusion and most recently, oral administration. Since the 90s, our treatments have been "evolutionary" instead of "revolutionary." We continue to improve our treatments so that they more effectively slow down the progression of the disease, reduce the severity and duration of relapses, and increase the amount of time between relapses. I have often said that there is no better time in history to get MS than right now. I knew of people with MS as a child in the 80s, and it wasn't pretty. It was going to progress, and there was nothing we could do to stop or slow down the juggernaut that was MS, *but there is today*. We've also made great strides in understanding the disease and its progression, and our diagnostic tools have improved immensely, but that elusive "cause" is still missing. However, our therapies have improved so much that lifespans for people with MS, who take an approved therapy drug, have nearly matched lifespans of those without MS.
