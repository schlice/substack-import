Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} TL;DR: I wouldn't go so far as to say you have a "right" to know exactly what the police are doing exactly when you believe you should know. The information is releasable; the timeframe in which a department releases the information is up to them. You can find out what calls the police were responding to at any given time. Most departments make public the "police blotter" which is a record of all of the calls which officers were dispatched to handle. One of our local departments' PIO (Public Information Office) posts blotter entries to their public Twitter feed (ah, technology...), usually within an hour or so of their entry. "Newsworthy" entries (i.e. shooting downtown) or entries which should be disseminated in the interest of public safety (i.e. crash on Main Street, please try to avoid the area) are released very quickly. Another local agency has an interactive online blotter which allows you to input a timeframe and it will show all blotter entries during that period. Not all departments do this, nor are they under any legal obligation to do so. For departments which do not publicly post their blotter entries, you can always call the dispatcher and ask. You might get an answer. Then again, you might get a generic "we can't disclose information about active incidents" response. In such a case, after the conclusion of the incident, you can file a FOIA (Freedom of Information Act) request since the police department is a government agency. You can request information about calls which were answered in your neighborhood during the timeframe in question. So long as there is not an open active investigation, the department will provide you with that information. The upshot of all of this is that the information is available. The timeframe in which you can receive this information is different for each organization.
