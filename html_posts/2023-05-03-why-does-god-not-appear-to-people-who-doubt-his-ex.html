--- title: '"Why does God not appear to people who doubt His existence?"' date: '2023-05-03' tags: - theology layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} You mean *visually* appear? Like in front of you? Yeah, that's not going to happen because **that's not the kind of believer He's looking for.** Instead, He's looking for the believer who believes *despite* not seeing him. The trick is that God gave us **free will**. We can choose to love Him or reject Him, with no adverse effects in our lifetime. None. Nothing different will happen to you today or tomorrow or the next day, until the day you die, if you either love Him or reject Him. That's the core of our free will. You have to be able to make that choice freely, without coercion. Now let's think about this for a second. If God appeared to people who doubt His existence, **would they be stupid enough to doubt Him any more?** Well, a few might, but most would not. They would be *convinced* to believe in Him. They would be ***coerced*** into believing in Him. *And He doesn't want that.* But why? Why doesn't He just make everyone to automatically believe in Him? **Because the very purpose of this life is to choose Him** ***freely*, with no coercion.** > One beauty of God's creation is this: if you're not willing to accept > Christianity, then you're free to reject it. This freedom to make > choices---even the freedom to reject truth---is what makes us moral > creatures and enables each of us to choose our ultimate destiny. > > This really hits at the heart of why we exist at all, and why God > might not be as overt in revealing Himself to us as some would like. > For if the Bible is true, then God has provided each of us with the > opportunity to make an eternal choice to either accept Him or reject > Him. > > And in order to ensure that our choice is *truly* free, he puts us in > an environment that is filled with evidence of His existence, *but > without His direct presence*---a presence so powerful that it could > overwhelm our freedom and thus negate our ability to reject Him. In > other words, God has provided enough evidence in this life to convince > anyone *willing* to believe, yet he has also left some ambiguity so as > not to compel the *unwilling*. In this way, God gives us the > opportunity either to love Him or to reject Him without violating our > freedom. In fact, the purpose of this life is to make that choice > freely and without coercion. For love can only be freely given, it can > never be coerced. > > C. S. Lewis said it this way: "the Irresistible and the Indisputable > are the two weapons which the very nature of \[God's\] scheme forbids > Him to use. Merely to over-ride a human will (as His felt presence in > any but the faintest and most mitigated degree would certainly do) > would be for Him useless. He cannot ravish.
