**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **Which enumerated power in the constitution grants the Federal Government the right to pass the Justice in Policing Act? Many of those issues addressed seem to be powers relegated to the states.** The Justice in Policing Act only really crosses into state authority when it starts mandating things like arrest control techniques which can and cannot be used by police departments. That particular portion of the Act, if it passes, will be quickly overturned by the courts as an unconstitutional overreach. But there are parts of the Act that are good, and which need to be implemented right away. Let's review some of the proposed changes which are directly related to the current issue: - Reforming "qualified immunity." I have been calling for this for some time now, and it's a good first step at holding officers accountable for their actions. Qualified immunity needs to be either seriously reformed, or scrapped altogether. This would not remove Constitutional protections for officers, but would allow more civil rights lawsuits to proceed on their merits. Granted, this will increase litigation costs for departments (and ultimately increase taxpayer expense of departments), but it should be done. - Reform to the federal police misconduct statute. Currently, it is only a crime for an officer to "willfully" violate an individuals's constitutional rights, meaning prosecutors must prove an officer acted with the intention of depriving the person of their rights. The bill plans to lower the standard of criminal intent to "knowingly or with reckless disregard." This, in addition to reform of qualified immunity, will make it easier for the Federal government to seek redress against officers who act recklessly and violate civil rights. I fear for the unintended consequences of this, and police unions will argue that this will cause officers to not act and will cause more officers to be harmed and die. Some may find that acceptable. I'm certain that the nearly one million police officers in the country might not. - Reform to the federal standard for the use of force by officers from "reasonableness" to only when it is "necessary to prevent death or serious bodily injury." The bill would require that federal officers use de-escalation tactics and use deadly force only as a last resort. This is acceptable for Congress to do with Federal officers (FBI, BATFE, USCBP, etc), but they also plan on making this a condition of grants to state and local police agencies as well, which is also likely to face Constitutional pushback. - Ban Federal law enforcement use of chokeholds or other carotid holds, and condition Federal funding to states on them doing the same. Again, while this may work for Congress to impose this on Federal law enforcement, forcing this on to the states is again likely to face a Constitutional challenge. From here, the legislation goes on to address other things which are unrelated to the current situation, but are things that police reformers have wanted for a long time, so they'll try to use the current crisis to get passed (in the spirit of "never let a good crisis go to waste"), like: - Bans to "no-knock warrants." This one is also unlikely to succeed, as they are already governed by judges, and are useful when trying to prevent evidence from being destroyed and suspects from escaping. The resulting unintended consequences of this could be devastating, more so than where no-knock warrants intersect the Castle Doctrine. - Limit the transfer of military excess hardware to police departments. All this will do will be to force the police to buy the hardware new, increasing policing costs to taxpayers. It will also cause manufacturers of this type of equipment to develop "military-style" gear, and "police-style" gear, but that both accomplish the same thing. They will find the gear that is most acceptable to the public and will produce that, again at increased cost (because the police will be barred from buying excess military gear, and will have to buy the new "police-style" gear). But hey, it's not "military" gear, it's "police" gear, designed especially for the police. This is a whole discussion unto itself. Ultimately, all this does is increase the cost to cities and towns of operating a police department, and will hurt small agencies and small communities much more than large ones. - Direct state and local law enforcement agencies to undertake a new effort to end discrimination in their practices by conditioning federal funding on the adoption of policies and training to combat racial and other discriminatory profiling. This is also wholly unnecessary, as it's already been done after the last few high-profile incidents which were also branded as "institutional racism." But sure, let's do it again. - Set new requirements on the Justice Department to come up with and deliver new policing standards for state and local agencies and collect new data on police actions all over the country. Again, this one steps across the line, where the Justice Department seeks to make changes to how state and local law enforcement operates, and is unlikely to pass in its current form, and if it does, will again face swift Constitutional challenge, so long as those standards are voluntary and are advice to departments instead of mandates.
