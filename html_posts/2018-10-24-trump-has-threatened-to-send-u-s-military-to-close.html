Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **Trump has threatened to send U. S. military to close the southern border. Would this be a violation of The Posse Comitatus Act of 1878?** No. The Posse Comitatus Act says that the military cannot enforce state law. The military can indeed enforce Federal law and can defend the country in general. Border protection falls under the purview of the US government, not the state; therefore, it is not a violation of Posse Comitatus to enforce the nation's border. (EDIT) But if that's not enough, I give you The Constitution, Article IV, section 4: > The United States shall guarantee to every state in this union a > republican form of government, **and shall protect each of them > against invasion;** and on application of the legislature, or of the > executive (when the legislature cannot be convened) against domestic > violence. I'm pretty sure that it would be drop-dead easy to make the case that tens of thousands of migrants attempting to illegally cross the border would constitute an invasion. The US Armed Forces exist to defend the United States against all enemies, foreign and domestic, which is expressly authorized by the US Constitution. In fact, guarding the Southern border was the Army's primary peacetime mission until 1940, and no one ever declared that doing so was in violation of Posse Comitatus. The Border Patrol wasn't even formed until 1924, so claiming the intent of Posse Comitatus was to prevent US Army troops from guarding the border is nonsense.
