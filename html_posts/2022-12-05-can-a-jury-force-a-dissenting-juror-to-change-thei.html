--- title: '"Can a jury force a dissenting juror to change their mind?"' date: '2022-12-05' tags: - uncategorized layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} *Force?* No, a jury cannot *force* a dissenting juror to change their mind. They may attempt to *pressure* the juror to do so, often bemoaning the fact that if they continue to deliberate but continue on past the end of the day, they may end up being sequestered for the night (but more importantly, they have to come back the next day to keep going). The jury foreman should prevent juries from devolving into pressuring any one juror to change their mind. If the fact is that all of the jurors are confident in their votes, and none of the jurors have any questions about the facts as presented, then a non-unanimous decision means that the jury is *deadlocked.* The foreman should make sure that everyone's questions are answered, but should ensure that no one feels pressured into changing their votes. If the jury finds themselves hopelessly deadlocked, the jury is considered ***hung***. This leads to a **mistrial**. No decision is made. It is then up to the state to decide if they are going to re-try the case in front of another jury, or the plaintiff or the state may simply decide to not re-try the case and the case is dropped.
