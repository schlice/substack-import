--- title: '"What is the common law background of the Fourth Amendment?"' date: '2016-10-12' tags: - law layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} During the time of the American colonies, there were two legal practices which were highly disliked among the colonists: *general warrants* and *writs of assistance*. A *general warrant* is a warrant obtained by a law enforcement or government official which does not specify the location to be searched or the item to be seized. It gives officials broad authority to search persons or places as they choose, without any real description of what it was that needed to be seized. These were routinely granted by the Crown, to the distress of colonists. *Writs of assistance* were similar. During the same time period, the Crown began enforcing some provisions of the Navigation Acts by granting custom officers writs of assistance. Much like general warrants, writs of assistance not only allowed the holder to search anywhere at any time, but they were permanent, and even transferable. They expired only upon the death of the King. The Fourth Amendment sought to provide citizens of the United States with some sense of security in their persons and property, providing that the government needed to be specific in their requests for searches and seizures.
