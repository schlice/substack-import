**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} It's a challenge, but they manage to pull it off. In order to characterize the Bible as fiction, you have to ignore all of the fact in it. And there's tons of archaeological and historical fact in the Bible, that even honest atheists must admit to. There are accounts of real historical figures and real geological events and real historical locations which archaeologists have discovered. When questioned, they certainly wouldn't take issue with these, because they have been physically discovered. If they can see it, feel it, etc., then it's proven. There's only one problem, then. *God, necessarily, exists outside of the physical realm, and can't be seen or touched.* You're never going to find one of God's hairs or see one of God's footprints, because God is not made of material (if He exists, and if He created the universe, then He necessarily exists outside of the universe). So you're never going to be able to find material evidence of an immaterial God. In order to reject the Bible then, you only need to limit your definition of *evidence* to that which can be proven by the Scientific Method, that which can be sensed by the physical senses. But of course, we know that things exist outside of the physical. We know that sunsets are beautiful and the Nazis were evil. We are certainly rational to believe these things, but not because we have physical evidence to prove them. So we all believe in some things which can't be physically detected. Still, it is a challenge to deny the Bible. It actually takes much more faith to deny the Bible than to believe it. Why? > Hundreds of years before \[the events in the New Testament were > written to have taken place\], ancient writings foretold the coming of > a man who would actually be God. This man-God, it was foretold, would > be born in a particular city from a particular bloodline, suffer in a > particular way, die at a particular time, and rise from the dead to > atone for the sins of the world. Immediately after the predicted time, > multiple eyewitnesses proclaimed and later recorded that those > predicted events had actually occurred. Those eyewitnesses endured > persecution and death when they could have saved themselves by denying > the events. Thousands of people in Jerusalem were then converted after > seeing or hearing of these events, and this belief swept quickly > across the ancient world. Ancient historians and writers allude to or > confirm these events, and archaeology corroborates them. Having seen > evidence from creation that God exists \[...\], Christians believe > these multiple lines of evidence show beyond a reasonable doubt that > God had a hand in these events. Atheists must have a lot more faith to > explain away the predictions, the eyewitness testimony, the > willingness of the eyewitnesses to suffer and die, the origin of the > Christian church, and the corroborating testimony of the other > writers, \[and the\] archeological finds.[\[1\]](#EPWTM){#cite-EPWTM > .citation_link} In other words, it takes a lot more faith to believe that these things didn't happen than that they did. So you certainly can deny the truth of the Bible, but it's not easy.
