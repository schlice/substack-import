**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **If Trump is pardoned by Pence, can the State of New York prosecute him for crimes committed there?** Currently, yes they can. However, there is a case that is going to be heard by SCOTUS during this term. In *Gamble v. United States*, the Supreme Court is considering whether the Double Jeopardy Clause of the Fifth Amendment, which prohibits any person from being prosecuted for the same offense more than once, bars a federal prosecution for a criminal offense when the defendant has already been prosecuted for the same offense in state court.[\[1\]](#AbljU){#cite-AbljU .citation_link} Currently, the theory of *dual sovereignty* governs the federal vs state exemption to double jeopardy. The Federal government and the state, as sovereign entities, can both define a crime separately, and even if it refers to the exact same occurrence, can both prosecute that crime separately, and it does not violate the double jeopardy clause. If SCOTUS rules in favor of Gamble, the dual sovereignty rule would be overturned. If a crime is prosecuted in a state court, the Federal government could not prosecute that person for the same crime at a Federal level. If a crime is prosecuted at the Federal level, a state could not prosecute for that same crime. And if the military prosecutes a crime under UCMJ, the Federal government would not be able to prosecute the same crime when the military is done. Now, back to the question. We're assuming that Trump is impeached, removed from office, subsequently indicted in a Federal court, and convicted, and President Pence pardons him. If dual sovereignty is ruled unconstitutional, then the pardon would prevent New York from prosecuting Trump for the same crime. Just because it's illegal under both Federal and state law would not allow New York to prosecute for the same crime. If dual sovereignty remains in effect, then a Federal pardon would not prevent New York from filing charges for the same crime under New York law. One conspiracy theory alleges that this is why Republicans pushed so hard to get Kavanaugh on the court: because he'd be the tipping point ruling for Gamble, and then this very chain of events would take place.
