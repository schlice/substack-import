--- title: '"In the Tamir Rice case, why are the officers not legally responsible for their failure to provide immediate medical attention?"' date: '2015-12-29' tags: - uncategorized layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} Department policies are all different, but there are several reasons why officers are not legally responsible for providing medical attention: 1. If an officer is solo, he has other concerns at that moment following a shooting, not the least of which is his continuing safety. He has to secure the scene, control the bystanders, and make sure none of them want to take immediate revenge for the shooting. Emotions run high, but the officer cannot focus on anything else until backup arrives. If an officer has a partner with him who can assist with securing the scene, and they don\'t have any other immediate safety or security concerns, the officer can then provide basic medical attention, but two-officer cars are becoming more and more rare these days. 2. Once backup arrives, the officer in question is generally taken aside so that evidence can be preserved (i.e. his duty weapon will be taken into evidence, GSR tests will be run, etc). The officer\'s priority is still not the suspect at that point. 3. Officers are generally not trained as EMTs. Officers are usually only trained as first responders (CPR/FA/AED), but not generally as EMTs. There is only so much that an officer can do before the EMTs arrive.
