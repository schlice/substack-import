**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **How do we both believe the victim and allow for due process when someone is accused of abuse?** FINALLY!!! Someone is asking the right questions!!! Well done [[David Hinckley](https://www.quora.com/profile/David-Hinckley)]{.qlink_container}. It's the right question because it's the one everyone is so confused about. Let's try to cut through the confusion. You're talking about two different things: believing the victim (or showing that we believe the victim) and being able to prosecute a case. One is a matter of belief, and the other is a matter of law. The reason why there is so much confusion is because we are trying to blend the two in some way, and we are failing miserably. We are telegraphing that we "believe" the victim by destroying the life of the accused, and this is a miscarriage of justice. Before we get into the meat of the question, we need to discuss a legal function called a **statute of limitations**. The statute of limitations defines a timeframe after which charges for a certain crime cannot be brought. The statute of limitations for crimes are different, and they are different in each state as well. The statute of limitations exists to protect the accused from bad prosecution, because over time, memories fade, evidence degrades, and the possibility that the accused can get a *fair trial* decreases. So when we pass the "point of no return" where it becomes nearly impossible for the accused to get a fair trial, we cut off the possibility of bringing charges. We need to discuss one more point before we address the question: **the truth.** Think of the truth as existing on a timeline. His story is on one end, and her story is on the other end. The truth is somewhere in between. It can be closer to one side than the other, but it is always somewhere in between. Why can't one side be telling the complete truth? Is this because everyone lies? Not at all. It's because everyone sees the world differently. Everyone's perceptions are colored by their life experiences. This is why two people can see the same incident and report that they saw it happen differently. Especially when we miss seeing things with clarity, our brains interpolate and fill in the blank spaces in our recollection. When our brains do this, it is subconscious. We don't know it's happening. We simply believe in the veracity of our memories, even though they may not be exactly what happened; they may have some pieces filled in by our brains. And when our brains interpolate the spaces between our memories, they may not be accurate, and what our brain adds to our memories is based on what we already know, and that is where our life experiences come into play. So the truth is always somewhere between the two stories. In law, we seek to get as close to the truth as our limited memories allow us to do. Evidence allows us to get closer than we've ever gotten before, and it helps to support our fallible memories. But while we always seek the truth, we actually are searching for what we can prove. And in court, we try to prove **guilt.** In other words, *is it more likely than not that this person did what they are accused of doing*. In criminal court, it's guilt beyond a reasonable doubt, and in civil court, it's liability based on preponderance of evidence. *But it's not exact truth*. Okay, so now back to the question. How can we believe the victim and also allow for due process? I think that in most cases, most victims are believable, and in most cases, we do believe the victim. There are some accusers who were later proven to have lied and made up their claims, but in most cases, I think the victims are fairly believable. What has happened in recent times is that victims of sexual abuse are coming forward *decades* after their alleged abuse and are making claims. Legally speaking, *nothing should happen in these cases.* Why? In America, you are innocent until proven guilty in a court of law. The probability of getting a fair trial where guilt or liability can reasonably be established, after so much time, is strikingly low. Does this mean we don't *believe the victim*? Certainly not. We likely do believe the victim believes that her account exactly explains what may have happened. But because of all the reasons we've discussed, it probably doesn't exactly describe exactly what happened. However, and this part is critically important, **it matters not what we believe. It only matters what we can prove.** And after so much time, we can't prove much. However, men's lives are being destroyed (they lose their jobs, their homes, their marriages, their families) over mere *allegations* which are unproven and the memories of which have likely degraded so badly over time that they are unlikely to depict much of exactly what happened. So how do we merge **believing the victim** with **achieving due process?** ***We separate belief from provability.*** Believe the victim. Believe that the victim endured *something* which happened a long time ago (assuming that the victim is following the current trend and is coming forward decades after an alleged abuse), and support the victim. Ensure that the victim is cared for, physically and psychologically. Understand that memories degrade over time, and that what the victim remembers may not be an exact replica of what actually happened, but believe that the victim endured *something* terrible, and support that victim. But (and here's the less-than-popular part), *don't expect that after decades of silence, we can destroy the accused's life.* We shouldn't expect his employer to fire him over an allegation. We shouldn't expect a TV and movie actor to lose his role on a TV show because of an allegation of abuse decades ago. We shouldn't expect a Congressman to resign because of an allegation of abuse decades ago. And this is part of societal expectations. If we expect these things to happen after an allegation, they will. If we begin to expect that they should not, then the trend will change. So in my opinion, two things have to change. 1. We the public have to stop believing that it is right that a man's life be destroyed over an allegation. 2. We need to encourage victims to come forward *while we can do something about it*. We should make sure that while we support the victims, we shouldn't accept the current trend of destruction of the life and livelihood of the accused so many years after the alleged abuse occurred. It is really up to us to show businesses and industry and government that this practice of retaliation against the accused, without so much as an investigation, let alone a trial, is unacceptable. Before you reach for the comment section to flame this, remember this is not an indictment of the victim. But the victim *shares some responsibility* to bring the allegation forward in a timely manner. We should be using this movement to encourage victims to come forward while we can still prosecute a case of sexual assault. In most states, the statute of limitations is only two to three years for civil sexual assault. If you want to see something done about these crimes, encourage victims to come forward while we can prosecute the crime.
