--- title: "Is there a correlation between low levels of sunlight and higher rates of multiple sclerosis (MS) or other autoimmune diseases in certain countries? What measures can be taken to address this potential issue?" date: "2025-06-05" tags: ["health-autoimmune"] layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} Yes, in fact there is a correlation. The low levels of sunlight results in a **vitamin D deficiency**, since humans generate vitamin D when the skin is exposed to sunlight. Vitamin D is known to be neuroprotective, and there has been a positive correlation between people who have vitamin D deficiency and MS. Even more tightly defined is people who grew up above 40° latitude (where the sun's rays arrive at a greater angle). They have a higher incidence of MS than people who grew up below 40° latitude. And we believe the reason is the same: lower angle of sunlight produces less vitamin D than higher. Remember, this is just a correlation; this is not a causation (i.e. MS is not caused by vitamin D deficiency, but it could be an exacerbating factor) The best way to potentially mitigate this problem would be to develop appropriate levels of vitamin D as observed in the blood, but we don't have that information yet. We do not know what an appropriate level of vitamin D is for anyone, so work with your doctor on what is safe to take. Or if you can, try to get at least 15 minutes of sun a day, but be careful to not overstay your welcome, especially in the hot summer.
