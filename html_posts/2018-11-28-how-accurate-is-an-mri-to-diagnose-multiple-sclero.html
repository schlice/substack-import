**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **How accurate is an MRI to diagnose multiple sclerosis?** The MRI is one of the most important tools used in diagnosing MS, *but it alone cannot diagnose MS.* MS is a diagnosis of *exclusion*, because there is no one symptom unique to it. Even the small, ovoid patches (in other words, little white spots) on an MRI can be caused by other diseases. Therefore, you have to *exclude* all other diseases and conditions which might cause little white spots on an MRI. Along with the MRI, the neurologist will begin to exclude all other known diseases and conditions which can cause an MRI to show white lesions on the brain. A panel of blood tests are performed (colloquially called, no surprise, the "little white spot series") which rule out other diseases which can cause both the symptom experienced and the white spots on the MRI. The MRI has become so important in the diagnosis of MS that the diagnostic criteria used to diagnose the disease were changed to include a positive MRI. This criteria is called the [[**McDonald criteria**](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=891dab&url=https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/McDonald_criteria){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="wikipedia.org"}]{.qlink_container}. Previously, the patient had to have two separate symptoms of MS "disseminated by space and time." Dissemination in space means in two separate areas of the CNS (i.e. the brain, the spinal cord, the optic nerve, etc). Dissemination in time meant no closer together than six months. Therefore, in order to get a full diagnosis of MS, you would have to have two relapses. In 2017, the McDonald criteria were updated to include evidence of one of those two symptoms appearing only on an MRI. Therefore, you can show symptoms in only one functional system of the body, and show evidence on an MRI of the inclusion of a second functional system, and of course, run the "little white spot series" to rule out any other possible causes of the symptoms or the little white spots. So for a person who has had only one attack, the MRI can provide the evidence that a second system is involved, and that the second event is older, disseminated by time (the MRI can show active lesions, and lesions which were active but are now inactive, using [[**gadolinium enhancement**](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=b22a95&url=https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MRI_contrast_agent){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="wikipedia.org"}]{.qlink_container}). Therefore, it's fair to say that the MRI is so important in the diagnosis of MS, that as of 2017, you can now be diagnosed after only one relapse, if you have an MRI which shows evidence of previous disease activity in another functional area of the CNS.[\[1\]](#vHswA){#cite-vHswA .citation_link} Footnotes [\[1\]](#cite-vHswA){.citation_index} [[Diagnosing MS](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=3985d2&url=https://www.nationalmssociety.org/Symptoms-Diagnosis/Diagnosing-MS){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="nationalmssociety.org"}]{.qlink_container}
