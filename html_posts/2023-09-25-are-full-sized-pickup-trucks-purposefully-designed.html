Answer

**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} It does indeed seem that full-sized pickup trucks are getting larger and larger in an apparent attempt to piss off everyone else on the road. It is true that trucks are indeed getting larger every year. But it's not happening to piss people off. **It's happening thanks to the US government.** And it happens because of a standard called CAFE. The Corporate Average Fuel Economy (CAFE) standards are fleet-wide averages that automakers must meet for their cars and trucks each year. The purpose of CAFE is to reduce energy consumption by increasing the fuel economy of cars and light trucks. The CAFE standards were first enacted by Congress in 1975. Since 1978, each automaker has had to meet the CAFE standards for their car and truck fleets. The CAFE standards for passenger cars and light-duty trucks in the U.S. is 54.5 miles per gallon by 2025. This was set under the Obama Administration, and it was repealed under the Trump administration. In place of CAFE standards, the Trump administration created Safer Affordable Fuel-Efficient (SAFE) standards to give manufacturers more freedom and reduce the quality-adjusted prices of vehicles by an average of \$2,200. While SAFE 1 still increased fuel economy standards, it did so at a slower rate than the standards proposed by Obama's administration. SAFE 1 also proposed that manufacturers comply with the standards by focusing on greenhouse gas emission standards because of the subsequent ability to help reach CAFE standards. SAFE 1 ultimately sought to loosen the annual fuel efficiency increase from 5% to 1.5% through 2026.[\[1\]](#XSAgK){#cite-XSAgK .citation_link} SAFE 1 has since been repealed by the Biden administration for overstepping the agency's legal authority and for not taking local and national interests into account. Under the new administration, the NHTSA finalized CAFE standards for model years 2024-2026 on March 31, 2022. These recently passed standards require about 49 miles per gallon for all light-duty vehicles by 2026 by increasing fuel efficiency by 8% annually for the years 2024 and 2025, and 10% annually for the year 2026. The National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA) found that the 49 miles per gallon standard could save consumers nearly \$1,400 in total fuel expenses over the lifetime of compliant vehicles. It could also save 234 billion gallons of gas between 2030 and 2050. The CAFE standards are determined with a mathematical formula based on vehicle footprints. The goal is to prevent automakers from meeting the mileage requirement by reducing vehicle size and weight, which could reduce vehicle safety. Making the vehicles smaller would automatically increase their fuel economy, as the engines have less mass to move. :::: ui_qtext_image_outer ::: qtext_image_wrapper ![](images/qimg-d343c0b31c1162ce3b0e03bfa768dad4){.landscape .qtext_image .zoomable_in_feed master_src="images/qimg-d343c0b31c1162ce3b0e03bfa768dad4" master_w="1000" master_h="778"} ::: :::: What has happened in the industry is the *inverse*. Rather than making vehicles *smaller* to meet the standards, they made the vehicles *larger* so that they could meet the *lower* standards. This growing of truck sizes has a natural limit, but it does push out the requirement for fuel efficiency a few years, and gives physics the opportunity to catch up. Therefore, you see trucks getting larger.
