**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} I saw a survey by Barna, their 2025 "[[State of the Church](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=d281c2&url=https://www.barna.com/research/belief-in-jesus-rises/){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="barna.com"}]{.qlink_container}" survey, earlier this month. The survey revealed that more and more young people are choosing to believe in Jesus. This is very good news. According to Barna's survey data, the percentage of U.S. adults who say "I have made a personal commitment to follow Jesus that is still important to my life today" hit a 21st-century low in 2021 at 54%. Since then, the percentage has jumped a full 12 points to 66%. It means that more millennial and Gen Z adults are curious about or are committing to a relationship with Jesus. In a world which was previously categorized by those same people *walking away* from their faith, this is something to celebrate. Yet, according to Barna's survey, many people who are interested in following (or already do say they follow) Jesus are less interested in engaging with the church. The survey found that a significant percentage (27%) of individuals who say they've made a commitment to Jesus *don't identify as Christians*. This means that while people are indeed finding Jesus and the truth of the Gospel (which is great), they're not wanting to be associated with the church (which is problematic). This matches what you've identified: people want Jesus as *savior*, but they don't want to pattern their lives after His; they don't want Him to be *Lord*. This may be because they've been turned off by differences in denominations as well as controversies in the church and how those play on social media. But these nascent Christians will still need other mature Christians who love Jesus to come alongside them and teach them what following Jesus looks like in a modern age. In short, they will need Christians to disciple them. They'll need the church. So millennials and Gen Z'ers are trying to split the baby. They're trying to have their cake and eat it too. Pick your metaphor, but they're trying to parse the difference between being a believer in Christ yet ignoring the parts of Christianity they consider to be tasteless: being a member of a church and living life as a committed Christian.
