--- title: '"Can a belief be valid even if evidence is not presented?"' date: '2022-02-14' tags: - law layout: post ---

**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} In order to answer this question, we have to determine what we believe in, and I think we can take as a basic premise that we believe in what is true. However, what is truth? How do we determine truth? So first of all, does truth exist? I think we can say quite plainly that truth does exist, and it is undeniable and absolute. You cannot say "truth cannot be known" because that is a self-defeating statement which claims to be a *known,* *absolute truth*. So what are the processes by which we discover truth? We can start with what we call the "[[First principles](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=6cc14a&url=https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_principle){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="wikipedia.org"}]{.qlink_container}." They are called the First Principles because nothing is used to prove them. They are true based on the nature of reality. They are what we call "self-evident." A few of those truths are the [[Law of noncontradiction](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=b63cdd&url=https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law_of_noncontradiction){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="wikipedia.org"}]{.qlink_container} and the [[Law of the excluded middle](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=b3564e&url=https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law_of_excluded_middle){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="wikipedia.org"}]{.qlink_container}. The Law of the excluded middle says that something either *is* or it *is not*. There is no third alternative. For example, either God exists, or he does not. We use the FIrst Principles to discover all other truths. Just as you need eyes in your head to see, you need First Principles in your mind in order to learn and understand all other truths about reality. Beyond the First Principles, we generally determine truth by using observation and then drawing conclusions based on what we observe. This is a process called *induction*. This is different from *deduction* in that deduction is taking a series of premises and drawing a conclusion from them, while induction is the process of determining whether the *premises themselves* are true. Most of the things you know by induction are not 100% certain, but are usually *highly certain*. For example, do you know with 100% certainty that gravity will pull all dropped objects to the earth? The answer is no, because you have not observed every object which has been dropped. So if the things we know by induction are not 100% certain, can they be *trusted*? Yes, with varying levels of certainty. Because none of us possess unlimited knowledge, it is true that some of our inductions could be wrong. But even when we are not 100% certain, we usually have enough information to make *reasonably certain* conclusions about most questions in life. So back to our example, can we be *reasonably sure* that gravity will pull all dropped objects to the earth? Since nearly every dropped object has been pulled to the earth, our conclusion that all dropped objects will be pulled to the earth can be considered **true beyond a reasonable doubt**. It's 99+% sure. It's not beyond *all* doubt, but it's beyond *reasonable* doubt. So even if we can't see something (since this is what most people call "evidence"), we can use inductive reasoning, logic, and scientific principles to determine the truth of the premise beyond a reasonable doubt. \ Original question: Can a belief be valid even if evidence is not presented?
