**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} To fully answer the question, we need to define "fake." If, by "fake," you mean filling out an application with fraudulent or incorrect information, it's nearly impossible to apply to University with a "fake" application. [[Alice Baker](https://www.quora.com/profile/Alice-Baker-31)]{.qlink_container} already detailed that in her answer, so I won't re-hash that here. If, however, by "fake," you actually mean "your true information, but you have no intention of actually attending the school you're applying to," then the calculus shifts slightly. While it's not *illegal* to apply to a school for which you have no intention of attending, it would be considered *wrong* to do so. Remember, the University does a non-trivial amount of work processing your application. Teams of reviewers pore over your records, determining whether or not you will be a good fit. And that's before we get to *financing*, which is a big deal in and of itself. Because when it comes to financing, government gets involved, with all of its grants and student aid programs. To do all that work and then to be potentially accepted when you have no intention of attending the school accomplishes little but wasting the time of the people at the university, and they'll likely remember that when you *are* ready to attend the college. Maybe, instead of filing a "fake" application, you could reach out to the Admissions department of the university in question and work with them (on their time, of course) to see if your application package is strong enough to be considered when you are ready to attend university. They may be able to give you some pointers to strengthen your application package, which could give you a leg up when it's time to actually attend. And your efforts won't go unnoticed at that university, and may actually help you when it comes time to actually apply.
