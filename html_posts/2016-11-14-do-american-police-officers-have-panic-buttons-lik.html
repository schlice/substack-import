--- title: "Do American police officers have panic buttons like British police do?" date: "2016-11-14" tags: ["law-enforcement"] layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} Every organization is different, and every organization uses different radios and different alerts. Most digital trunked radios have RD (Radio Distress) buttons which send a signal to dispatch with the officer's radio number. On some models, the mic is held open for 10--15 seconds when the RD is activated. Since every radio is different, I can't tell you where each and every RD button is on the radio, nor would that be a wise thing to do, as it is generally accepted to be "close-hold" information, so that you don't spook a potential attacker by pressing what they know to be the RD button. :::: ui_qtext_image_outer ::: qtext_image_wrapper ![](images/qimg-15aab7a300e9450fc0e9f395f9764133){.landscape .qtext_image .zoomable_in_feed master_src="images/qimg-15aab7a300e9450fc0e9f395f9764133" master_w="586" master_h="401"} ::: :::: *(This is an example of a RD button on an older digital radio; the last agency I worked at where we had functioning RD buttons used one that looked similar to this, including the mic extender)* At one military base at which I worked, the RD button was on top of the radio. It was recessed below the surface of the radio so that it could not accidentally be bumped. It was off to the side of the upper part of the handheld radio, next to the volume and channel knobs. You would have to work pretty hard to push the button; it wasn't something you could bump with your elbow. At the beginning of the shift, each post and patrol would call out their status and then press their RD. This allowed dispatch to record the RD number for each radio, since they were issued at the beginning of the shift. We would let it broadcast for a few seconds, then power-cycle the radio to stop it (that was the only way to stop the RD from broadcasting). It took less than a second for the radio number of the radio broadcasting the distress to appear on the display in dispatch.
