**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} In the United States, the problem is not with the correctional system. The problem is with the medical community. The largest quantity of the prison population is there [[because of drugs or drug-related offenses](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=ee4045&url=https://www.bop.gov/about/statistics/statistics_inmate_offenses.jsp){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="bop.gov"}]{.qlink_container}. By orders of magnitude. And most of those are drug **users**, not drug **dealers**. Fundamentally, this is due to a flaw in the **medical system**, which chooses to treat drug addicts as \"bad actors\" instead of people with a disease. Medical science has, as of today, been unable to quantify the exact mechanisms within the brain which are affected by the disease of addiction. Since they have been unable to do so, addicts are not treated as patients. Since the medical community does not see them as ill, the only other choice for their behavior is, well, choice. The addict must therefore be choosing to do drugs; he is not unable to control himself because of a disease. Therefore, if he does not have a disease, he must be a criminal. For this reason, the jails are teeming with drug addicts. When the medical community can finally find the exact pathways in the brain which are affected by addiction, we can start to treat addicts as **patients** instead of **prisoners**. The effect on the prison system will be overwhelming. The government can divert some money that was normally sent to prisons to drug rehabilitation centers, and the effect on drug addiction will be staggering. But it all comes down to the greater medical community to fix.
