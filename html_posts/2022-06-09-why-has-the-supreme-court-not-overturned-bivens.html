**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} So if you haven\'t been following the news, they basically just did. [[https://www.vox.com/platform/amp\...](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=9134bd&url=https://www.vox.com/platform/amp/23159672/supreme-court-egbert-boule-bivens-law-enforcement-border-patrol-immunity){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="vox.com"}]{.qlink_container} While this case applies to Border Patrol agents, there\'s no reason to think that this case shouldn\'t apply to all federal law enforcement officials. > Before Wednesday's decision in *Egbert*, \[...\] it was > well-established that federal law enforcement officers who use > unconstitutionally excessive force may be sued in federal court. > *Egbert* explicitly exempts all Border Patrol agents from this rule, > and it could be read to exempt nearly all --- if not all --- federal > law enforcement officers from *Bivens* suits. *Bivens* is exactly like *Roe* in that while people may agree that a remedy is needed, the court is not the right place for that remedy to have been found. Congress could very easily pass a *Bivens* remedy so that individuals can seek damages when it is alleged that a federal agent violated their constitutional rights. Or, Congress could eliminate qualified immunity. Or, Congress could take a number of other actions in order to enact something like *Bivens*. But Congress has failed to act. However, some would argue, that if Congress does not act, that is an act unto itself. This could signal the fact that there is not enough political will in Congress in order to enact such a remedy. So it may not necessarily be that Congress simply failed to act, Congress may have not acted specifically because they chose not to. This case, along with the forthcoming decision in *Roe*, should signal to anyone watching the courts that this particular Court is very much opposed to any form of judicial activism, and will root it out whenever it finds it.
