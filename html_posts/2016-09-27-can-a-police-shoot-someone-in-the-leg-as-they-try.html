--- title: '"Can a police shoot someone in the leg as they try to run to get away if they are known to be guilty? Do they have to shoot to kill?"' date: '2016-09-27' tags: - law-enforcement layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} Short answer: no. Longer answer: It sounds like you are trying to find a middle ground surrounding the use of deadly force. But there are a few things you need to know: The firearm is considered *deadly force*. I think that has been fairly well established by now. Guns are designed to kill, and they do so very efficiently. Police are not permitted to use lethal force in a specifically non-lethal way. *Well, that just seems silly. Why can't they aim for the leg or something?* Several reasons: 1. It's hard enough to hit what you're aiming at. Now add in the adrenaline in a high-stress situation, and it gets even harder. New York City police statistics show that simply hitting a target, let alone hitting it in a specific spot, is a difficult challenge. In 2006, in cases where police officers intentionally fired a gun at a person, they discharged 364 bullets and hit their target 103 times, for a hit rate of 28.3 percent, according to the department's Firearms Discharge Report. The police shot and killed 13 people that year. In all shootings --- including those against people, animals and in suicides and other situations --- New York City officers achieved a 34 percent accuracy rate (182 out of 540), and a 43 percent accuracy rate when the target ranged from zero to six feet away. 43% standing no more than six feet away. You really think someone can accurately hit a person in a foot at 50 yards? In reality, it would be a miracle if it did happen (note that more recent statistics are not available as NYPD has gone away from hit ratios in reporting). 2. Police do not shoot to kill. They also do not shoot to "not kill." They shoot to stop the threat. The most effective way to stop the threat is to shoot in the "center of mass" of the individual. Shooting the periphery of mass may wound, but may not stop the threat. Our military fighters found this to be true with their M-16s firing 5.56 NATO rounds. The high-velocity darts would punch clean through an adversary (which is what they are designed to do --- take the person out of the fight). In modern warfare, the military found that not only is that type of wound survivable (good), but it didn't actually stop these fighters from fighting (not good). They had to change their bullets to something more substantial to actually stop the opponent (7.62 rounds, and change from the M-16 to the SCAR). You stop the act in the most effective way possible. You have a much greater chance of missing if you don't aim for center mass. Additionally, even if you shoot for a leg, you can hit something like the femoral artery and the suspect will bleed out before help can arrive, and he will be dead anyway. 3. If you have the authority to use your firearm, it is because you need to stop someone who poses a threat to life or poses a threat of bodily harm to yourself or to others. The only time police are shooting a fleeing suspect is when he poses a danger to the lives of others. This would be a situation which *clearly* called for the use of lethal force. If lethal force is authorized, trying to use non-lethal force is counter-intuitive.
