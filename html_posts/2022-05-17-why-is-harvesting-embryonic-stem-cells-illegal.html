**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} It's not. Stem cell research is legal in the United States, however, there are restrictions on its funding and which stem cells you can use. And the other problem: in order to harvest embryonic stem cells (ESCs), the fetus must be destroyed. As you may be aware, destruction of a fetus is currently a hot topic in the United States. > State laws regarding research on stem cells vary widely, particularly > in regard to use of embryonic stem cells. On one end of the spectrum, > eight states (California, Connecticut, Illinois, Iowa, Maryland, > Massachusetts, New Jersey and New York) encourage embryonic stem cell > research, while on the other end of the spectrum, South Dakota > strictly forbids research on embryos. > > A number of states restrict research on aborted fetuses or embryos, > but in some cases, research may be permitted with consent of the > patient.[\[1\]](#QNAFv){#cite-QNAFv .citation_link} The final problem: ESCs have yet to be proven to treat any condition successfully, while adult stem cells (with induced pluripotency) have been used for decades to successfully treat a wide variety of diseases and conditions. Therefore, the lack of usability of ESCs coupled with the controversy surrounding destroying a fetus make harvesting ESCs difficult at best. \ Original question: Why is harvesting embryonic stem cells illegal?
