Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} That is not what the Supreme Court said. If you're going to ask a question, at least ask the right question. Neither business in question (*Masterpiece Cake Shop* or *303 Creative*) has ever refused service to an LGBT customer. Ever. You may be confusing "refusing to speak a message with which they disagreed" with "refusing service to an LGBT customer." In *Masterpiece*'s case, Jack Phillips never refuses to serve anyone. He'll sell a rack cake to anyone for any reason at any time. Lorie Smith will create websites for anyone, regardless of their race, sex, religion, or sexual or gender orientation. What they *will not* do is be forced by the government to speak a state-approved message with which they do not agree. The case proves that the First Amendment not only protects your right to speak, but it also protects your right to *not* speak. If the cake shop or the web designer were actually refusing service to LGBT people, then you'd have to show where the LGBT person entered the store and asked for a cake with absolutely zero message and zero meaning. Just a cake. Or just a website for a generic business, not for a gay wedding, and was denied simply because they were LGBT. **This never happened.** Businesses can still not refuse service to LGBT customers if the state has decided that sexual orientation and gender identity (SOGI) is a protected class. It is not protected at the federal level but some states have added SOGI as a protected class. But business owners cannot be forced to speak state-approved messages with which they disagree. Jack Phillips cannot be forced to use his artistic talents to design a cake for a gay wedding, and Lorie Smith cannot be forced to use her talents to create websites celebrating gay weddings. The First Amendment protects their right to not speak messages with which they disagree. They'll make any other cake or any other website, but not those. The state cannot tell them that they must design cakes celebrating LGBT events or risk losing their business license.
