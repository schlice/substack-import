**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} > *Question Details:* > > *I had this problem once. The police came to write a ticket because I > parked in a red zone (I\'m color blind). During the process of writing > the ticket, I tried to open the door and drive away, but he said no.* > > 1. *Should I have gotten in my car and drove away regardless? Just > push the gas pedal and drive away? He can get out of the way if he > wants, right?* > 2. *Also, what if I said, \"Dude, I\'m driving away, get the fuck > away from my car.* > > *What would be the consequences if I did one or both of those?* Two issues here: 1\. Parking in a \"Red Zone\"\ 2. Leaving the scene 1\. Parking in a \"Red Zone\": Color-blindness would only be an affirmative defense if you could show that there was no \"text\" anywhere that stated that it was a \"no parking\" zone (such as a sign, or words spray-painted on the curb). If the curb was only colored, and there was no other indication using text that described the no-parking zone, you could claim it as a defense against the ticket in court. 2\. However, you have to receive the ticket before you can contest it. If you get in your car and drive away, your license plate (which was already recorded) will be reported to the police, and you will be charged not only with the parking violation, but with leaving the scene, as well as potential charges of bodily harm against the parking enforcement personnel. Bottom line: bad idea. Allow them to complete the ticket, take pictures of the area in question, and fight it in court. As many of us have said when dealing with law enforcement, you have many options and remedies. NONE of these remedies takes place AT THE SCENE. Let them finish what they are doing and then take them to court. (EDIT) In most jurisdictions, leaving while a parking enforcement official is writing a ticket is, in and of itself, another traffic offense. So more than likely, what will happen is you will get two tickets. In the mail.
