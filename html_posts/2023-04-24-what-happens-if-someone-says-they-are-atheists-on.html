--- title: '"What happens if someone says they are atheists on their paperwork for joining the U.S. military? Does it disqualify them from service?"' date: '2023-04-24' tags: - uncategorized layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} The United States government cannot endorse any religion, because of the First Amendment to the Constitution. Therefore, requiring a religion in order to serve in the military would be unconstitutional. The religion question really only pertains to last rites or military hospital stays. Also these days, it pertains to when you expect to be able to have time to worship. Your religious preference gets stamped on your dog tags, so that they know which chaplain to call if you're dying. :::: ui_qtext_image_outer ::: qtext_image_wrapper ![](images/qimg-a9b28487bbdec45951cce77a779398a9){.portrait .qtext_image .zoomable_in_feed master_src="images/qimg-a9b28487bbdec45951cce77a779398a9" master_w="2999" master_h="4000"} ::: :::: *(My dog tags, Social Security Number removed from the photo. I was a practicing Catholic at the time of my military service)* From the US Army website: > During World War II there were only three religious categories that > could be put on dog tags: P for Protestant, C for Catholic and H for > Hebrew (Jewish). Obviously, that proved to be too limiting. \"No > Religious Preference\" and \"None\" were eventually added; today many > faith groups and broad denominations are available, reflecting the > diversity of the armed forces. Service members can generally put > whatever religious preference they want on their tags, including > \"Atheist\" or \"Agnostic.\" Some even ask for \"Jedi\" or \"Druid.\" > There is no list of official or approved religions\--after all, that > would constitute government endorsement of a particular religion. > [\[1\]](#SvwQx){#cite-SvwQx .citation_link} So if you are atheist, you can definitely have "ATHEIST" added to your dog tags, and no chaplain will be called to administer you last rites if you're dying. You also won't be expected to ask for time to worship during Basic Training. You'll simply have an appropriately-sized period of "unstructured time" to yourself.
