--- title: '"What are the rights of individuals when it comes to parking in spots that do not belong to them?"' date: '2024-07-10' tags: - law layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} It depends on who owns the parking lot. If the parking lot is privately owned, they can set whatever rules they choose as to who can park in their lot. Their facilities (including any parking lots owned by them) are theirs to use as they see fit. There are some requirements set by the state in terms of accessibility (i.e. a certain number of spaces need to be designated as handicapped, etc), but beyond that, it is up to the business to determine how they manage parking spaces. Sometimes, spaces are individually assigned, such as in some lots adjacent to (or sometimes, underneath) apartment complexes. The complex can assign spaces, and can tow vehicles which are parked there when they do not belong. The tow companies will usually post signs at the entrance to the lot indicating that the lot is controlled and parking is by permit only and violators will be towed at the owner's expense. Your rights, if you are one of these people who parked in a controlled lot or space and your vehicle was towed, are limited. You must pay the tow operator a fee to cover his costs to tow your car. There are limits (set in state law) as to how much they can charge, but those limits are agreed upon and are permitted by state law. If the spaces are on public roads or public property, there really is no ownership of the spaces other than by the government which controls that area (city, county, etc). That government can set parking rules on those spaces, and can similarly tow vehicles in violation to an impound lot.
