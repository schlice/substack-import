--- title: "If two people agree to break the law but one person backs out, is the other person still guilty of committing a crime?" date: "2025-04-14" tags: ["law"] layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} As with all legal answers, this one too is "it depends." The crime you are talking about is called **conspiracy** **to commit a crime.** Conspiracy happens when two or more people **agree** to commit a crime, and then at least one person makes an **overt act** in furtherance of that crime. - **Agreement.** The core of conspiracy is a mutual understanding or plan between two or more people to commit a crime. This agreement can be express or implied. Conspirators must intend to agree to the criminal plan and intend for the underlying crime to be committed. **The act of conspiring itself is the crime**, and it can be prosecuted even if the underlying crime is not actually completed. It can be prosecuted even if one person backs out (but that back-out can be cited as a mitigating circumstance at sentencing). - **Overt act.** Most jurisdictions require that *at least one* of the conspirators take a concrete step (called an *overt act*) to advance the agreed-upon criminal plan. This can be a physical act, a communication, or any action that shows progress towards the crime. So the answer to the question is **yes**, the person who backed out is guilty of **one** crime: conspiracy to commit a crime, even if they back out before the first person actually goes ahead with committing the crime.
