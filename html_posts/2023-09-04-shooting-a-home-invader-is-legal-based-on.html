**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} Ah yes, the "abortion as self-defense" argument. So let's first start all discussions about abortion with the acknowledgement that pregnancy is hard. It is life-changing for the woman. We fully understand how difficult pregnancy can be on a woman. But is it "life-threatening?" Well, it can be. But does that mean we can kill the baby in self-defense? Shouldn't we have the right to protect ourselves, even if it means killing someone, if our life is at risk? First, we must understand the difference between a **threat** and an **aggressor**. Calling the baby a violent aggressor, even an innocent one, is a misleading description of what happens in pregnancy. **Aggressors directly put others in danger; threats put others in danger only indirectly or without intention, by proximity or circumstance.** For example, if I drop a bowling ball out of the window of a tall building, I'm the *aggressor* towards the person walking below, the bowling ball is only the *threat*. While complications in pregnancy may add risks for the mother, the fetus never *directly* causes the mother harm. While **aggressors** are *directly* responsible for their actions and subject to punishment, this isn't true of **threats**. You don't punish the bowling ball for the actions of the dropper. You don't punish the gun for the actions of the gun operator. And **you can't use disproportionate force to repel a threat** ***without yourself becoming an aggressor*, which makes you the one in the wrong.** To that end, it is clear that the baby is not an aggressor (because the baby does not directly put the mother in danger), but only a threat. \ Now that we have determined that the baby is a threat (and not an aggressor), before we determine whether or not we can kill the baby to protect ourselves from the threat, we have to make sure we only use proportionate force to do so. In other words, we need to evaluate *how much* of a risk the baby is to the mother's life. If there is a 75% chance she will die, then morally permissible options look very different from a 10% chance. ***The risk to the woman's life that pregnancy poses doesn't even come close to a reasonable risk to justify the use of force to end it.*** It's not a 75% risk. It's not a 10% risk. *It isn't even a 1% risk.* In the United States in 2016, a pregnant woman had a [[**0.017% chance of dying**](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=9daadc&url=https://www.cdc.gov/reproductivehealth/maternal-mortality/pregnancy-mortality-surveillance-system.htm%23ratio){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="cdc.gov"}]{.qlink_container} **because of a pregnancy-related complication.** You actually have a higher risk of death from getting your appendix removed, which is[ [0.21% chance for patients under 60 years old](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=06ef79&url=https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27535664?fbclid%3DIwAR0_4Mj8SomiMwLXi-j9c-fUGguNtwLaxfhf3BGgfiEmWdTp_1dMh9d7REY){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="nih.gov"}]{.qlink_container}. \ There are some other facets to this argument that must be accepted in order to cogently state that abortion as self-defense is valid, along with the idea that the fetus is an aggressor: 1. The idea that abortion is safer for the woman than childbirth. 2. The idea that consent to sex is not consent to pregnancy. We can individually evaluate each one of those, but those arguments are not strong either, and you need to make those arguments in order to make the case that abortion is a valid form of self-defense. So the "abortion as self-defense" argument is already weak, and it sits on a foundation of weak premises.
