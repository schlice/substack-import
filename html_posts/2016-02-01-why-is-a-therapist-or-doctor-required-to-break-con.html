**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} Separation of church and state. The state cannot impose a law on a church which would violate church rules or beliefs. Priests are bound by \"the sacramental seal.\" This is also called \"the seal of the confessional\" or \"the seal of confession.\" From [[the Catholic Education Center](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=5e2f23&url=http://catholiceducation.org){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="catholiceducation.org"}]{.qlink_container} (emphasis mine): > The sacramental seal is inviolable. Quoting Canon 983.1 of the Code of > Canon Law, the Catechism states, \"\...It is a crime for a confessor > in any way to betray a penitent by word or in any other manner or for > any reason\" (No. 2490). A priest, therefore, cannot break the seal to > save his own life, to protect his good name, to refute a false > accusation, to save the life of another, to aid the course of justice > (like reporting a crime), or to avert a public calamity. **He cannot > be compelled by law to disclose a person\'s confession or be bound by > any oath he takes, e.g. as a witness in a court trial.** A priest > cannot reveal the contents of a confession either directly, by > repeating the substance of what has been said, or indirectly, by some > sign, suggestion, or action. A Decree from the Holy Office (Nov. 18, > 1682) mandated that confessors are forbidden, even where there would > be no revelation direct or indirect, to make any use of the knowledge > obtained in the confession that would \"displease\" the penitent or > reveal his identity. Since the state cannot make a law compelling a priest to violate the teachings of his religion, a priest cannot be compelled to divulge anything he heard in confession, regardless of the benefit of the outcome of such a reporting. Such a law would be in violation of the First Amendment freedom of religion (\"Congress shall make no law respecting an establishment of religion, or **prohibiting the free exercise thereof**\"). This has been interpreted to mean that Congress cannot legislate the practices of any individual religion, or religion as a whole. The Catholic Church holds the seal of confession inviolable; therefore, the state cannot create a law mandating that the church violate that seal. On the other hand, psychiatrists and other medical professionals are bound by **doctor-patient privilege**, which is a *state* construct, not a *religious* one. Inherent in doctor-patient privilege is the notion that a doctor must report someone who is a danger to himself or others to proper law enforcement personnel. This is part of the rules of doctor-patient privilege: under most circumstances, a doctor cannot reveal anything about your personal health to anyone you do not specify. You can specify persons to whom a doctor is allowed to discuss your personal health information (e.g. your spouse); you cannot similarly give a priest the authorization to break the seal of confession.
