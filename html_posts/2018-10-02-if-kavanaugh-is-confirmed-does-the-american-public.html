**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **If Kavanaugh is confirmed, does the American public have any legal course of action to have Kavanaugh removed?** 1. Federal judges as well as Supreme Court judges are subject to impeachment. The Constitution grants the House the power to impeach \"The President, the Vice President, and all civil Officers of the United States.\" Federal court judges and Supreme Court justices are agreed to be civil Officers of the United States, and are subject to impeachment. The process for impeachment is the same for a Supreme Court justice as it is for the President: the House of Representatives must pass Articles of Impeachment, and the Senate must have a trial to vote to remove that person from office. 2. A Supreme Court justice is not the President and so is subject to criminal indictment. If evidence exists of a crime which a Supreme Court justice is alleged to have committed, that justice can be indicted, which would probably lead back to #1 (impeachment). Dr. Ford can go to Maryland and file charges of sexual assault against Kavanaugh if she chooses; there is no statute of limitations on sexual assault in Maryland. However, you asked the question about the "public" having any recourse to having him removed, and the answer to that is **no**. The President appointed him, and if he is confirmed, he will have been appointed with the "advice and consent" of the Senate, in accordance with the Constitution. The Constitution provides no injunctive relief for a SCOTUS justice you don't like. In order for "the public" to have some recourse to remove a SCOTUS justice from the bench, you'd have to amend the Constitution. The whole point of lifetime appointments is to avoid politics and the political cycle influencing the court and its opinions. Allowing people to remove SCOTUS justices undoes that idea.
