--- title: "How does the First Amendment protect free speech and expression, even when it is offensive or advocates violence?" date: "2023-11-03" tags: ["policy-analysis", "law"] layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} The First Amendment protects free speech and expression by preventing the government from writing laws which restrict free speech and expression. We also have specific categories of speech which are ***not*** protected by the First Amendment. For example, **the First Amendment does not protect speech that is intended to incite or produce imminent lawless action and is likely to incite or produce such action**. This is known as the "Brandenburg Test". The Brandenburg test is a legal test for incitement that states that the government can only punish someone for speech that incites violence. The U.S. Supreme Court first defined "fighting words" in *Chaplinsky v New Hampshire* (1942) as words which \"by their very utterance, \[...\] tend to incite an immediate breach of the peace\". Speech which incites violence can only be punished if the government can prove that: - The speaker intended for unlawful violence to occur - Unlawful violence is likely to occur - The unlawful violence is imminent The Supreme Court established the Brandenburg test in the 1969 case Brandenburg v. Ohio. In the case, a Ku Klux Klan leader gave a speech at a rally to his fellow Klansmen. After listing a number of derogatory racial slurs, he then said that "it\'s possible that there might have to be some vengeance \[sic\] taken". The Brandenburg test states that **the First Amendment does not protect speech that is \"directed to inciting or producing imminent lawless action and is likely to incite or produce such action\"**. A law that forbids speech that is intended to cause imminent violence or other criminal activity will pass the Brandenburg test and be deemed constitutional. There are three prongs which must be tested: - **The speaker must intend to incite violence**. If I say something that is so beyond the pale to some people that they spontaneously are spurred to unlawful action, it is not the fault of the speaker if he did not intend for lawless action to take place following his words. - **Unlawful violence is likely to occur**. I can say that I think people should all get in boats and storm Europe, but this wouldn't be incitement of violence because it is unlikely that this will happen. - **The unlawful violence is imminent**. I could say that people should all someday stand up to law enforcement and kill all those pigs, but that's legal because the unlawful violence is "someday," not "right now, go attack that cop over there". The unlawful violence must be imminent. So we do have laws which protect against speech which incites violence. \ You also mentioned when speech is offensive. I went back and re-read the Constitution (it's not really all that long) and I can't find any right to never be offended. So I'm pretty sure that the First Amendment does not protect against offensive speech. In fact, I'd say that *the whole reason we have a First Amendment is to protect speech that other people won't like!* If your speech is kind and caring, if your speech "ruffles no feathers," there is no need to restrict it. If all speech was always amenable and agreeable, we would never need a First Amendment. I would argue that we need the First Amendment *to protect offensive speech*. Sometimes, the things we say cause others to be offended. Sometimes, we intend that reaction (which is quite evil, but that's not the point). Most times, we don't intend that reaction. We simply intend to make a point, but others may find that point offensive. If we are not intending to be offensive, it is the hearer who has the problem, not the speaker. Regardless, the First Amendment has nothing to do with offense, because you do not have a right to never be offended.
