**Content:** [In actuality, you should ask what you couldn\'t do if you used all of your brain at once.\ \ Basically, the 10% thing came about in the early days of brain understanding, when a metal rod or some other object pierced the brain and the victim was nearly unharmed. Doctors surmised because of this that parts of the brain sat unused.\ \ In actuality, you do use your whole brain as [[Dan B](https://www.quora.com/profile/Dan-B-1472)]{.qlink_container} mentioned. But when you are injured, your brain has the ability to \"re-route\" around damaged areas and rebuild neural pathways. Some damage can\'t be repaired or routed around, but much can. There are a certain number of \"reserve\" stem cells available for rebuilding brain cells, but by adolescence, your full complement of adult brain cells has been formed, along with a reserve of cells not in use. This reserve can be used to rebuild damaged areas and re-route around damage if needed.\ \ Using 100% of your neural capacity (active and reserve) would mean that you have no more reserve available for when brain cells die off or are damaged. This would mean that you would lose brain capacity daily for the rest of your life from the moment that you used 100% of your neural load.]{.rendered_qtext}
