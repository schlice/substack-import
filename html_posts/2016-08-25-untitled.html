**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} *We\'ve seen recent video that leaves the reasonable citizen to question Police reason* First of all, you've only seen a video, which is usually a little snippet of time. It usually doesn't show the extent of the interaction. It rarely shows the lead-up to the officer making the decision that deadly force is authorized. The video is not the end-all, be-all of evidence. The camera does not "see" everything, it only sees what is right in front of the officer. There are still things that happen on the sides, or behind, that you never see. Generally, you only see an officer applying deadly force. That's it. You don't see anything that happened before. Essentially, the camera does not tell the whole story. *We were lucky to see these videos; Police certainly have shown no initiative to turn over this type of evidence in asking for public critique.* Yes, you were lucky. Normally, that video is what we call **evidence** and the only people who are *required* to see that video, who are *supposed* to see that video, are a judge and a jury. Unless you're sitting on the jury, **you have no right to see that video. YOU ARE NOT THE JUDGE, NOR ARE YOU THE JURY!** While you may think that the "court of public opinion" is a valid place to try an officer for his actions, while this nation is still a nation of laws (barely), everyone in this country, including police officers, is still afforded a trial by a jury of their peers, not a trial by YouTube comments and upvotes. Someone wanted their 15 seconds of fame, so they posted a snippet of a video on YouTube. In the grand scheme of things, that video is completely and totally **irrelevant**. It **might** get submitted as evidence, but a defense attorney would have a field day tearing the authenticity of the video apart. We don't know if it had been edited; we don't know what parts of that video had been cut out to make it look like the cop shot someone without provocation. That video could be highly inflammatory, and may never be shown to a jury. But again, it's irrelevant. All it does is spin people like you up, and goad them into thinking that police are mindless killers that we've loosed onto an unsuspecting society, while statistics tell us otherwise. What's more important is that the district attorney has enough evidence to review to make a decision about whether or not the officer committed a crime. The video speaks to ***a very small part*** of that body of evidence that the prosecutor uses to determine whether or not a crime has been committed and whether or not he would seek to charge the officer with one. It's also clear that you believe officers are somehow above the law and never face scrutiny for their actions. If you shot someone, your actions would be reviewed by the District Attorney who would determine whether or not you committed a crime. If a police officer shoots someone, not only will his actions face the same scrutiny by the DA, the department will conduct a "Use of Force Review" (UOF Review), as they do for ***every*** use of force by a police officer. Use of force could be anything from application of hands to pepper spray, baton, TASER, or deadly force applications such as baton or firearm. That UOF Review panel is made up of department senior staff, the officer's chain of command, the department's legal team, and the department's training staff. The review will focus on the officer's use of force and determine if it was correctly employed, and whether or not it met department policy for application of force. If not, they will determine if the officer needs re-training in any area of employment of force, or if the officer should face disciplinary actions, up to and including termination. Depending on department, there can be civilians present in UOF Review Boards to observe the proceedings, or there may be a civilian oversight committee appointed by the Mayor which reviews the facts and determinations of the department's UOF Reviews, to ensure that departments are conducting them fairly and not allowing officers to "get away with murder," so to speak. Finally, officers may now face a third avenue of investigations, which are becoming more and more common these days: the US Department of Justice may also conduct an independent review of officer actions to determine whether or not the suspect's civil rights were violated. So as you can see, not only are officers ***highly*** scrutinized any time they employ force, but there are civilians present at every level of that investigation (the DA, the civilians overseeing the UOF Review Board, and the lawyers at the DOJ) who ensure that the police are not ignoring bad officers or summarily excusing their actions. It appears, based on the differences in investigations between civilians and police, that many people have asked the same questions as you, over many years. The high level of scrutiny applied to police officers, and the presence of civilians at every level of those investigations, would lead the "reasonable man" to believe that police actions are very well reviewed, and police officers can get away with very little in today's society. If officers lack the required training, they get re-trained. If they willingly abuse the law, they are either arrested and charged, or they are fired, or both. *\[N\]ot only has this citizen seen these practices play out \'unreasonably\' in real time, most troubling of all, he\'s seen Police continuously absolve their own of \*wrongdoing (\*another vague and highly subject concept).* There are over 17,000 police departments in the United States, at both the state and local level. Most departments have their shit together and conduct themselves ethically. But there have been plenty of examples of police departments that have grown so large and unwieldy that corruption has seeped in, and they end up with two modes of operation: "the way things are supposed to be done" and "the way things are done in real life" and those two don't match up. Your own anecdotal evidence aside, I don't find it surprising at all that some departments have issues. But rather than me trying to tell you what *should* be done, I want you to go fix it. How? I want you to take your issues to the civilian governmental body which oversees that police department. If it's a city police department, go to City Council or the Mayor. If it's a County, go to the Board of Commissioners. If it's a state police department, speak to your representative in your state's Congress. Get yourself on the citizen review boards which review UOF cases. If they don't have one, force them to make one. Get involved. ***Be the change you want to see in the world.*** Don't just go out and prattle on about what needs to change; that accomplishes little. Go out and *be the change!*
