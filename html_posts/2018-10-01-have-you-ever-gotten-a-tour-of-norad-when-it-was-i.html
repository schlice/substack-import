**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **Have you ever gotten a tour of NORAD (when it was inside of Cheyenne Mountain)?** I used to *give* those tours. I worked in the Systems Center, so I knew everything about the workings of the facility. I also worked in Security Forces, which meant that sometimes I'd provide armed security on some tours. The Mountain is a fascinating relic of Cold War paranoia, but it's still relevant today, for several reasons: 1. Heat is a *negative* cost inside the Mountain. We re-circulate the heat from the people and the computers throughout the facility, which is naturally cooled by the surrounding granite to between 55 and 65 degrees F, so only a little bit of additional heat is needed to bring the facilities inside the Mountain to a comfortable working temperature. 2. Water is a *negative* cost inside the Mountain, due to several prolific natural springs which were hit while excavating the facility. The springs provide *so much* water that it is more than we can conceivably use for drinking and for mechanical (cooling towers, fire suppression, etc). We store 1.5 million gallons of potable water and 4.5 million gallons of non-potable water, and the spring produces so much extra that it's literally dumped out the side of the Mountain. 3. Security is a *negative* cost inside the Mountain. We don't have to worry about stray radio signals listening to our gear, because science has so far had a hard time propagating a radio wave through 1500 feet of granite. Physical security is simple when you only have one way in to your facility and it's got big blast doors on it. 4. While it may not be able to survive a direct nuclear strike, nothing else can touch it. Storms (hurricanes, winter weather, etc) won't knock it out. Its redundant power systems mean that it will never lose power. Its redundant communications systems means that it will never lose connectivity. You can't fly a plane into it. It's about the securest facility you can think of, as secure as any subterranean facility, except it's built into a mountain.
