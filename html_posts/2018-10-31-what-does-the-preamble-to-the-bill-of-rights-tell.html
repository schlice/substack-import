**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} > One of the many points of contention between Federalists, who > advocated a strong national government, and Anti-Federalists, who > wanted power to remain with state and local governments, was the > Constitution's lack of a bill of rights that would place specific > limits on government power. Federalists argued that the Constitution > did not need a bill of rights, because the people and the states kept > any powers not given to the federal government. Anti-Federalists held > that a bill of rights was necessary to safeguard individual liberty. > > Madison, then a member of the U.S. House of Representatives, altered > the Constitution's text where he thought appropriate. However, several > representatives, led by Roger Sherman, objected, saying that Congress > had no authority to change the wording of the Constitution. Therefore, > Madison's changes were presented as a list of amendments that would > follow Article VII. > > The House approved 17 amendments. Of these, the Senate approved 12, > which were sent to the states for approval in August 1789. Ten > amendments were approved (or ratified). Virginia's legislature was the > final state legislature to ratify the amendments, approving them on > December 15, 1791.[\[1\]](#VWWJJ){#cite-VWWJJ .citation_link} In order to quell dissent between the Federalists and the Anti-Federalists (and in order to get all the states to sign the Constitution, the promise was made that the first Congress would pass a Bill of Rights, which is very similar to, and strongly influenced by, the Virginia Bill of Rights. The preamble to the Bill of Rights begins this way: > THE Conventions of a number of the States having at the time of their > adopting the Constitution, expressed a desire, in order to prevent > misconstruction or abuse of its powers, that further declaratory and > restrictive clauses should be added: And as extending the ground of > public confidence in the Government, will best insure the beneficent > ends of its institution The preamble basically says that in order for "a number of the States" to ratify the Constitution, they had to add protections in the form of "declaratory and restrictive clauses" "in order to prevent misconstruction or abuse of \[the Constitution's\] powers\[.\]" Footnotes [\[1\]](#cite-VWWJJ){.citation_index} [[Bill of Rights - Bill of Rights Institute](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=b4bb7a&url=https://billofrightsinstitute.org/founding-documents/bill-of-rights/){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="billofrightsinstitute.org"}]{.qlink_container}
