**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **Can having multiple sclerosis make your food tastes change?** There are two separate questions here, and you could be asking either one. My colleagues have been answering the first question: *does having MS cause food to taste different?* There is a second question: *does having MS cause your food "tastes" (i.e. preferences) to change?* I think that the appropriate question for this cohort is the first one: *does MS cause your food to taste different?* One thing I will caution you about MS: Because MS affects everyone differently, we can't really say whether or not any particular symptom is or is not MS. There is a tendency, therefore, to assume *everything* that is unknown is MS. We with MS tend to blame anything unusual or unknown on MS. "My hip is constantly itchy. Think that's MS?" "My hair has been so dry lately. Think that's the MS?" "My toenail is growing crooked. It must be MS." In general, if it's highly unusual that a symptom is found in the general population of people with MS, then it's highly unlikely that your particular symptom is directly related to MS disease activity, and you should probably rule out any other cause of the symptom before assuming that it might be MS. That said, it is at least *conceivable* that MS could cause your food to taste different (i.e. could the nerve pathways be affected in such a way that food now tastes different). However, it's highly unlikely. The area of the brain which controls taste and the nerve pathways from the tongue to the brain are not *usually* impacted by MS. However, there was at least one rather fascinating study about taste and MS, and the observation really nails why this might be important (**emphasis** mine): > *Our senses, particularly smell and taste, are critical to our > appetite. If something is not appealing, we often choose not to eat > it. That's no big problem if it is simply a few food items such as > broccoli or salmon, both of which do not taste good to me and I skip > those when offered; there are plenty of other foods such as steak and > chocolate which do tickle my taste buds and appetite is not one of my > problems. But for some people with MS, there is a concern about their > nutrition and lack of desire to eat, which might be connected in some > way to the food or beverages just not tasting good. This problem can > also be a serious factor in affecting our quality of life. The authors > \[of the study\] mention that* ***this problem will not affect a large > number of people with MS but it is worth taking note of by > neurologists who are caring for people who struggle with caloric > intake and maintaining weight.*** > > *Although there is no 'cure' for faulty wired taste buds, knowing this > problem could be caused by food and drink not tasting right thanks to > multiple sclerosis, might allow for adjustments to the diet of people > with MS to meet their nutritional needs.*[\[1\]](#Otoly){#cite-Otoly > .citation_link} To summarize briefly, the study found that people with MS had a significant difference in which categories of flavors (sweet, sour, bitter, and salty) they were able to distinguish, with the largest variances in the salty and sweet categories. What the study *didn't* account for was MS medications. The medications all work differently and have different methods of administration. It is also at least conceivable that MS medications affect taste as much as MS does. Nevertheless, there is at least one clinical study which has shown that some people with MS do indeed taste things differently, and while it's not a tremendous number of people, it's at least statistically significant. This is incredibly important for people with MS who also have nutritional issues and who struggle with getting enough calories. In other words, *part of the problem with their caloric intake may simply be that food doesn't taste good anymore*.
