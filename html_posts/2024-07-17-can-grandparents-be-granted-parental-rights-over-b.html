--- title: '"Can grandparents be granted parental rights over biological parents? How does this process work legally?"' date: '2024-07-17' tags: - policy-analysis layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} Yes, but in most cases, it is unlikely. First of all, these are all laws governed by the individual states, so there are state dependencies here. But in the vast majority of cases, a competent court will grant parental rights to biological parents unless they are adjudicated to be unfit (which is a high bar to overcome), or if the parents have acted in a way contrary to their constitutional rights as parents. Generally, courts try to keep children with their biological parents., but ultimately it's not about who makes a better parent. It's about **the best interests of the child**. Grandparents can petition the court to award them custody by proving the parents are unfit, showing that they have a history of drug or alcohol addiction, mental illness, neglect, or abuse. In these cases, the court has options. They can grant temporary custody for up to a year, and if the parents have not cleaned up their act in that timeframe (or made significant steps towards recovery) that order could become permanent. Other reasons that grandparents can be granted parental rights over biological parents: - Mutual consent. If the parents and grandparents agree that such a move is in the best interests of the children, the court can order it. - Pre-existing habitation. If the children have been living with the grandparents for more than a year, the court may want to keep the children where they are to avoid causing unnecessary trauma.
