**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} Not all states require former employers to provide you with reference letters. [[https://www.nolo.com/legal-encyc\...](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=48b1a3&url=https://www.nolo.com/legal-encyclopedia/free-books/employee-rights-book/chapter9-6.html){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="nolo.com"}]{.qlink_container} If you don't get one, you are still right to put that experience on your résumé, but your prospective employer may not be able to validate it. It is similar for requests for employment verification. Unless state law requires it, or unless they get a request from a federally-backed institution (like a bank doing a loan request verifying employment), employers are not generally under any obligation to verify your employment. Most employers voluntarily comply with such requests, but they are not legally obligated to do so outside of some state laws.
