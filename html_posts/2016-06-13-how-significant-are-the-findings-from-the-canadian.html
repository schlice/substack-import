--- title: "How significant are the findings from the Canadian study of stem cell therapy for Multiple Sclerosis (June 2016)?" date: "2016-06-13" tags: ["veteran-benefits", "health-autoimmune"] layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} This is a very radical therapy option. It's something that we have known about for many years. The findings are significant in the sense that it shows that autologous hematopoietic stem cell transplant (HSCT) may potentially have some benefit in patients with Multiple Sclerosis. It is, however, only a Phase 1 study, which means that it was only tested against a very small group of patients for safety. There are significant safety concerns with a therapy like this, since it is so very radical. To clarify, in order to accomplish this therapy, the patient's immune cells are harvested and then duplicated in a lab. Next, the patient's entire immune system and bone marrow is destroyed using chemotherapy. Finally, the patient's harvested and reproduced immune cells are re-introduced to the patient to grow new bone marrow. The patient is at great risk during the therapy, and an extended hospital stay is required. Slow re-introduction to normal society is also required, since the person will essentially have no immunities following the treatment. Re-population of all previous immunities will need to be done; essentially, all vaccinations that you would normally receive as a baby will need to be performed again, from scratch. Therefore, because of the significant risks in performing such a procedure, it is only something that would currently be recommended for someone who is not responding to any therapy we can currently provide. Until the procedure can be refined and made to be more safe, it will only be something we relegate to the most severe cases in which no other therapy has been effective.
