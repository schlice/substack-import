Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} It depends on your definition of "unsatisfactory service." The only sort of unsatisfactory service which is *actionable* is called "malpractice." Malpractice occurs when your attorney does not provide competent representation, or when they engage in professional misconduct. Some of the things which may indicate incompetent representation could include: - Losing evidence, especially evidence crucial to your case - Failing to file paperwork on time - Lacking the skills needed to perform the duties hired to perform Some of the things which could be considered professional misconduct are: - Intentionally overbilling - Abandoning a client - Concealing evidence - Conflicts of interest If you are unsatisfied with the services provided by your attorney, you can do a few things. 1. **Talk to your lawyer.** Tell him your concerns and see if you can work out a plan for improvement. 2. **Get a second opinion.** To do this, ask your lawyer to send you copies of everything in your file, and take those copies to another lawyer for a consultation. Second opinions are relatively inexpensive, and are a really good way to "gut check" yourself to see if your lawyer is indeed really misrepresenting you. 1. To get that second opinion, you have to "get copies of everything in your file," or in other words, **get your file**. This step is really important, especially if there are deadlines and motions have been filed and trial dates are looming. You need to get all the work done so far so another attorney can better assist you. You may even ask your new lawyer to help you get your file. You may have to ask your state Bar Association for help to get your file. And if all else fails, you may have to sue your old attorney in small claims court to get your file and to reimburse you for the time spent redoing work in your file or the time and expense you incurred just to get the file. 3. **Fire your lawyer.** You can always fire your lawyer and hire another one. But be warned, this may not be cheap, depending on how much work your first lawyer did, the quality of that work, and how much time you have until trial. 4. **File a complaint**. File a complaint with your state Bar Association, which is usually the group that licenses attorneys. If your state uses a different group, complain to them. 5. **Sue**. Yes, you can sue an attorney, *if you lost money because of the way your lawyer handled your case*. But suing for malpractice is a tough row to hoe. First, you have to show your lawyer mishandled your case and it cost you money. Then, you must show that the mistake lost you money that you would not have if your lawyer had handled your case properly in the first place. \ ***Disclaimer****:* *This answer is not a substitute for professional legal advice or a solicitation to offer legal advice regarding specific facts.
