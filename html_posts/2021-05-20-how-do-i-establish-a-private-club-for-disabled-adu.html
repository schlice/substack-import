**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} I was a little sleepy when I wrote my original answer, but in thinking about it, I think I have a better understanding. I was thinking of clubs that are membership-based but **open to the general public.** If you have a club that is ***truly*** private (i.e. not open to the general public, facilities open to members only, true selectivity in the membership process, etc), you are not subject to Title III of the ADA. > Many organizations that meet the criteria of a private membership club > may hold events that are open to the general public. Clubs may hold > such events to promote their views, raise funds for a charitable > cause, or recruit potential members. A club may lose its exemption on > a temporary basis; for example, if an organization holds a charitable > fundraiser, opening its facility and selling foods and beverages to > the general public for one evening every five years, it may only be > subject to Title III for the purposes of that specific event. It's interesting though, your requirements under Title III would be to make your club accessible to everyone; however, the people you are excluding do not need accommodations. So even if you do open your club temporarily to the general public (like to do a fundraiser), there wouldn't be anything special you would need to do to bring your club into Title III compliance. Your club would already be *de facto* Title III compliant. **TL;DR: Private membership clubs do not have to comply with the ADA as they are not "places of public accommodation."**[\[1\]](#ihEcN){#cite-ihEcN .citation_link} Footnotes [\[1\]](#cite-ihEcN){.citation_index} [[Private Clubs Under the Americans With Disabilities Act](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=747efd&url=https://adata.org/factsheet/private-clubs-under-americans-disabilities-act){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="adata.org"}]{.qlink_container}
