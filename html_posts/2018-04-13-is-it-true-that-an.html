**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **Is it true that an \"analysis of data from 1988 to 1997 showed that states with \'high\' gun ownership had three times the rate of homicide than states with few guns?\"** It may be true, in exactly the same way that the number of people who drowned in a pool in a given year shows a correlation to the number of films in which Nicolas Cage appeared in during that year. :::: ui_qtext_image_outer ::: qtext_image_wrapper ![](images/qimg-a8225c8ef515efe9768d6143f54b92f0){.landscape .qtext_image .zoomable_in_feed master_src="images/qimg-a8225c8ef515efe9768d6143f54b92f0" master_w="2080" master_h="820"} ::: :::: *(Chart courtesy* [[*Tyler Vigen*](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=50be2c&url=http://www.tylervigen.com/spurious-correlations){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="tylervigen.com"}]{.qlink_container}*)* So clearly, we must stop Nicolas Cage from appearing in movies so that people will stop drowning in pools. Right? Clearly, that's not right. But why not? **Because correlation does not equal causation.** This is sometimes called the "[[false cause](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=22885e&url=https://yourlogicalfallacyis.com/false-cause){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="yourlogicalfallacyis.com"}]{.qlink_container}" logical fallacy. Just because states with high gun ownership *may* have higher rates of homicide **does not** mean that rates of gun ownership **causes** higher rates of homicide.
