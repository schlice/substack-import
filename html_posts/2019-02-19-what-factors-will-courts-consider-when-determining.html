**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **What factors will courts consider when determining whether Trump's emergency declaration to build a wall is constitutional?** This will be a quick case. It's highly unlikely that the courts will rule the National Emergencies Act is unconstitutional. Let's take a brief look at the history of the National Emergencies Act. Presidents were declaring national emergencies well before the National Emergencies Act was passed, but those emergencies were *endless*, and the President was assuming power that he did not have, but no one stopped him because *he's the President*. This is partly because we in the United States have elevated the Presidency to such a level that we basically have, almost, an elected king. Did we mean to do this? Some may have, but I think it's mostly unintentional. Regardless, presidents assume authority they do not have, and if no one stops that President from doing so, then presidents will continue to assume that authority, unchecked. This happened with sending troops places because he's the Commander in Chief, even though Congress is supposed to be able to declare wars. So Congress passed the War Powers Act to check the President, and add some procedure around limited Presidential deployments and reporting back to Congress later. When it came to national emergencies, Congress did the same thing in writing the National Emergencies Act. They put in place some mandatory reporting requirements, and gave themselves a way to limit national emergencies by giving Congress the ability to review active national emergencies and to end them with a joint resolution. However, in doing so (both with the War Powers Act and the National Emergencies Act), Congress essentially provided the legal framework under which presidents are *authorized under law* to declare national emergencies. To that end, Congress essentially *legalized* and *codified* the process to declare national emergencies. Congress also delegated certain Congressional powers to the President to use during periods of national emergency. They wrote those powers into the law. There are approximately 470 Federal laws which contain provisions which a president can activate during time of war or declared national emergency. Over 130 specific powers are granted to the president to use during those national emergencies. Congress **did not** put any guardrails around the President in his ability to declare a national emergency. They give wide latitude to the President to determine when a national emergency is necessary. Presidents have other things they can do besides declaring national emergencies, also codified in law. They can declare "public health emergencies" which release certain money to the Department of Health and Human Services. They can declare areas of "natural disaster" which releases money to the Federal Emergency Management Agency (FEMA), among a few other things codified in law that presidents can do without Congressional approval. Since the National Emergencies Act was passed, presidents since Carter have declared 54 national emergencies, 28 of which are still active. The argument they are trying to make now is that Congress allotted a certain amount of money to the border barrier, and Trump is using his declaration of a national emergency to bypass Congress and get more money for something the people do not want, and this is an unconstitutional allocation of money, which is not allowed under Article I. However, there is a strong case to be made that the President is specifically aiding the military in their counterdrug and illegal immigration issue on the southern border, which is a different mission than the one for which Congress allocated funding. Additionally, Congress already granted this authority under the National Emergencies Act, and now that he is doing something they don't approve of, they want to declare it unconstitutional. If there is a strong odor of abuse of power with a president overstepping his authority in using the National Emergencies Act when Congress wouldn't give him enough money, there is just as strong an odor of bad faith in Congress wanting to declare the National Emergencies Act unconstitutional just because a president is doing something they don't like. The correct thing for Congress to do is to impeach the President for abusing his power, not to try to go through the courts to declare unconstitutional a law that they wrote. I think they're not going to be happy with the results. But why go this route instead of impeachment? This Democratic House *just can't give Trump a win*. They simply cannot allow him to build the wall. If he gets this campaign promise enacted, he is virtually guaranteed to win re-election in 2020. If he gets this, he's a shoo-in for a second term. The only way the Democrats have a shot at re-taking the White House in 2020 is to deny him the wall. Oh, don't misunderstand. They will impeach him anyway, right before the 2020 elections. My crystal ball says that Mueller will release his findings in the summer of 2020, and the Democrats will have Articles of Impeachment passed through the House by September. They're hoping, of course, that the specter of impeachment will cast enough of a cloud over Trump that they can pull out a win.
