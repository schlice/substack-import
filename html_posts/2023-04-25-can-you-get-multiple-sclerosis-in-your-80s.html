**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} Yes. It is called "Late-Onset MS" and it is fairly rare. One [[study](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=c2ac9b&url=https://doi.org/10.1590/0004-282x20170070){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="doi.org"}]{.qlink_container} shows that as many as 4% of MS patients are diagnosed with Late-Onset MS, which is characterized as a primary diagnosis of MS after age 50. I actually knew a PA in Colorado who was diagnosed with Late-Onset MS. He was in his 70s and already had heart trouble, so he and his neurologist decided that they would not treat it. Treatment is the same whether you're 40 or 80, but not treating MS diagnosed in your 80s is also an option. There is a risk/benefit calculation you have to take: how disruptive to my life will the medicine be in order to receive a moderate ability to slow down the progression of the disease? At a certain point, the interference with normal life and side effects caused by the medication becomes too great. But that's a decision between you and your neurologist.
