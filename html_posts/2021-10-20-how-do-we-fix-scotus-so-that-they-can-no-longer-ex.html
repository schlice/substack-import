**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} SCOTUS does not extend rights to anyone. Unless, of course, you're talking about *Griswold v. Connecticut,* the most recent example of SCOTUS extending rights to anyone. *Griswold* was the last case in which SCOTUS invented a right to give to someone, and that was the right to privacy. This right does not exist anywhere in the Constitution, but SCOTUS at the time found it. Where, you might ask? In the "emanations and penumbras" of the Constitution. Basically, it works like this. Let's assume a hotel has a sign which says "No Dogs, Cats, or Birds Allowed." One type of SCOTUS justice would read that sign and say, "Your pet snake is allowed because the sign does not say that snakes are not allowed. If the owner wanted to disallow snakes, he would have put 'Snakes' on the sign." These type of justices are known as "textualists" or "originalists." They believe that the Constitution means exactly what it says, its interpretation should be based on the common understanding when it was written, and if you want it to mean something else, you have to modify the Constitution using one of the documented methods to do so. A second type of SCOTUS justice would read the same sign and think, "Well, the person who wrote that sign was generally trying to exclude any sort of common pet from the hotel, so your pet snake is therefore not allowed." These types of justices are generally known as "non-originalists." They believe that the Constitution should be interpreted in accordance with the climate of the day, not based on the interpretation when it was written, because the framers indicated that they didn't want their specific intentions to control interpretation. It is the non-originalists who fashioned the findings in *Griswold*, the most classic example of imputing meaning *into* the Constitution instead of reading it for exactly what it says. Therefore, **to stop SCOTUS from extending rights to anyone, you must remove anyone who has a non-originalist mindset**. The Constitution means exactly what it means, and if the people want it to mean something else, they can amend it. It's been amended before, even very recently (i.e. within the last 30 years), so it can be amended again. Yes, it is not easy. But it was not designed to be easily amended.
