--- title: "If a police officer mistakenly tries to arrest someone who is not breaking the law, and the citizen does not comply, can the citizen be charged with resisting arrest even though the reason for arrest was in error?" date: "2018-03-01" tags: ["policy-analysis", "law-enforcement", "law"] layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} *"You may beat the rap, but you can't beat the ride."* Police officers have a lower threshold than courts do when they place someone under arrest. Police only need to have "**probable cause**" in order to arrest someone. Probable cause means that there must be a *reasonable basis* for believing that the suspect committed a crime. This is why the person is brought in front of a judge for *arraignment*, a process which involves reviewing the officer's cause for arrest and determining if there is enough evidence to hold the suspect over for trial. The judge will determine if the police did, in fact, have probable cause to make the arrest. If they did not, the person must be released. Later, when the person appears in front of the judge at his or her trial, it then becomes the job of the prosecution to *prove* *beyond a reasonable doubt* that the person did, in fact, commit the crime. Remember too, the erroneous nature of an arrest is only determined by a competent court in that jurisdiction; it is not determined during the arrest. Therefore, since no one, not the suspect, not the police, not the supervisor, no one other than a court can determine whether or not the reason for arrest was legally in error, you must operate under the *presumption* that the reason for the arrest is *reasonable*. It doesn't have to be *right*, it only has to be *reasonable*. Therefore, resisting arrest because ***you think*** that the officer does not have probable cause is illogical, since it is the judge who determines whether or not the officer did, in fact, have probable cause. You have to be arrested first before the judge can make that determination. Therefore, you can be charged with resisting arrest ***even if the basis for the arrest was in error.***
