**Content:** [There\'s not much I can add to [[Cliff\'s](https://www.quora.com/profile/Cliff-G-13)]{.qlink_container} answer. I\'d say your best bet at this point is just to pay it and move on. It\'s probably going to cost you more to fight it because you may not be able to fight it remotely (i.e. if your remote attorney can\'t get the charge resolved without a court hearing, you\'d have to go to California to appear in court with your attorney, so you\'ll have travel costs as well as attorney\'s fees).\ \ Do you think it will be cheaper to hire an attorney to argue on your behalf, and risk that they may not be able to resolve the ticket without a hearing, in which case you\'ll have to travel back to CA to appear in court? I think it\'s far cheaper and more convenient to just pay it.\ \ At this point, it\'s just a fine. Points are generally not assessed on red light camera violations. But even if they are, it would be relatively low. And if you don\'t drive much anyway, it\'s not something to be seriously worried about.\ \ Additionally, the fine is assessed against the registered owner (or renter) of the vehicle at the time the infraction occurred. It makes no difference who was driving the car. If you were the primary driver on the rental agreement, you are getting the ticket. If the car was not a rental, you should not have received the ticket in the first place, because it\'s not your car.\ \ If you\'re saying it\'s not a rental, and it\'s not your license plate, then it should be pretty straightforward to get resolved. The extended time can be explained by the move out of state.\ \ So I think your best bet is to contact a traffic violation attorney in California and see if they are willing to try to resolve the issue for less than \$780. If they can\'t do it, just pay it and be done with it.]{.rendered_qtext}
