**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **What role does gut health appear to play in psychiatric and neurological disorders?** At some point, take some time and review [[the gut-brain connection](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=365aac&url=https://www.google.com/search?q%3Dthe%2Bgut%2Bbrain%2Bconnection%26oq%3Dthe%2Bgut%2Bbrain%2Bconnection){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="google.com"}]{.qlink_container}. There's really too much information to re-post here. But it's good stuff, and it's not just marketing material from supplement makers. There are some very interesting papers from [[Johns Hopkins](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=3d57f3&url=https://www.hopkinsmedicine.org/health/healthy_aging/healthy_body/the-brain-gut-connection){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="hopkinsmedicine.org"}]{.qlink_container} and [[Harvard](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=34553a&url=https://www.health.harvard.edu/diseases-and-conditions/the-gut-brain-connection){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="harvard.edu"}]{.qlink_container} discussing the CNS-ENS connection and our new understandings of these. But if you've ever had "butterflies in your stomach," or if you've ever had a "gut feeling" about something, you intuitively know that there is a connection between the gut and the brain. Yet only recently have we discovered some of the real insights into this connection. Some brief highlights: - Along with the central nervous system (CNS) and the peripheral nervous system (PNS), we also have the enteric nervous system (ENS). This is sheaths of nerve fibers which surrounds the entire GI system from esophagus to anus. - The ENS does the majority of the "heavy lifting" in controlling the digestion process: releasing enzymes, contracting muscles, etc. - There is a very tight connection between the ENS and the CNS. Just as a distressed mind can send signals to, and influence, the intestines, a troubled intestine can send signals to, and influence, the brain. The result: a distressed intestine can lead to psychological symptoms like depression and anxiety. In short, we knew that a troubled brain could result in an upset GI system, but we're learning now that it works both ways. As far as neurologic disorders, think about this: drugs meant to affect the brain, like SSRIs, also induce GI changes. Irritable Bowel Syndrome, or IBS, is partly caused by too much serotonin in our gut. You might also think of IBS as a "mental illness" of the ENS.[\[1\]](#YMgoP){#cite-YMgoP .citation_link} We still have much to learn about this strange and complex interplay between the "big brain" and the "little brain," but what we have learned so far is very enlightening, and can help us in our understanding and treatment of some very hard-to-manage diseases. We have much to learn. For example, if there is such a connection between the gut and the brain, could poor gut health be partly responsible for other diseases within the brain, such as Parkinson's and MS? While probably not the primary cause, they may definitely be related. More research is needed into this fascinating area of medical study.
