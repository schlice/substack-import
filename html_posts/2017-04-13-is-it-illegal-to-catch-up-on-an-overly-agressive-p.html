--- title: "Is it illegal to catch up on an overly agressive police intervention?" date: "2017-04-13" tags: ["policy-analysis", "law-enforcement", "law"] layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} Police takedowns look inherently violent. They look aggressive. They look painful. In some respects, they are intended to look this way. They present an image of force, an image of control. They are designed to remove the threat, which is not so much physical. The threat is *mental*. A person who has the will to resist will continue to resist. Once that person has mentally decided that it is not in his best interest to resist, then he will stop. But that action looks very aggressive. What you may see as a "brutal arrest" is in fact a lawful takedown procedure, executed correctly, and when performed correctly, stops the aggressor without causing excessive physical harm (there may be some minor cuts and bruises, but no major broken bones, no internal damage, etc) **and** convinces the aggressor that continuing in his resistance will not net him any additional gain. *That's* the big victory: getting the aggressor to *believe* that he should not continue being aggressive. And that's only the beginning, as we have only discussed appearances here. Legally, you have no authority to intervene when a police officer is carrying out his lawful duties. "But a brutal arrest isn't lawful!" you may say. It may be lawful, it may not be lawful. The only entity in the United States which has the legal authority to decide whether or not it is lawful is a court of law. Not you, not the police officer, not the suspect. Only the court. It's up to the police to get the suspect into custody and in front of a court so it can make that determination. *So what can you do?* Your best option is not to intervene. Your best option is to **record everything you see**. If you don't have a phone that can record video, **be a good witness**. Remember everything you can, in as much detail as you can. Remember to answer the who/what/when/where/why questions when you are focusing on remembering what was happening.
