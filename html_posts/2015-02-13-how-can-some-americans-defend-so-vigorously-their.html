**Content:** [Your argument is known as a [[false equivalence](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=d6f1d5&url=http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/False_equivalence){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="wikipedia.org"}]{.qlink_container} so it\'s easy to see how you can get caught up in believing that these two ideas (gun control and mandatory vaccinations) are equivalent. They both are related to rights and whether or not you have the free exercise thereof. Other than that, there is no relation between the two concepts.\ \ First, I didn\'t see any right to be free from vaccinations in the Bill of Rights. \"The right of the people to be free from vaccinations shall not be infringed.\" I missed that amendment. There is, however, a right to keep and bear arms in the Constitution, right after the amendment that allows you free speech and the free exercise of religion.\ \ Second, owning or carrying a firearm is not a public health issue; not getting vaccinated is. You benefit from the vaccinations of others even if you choose not to vaccinate; as the population density increases, the number of vaccinated individuals assists you with staying disease-free, because others will be less likely to transmit the disease. You not getting vaccinated risks a public health pandemic, allowing the disease to spread throughout the population to people who can\'t be vaccinated because of immune system complications and other reasons. Your *choosing* to be unvaccinated threatens them. This is where the call for mandatory vaccinations comes into play.\ \ No such similar threat exists when you carry a firearm. In fact, the opposite effect happens: when you carry a firearm, others around you become safer. You benefit from the safety of the person carrying a firearm; criminals will think twice before acting in your vicinity, thus making you safer.]{.rendered_qtext}
