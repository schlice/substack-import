**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} An excellent question! Time for a history lesson. So marriage has existed for a very, very long time. The first recorded evidence of marriage ceremonies dates back to about 2350 B.C. in Mesopotamia. Over the next several hundred years, marriage became a widespread institution embraced by the ancient Hebrews, Greeks, and Romans. In other words, marriage started as a religious institution. As you noted, "marriages" are performed by a religious figure in a religious building as part of a religious ceremony. According to the Genesis creation account, God instituted marriage after the creation of the first woman, Eve, from Adam, the first man. The first marriage was based on a covenant promise. So why is the state involved? In the United States, the federal government formally recognized marriage in law for the first time with the passage of the Revenue Act of 1913. In 1929, all states had laws regarding marriage licenses. In 1933, married women were granted the right to citizenship independent of their husbands. The state, in the early 1900s, started licensing and regulating marriage. They also started performing "marriage" ceremonies of their own, without a church involved. So again, why does the state care what churches do? Why does the state have to *sanction* a church function? There is one overarching reason why states license marriage: **sex between men and women regularly produces children.** The reason why states care about marriage is because of its **generativity**. The state licenses married couples because it provides an easy way to determine who the parents of children are. The state has a compelling interest in ensuring that children have well-known and easily recognized parents, and even today, most children are born to people who are, indeed, married. Once a child is born, if the parents are married, the state *assumes* that the married couple are the biological parents of that child. They are called the "presumptive parents." The only way we can conclusively determine who a parent is is for a person to claim that they are the child's parent, or for one of the presumptive parents to claim that they are not the child's parent, and then blood tests ensue. The paradigm of presumptive parents eliminates the need to do blood tests every time a child is born in order to establish parental identity. State-sanctioned marriage provides this. \ So now that we know why the state stuck its nose into, and co-opted, the religious institution of marriage, we can look at your question. You said "Since religion is the real source of marriage (a religious person performs the ceremony in a religious place as part of a religious ceremony), how do you get to say that homosexuals can't marry?" Well, the answer is obvious. Since it's a religious ceremony, the church in question gets to make its own rules. If that church says that homosexuals cannot marry, then they can't. And if you look at the (very basic outlined) history of marriage I gave above, you'd see that the state's interest in marriage has to do with children being produced by sex between men and women. ***This is a very specific issue which is not something that homosexual couples have to worry about.*** Therefore, marriage, in a civil sense, makes no sense to the homosexual couple. More importantly, allowing homosexuals to marry, when the institution of marriage doesn't make sense for them, tells the world that marriage is less about **the needs of children** and more about **the desires of adults**. Marriage isn't about recognizing loving relationships. The state has no compelling interest in recognizing any relationship, let alone loving ones. If this were the case, then there are plenty of relationships which should also qualify for marriage that currently do not. Marriage isn't even about raising children. Because if that's the case, there are plenty of other family arrangements in which children are being raised in which marriage should be applied, and it is not. I will say that the states attached a lot of benefits to marriage in order to encourage adults to structure their lives in such a way as to benefit children (who, as we already said, they have an interest in ensuring are cared for). To that end, marriage is an easy way for homosexual couples to gain those benefits too, especially if they adopt children. Marriage is about generativity, when men and women have sex and create children. The marriage helps the state presume who the parents of that child are, and homosexuals never have that problem.
