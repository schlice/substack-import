--- title: "How can I tell the difference between civilian and military aircraft with only my eyesight?" date: "2017-01-27" tags: ["uncategorized"] layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} Unless you know the actual aircraft (model, manufacturer, etc), it would be challenging to distinguish a military aircraft from a civilian aircraft while in flight. If the aircraft is close enough for you to see markings, you might be able to make out military designations. For example, Air Force aircraft will usually say "USAF" on them somewhere. You may also see this image on the aircraft: :::: ui_qtext_image_outer ::: qtext_image_wrapper ![](images/qimg-a8d817ad59ead70ce4a8ea1497062da7){.landscape .qtext_image .zoomable_in_feed master_src="images/qimg-a8d817ad59ead70ce4a8ea1497062da7" master_w="512" master_h="271"} ::: :::: This is called a *roundel.* Military aircraft used these for quick visual identification, allowing others to see quickly which nation or alliance the aircraft belonged or was aligned with. The image above is the current roundel in use by the Air Force, Army, Navy, and Marine Corps. We have been using roundels on aircraft since the early 1900's. :::: ui_qtext_image_outer ::: qtext_image_wrapper ![](images/qimg-64d03d60e16ccebcc67a8335b6a4188b){.landscape .qtext_image .zoomable_in_feed master_src="images/qimg-64d03d60e16ccebcc67a8335b6a4188b" master_w="550" master_h="384"} ::: :::: This Curtiss JN-3 "Jenny" has a red star on the tail, and this image is from 1915. The first recorded use of roundels was the red star on the vertical stab, used during the [[Pancho Villa Expedition](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=a1a876&url=https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pancho_Villa_Expedition){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="wikipedia.org"}]{.qlink_container}, which was approximately a year before the US joined World War I. :::: ui_qtext_image_outer ::: qtext_image_wrapper ![](images/qimg-62e73951b6f5c8f1dc3c0e12d4a96c79){.landscape .qtext_image .zoomable_in_feed master_src="images/qimg-62e73951b6f5c8f1dc3c0e12d4a96c79" master_w="1689" master_h="1216"} ::: :::: You can see this A-10 has a *subdued* roundel on the side. There's little doubt who flies this pig, so the roundel is more of a marking of historical and traditional significance rather than identification of a US Armed Forces aircraft. Note that this roundel will also be present on the top and bottom sides of the wings, for easy visual identification. Interesting USAFA side note: during our freshman year, when we were being filled with knowledge (รก la drinking from a firehose), we used to do *aircraft identification*. At the time, they would use an overhead projector and flick it on and off, or rapidly display and cover an aircraft silhouette. We would have to identify the manufacturer, the model, and the primary mission designation. So for example, if we saw this: :::: ui_qtext_image_outer ::: qtext_image_wrapper ![](images/qimg-de1e984576a354941b024a6f00c82978){.portrait .qtext_image .zoomable_in_feed master_src="images/qimg-de1e984576a354941b024a6f00c82978" master_w="400" master_h="720"} ::: :::: we would say, "Rockwell International B-1B Lancer/Excalibur, primary mission: low altitude penetration bombing." Fun. But now I can look up at an aircraft in flight and almost always say exactly what it is.
