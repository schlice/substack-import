**Content:** [Since you live in India, Intel would have to take you to court in India. I\'m not sure how strong cybersquatting laws are in India, so I can\'t comment on whether or not you have a chance of winning there.\ \ But there are other avenues for Intel to pursue. The United Nations (in which India is a member nation) has a copyright agency known as the [[World Intellectual Property Organization](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=a7a03e&url=https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Intellectual_Property_Organization){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="wikipedia.org"}]{.qlink_container}, which hears and settles international disputes over cybersquatting. In 2007, the organization said that 84% of the claims brought since 1999 were settled in the complaining party\'s favor.\ \ Basically, world opinion is that cybersquatting is frowned upon, and the squatter will only be refunded reasonable name acquisition costs. However, if they end up filing lawsuit against you, you may also be saddled with court costs, which would be much more than what you paid for the domain name.]{.rendered_qtext}
