Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} I think this is a really great question. It illuminates one of the major misconceptions about Creationists vs Evolutionists: that Creationists are "anti-science." The media perpetuates this myth, through tropes like the ones immortalized in the book/movie *Inherit the Wind* which fictionalized the 1925 Scopes "monkey trial." The trope goes like this: here come those crazy Fundamentalist types imposing their religious dogma and ignoring objective science. Let's be clear about this: Creationists are not anti-science. This debate is often couched as a *religion vs science* or *Bible vs science* debate, when it is nothing of the sort. It is actually a *good science versus bad science* debate. And Creationists certainly do not corner the market on bad science. Here's just one example of Evolutionist bad science: Science is a search for causes. Logically, there can only be two types of causes: intelligent or non-intelligent. When we look at the question of the First Life, Evolutionists rule out the idea of an intelligent cause before they even look at the evidence. Their conclusions are pre-loaded into their assumptions. Spontaneous generation of life by natural processes *must* be the cause of life because they don't consider any other options. *This isn't an example of good science.* Creationists do believe in **good science**. Since we know that Creationists do believe in good science, there is no reason to believe that Creationists would not get vaccinated. There is nothing in the Creationist worldview which would argue against vaccination. Believing that God controls all doesn't mean that we shouldn't use our brains (also given to us by God) to impart a little bit of protection to ourselves. That's simply being smart. Believing that God has already pre-ordained an outcome doesn't mean that we shouldn't take precautions. That's just fatalism, not good science. Some people I know who refused the vaccine simply **don't believe that the implementation of this vaccine qualifies as** ***good science*.** They were worried about the lack of sufficient product testing as was done with other vaccines in the past. They worried about the rushed implementation. They worried about the unknown long-term effects of the delivery method. But they don't avoid vaccination *because* they don't believe in science. They simply said that they would risk it, and if they contracted COVID, it would be God's will whether or not they lived. And I know at least one person in my circle of acquaintances who *did* die in exactly this way. But she died with her eyes wide open, making her own choices in her own life. I can tell you from personal experience that I (a Creationist) was fully vaccinated. And I still got COVID anyway. I simply believe that this was all part of God's plan for me. I don't blame the vaccines. I don't think that the vaccines are fake. I don't think that it's some sort of massive government conspiracy. I just believe that it was all part of the plan. God has a plan for me, and it included contracting COVID at Christmas. I don't know the details of his plan; God doesn't often reveal the details. But I know he has a plan, because he has never failed to keep any of his promises, and he promises us that he has a plan for us, a plan of fullness, not of harm, to give us a future and a hope (Jeremiah 29:11). \ Original question: Do Creationists get vaccinated? If so, why?
