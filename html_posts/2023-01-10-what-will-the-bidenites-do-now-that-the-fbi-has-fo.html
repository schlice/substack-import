Because they've shown in the past just how objective and clear-thinking they are... /s They will absolutely downplay the documents (EDIT: [[they did](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=59c8e5&url=https://www.cnn.com/2023/01/10/politics/classified-documents-joe-biden/index.html){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="cnn.com"}]{.qlink_container}), but the explanation for why Biden had classified documents in his private office at the University of Pennsylvania is ***entirely different from why Trump had classified documents at Mar-a-Lago.*** Normally, when a President (or Vice President) leaves office, a third party contractor packs up all of their documents and delivers them to the person's personal residence. The documents are then hand-sorted by the person's representative and a representative from the National Archives, and they collectively determine, for each document, whether it goes to the person's private collection (or into their Presidential Library), or is relinquished to the government to be filed at the National Archives. While this isn't how it's supposed to happen under the rules in the Presidential Records Act, this is how it has actually happened for every single president since the law was passed, and the system seems to work acceptably. That document haul has routinely included classified documents, which are simply reverted back to the government. This is how Trump ended up with classified documents in his possession. But the possession of documents in Biden's personal office at the University of Pennsylvania is, as of yet, unexplained. Biden used that office while he was an honorary professor at the University from 2017 to 2019. Of course, as was already mentioned, the White House is downplaying these documents, classifying them as "fewer than a dozen files," none of which are "particularly sensitive" and "not of high interest to the intelligence community." However, this is different in a number of very key ways: - This is "fewer than a dozen files" which means it's unlikely to be a bunch of useless documents just shuffled someplace in a massive document dump. They're more likely to be specifically taken to that location by the principal (in this case, Biden). So he can't claim innocence; he can't claim he didn't know they were there (even though he already has). The small number of files indicates that they were likely deliberately brought there. - These files were **TS/SCI** (Top Secret, Sensitive Compartmentalized Information) sensitivity, the release of which could cause "***exceptionally grave damage to the United States or her allies***." Files which are "not particularly sensitive" are never labeled as TS/SCI. - And I really don't care what the intelligence community finds interesting, because whether or not something is interesting to some rando intelligence analysts does not determine its classification level.
