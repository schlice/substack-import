**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **Why do victims of PB (police brutality) sue police departments or the city instead of the officers directly? It makes more sense to punish the policemen instead of the PD or city?** Short answer: they can't. While they are on the job, police officers are protected by what's known as *qualified immunity.* Qualified immunity is a doctrine that states that so long as the police are not violating an established law, and they are acting under color of law, they cannot be personally sued for actions that they take as part of their job. It extends to all government officials, and basically says they can't be sued unless you can show that they violated \"clearly established statutory or constitutional rights of which a reasonable person would have known\". It was instituted in 1967 to protect police officers from frivolous lawsuits and to protect them from financial liability in situations in which they acted in good faith when the situation was legally unclear. Over the years, it has been expanded. Most notably, the "clearly established law" test has been expanded to require that in order to be "clearly established," there must be case law already on the books regarding that issue. Critics have claimed that this makes it far more difficult to recover damages when police officers violate civil rights, because most suits don't even qualify.
