**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **Full disclosure: l am a gun owner and have held an AK-47, and fired an AR-15. As a progressive, I have advocated closing the "gun show loophole" to mandate universal background checks. Why have so many Conservatives never even HEARD of that loophole?** Because it doesn't exist. Please allow me to explain. There are only two types of people who sell guns: people who sell guns for a living, and people who don't. Those categories are defined by BATFE. If you fall into the former, you must obtain a Federal Firearms License, and every person to whom you sell a gun must be run through NICS (the National Instant Criminal Background Check System). If you fall into the latter group, in the vast majority of states, you do not need to do background checks. For example, I own "a few" guns but I don't sell guns for a living. That doesn't mean I can't sell a gun I own. If I do, I'm not required under Federal law to perform a background check on the buyer. Now my state may require me to do so (and Colorado does), but Federal law does not. This began with the Gun Control Act (GCA) of 1968, which said that unlicensed sellers (i.e. private citizens) can only sell guns to people who live in the same state, and who they reasonably believe are not a prohibited person. It doesn't require that they do background checks. The Firearms Owners Protection Act (FOPA) was passed in 1986, and helped to clarify the GCA. The scope of those who \"engage in the business\" of dealing in firearms (and are therefore required to have a license) was narrowed to include only those who devote \"time, attention, and labor to dealing in firearms as a regular course of trade or business with the principal objective of livelihood and profit through the repetitive purchase and resale of firearms.\" FOPA excluded those who buy and sell firearms to \"enhance a personal collection\" or for a \"hobby,\" or who \"sell all or part of a personal collection.\" The DOJ said that this clarification was necessary to ensure the GCA did not \"place any undue or unnecessary federal restrictions or burdens on law-abiding citizens\[.\]" While the vast majority of sellers at gun shows are actually dealers (who must actually do background checks), there are always a few private party sellers who come to sell their collection. These private party sellers still don't have to do background checks, even if they are selling at a gun show. Still others will bring personal firearms to a gun show, leave them locked in their car, and watch for other attendees to look at a dealer's gun, and then offer to sell their own private firearm for less. The two then proceed out to the car and make the transaction, with no background check needed, so long as the prospective buyer wasn't background checked at the table and denied. The moniker "gun show loophole" makes it appear that *every* sale at a gun show is background check-free, and this simply isn't the case. It's a sloppy name for a provision entered into the GCA to reduce the load on the NICS system. Additionally, a "loophole" is when there is a provision of the law that, while completely legal, produces a completely unintended result. In this case, private sales not requiring background checks is by no means a "loophole." It is an intentional feature added to the law to not \"place any undue or unnecessary federal restrictions or burdens on law-abiding citizens\[.\]" In order to provide all private citizens access to NICS, it would have to be completely redone, as its current implementation couldn't handle the load, and it would pose that unnecessary burden on law-abiding citizens. This would open it up to Constitutional challenges, and I don\'t think lawmakers want to risk the whole thing being struck down as unconstitutional.
