**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} it\'s an interesting question. What if the government conditioned your ability to vote on whether or not you had received the vaccine? Right away, I see a few problems. - There are certain groups of people who simply cannot receive a vaccine. These people may have medical conditions that preclude them from getting vaccinated. Unless you have made some exception for these people, you instantly disenfranchised them, which is unconstitutional. - Next, there are certain people with legitimate religious exemptions. You have just disenfranchised those voters as well, which is also unconstitutional. - Finally, and this is a big one, at least 100 million people in the United States have already contracted COVID-19 and have recovered. And the jury is still out on whether or not it is medically advisable for somebody who already has COVID-19 antibodies to take the vaccine. The prevailing medical opinion is, "Well it can\'t hurt," but that\'s not necessarily true. From a medical standpoint, it\'s not really necessary for those people to get a vaccine, and there may be a higher risk of side effects in giving the vaccine to people who have already contracted COVID-19. - And just as we do for every other vaccine in this country, we must have allowances for those people who have legitimate, science-based objections to this vaccine. It just seems like this is yet another scheme to try to find a way to "convince" people to get the vaccine against their will. Maybe if we can condition it on something they really really want, maybe they\'ll go get it. But in the vast majority of cases, the unvaccinated are not so because they\'re obstinate. They are unvaccinated by choice. Well-informed, science-based choice.
