**Content:** [MS was, and still is, a diagnosis of \"exclusion.\" You have to \"exclude\" all known causes of the symptoms being experienced before you can conclude that the patient has MS. In the early days, this diagnosis of exclusion was paramount in diagnosing MS.\ \ The diagnostic criteria formerly included two episodes in different functional systems disseminated by space and time. This means that you could not make a positive diagnosis of MS based on one symptom. You had to have multiple symptoms over time to positively diagnose MS. This meant that the disease had to progress before you could begin to effectively treat it.\ \ Diagnostic testing pre-MRI was used in addition to excluding other known conditions. One of the methods that was used was called the \"tub test.\" As you may know, heat exacerbates MS symptoms. Removal of the heat causes the symptoms to decline again. Doctors would put suspected MS patients in a hot tub and see if their symptoms got worse, and then see if they subsided once they were removed from the tub. If their symptoms exhibited this property, doctors suspected MS.\ \ The LP (lumbar puncture or \"spinal tap\") was also used, looking for the protein remnants from demyelination. LPs are still used today to add to the \"body of evidence\" that suggests a person has a demyelinating condition like MS. Today, however, the MRI is the primary visual diagnostic test; so much so, that in 2010, the McDonald criteria for diagnosis of MS were updated to include one positive symptom and a positive MRI.]{.rendered_qtext} ***Disclaimer:*** ***This answer is not professional medical advice****, nor is it a substitute for professional medical advice.
