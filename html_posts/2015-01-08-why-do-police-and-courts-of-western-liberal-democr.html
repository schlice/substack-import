**Content:** [There are definitely limitations on free speech. You can\'t shout \"Fire!\" in a crowded movie theater if there is no fire (the classic example). You can\'t incite a riot. You also can\'t lie about someone (this is a separate crime called slander). So there are limitations on free speech.\ \ Inciting a riot is one thing. Inciting hatred is another thing altogether. Under the Constitution, it is legal to incite hatred. If I speak and it causes you to hate someone else, that\'s legal. If I speak and it causes people to inflict injury on others, that\'s illegal.\ \ In 1969, the Supreme Court developed the \"imminent danger\" test in the [[*Brandenburg v. Ohio*](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=40da47&url=https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brandenburg_v._Ohio){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="wikipedia.org"}]{.qlink_container} case that states, in part, \"the constitutional guarantees of free speech and free press do not permit a state to forbid or proscribe advocacy of the use of force, or of law violation except where such advocacy is directed to inciting imminent lawless action and is likely to incite or produce such action.\" This theory has been tested many times, most recently in 2011 in Snyder v. Phelps, where hate speech was again protected because it did not immediately incite dangerous or lawless action.\ \ The Internet is also being tested as to how the Constitution applies to speech there. But regardless, the speech has to incite \"imminent lawless action\" in order to be a 1A violation, which, while hate-filled, Islamic clerics do not do. Therefore, their speech is protected under 1A.]{.rendered_qtext}
