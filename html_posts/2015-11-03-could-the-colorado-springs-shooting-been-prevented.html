**Content:** [So your thought here is that if we ban open carry, then the police would have responded to a MWG call (man with a gun) and they would have stopped him.\ \ I agree with you on principle. In the city, there is really no reason you need to be carrying a rifle. In my humble opinion, anyone carrying a rifle is fishing for attention.\ \ But what about someone safely carrying a pistol in a holster on his/her belt? (Yes, in Costco, I saw a woman with a shopping cart, three kids in her cart, and a pistol on her hip). Clearly, they\'re not aiming to hurt anyone, they are just carrying for self-defense. Should we disarm them as well? Or should we simply get rid of open carry altogether and make everyone carry concealed?\ \ The issue the Colorado legislature was attacking by allowing open carry is twofold.\ ]{.rendered_qtext} 1. The state is very large, and not every portion of the state is covered by a city. Cities are blotches on the state map. The rest of the area is covered by farms and ranches. Should we force ranchers and farmers to leave their rifles and pistols at home, which they rightly use for protection while out on their acerage? 2. The state is a \"shall issue\" state when it comes to concealed weapons. While it may not be terribly expensive (all things considered) to get a concealed weapons permit, you do have to attend (and pay for) your own training classes, you have to pay for background checks, and you have to pay adminisrtative costs. Altogether, starting from scratch, assuming you get good training and not just \"hunter safety\" (which still qualifies in El Paso County), we\'re talking over \$300 to get your permit, which is, in many cases, more expensive than the actual gun itself. Plus, there are annual administrative fees to keep your permit. You\'ve now limited concealed carry to the \"rich.\" Whether or not you believe that people who can afford a permit are \"rich,\" you\'ve essentially ostracized an entire class of citizens: people who can\'t afford a permit. What if they received a gun as a gift? Or as a family heirloom? If they can\'t afford the training and licensing fees, they can\'t conceal a firearm. Denver successfully fought and won the right to override the state open-carry ruling; in the city of Denver, you cannot open-carry, even though the state allows it. Colorado Springs could easily do the same thing, if they choose. The question remains: does the Mayor and City Council believe that there is enough support for such a measure? If they do, perhaps they will propose it.
