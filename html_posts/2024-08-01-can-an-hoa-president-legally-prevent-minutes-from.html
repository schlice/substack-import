--- title: "Can an HOA president legally prevent minutes from being recorded during meetings? Is this to avoid accountability for their actions?" date: "2024-08-01" tags: ["law", "homeowners-assn"] layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} The answer is "it depends." The rules for meetings will be in the Governing Documents, and not all Governing Documents are alike. There may be a provision in your Governing Documents which permits the president to suppress taking the minutes for the meeting. But in the vast majority of cases, this cannot happen. It all comes down to the structure of the HOA. Most HOAs in the United States are 501(c)(4) non-profit corporations and as such, are governed by some individual documents which make up what we call the Governing Documents of the HOA, such as the Articles of Incorporation and the Bylaws. These are standard corporate documents which dictate the structure and operations of the legal entity we call a corporation. You probably aren't even aware, but usually the "official" name of your HOA isn't just your neighborhood name plus HOA. It's usually "neighborhood name" HOA, Inc, because you are a corporation. You have a Board of Directors, you have elections, you have officers, you have committees, you have a budget, and you have Annual Meetings. All of these things are governed by those articles of incorporation and bylaws, which direct how all of those things are to happen. You may have a rule in your Governing Documents which says that minutes must only be taken during the Annual Meeting and are optional for other Board meetings. You may not. Only your Governing Documents will tell you for certain.
