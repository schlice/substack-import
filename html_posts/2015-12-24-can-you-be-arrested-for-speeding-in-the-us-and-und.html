--- title: '"Can you be arrested for speeding in the U.S. and under what conditions?"' date: '2015-12-24' tags: - law layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} As with all answers that involve the differences between individual states, the true answer is \"it depends.\" At a certain point (which is different among states), a moving violation can be judged by a law enforcement officer to be \"careless\" or \"reckless.\" For example, in Colorado, \"careless driving\" means operating a motor vehicle \"in a careless and imprudent manner, without due regard for the width, grade, curves, corners, traffic, and use of the streets and highways and all other attendant circumstances.\" This is a Class 2 misdemeanor, unless the carelessness causes bodily injury, in which case it is a Class 1 misdemeanor. \"Reckless driving\" means operating a motor vehicle \"in such a manner as to indicate either a wanton or a willful disregard for the safety of persons or property.\" While reckless driving in Colorado is an automatic Class 1 misdemeanor, subsequent convictions of reckless driving automatically include a mandatory fine anywhere from \$50 to \$1000, imprisonment from 10 days to 6 months, or both. Note that you don\'t necessarily have to be speeding in order to be convicted of \"careless driving\" or \"reckless driving,\" but at some point, excessive speed is simply careless. Aggravating circumstances surrounding speed may lead an officer to believe that the operation of the motor vehicle was \"careless\" or \"reckless.\" In some states, at a certain amount over the posted speed, this transition is automatic. For example, at 40 miles an hour or more over the posted speed, you no longer get a speeding ticket, but automatically (by statute) get a reckless driving charge. Off the top of my head, I can\'t remember what states have statutory reckless driving statutes.
