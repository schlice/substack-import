**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} An excellent question! I wonder if there's any historical document which records just such an event... Oh wait... Let us turn to the book of Jonah, please, Chapter 1, Verse 1. To try to make this a little easier for the reader to follow along, I won't quote from a Bible version full of thee's and thou's. Instead, I'll quote from the New International Reader's Version. This, by the way, is the entirety of Chapter 1. > 1 A message from the Lord came to Jonah, the son of Amittai. So he went > down to the port of Joppa. There he found a ship that was going to > Tarshish. He paid the fare and went on board. Then he sailed for > Tarshish. And they made promises > to him. > > 17 Now the Lord sent a huge fish to swallow Jonah. And Jonah was in > the belly of the fish for three days and three nights. --- Jonah > 1:1--17 NIRV So yes, if you take the Bible at face-value, sometimes God calls you to do things you don't want to do. In Jonah's case, it was to go to the city of Nineveh and preach against their wickedness. Think about that. It's like today, God told you to go to Vegas and preach against their sin and vice. Would you want to do that? Yeah, me neither. But God told Jonah to go to Nineveh and do just that. And Jonah said, "Yeah, thanks but no thanks." And so he ran down to the wharf at the Port of Joppa and boarded a ship bound for Tarshish (clear on the other side of the Mediterranean). In other words (and as illuminated by the text), Jonah was trying to run away from God. :::: ui_qtext_image_outer ::: qtext_image_wrapper ![](images/qimg-4c03a1e6677e0e39a1eb762b88b653ac){.landscape .qtext_image .zoomable_in_feed master_src="images/qimg-4c03a1e6677e0e39a1eb762b88b653ac" master_w="234" master_h="151"} ::: :::: Except for the pesky fact that *you can't run away from God.* There's nowhere that you can go that is "away" from God. While God may not be causing giant fish to swallow us up for three days or storms to suddenly pick up and then subside just as suddenly in the middle of the Mediterranean, He still is displeased when we do not follow Him. But He doesn't routinely punish us that way any longer, as we are all saved by the blood of Christ. Our sins already have their forgiveness through Christ. He does punish us (what good father doesn't discipline their child?), just not in the same way as He used to, before Christ came. The Bible says this about our current discipline: > As you endure this divine discipline, remember that God is treating > you as his own children. Whoever heard of a child who was never > disciplined? If God doesn't discipline you as He does all of His > children, it means that you are illegitimate and are not really His > child after all. Since we respect our earthly fathers who disciplined > us, should we not all the more cheerfully submit to the discipline of > our heavenly Father and live forever? For our earthly fathers > disciplined us for a few years, doing the best they knew how. But > God's discipline is always right and good for us because it means we > will share in his holiness. No discipline is enjoyable while it is > happening---it is painful! But afterward there will be a quiet harvest > of right living for those who are trained in this way. --- Hebrews > 12:7--11 ESV Note that God does not discipline *everyone*, but He disciplines *His children*. Notice what the passage says: "If God doesn't discipline you as He does all of His children, it means that you are illegitimate and are not really His child after all." In other words, if you are not being disciplined by God, you may not be one of His children. It says that He disciplines all of His children, and some are not His children. It also says that God's discipline is not eternal. There is a period of not-enjoyable discipline, but then there is "a quiet harvest of right living" for believers. This passage thus tells us that hope abounds, even through suffering. If I could sum it up, I'd use the lyrics of the song *Sunday's On The Way* by Take 6: > *So when problems try to bury you (six feet deep),\ > And make it hard for you to pray,\ > It may seem like that Friday night,\ > But Sunday's on the way.* It may be tough while you're going through it, but believers have the hope that our redemption is not far off. God is near to those who love Him.
