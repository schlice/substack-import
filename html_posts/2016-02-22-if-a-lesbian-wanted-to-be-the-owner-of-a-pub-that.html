--- title: '"If a lesbian wanted to be the owner of a pub that accepted just lesbian customers, would the law of your country allow her to exclude straight people?"' date: '2016-02-22' tags: - law layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} In the United States, broadly, we are covered by Federal, state, and local laws called the **Civil Rights Act of 1964**. It states that in all places of \"public accommodation,\" discrimination on the basis of race, color, creed (religion), sex, or national origin is illegal. Note that at the Federal level, there is no protection for sexual orientation. While all states have a version of the Civil Rights Act that matches the Federal statute, only about 20 states have included protection for sexual orientation. And for those that do not have such protection, some cities within those states have included sexual orientation for protection. So if a lesbian owned a pub and wanted to accept just lesbian customers, she would have to do so in a city and state which does not have protections against discrimination on the basis of sexual orientation. Then, she could exclude everyone who is not a lesbian. However, this would run afoul of the law banning discrimination on the basis of sex, so she could still be sued for discrimination for failing to allow a man into the pub (gay or straight). She likely couldn\'t be sued for denying entrance to a straight woman, but denying entrance to any man would be grounds for a discrimination lawsuit under both Federal and state law.
