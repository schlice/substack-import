**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} The answer, as with all legal answers, is "it depends." In most cases in most places around the United States, you are allowed to use a security camera to protect your property, even if it incidentally captures your neighbor's property. That word, *incidentally,* means that the capture of the neighbor's property is *incidental* to the capture of your own property. In other words, if the camera is *primarily* capturing your yard, but it is also *incidentally* capturing your neighbor's yard, then that incidental capture is acceptable. The only time that it would not be acceptable (and a situation in which local code enforcement might be able to step in) is if your camera is *primarily* capturing the neighbor's yard and only *incidentally* (or not at all) capturing your own yard. \ Now, when it comes to **backyards**, we get into much different territory. There is, at least, a modicum of privacy expected in a backyard, especially one surrounded by a privacy fence. There is, at least, an expectation that **from the ground level**, there is limited to no visibility into that backyard. Now, if your neighbor has a two-story house, then it's a foregone conclusion that they can see into your backyard. Whether they use their eyeballs from a second-story window or a security camera is irrelevant. But if they only have a one-story house, and they have positioned their camera in a high location, now you have to go back to what I said in the first section: it depends on what the camera is *primarily* capturing, and what the camera is *incidentally* capturing. \ So how do you deal with this situation? There are a number of ways to address it. You can install more tall stuff. Install privacy netting over the top of your backyard. Or, you could go old school. And by "old school," I mean do what polite society used to do back in the day: ***talk to each other***. Express your concern that their security camera may incidentally be looking into your yard and you're not entirely comfortable with that. Maybe assist them with finding a camera location that more directly captures their own yard while maybe seeing less of yours. If worse comes to worse, hire an attorney in your area who specializes in civil rights law and privacy matters to send a strongly worded registered lawyer letter telling them that you are threatening legal action if they don't move their camera, and then sue them for violations of your right to privacy in your backyard. There are other high-tech solutions you can employ; you can get some really high power infrared floodlights and install them on your house pointed right at their camera. For most surveillance cameras which are tuned to use IR illuminators, any camera looking into your yard will look like they're looking directly into the sun and they won't be able to see a thing.
