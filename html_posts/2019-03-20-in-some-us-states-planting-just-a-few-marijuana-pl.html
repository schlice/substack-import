**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **In some US states, planting just a few marijuana plants for your own use in your basement is legal. Do you think it\'s a good idea to make this a federal law?** When you ask yourself one of these questions (about whether or not something should be federal law), ask yourself the following really important question: *Does the Federal government have a compelling interest in imposing a law on* ***all*** *states?* Perhaps there is a safety issue. Or perhaps uniformity between states is the only way a particular thing will work properly. So: is there a compelling reason that the federal government needs to tell **all** states to allow marijuana? The answer, truly, is **no**. In fact, the Controlled Substances Act, designating controlled substances at a Federal level, is only useful to determine what substances can and can not be prescribed by a doctor (since prescriptions are controlled at a federal level). It's also useful in controlling what happens *between* states (i.e. what substances can and can not move between states). It's not useful to *tell* a *state* what it can and can not do with substances within that state. That should rightly be left to the state to determine. If all of the residents of a state vote to allow marijuana, it should be allowed. If the residents vote to disallow it, it should be disallowed. But this is something which is best left to states to decide on their own. Put another way, something which is good for Oregon may not be good for Illinois. Something which is good for California may not be good for Texas. You get the point. The United States is *huge* and has a varying population across its states. The most effective government is at the state level. It's also important to note that the Controlled Substances Act currently makes it a federal crime to possess marijuana. The federal government (i.e. the FBI and the DEA) can prosecute anyone, in any state, for possession of marijuana. What states are doing by "legalizing" marijuana is removing any state penalties for violating the federal law. In other words, the *state* will not prosecute an individual found to be in violation of this particular facet of the Controlled Substances Act.
