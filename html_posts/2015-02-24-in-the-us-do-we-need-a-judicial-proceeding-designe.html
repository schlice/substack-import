--- title: "In the U.S., do we need a judicial proceeding designed to perform a thorough, public investigation of matters that are of great public interest or importance for which a trial is not possible or appropriate?" date: "2015-02-24" tags: ["policy-analysis", "law-enforcement"] layout: post ---

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} > *Question Details:* > > *A trial seems to be the only routine proceeding we have available to > make a thorough public investigation. A trial can't be held if the > suspect is dead and shouldn\'t be held unless there is strong evidence > that the suspect committed a crime. Grand jury investigations, despite > their recent misuse, are not appropriate because they are not public, > and the only thing that a failure to indict demonstrates is that the > prosecutor didn\'t want an indictment. Yet, there are cases when there > is a need for public examination of all available evidence and a > determination of what happened. Recently, there were the cases of > Michael Brown, Eric Garner, and Tamir Rice. Other cases where I think > such a proceeding would be appropriate include Emma Hernandez and > Margie Carranza, the newspaper carriers shot by Los Angeles police who > mistook them for Christopher Dorner, and Bruce Ivins, who was accused > of sending anthrax-laced letters, but could not be tried because he > was dead. After President Kennedy's assasination, we had the Warren > Commission; it didn't lay all doubts to rest, but it was a lot better > than what we get in cases of lesser national importance.* We already have it, it\'s called an [[*inquest*](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=805146&url=http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inquest){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="wikipedia.org"}]{.qlink_container}. It\'s handled by a coroner. And it shouldn\'t be judicial. Most inquests are quasi-judicial, or non-judicial. But their outcomes function much as the outcome of a grand jury in some areas of the country, while others are more judicial, making the accused liable for arrest. In some jurisdictions, an inquest can be called by a formal request by the public. We should expand the use of the inquest in cases where there is great public interest in the actual cause of death. Your assertion that the grand jury system is broken is also an indication of public distrust of anything that is not done in full public view, even though the Supreme Court has already validated, on several occasions, the validity of the privacy of a grand jury.
