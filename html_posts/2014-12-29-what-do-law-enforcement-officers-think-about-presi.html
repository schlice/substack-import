**Content:** [[[The Bureau of Justice Statistics](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=71a913&url=http://www.bjs.gov/index.cfm?ty%3Dtp%26tid%3D71){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="bjs.gov"}]{.qlink_container} shows that there are just under 500,000 sworn peace officers in the United States. Funding 50,000 cameras touches barely 10% of the officers in the US. So if he wanted body cameras on every sworn officer, he would have needed to fund ten times the amount of cameras he did.\ \ This is purely a token effort by the government to show that they are \"doing something\" without actually doing something.\ \ It will still be up to local departments to actually fund cameras for their officers, since this funding will only get cameras for 10% of their officers.\ \ (ED) I think that the biggest problem departments will face is not the actual cameras themselves. It is funding the infrastructure the department will need to store, manage, and retrieve the video. Additionally, while the funding may buy the initial round of cameras, there is still a \"serviceable life\" that a camera has, plus you have to account for actual wear-and-tear and breakage during normal use. Until the cameras get into regular use, we won\'t know what the actual service life of a camera is. It could be as high as 30% of the cameras in a year needing replacement, and the funding doesn\'t take that into account.\ \ Additionally, we are talking about **petabytes** of data that must be stored. The actual amount of data that is stored is dependent on several factors (how long must non-evidentiary recordings be stored, how often are officers required to record their public interactions, etc). The longer department policy requires cameras to be running, the more storage space will be required to store the videos, and the longer departments keep non-evidentiary recordings, the more space is required. While most non-evidence recordings can usually be deleted after 90-180 days (which is what privacy advocates would like), people can bring complaints against officers any time, and it would have been nice to have the video of that contact that you just deleted. Most departments are not opting for cloud-based storage, as the security isn\'t well defined enough, so all the data will have to be stored in-house.\ \ Beyond just the data storage hardware (which is expensive enough), most departments do not have dedicated Storage Admins on staff, and will have to get approved and budgeted a new tech position just to handle ops data storage (at \$70-\$110K per year). Dash-cam recordings are much smaller and don\'t run all the time in all departments.]{.rendered_qtext}
