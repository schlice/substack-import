**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **If mobile phones and electronics shouldn\'t be on during flights, why do airplanes get equipped with wifi on board?** The problem is not with electronic devices being "on" during flight. The problem has to do with **cellular radios** being on during flight, and the reason has nothing to do with technical incompatibilities between cellular radios and aircraft avionics and automatic flight controls. When cellular telephones first came out, airlines required them to be off for the duration of the flight, because they were "transmitting or receiving devices" and they could potentially interfere with avionics. There were some anecdotal stories of someone receiving a cell phone call in-flight and an aircraft avionics cluster experiencing some strange readings, but there has never been any large-scale testing, as doing so would require massive amounts of clearance to violate FCC rules, inside of a flying airplane. No aircraft manufacturer has yet desired to jump through the regulatory hoops to even test, and the airlines simply found it easier to disallow their use during flight (incidentally, Mythbusters tried to test this with an airplane on the ground, but even that testing was problematic. Ultimately, they didn't find much interference. Maybe with some older planes, but indeed, most planes are shielded pretty well against electromagnetic interference). Additionally, what would you test? Phone hardware changes so frequently, and testing is so slow and methodical, that by the time your findings were released, the industry would have moved on to the next phone. Finally, when you use your cell phone in-flight, you don't stick to just one cell, you bounce from cell to cell mid-flight. If you allowed thousands of people in flight to use cell phones, you'd disrupt the cell towers on the ground, not the airplane. This is why it is the FCC, not the FAA, that makes the rules regarding cell phone use in flight, and prohibits their use. When phones moved into the "smartphone" category, people wanted to use them to listen to music, or play games, or read books, just like any other portable electronic device. Smartphones have an "Airplane Mode" specifically designed to disable all of the radios so that the phone can be used in an offline mode. Phones were then allowed to be activated after takeoff, so long as they were in the "Airplane Mode" or other suitably non-transmitting mode. Finally, with Wi-Fi becoming nearly as ubiquitous as cellular towers, and airlines wanting to provide more value to (read: make more money from) their customers, they began partnering with in-flight Wi-Fi solutions which utilize special in-cabin Wi-Fi antennas. Wi-Fi is a totally different frequency and uses much lower power transmitters than cellular radios. Wi-Fi can be used safely, and more importantly to the airline, a captive Wi-Fi portal can be established inside the aircraft, and the airline can provide a pay portal to access Internet content. Next up in the airline's move to modernize: opening ports for Wi-Fi calling (and charging for the privilege). We will see how well that goes over. It will only be different from the Airphone days in that the price won't be nearly as expensive as it used to be. Probably something like \$5 to \$10 to allow Wi-Fi calling for the entire flight, similar to the price of allowing Internet access for passengers.
