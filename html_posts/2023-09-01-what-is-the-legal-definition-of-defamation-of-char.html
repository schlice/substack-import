Written defamation is called \"libel,\" > while spoken defamation is called \"slander.\" Because written > statements last longer than spoken statements, most courts and > insurance companies consider libel more harmful than slander. > > Defamation is not a crime in most states, but it is a \"tort\" (a > civil wrong, rather than a criminal wrong). The person who has been > defamed (the \"plaintiff\") can sue the person who did the defaming > (the \"defendant\") for damages.[\[1\]](#oEjUA){#cite-oEjUA > .citation_link} When it comes to a former employer and reference checks, there are some things that the former employer **must** provide in order to protect themselves from lawsuit, there are some things that they **cannot** provide, and then there are some things that if they do provide, open them up to the possibility of a lawsuit. Previous employers **must** provide basic information regarding your employment with them. This includes: - Employment dates - Job responsibilities - Salary history - Whether the employee was dismissed or left on their own The information provided should be factual and based on documented evidence of the employee\'s job performance. In other words, if the employer says you're a bad employee, this should only be based on documented performance reports, which you acknowledged. Employers **cannot** provide *personal information*, such as race, religion, age, or disability status. So now to your big question: **if an employer lies about you (i.e. lists false information about you on a reference check), are they liable?** The answer is **yes**. While it is not illegal for an employer to provide a bad reference, that employer crosses the liability line when they provide **untrue** information about a former employer. That employer then becomes liable for a defamation of character lawsuit. In order to prevail in such a lawsuit, several key elements must be proven at trial: - The employer made a false and defamatory statement about you, either spoken or written. - The employer published the statement to a third party, likely the prospective employer, without authorization. - The employer acted with some degree of fault when making the statement (i.e. the characterization of your employment does not align with your signed performance reports). - The statement caused harm to you, likely because you were rejected from the job.
