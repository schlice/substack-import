**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **Can you arrest a cop if he is no longer acting under the color of law?** You totally misunderstood what that phrase means. "Color of law" refers to the appearance of the legal authority to take an action. However, acting with the "color of law" says nothing about the legality of the underlying action. In other words, police act with "color of law" authority, when the underlying action may actually be illegal. For example, let's say the police act with "color of law" authority to arrest someone. If, for example, the arrest was made without probable cause, then the arrest, even though it was made with "color of law" authority, would actually be illegal. The problem is that you can't arrest a police officer for the performance of his or her duties. Arguably, placing someone under arrest is a police officer's duty, and doing so, he is acting "under color of law." Whether or not the actual arrest (or whatever the officer did under color of law) was lawful is a matter of law to be decided by a jury. It's not something you can decide real-time. Also, police can't "divest" themselves of the color of law. Police officers are always police officers 24x7, even when they are off-duty. Therefore, police officers always act with the "color of law," because they always retain their police powers under the law. So technically, a police officer can never act without the color of law. Then, there is the functional aspect of trying to arrest an active police officer. What's the reasonable amount of force you can use to arrest a police officer? Good luck sorting through all the other cops who will come racing to protect their brother/sister, whether or not you think you're legally justified in making an arrest. At best, you'll be arrested and found to be not guilty a few months later at trial. At worst, the police will kill you after you assaulted a police officer thinking that your cell phone is a gun, while the whole while you were yelling something about "color of law."
