**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **I know an attorney who works for Arizona Family Court / DCFS. The attorney talks freely about her client's including names and various detailed information. Is this a HIPAA violation?** No, HIPAA is a law which protects healthcare information. What you're referring to is what is known as **attorney-client privilege**. However, not all conversations between an attorney and a client are covered by attorney-client privilege. > \[T\]he elements necessary to establish the attorney client privilege > generally are: > > 1. The asserted holder of the privilege is (or sought to become) a > client; and > 2. The person to whom the communication was made:is a member of the > bar of a court, or a subordinate of such a member, and in > connection with this communication, is acting as an attorney; and > 3. The communication was for the purpose of securing legal advice. > > There are a number of exceptions to the privilege in most > jurisdictions, chief among them: > > 1. the communication was made in the presence of individuals who were > neither attorney nor client, or was disclosed to such individuals, > 2. the communication was made for the purpose of committing a crime > or tort, > 3. the client has waived the privilege (for example by publicly > disclosing the communication).[\[1\]](#XMmKg){#cite-XMmKg > .citation_link} Footnotes [\[1\]](#cite-XMmKg){.citation_index} [[Attorney--client privilege - Wikipedia](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=d0db25&url=https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attorney%25E2%2580%2593client_privilege){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="wikipedia.org"}]{.qlink_container}
