**Content:** [As the other posters alluded to, Ampyra is a medicine, the first of its kind, designed specifically to treat a symptom of MS; that is, foot drop. Ampyra is an option for people who experience moderate to severe foot drop, and it does not work for everyone as Christine attested to. However, it is a relatively new treatment for MS foot drop.\ \ Several MS medications have fairly recently been approved, such as fingolimod (marketed as Gilenya), which is an oral MS medication, the first to be approved. More oral medications for MS are in the approval pipeline today.\ \ Also recently, Tysabri (natalizumab) was approved for use, but under strict prescribing guidelines due to its propensity to cause PML (progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy), a rare but often fatal viral disease. Tysabri is used to treat MS and Chron\'s disease, and it is believed to work by preventing inflammatory immune cells to pass through the cell layers of the intestines and the blood-brain barrier. Tysabri has proven effective in slowing relapse rates, improving cognitive function, and preventing vision loss. In clinical trials, Tysabri was shown to reduce relapses by 68% vs. placebo, which is the highest relapse reduction rate of any of the approved MS therapies.\ \ The use of LDN (low-dose naltrexone) in MS patients continues, but has not yet been supported by clinical trials of any large scale. There is much anecdotal evidence to support its use, but there is not enough evidence in terms of rigorous study to show that LDN is effective, or even safe. Any use of the drug would be \"off-label\" and doing so would be considered an \"unproven treatment\" which should not be used in place of proven, approved therapies.]{.rendered_qtext} ***Disclaimer:*** ***This answer is not professional medical advice****, nor is it a substitute for professional medical advice.
