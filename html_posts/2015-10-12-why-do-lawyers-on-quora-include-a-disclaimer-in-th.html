Information posted to Quora publicly or sent in an unsolicited > message to a Legal Contributor is not confidential and does not > establish attorney-client relationship without the express consent of > the Legal Contributor.\ \ So in general, people who use Quora are bound by the terms of service on the site, which state very clearly that not only is Quora not to be held liable for any answers, but the answerers of said questions are also not to be held liable. When you create and use an account, you agree to this contract.\ \ Also, under the legal question section, it says very clearly that content is provided for **informational use only**, and no action should be taken based on the content. If someone chooses to take action based on this content, they really lack the legal basis to come back and sue based on simply what they read on a website where the TOS says very clearly that you must not take any action based on this information.\ \ So this covers you in general. However, knowing that laws vary by state, if a lawyer is licensed in a particular state and they require certain disclaimers on any of their academic works, Quora gives them the ability to add a tag on the bottom of each legal post they make that they can modify to meet their state standards. In general, though, most medical/legal question answerers leave their disclaimers to match the boilerplate version Quora provides. Unless your state requires it, the tag on the message is not necessary, since the TOS covers you in general. However, if you have a specific state requirement for specific wording for any of your academic works, the tag system allows you to add that. It\'s always best, however, to drive that point home every time (both in the TOS and the answer itself) that the answer is not specific legal advice, and you should always consult your own lawyer with specific legal questions.
