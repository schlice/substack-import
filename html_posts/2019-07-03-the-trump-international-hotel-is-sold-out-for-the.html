**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **The Trump International Hotel is sold out for the 4th of July Trump extravaganza despite charging double what similar properties are charging. Does this violate the emoluments clause?** Short answer: no. Longer answer: The Emoluments Clause does not prevent a president from running a business. The Emoluments Clause prevents a president from working for (and being compensated by) a foreign government. Trump has already stepped down from the board of directors of the Trump organization, and has placed his assets into trust. Some have argued that he hasn't done enough, because his assets are not in a *blind trust*. However, it's difficult to put the Trump name into a blind trust; the Trump name on a hotel makes money regardless of whether or not the assets are in a blind trust. Now, does this mean that the president can't violate the Emoluments clause using his hotels? Not at all. For example, if the president charges foreign dignitaries triple the going rate at the Trump Hotel and then later on makes a foreign policy change which positively benefits that foreign country, the Justice Department should be looking very closely at whether or not Trump was "paid" (however indirectly) to make that particular policy change. But running a business (and charging *market value* for a hotel room, whatever the market will bear) is not in and of itself a violation of the Emoluments Clause. The Constitution never intended the president to lose his businesses just to be President.
