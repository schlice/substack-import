**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} When Congress passes a law, they may have an intention in mind. They may wish the law to do a certain thing, or have a certain effect. But when it gets through the various writers and legal staff and everyone else who writes, reviews, and approves the law, it may not say exactly what they intended it to say. The court found that neither of these two laws violate Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act. If Biden and Congress were actually trying to make it legal to submit provisional ballots in whatever precinct you wanted, rather than your home precinct, and if they didn't want it to be a felony to submit someone else's ballot for them, they should have written the law to say that. However, as Section 2 is written today, and in accordance with today's ruling, these things do not constitute a denial or a limiting of voting rights based on race, color, or membership in a language minority group. Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act, which was initially adopted as an enforcement mechanism for the 15th Amendment's guarantee that the right to vote cannot be abridged "on account of race, color, or previous condition of servitude," allows voters to seek judicial relief if they believe that a state or local government has denied or limited their voting rights on the basis of their race, color or membership in a language minority group. As amended in 1982, Section 2 now provides that a voting process or requirement that *results in* the abridgment of the right to vote on account of a voter's race, color or language minority status is unlawful, whether or not an intent to discriminate can be proven.[\[2\]](#JRWpz){#cite-JRWpz .citation_link} **TL;DR:** Congress can go back and amend Section 2 if they actually wanted to make the law act in the way they desire. But as the law is written today, the claim that Arizona's voter laws discriminate based on race was not upheld. \ Original question: Biden has called on congress to fix the damage done by the Supreme Court voting right's decision.
