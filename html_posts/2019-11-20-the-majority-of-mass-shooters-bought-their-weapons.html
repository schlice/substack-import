**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **The majority of mass shooters bought their weapons legally. However, most had criminal or mental histories, precluding the purchase of those weapons. How can different agencies share that information so current firearms laws can be enforced properly?** The reason you're having trouble with this is because you have one inaccurate statement, and I'll bold it for you: > However, most had criminal or mental histories, **precluding the > purchase of those weapons.** While it is true that most of the mass shooters we've had so far have had criminal or mental histories, **most of those did not preclude the purchase of weapons.** In order to be precluded from purchasing firearms, you have to be a convicted felon, or have been adjudicated a mental defective in court. There are two incidents that I know of where a prohibited person may have been inadvertently allowed to purchase a weapon: 1. Sutherland Springs: The Air Force may have been responsible to pass information on to NICS, but it's still unclear based on the disposition of his discharge as to whether or not that information exchange was mandatory. 2. Charleston. Dylan Roof was indeed prohibited because of a prescription drug charge, and should have been denied. However, a clerical error led to the investigation not being done within the requisite time frame, and a "default allow" being issued. All of the other shooters did, or would have, passed their background checks and would have been allowed to purchase firearms. Also important to note: the two situations I know of were convicted felons, not mental defectives, even though most of them had mental issues which should have, in some way, told someone that they should probably stay away from firearms. But the system isn't set up that way.
