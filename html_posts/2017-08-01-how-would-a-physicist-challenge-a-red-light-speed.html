Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} First, you're conflating two technologies. Red light cameras take a picture of your car if it enters an intersection (i.e. crosses the white line) after the light turns red. Speed cameras are cameras with integrated RADAR units which check your speed on a length of road and take a picture if your car is exceeding the speed limit. Speed cameras can be challenged in exactly the same way that standalone RADAR units can be challenged. The size of the aperture, the distance from the target, and the wavelength of the radar beam are all used to determine the width of the beam at the target. If the width of the beam is greater than one lane, then it is possible that the camera targeted the wrong vehicle. Easy peesey. Challenging a red light camera is much harder. The camera shows the vehicle entering the intersection clearly after the light was already red. The color shift argument is also not likely to ever succeed. The reason that the lights are positioned the way they are (and the reason that we don't have a single, color-changing light) is so that ***the color of the light is irrelevant***. A gray light at the bottom of the stack is the green one. A gray light in the middle of the stack is the yellow one, and a gray light at the top of the stack is the red one. You can be color blind and still drive effectively. On horizontal stacks, the green light is always on the right, and the red light is always on the left. In the US, this is a national standard.
