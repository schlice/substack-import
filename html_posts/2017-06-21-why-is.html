**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} To continue to deconstruct your erroneous question, the police have not been acquitted of "wrongdoing." The police officer in question was not charged with murder, because his actions did not meet the standard for murder. The officer believed that there was an "imminent threat of unlawful force which was intended to, or likely to, result in death or great bodily harm." The District Attorney found that this belief was reasonable, and therefore did not bring murder charges against the officer. The officer still faces several further challenges: - An internal police department investigation to see if any department policies were violated. This may coincide with the "use of force" review that the department will conduct, to see if the officer's use of force was reasonable. I suspect that they will find that the conflicting directives the officer gave will factor heavily into their decision as to what to do with him. They can reprimand him, refer him for remedial training, or discipline him further, up to and including termination (i.e. he could get fired). - A wrongful death lawsuit, brought by the family on behalf of Castile. Again, because it was the officer who induced the situation in the first place, I suspect he will lose this lawsuit. - Possibly a review by the Department of Justice for civil rights violations. If such a review occurs, I believe the officer would also be found to have violated Castile's civil rights. I suspect that the sum total of all of this will be losing a wrongful death lawsuit and losing his job.
