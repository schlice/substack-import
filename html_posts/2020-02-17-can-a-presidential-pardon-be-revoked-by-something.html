**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **Can a presidential pardon be revoked by something that the person receiving the pardon does or fails to do? My understanding is that a pardon involves a full allocution and there is no recourse to invoking the Fifth?** Once a person is pardoned, the penalty for the crime is vacated. A pardon does not invoke full allocution. Whether or not accepting a pardon is an admission of guilt is still a topic being debated by legal analysts. Presidents sometimes grant pardons to people who are innocent. If the President thinks someone is innocent and grants a pardon, the person accepting that pardon wouldn't be declaring their guilt. **Regardless of allocution, the original conviction, and the record of it, still remains after a pardon.**[\[1\]](#prNHa){#cite-prNHa .citation_link} Any declaration required on official forms of whether or not the person has been convicted remain true. Also, any state actions against persons convicted remain (such as losing the right to vote or hold public office). Therefore, we presume that the person is still a *convicted felon*, still having been convicted of that crime, even though they have been pardoned. To that end, the person who has been pardoned **cannot be prosecuted for the same crime again**, because he is protected under the Double Jeopardy clause. Therefore, it follows that the person cannot invoke the 5th Amendment when testifying about that crime (if someone else is indicted), because he cannot incriminate himself because he cannot be prosecuted for his testimony. He may not voluntarily testify, but if someone is indeed indicted for that same crime, the person pardoned can be subpoenaed to testify, and cannot invoke the 5th Amendment to avoid testimony. What this effectively means is that if the President pardons someone for a crime, that person could then be called to testify against someone else, and could not refuse to answer under the 5th Amendment.
