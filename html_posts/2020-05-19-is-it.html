**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **Is it "assault" when someone follows me to the door of the bathroom after I announce I'm going to shower?** As always, the answer to legal questions is "it depends." The definition of assault varies by state, but in general, a simple assault takes place when someone's intentional act puts you in reasonable fear of imminent harmful conduct. Also in some states, "assault" is called "attempted battery." In some states, assault can be combined with battery (actual harmful conduct) to make a crime called "assault and battery." In an assault, the *fear* happens when you believe that the person's conduct will result in imminent harm. You don't have to believe that the person will be *successful* in causing harm, just that the person is *capable* of causing harm. Your fear must also be *reasonable*. Something must cause you to believe that the person intended to harm you. If there was a male locker room adjacent to the female locker room, for example, then the simple fact that the person followed you may be nothing more than coincidental timing. So there must have been something else that caused you to believe that the person was contemplating harming you, and that belief needs to be reasonable. Courts use what's called the "reasonable person" test to figure this out. After hearing what caused you to believe the person would harm you, they would say, "Would a reasonable person, in the same situation, believe the same as you?" If a court would answer that question as yes, then your circumstance passes the reasonableness test.
