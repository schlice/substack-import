**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} Morality is not *dependent* on religion. But one must ask themselves: *from where is the source of that morality?* In order to have a common, shared morality, there must be a morals-giver. Remember, even babies have inborn morals. > Morality is not just something that people learn, argues Yale > psychologist Paul Bloom: It is something we are all born with. At > birth, babies are endowed with compassion, with empathy, with the > beginnings of a sense of fairness.[\[1\]](#hjgRv){#cite-hjgRv > .citation_link} But it doesn't make sense that this is an *evolved* trait. It is antithetical to "survival of the fittest." Morals don't make me survive better. Morals don't improve my chance for survival. And even if you argue that morality is evolved, the Law of Causality says that an effect must resemble its cause. It is therefore illogical that a meaningless, purposeless, amoral universe can imbue us with morals, and a sense of meaning and purpose. Therefore, if the universe is not the source of our morality, there must be another cause. And the only other logical choice is that some moral being (you must have morals to give morals) imbued us with morals. Atheists can't explain this at all. Agnostics say that they don't know how to explain this. It could be a creator, but they just don't know. \ Original question: Can atheists be moral people? What about agnostics?
