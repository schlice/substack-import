--- title: '"Why does the Bible condone slavery and no where condemns it?"' date: '2024-08-15' tags: - theology - policy-analysis layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} The Bible was never intended to be a political manifesto. There are plenty of other human rights violations that the Bible also does not condemn, but that wasn't the purpose of the Bible. The other thing to consider is that Americans tend to look at slavery in the past, because we successfully freed the black slaves in the American South. **But slavery still exists today, in the United States and around the world.** It's estimated that over 27 million people in the world are victims of slavery today: forced labor, sex trade, inheritable property, etc. So why does the Bible not speak out strongly against slavery? Why does the Bible, in fact, seem to support the practice of human slavery? The Bible never demanded that every slave owner immediately emancipate their slaves. Instead, the apostles gave instructions to slave owners to act in godly ways *within the existing social system.* > Masters, do the same to them \[treat them with goodwill\], and stop > your threatening, knowing that he who is both their Master and yours > is in heaven, and that there is no partiality with him. \-- Ephesians > 6:9 ESV > > Masters, treat your bondservants justly and fairly, knowing that you > also have a Master in heaven. \-- Colossians 4:1 ESV So why didn't the Bible writers immediately command slave owners to emancipate their slaves? ***They didn't need to.*** God's Word affects people *individually*. Those changed people, in turn, go on to change the lives of their families, their communities, and eventually, their nations. Christianity is not a political movement, but over time, it naturally affects political policy. In countries where Christianity spread, the abolition of slavery was often influenced by the actions of converted individuals. The Bible does contain the foundations of what people need to end slavery: the truth that all men are created by God and made in His image (Genesis 1:27), condemnation towards those who kidnap and sell a person (Exodus 21:16; cf. 1 Timothy 1:8--10), and that a slave can truly be "a brother in the Lord" (Philemon 1:16). Born-again Christians used these truths to influence their communities and eventually bring slavery to an end. But evil still exists, and so the slave trade is alive and well, despite the efforts of Christians worldwide. The fight is not over until He destroys evil once and for all.
