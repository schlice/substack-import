--- title: '"What are some thoughts on the recent Gallup poll that shows that the majority of Americans and even more gun owners now want stricter gun laws in the United States?"' date: '2017-10-25' tags: - law-enforcement - law layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} First of all, this article is two years old. There's nothing "recent" about it. Secondly, the only thing this poll shows is that Americans don't know what they want. The first question in the poll shows that "more Americans want stricter gun laws." But when you break it down, the increase came from only one political party. Any idea which one? > The rise in the proportion of Americans who want stricter laws on > firearm sales can be attributed partly to an increase among certain > demographic groups. Support rose among Democrats and especially > independents from 2014, and among those who do not personally own a > gun. [\[1\]](#Tbgrz){#cite-Tbgrz .citation_link} Then, and even more telling, is the second question, which asks whether or not there should be a law that limits gun possession to only the police. > The percentage of Americans who favor a law providing that only > authorized persons (including the police) would be allowed to possess > handguns has remained low since the 1990s. This year, 27% \-- near the > record low \-- say there should be this type of ban. This trend has > been generally declining since Gallup began asking this question in > 1959, when 60% said such a law should exist. So while some people think that laws should be tougher (even while not saying which laws, or *how* they should be tougher --- "well, I dunno, they just *should be*"), they also continue to say, in greater numbers every year, that there should not be restrictions on who should be allowed to own a handgun. If anything, such a poll could lead to little more than increased quantities of lazy data analysis and confusion.
