**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} [[Josh Crowe](https://www.quora.com/profile/Josh-Crowe-4)]{.qlink_container} is absolutely right. I assume you\'re talking about **aspartame**, the artificial sweetner used in many diet soft drinks. No other consumable substance on the face of the earth has been studied more than aspartame. Over 30 years and over 200 peer-reviewed, double-blind, placebo-controlled, and randomized scientific studies have proven, re-proven, and reaffirmed, that aspartame is safe. Researchers have spent a great deal of time reviewing every claim by people who believe that aspartame causes everything from MS and autism to migraines and bulging eyes. Not a single claim of any cause has panned out under true scientific scrutiny. If you do have \"proof\" that aspartame causes any sort of disorder, look through your proof and see if there are any scientific studies that were done. Scientific studies are peer-reviewed, randomized, placebo-controlled, and double-blind. Those tenets of a true scientific study ensure that there is no bias in the group and that the substance is tested against a \"placebo,\" or a control substance. In essence, if there is no difference between the group which took the tested substance and the group which took a placebo, you can say that the substance did not appreciably contribute to those people developing the symptom in question. Now, to more of the scientific detail. Aspartame is a compound made of two ingredients: aspartic acid, and phenylalanine. When aspartame is consumed, it is broken down in the body into three components: aspartic acid, phenylalanine, and methanol. These three substances are then absorbed by the body and used in natural body processes, the same way your body uses those three substances when they are derived from food. None of the components of aspartame, nor aspartame itself, builds up in the body. The amounts of the three substances derived from aspartame are far smaller than you would get from food as well. For example, if you were to have a glass of tomato juice, you\'d get about six times the methanol than you\'d get from an aspartame-sweetened drink of roughly the same size. A glass of skim milk would give you six times the phenylalanine and thirteen times the aspartic acid you\'d get from an equivalent aspartame-sweetened drink. There is a sensitivity that is out there which could be triggered by aspartame. It\'s called \"phenylketonuria,\" also known as PKU. This condition is an inability to metabolize phenylalanine in the body into the amino acid tyrosine. For these people, phenylalanine accumulates and is converted into phenylpyruvate, also known as phenylketone. This buildup of phenylketone in the body can lead to serious medical problems, like seizures and mental retardation. Since PKU is a genetic disorder, there is no cure, and the only treatment option currently available is a strict diet restricting anything with phenylalanine, or anything that can break down into phenylalanine. So anything made with aspartame has a warning on the label: \"PHENYLKETONURICS: CONTAINS PHENYLALANINE\". Let me caution you: don\'t take my word for it. Don\'t take anyone else\'s word for it either. Do the work yourself; look it up yourself. But when you read the websites that tell you all the diseases that aspartame \"causes,\" look for the proof. Look for the true scientific studies, and then go look those studies up yourself. Do they exist? Were they done to scientific standards? Were the results duplicated in other studies? Were the results published in any scientific journals so that the scientific community in general could review and analyze the results? Did a doctor see this in 150 of his patients? That\'s not a scientific study. Was a study done on all the people in the Memphis area who drank Diet Coke? Also not a scientific study. The actual scientific studies that are out there prove that the substance is safe. The CDC researched 517 of these claims in 1984, and reported the following: \"\[T\]he majority of frequently reported symptoms were mild and are symptoms that are common in the general populace.\" This means that there is no greater risk of any of these symptoms in someone who consumes aspartame as there is in the general population in people who do not consume aspartame.
