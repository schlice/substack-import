**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} I was waiting for [[Rick Bruno](https://www.quora.com/profile/Rick-Bruno-1)]{.qlink_container} to answer this, but I will answer from what I know. I was not a K9 handler, but I often participated as a \"chew toy\" for the K9s. I\'m a dog person; I love dogs, so when they asked the other guys on the shift if anyone wanted to \"play\" with the dog, I totally volunteered. From my experience, the dog bites hard, but the thing that really \"strikes\" you is the **force** with which the dog hits. Doesn\'t matter how fast you can run, the dog is faster. Doesn\'t matter how big you are, the force of that initial hit will knock you off your feet. I\'ve tried it both ways: running, and feet firmly planted. Dog kicked my butt. And most importantly (and answering your question), the dog will continue to attack until the officer gives the command to stop, and the dog will stop instantly. Dogs do not understand the nuances of \"surrender,\" nor should they have to. It\'s up to the K9 officer to control the dog and \"release\" the dog when the suspect is no longer deemed a threat. So to answer your question, the dog will attack when it receives its attack command, and it will continue attacking until it gets its release command. The dog doesn\'t take cues from the suspect, it takes cues from its handler.
