I'm not sure what more I can add to the other answers here, but I'll give it a crack since you asked. **Does the underlying crime need to be proven in order to be guilty of obstruction? No,** it does not. Obstruction charges will stand on their own regardless of whether or not the underlying crime, the investigation of which the person is attempting to obstruct, is actually proven. However, t*he presence or absence of the underlying crime* does indeed make a difference, as it speaks to *intent*. One of the elements of obstruction of justice, which must be proven beyond a reasonable doubt, is that the alleged obstructor must have acted **with corrupt intent**. This means that the alleged obstructor knew that a crime was committed, and corruptly attempted to stop, confound, confuse, or obfuscate the investigation into that crime. The lack of an underlying crime implies that the alleged obstructor could not have acted with corrupt intent, since there was no underlying crime into which he was obstructing an investigation. In other words, you can't intend to stop the investigation into a non-crime, because a non-crime never happened.
