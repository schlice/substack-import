--- title: '"Why do police allow pan handling?"' date: '2016-12-05' tags: - law-enforcement layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} What "police" allow is up to their supervisors and chiefs of police and mayors. The other undeniable fact: ***policing is political***. Just look at the decrease in assertive policing in New York City since the uptick in resistance by groups like Black Lives Matter. Once a powerful political bloc has denied the legitimacy of a specific policing technique, you will see less of it. This is understandable, and if we're honest, is how things should work. Depending on the current political climate then, you will either see more or less disruption of panhandlers by police. If the current political climate was like the climate in NYC in 2012, they were "cleaning up Times Square" and rousting the panhandlers and prostitutes, cracking down on unwanted windshield cleaning, D&Ds, etc. (This, by the way, goes back to "broken windows theory" of policing adopted by Commissioner William Bratton, which led to "Compstat," the most successful government program ever). Since the mood has somewhat shifted, everyone from the Mayor on down has softened on assertive policing (as is happening nationwide). As a result, we are seeing now the first sustained spike in violent crime in over two decades. Eventually, people will get tired of seeing the increased crime and will demand more loudly than the protesters, and things will swing back the other way again.
