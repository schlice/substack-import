**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} Parents cannot kick children out. That's a ridiculous notion. Parents are literally legally responsible for their children until they turn 18. Such parents will be arrested for child abuse and their children will be placed with relatives or placed into the foster system. But I can understand the concern about *abuse* at home for a kid coming out. However, we need to balance that concern with the **data,** as well as the rights of parents to care for all the needs of their children. If parents are the ones legally responsible for the wellbeing of children, then parents must have access to all the relevant information, despite the risk. Depression is much higher amongst LGBTQ youth than it is for heterosexual youth. And "those who felt greater social support from parents tended to have fewer depressive symptoms, whereas those who reported greater psychological control from parents had more depressive symptoms," as explained by Amy McCurdy, postdoctoral scholar at The University of Texas at Austin. "For youth whose parents did not know their LGBTQ identities, having a combination of high psychological control and high social support from parents was linked with greater depressive symptoms."[\[1\]](#LbeZs){#cite-LbeZs .citation_link} In other words, studies show that **support from home decreases depression.** The new law you're referring to was introduced in California in March by Rep. Bill Essayli, R-Riverside, and Assemblyman James Gallagher, R-Yuba City. Essayli acknowledged the risk of abuse, but said teachers are mandated reporters who should report abuse. The real pull quote that answers the question comes from Essayli himself: "That can't be the default position, that we're going to keep parents in the dark because they might abuse their kids," [\[2\]](#Ptnpu){#cite-Ptnpu .citation_link}he told The Sacramento Bee.
