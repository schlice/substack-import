--- title: '"Is the catechism of the Catholic Church infallible, or can it be changed? Has it ever changed in the history of the Church?"' date: '2017-06-13' tags: - theology layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} The catechism is infallible insofar as it is interpreted by the Pope in whatever form he chooses to do that. Once the Pope interprets the catechism (or re-interprets it), it is infallible. That said, the catechism of the church has indeed changed, most notably during the [[Counter-Reformation](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=cbd059&url=https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Counter-Reformation){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="wikipedia.org"}]{.qlink_container}. This was a response to the Reformation and the schism between Catholic and Protestant religions through Martin Luther's 95 Theses. During the Counter-Reformation, some practices were upheld (transubstantiation, salvation through grace *and* works, etc), and some were changed (Mass from Latin to English, priest with his back to the congregation to facing front, some formerly apocryphal books of the Bible were added, the adoption of the Nycene Creed, etc).
