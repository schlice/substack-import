--- title: '"If my roommate wants to let the police search our condo, can he just wait till I leave for work and then allow the search?"' date: '2015-01-21' tags: - policy-analysis - law-enforcement - law layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** [If the police have a warrant, whether or not your roommate gives permission is irrelevant. The search covers what the warrant lists as covered. If it covers your room, then no amount of locked doors will prevent anything.\ \ I will assume that the police do not have a warrant, and they can only search what they are given permission to search. In that instance, in most jurisdictions, **your roommate is allowed to consent to a search of your room**. Your lease does not define \"areas\" within your apartment or home that exclusively belong to one person or another. You both are listed on the lease, so you both have full access to the full facility.\ \ You may have defined areas in your apartment or home that \"belong\" to one of you or to the other of you, but that\'s an \"agreement\" between the two of you and is not legally binding in most jurisdictions. Your roommate can grant access to search a room, and if he can get in, so can the police. If your roommate can\'t get in, the police can enter the room with your roommate\'s permission. So hopefully your roommate doesn\'t give them permission to search your room. Doesn\'t matter what you have posted on your door. Doesn\'t matter that your door is locked. If your roommate is on the lease, he can give permission for any part of the home to be searched. Legally, this is allowed because the roommate is on the lease. Again, this is variable dependent on jurisdiction. In practice, though, it is unlikely that this would happen because you\'re crossing into legal gray area, and police want whatever they find to be admissible in court, so they are going to make sure they cover their bases.\ \ Ultimately, what would happen in that circumstance is that if the police encounter a locked door, even if the roommate gives permission, but the person who exclusively uses that space is not home to grant access, the police will get a telephonic warrant to search that location if they have probable cause to believe that something they want is in there. Then they will break the door and enter. Should only take them about 20 minutes to secure a telephonic warrant, and then they will enter your room anyway.\ \ It\'s likely that if the police don\'t know or can\'t articulate what they are looking for, even if they have the roommate\'s permission, they won\'t enter your locked room without a warrant.\ \ The only exception to this would be exigent circumstances. If they believe you are holding a prisoner in there against their will or if they believe that evidence is in there in the process of being destroyed (i.e. you stareted a wipe of a hard drive and then locked your door and left), then they can enter without a warrant.]{.rendered_qtext}
