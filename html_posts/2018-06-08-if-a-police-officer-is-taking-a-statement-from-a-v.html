**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **If a police officer is taking a statement from a victim of sexual assault, is he/she allowed to turn their body camera off so the victim feels more comfortable sharing intimate details?** The answer is "it depends." As you know, Body-Worn Cameras (BWCs) are very new, and every agency and state has different rules around their use and implementation. If an agency has a "record everything" policy, well first I'd love to meet them and see how exactly they're swimming in money, but more to the question, the officers would likely not be able to turn off the cameras. They would have to cite the fact that the complainant does not have a reasonable expectation of privacy when speaking to a police officer, and whatever they say can be recorded. That doesn't mean that it can be released to the public; videos of a sensitive nature can be protected from FOIA requests. But in those departments, the officers would not be able to deactivate their cameras. If an agency allows officers to switch on and switch off the cameras based on policy (which is the majority of departments' procedures because storage space costs money), then policy *should* dictate that the officer can deactivate the camera if a witness is giving a statement. The contents of the statement would be verified and authenticated (initialed) by both the complainant and the officer on the statement form before the officer left, so there should be no need to record that particular conversation on the BWC. As I mentioned, this is a very new area of law, and policy is being developed and refined continuously. Departments have many things to deal with, including state and local laws, as well as how much storage space is available for evidentiary and non-evidentiary video, and storage policies and release policies must be tailored for each specific type of video and for the amount of available storage space. Laws must be created which balance the need for protecting the privacy of victims while still providing transparency into law enforcement operations and good evidentiary video. It is going to be a work in progress for some time.
