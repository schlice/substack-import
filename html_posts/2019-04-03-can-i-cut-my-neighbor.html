**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **Can I cut my neighbor\'s tree limb without their consent? The branch runs into the master bedroom window on the 2nd floor, and I am afraid of small rodents getting access to my roof. I\'ve asked them for two years to do something about it.** You will have to check your local ordinances, but in most cases, the other property owner is committing a tort trespass by allowing his tree to grow on to your property (which you own up to 500 feet over your property (in most places), where the FAA takes over). In most places, you can cut any tree that is extending into your property. Of course, it's best to get the homeowner's permission before you do that, but if you can't (or they won't give permission), you should be able to safely cut any of the tree extending on to your property. Make sure you take before/after pictures of the tree so that you can document exactly what you removed and that you are not harming the tree by doing so. If you really want to be a stickler about it, you can hire a professional arborist to do the work, and then bill the homeowner for the amount due. If he doesn't pay, you can take him to small claims court to recoup your investment. Ideally, according to most extension offices, the best time to trim a tree is late winter, when fungi and diseases are at their lowest activity level. The only difference is for spring-blooming trees; trim those immediately after they bloom. For all others, prune in late winter, while the trees are fully dormant.[\[1\]](#ljfbJ){#cite-ljfbJ .citation_link} Footnotes [\[1\]](#cite-ljfbJ){.citation_index} [[When to Trim Trees and Shrubs \| Today\'s Homeowner](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=e0df0d&url=https://www.todayshomeowner.com/when-to-trim-trees-and-shrubs/){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="todayshomeowner.com"}]{.qlink_container}
