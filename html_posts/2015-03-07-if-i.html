**Content:** [It depends a lot on jurisdiction, but most states have some form of [[castle doctrine](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=577621&url=http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Castle_doctrine){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="wikipedia.org"}]{.qlink_container}. The \"castle doctrine\" is termed from the old saying, \"a man\'s home is his castle,\" meaning that a resident should feel ultimately safe and secure within the confines of his home. And he should feel utterly justified in defending his home with force against those who wish to do him harm, up to and including deadly force.\ \ You will need to research the laws in your particular jurisdiction concerning whether or not your home is in an area where a castle doctrine is enforced.\ \ There have been occasions where the police have entered the wrong house. The intersection of \"no-knock warrants\" and the \"castle doctrine\" is a very violent intersection, to be sure. Assuming it\'s not a \"no-knock warrant,\" the police will be very vocal in announcing who they are and the fact that they have a search warrant, as they bash through your front door.]{.rendered_qtext}
