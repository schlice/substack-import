**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **What exactly is involved in the \"universal background checks\" that gun control advocates want?** Look at Colorado, which recently implemented universal background checks at the state level. Basically, universal background checks means **any time a firearm changes ownership, a background check must be performed, no exceptions.** Buying or selling a private firearm? Background checks. We didn't need to do this previously under state law, so this might be the only area where such a law is beneficial. Private party sales. But we'll come back to the only potentially useful scenario later. However, implementing UBCs without exceptions opens up a whole series of problems. - Gifting a firearm to a family member? Background checks. That's right, if I bought that pretty pink M&P Shield for my wife, I have to do a background check on her. Or if I bought a Henry rifle for my son on his 18th birthday, I have to do a background check on him. Does this sound reasonable? - One year while I was living in Colorado, my house was placed under pre-evacuation orders due to wildfire. I gave my firearms to my friend to place in his gun safe for safe keeping, which worked out great. Incidentally, I did the same for him when his own house was under evacuation orders for a different wildfire. Under the UBC law, I would have to do a background check on him before he can take my firearms to store them, even in an emergency. Administratively, this becomes a nightmare. How long do I have to retain the record of the background check? How long do I have to retain the record of the sale? Who gets punished if the background check wasn't done? Who is going to follow up on all of these violations of the law? The police? Sure, they've got plenty of extra time to take care of that. And finally, I can't do background checks on my own; I need someone with a Federal Firearms License (FFL) to do the check for me. Great for business for local FFLs, but now any gun sale costs me more because I have to do a background check, and FFLs charge money to do a background check. Which means that all guns now cost incrementally more, which means we put the burden on the segment of society that needs cost increases the least: the poor. This law effectively may price the poor out of the gun market, which means that the law effectively denies them the ability to exercise their rights. State data for the states which have implemented universal background checks shows that the checks have done nothing to stem the rise in homicides. Delaware saw a significant increase in background checks, but also saw its homicide rate significantly increase as well; from 4.4 in 2013 to a 30-year high of 6.6 in 2015, before dropping slightly to 5.9 in 2016. Colorado saw a modest increase in background checks, but also saw an increase in homicides, 3.3 to 3.7. In all the states studied which implemented universal background checks, they saw increases in homicide rates.[\[1\]](#NebqW){#cite-NebqW .citation_link} It's almost as if background checks had no effect at all. Maybe it's because the people who are complying with background checks are not the people committing crimes. Criminals aren't buying their guns from people who are going to conduct background checks. Instead of focusing our time on getting tougher on gun crimes and the people who commit them, we instead go after people who are already following the law. Does that seem right to you?
