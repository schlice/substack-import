**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} I think that's a pretty short-sighted view of things. Let's do "turnabout is fair play" today. I say, **The lack of evidence for the existence of gravity should be considered evidence for the non-existence of gravity.** To which you would immediately say: *Nonsense! Without gravity, we'd all just float off of the surface of the planet!* True, but what is gravity made of? What is the atomic weight of gravity? Gravity is not a thing, so gravity must not exist. Clearly, the argument is nonsense, you'd reply, because we can see the *effect* of gravity all around us, even if we can't see the *cause*. Thank you for making my point for me, I would say. Science cannot account for everything. In fact, there are many things that scientific inquiry cannot explain, but we are all rational to accept. For example: 1. mathematics and logic (science can't prove them because science presupposes them) 2. metaphysical truths (such as, there are minds that exist other than my own) 3. ethical judgments (you can't prove by science that the Nazis were evil, because morality is not subject to the scientific method) 4. aesthetic judgments (the beautiful, like the good, cannot be scientifically proven), and, ironically 5. science itself (the belief that the scientific method discovers truth can't be proven by the scientific method itself) So no, the lack of "evidence" which proves the existence of something should not be considered evidence that something does not exist. \ We should discuss whether or not you can actually *prove* that something *does not* exist. Philosopher and scholar Mortimer Adler said, "An affirmative existential proposition can be proven, but a negative existential proposition---one which denies the existence of something---cannot be proven." For example, I can tell you that red eagles exist, and you can tell me that red eagles do not exist. I only need to show you one example, at any point in history, to prove that red eagles exist. You would need to be present at all places and at all times to prove that red eagles do not, and have never, existed. This, of course, is impossible to do. \ Finally, I take issue with your statement regarding "evidence." What is it that constitutes "evidence" of the existence of God? Is it the material sciences? Because as I've already shown, there are plenty of things which exist outside of the realm of material science which we are all rational to accept (and we probably daily accept). So if God cannot be proven through the use of the material sciences, how can God be proven? We use the other methods of scientific inquiry: logic, philosophy, and forensics. These are all valid methods of scientific inquiry, even if they do not involve the material, physical sciences. Science is, at its core, a search for causes. In fact, Francis Bacon (the father of modern science) said, "True knowledge is knowledge by causes." That's what scientists do---they try to discover what caused what. So we can use logic, philosophy, and forensics to prove that a creator God exists. But you have to accept these lines of scientific inquiry as valid before you can accept that a creator God exists. And most people who say there is "no evidence" for the existence of God simply don't accept logic or forensics as "evidence."
