**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} First of all, there are no international "laws." There are only international agreements about certain things which can be accepted or not by individual nations. Secondly, the closest thing to international agreement about life is the UN and its *Universal Declaration of Human Rights* (UDHR). However, it is intentionally ambiguous on when life begins, and whether or not they regard that life as protected. For example, Article 1 says, "All human beings are born free and equal in dignity and rights." The UN Human Rights Committee, an organization within the UN which deals with treaties, reminds us that the language says that all human beings are ***born*** free, thus their human rights do not begin until birth. However, the UDHR does not explicitly say this. Then, Article 2 says, "Everyone is entitled to all the rights and freedoms set forth in this Declaration, without distinction of any kind, such as race, colour, sex, language, religion, political or other opinion, national or social origin, property, birth or other status." We should note that it clearly says that everyone is entitled to all the rights and freedoms in the UDHR, without distinction of **birth**. To me, this is a plain reading that you don't have to be born in order to be entitled to the rights in the UDHR. Of course, Article 3 says, "Everyone has the right to life, liberty and security of person." They do not clarify who "everyone" is. Is it only those who are covered by Article 1, or does that mean "everyone," including the unborn? So even the United Nations, which is the closest body to any representing any sort of international understanding of the issue, is conspicuously vague about exactly to whom these rights apply. No matter, because the UDHR is just *boilerplate* for nations to build their own, *binding*, human rights documents. Sovereign nations can still do as they choose, and Columbia is no exception. \ Original question: Recently, Colombia legalized abortion of babies up to 24 weeks. Is this not against international human rights laws?
