--- title: '"Since the possibility of martial law frightens so many people in the US, should it be amended away?"' date: '2017-05-18' tags: - law layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} First of all, it's a fear, not a possibility. Secondly, it's borne of a misunderstanding of what martial law is in the United States, which in short means that martial law and overriding the 22nd Amendment to the Constitution are completely unrelated. Martial law involves the suspension of *habeas corpus*, which is basically being arrested and held without a hearing. More broadly, it is the oversight of law enforcement by the judicial branch. Article 1, Section 9 states, \"The Privilege of the Writ of *Habeas Corpus* shall not be suspended, unless when in Cases of Rebellion or Invasion the public Safety may require it.\" In these cases, the military takes over specific duties as appointed by the president, to keep order or to preserve or maintain peace (the Posse Comitatus Act covers this as well). Since the military would be in charge, and is required to follow the Constitution (*sans* habeas corpus), the military would *strongly encourage* the President to uphold the 22nd Amendment. However, just because habeas corpus is suspended doesn't mean the rest of the Constitution is. There is still the First and Second Amendments to deal with... So in short, no president is going to extend his or her term until the 22nd Amendment is repealed. This means you would need widespread national support to do so, and no president will engender such support.
