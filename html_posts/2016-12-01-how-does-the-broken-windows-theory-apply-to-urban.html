**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} I can answer half of your question: I don't know enough about land use theory and its application to rural and suburban crime, but I know about the broken windows theory. This theory spawned the "Compstat" initiative in New York City, and when it spread across the nation, resulted in the longest and largest decline in felony crime in this nation's history --- well over two decades of decline. Brittanica has this definition of "broken windows theory" which was originally written by James Q. Wilson and George Kelling in 1982. They start by examining traditional crime prevention methods, which focus the most resources on the most serious crimes. > Wilson and Kelling took a different view. They saw serious crime as > the final result of a lengthier chain of events, theorizing that crime > emanated from disorder and that if disorder were eliminated, then > serious crimes would not occur. > > Their theory further posits that the prevalence of disorder creates > fear in the minds of citizens who are convinced that the area is > unsafe. This withdrawal from the community weakens social controls > that previously kept criminals in check. Once this process begins, it > feeds itself. Disorder causes crime, and crime causes further disorder > and crime.[\[1\]](#PqaNF){#cite-PqaNF .citation_link} The "broken windows theory" was directly tested in an urban setting by Police Commissioner William Bratton in the 90's in New York City. Between 1990 and 1992, when Bratton was chief of the New York City Transit Police, he implemented "broken windows theory" policing in the city subways. He stationed plainclothes transit officers at turnstiles to catch turnstile jumpers. As arrests for misdemeanors went up, subway crimes of all kinds decreased dramatically. In 1994, Bratton was promoted to Police Commissioner, and applied the same theory which worked so well in the subway to the entire department, calling it the "quality of life initiative." He began cracking down on panhandling, unsolicited windshield washing, D&D (drunk and disorderly), and prostitution. When he retired in 1996, homicide was down 50%, and felonies in general were down almost 40%. Bratton's system, which tracks smaller crimes to reduce bigger ones, is called "Computer Statistics," or "Compstat." Compstat was so successful in New York City that it has been exported and employed in police departments across the country, and has resulted in the longest and largest decline of serious crime in this nation's history. Before Bratton started his program in 1993, there were 1946 murders in New York City. In 2015, there were 352.
