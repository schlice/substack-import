**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **Is it legal to use a fake gun as a bluff for self-defense?** This is a great question, because it is far more legally nuanced than you might think. The TL;DR version: **fake guns are treated just like real guns as far as the laws you are subject to are concerned.** First, let's define the environment we are discussing. We are talking about guns that *look* like real guns, but are not. They may even be made by the gun manufacturer, using the same materials as the real gun, and actually respond and feel like the real gun, but lack certain key components (like a firing pin) and lack the ability to have the component added (the slot for the firing pin isn't even milled out) and so while they can even accept a real bullet, they simply cannot fire. (EDIT) Since I was specifically asked, this *also* includes BB guns, Airsoft guns, electronic kinetic energy BB guns, and any other non-firearm (a firearm uses a black powder charge to propel a bullet) **designed to look and feel identical to a firearm.** If your Airsoft gun is electric blue with the required blaze orange tip, it is not designed to look and feel identical to a firearm. But if you can't immediately tell that it is not a firearm within a second of looking at it, it is classified as a *replica* or *imitation* firearm. Let's say you were carrying one of these bad boys for some reason (we'll talk about why this is a terrible idea in a minute). In most states, you can carry one of these, but some states do prohibit them like actual firearms. *Could you lawfully use that fake gun and point it at someone to defend yourself?* Of course you can. However, the legal consequences are *exactly the same* as if you pointed a real gun at someone. As far as "brandishing a firearm," most states have brandishing laws which include "replica" or "imitation" firearms, so you're subject to the same penalties for brandishing a fake gun as you are for brandishing a real gun. Pointing any gun (real, fake, BB, whatever, so long as it looks real) at someone is a crime called "assault." The only difference is that in some states, you may not be charged with the more severe "assault with a deadly weapon," although some states do include replica or imitation firearms in their "deadly weapon" laws. If the state has "assault with a firearm" statutes, usually replica and imitation guns (designed to look and feel real) are included, so you would be subject to those. In other words, **if you're going to point a** ***fake*** **(yet real-looking) gun at someone, you must meet all the same legal criteria for being a person who would point a** ***real*** **gun at someone.** In other words, you must be in fear for your life in order to defend against the assault and brandishing charges we listed above. So can you see why doing this would be an *extra-bad idea?* You have all the intention to use a gun, and you have (hopefully) met all the legal requirements to be in a condition in which you *can* use a gun, but you have a non-functional gun. You have all the *intention*, all the *motivation*, but none of the *ability.* If you actually *needed* to use a gun at that very point (which would be the only legal way in which you could brandish or point that gun), you would not be able to do so. (EDIT) Here's another reason why this is a bad idea. If you point your fake gun at someone, that someone has been legally threatened by what he reasonably perceives to be deadly force. That person can use ***real*** deadly force to counter such a threat. In other words, if you point a fake gun at someone who has a real gun (either an armed civilian or law enforcement), they can ***legally*** use that real gun in response. This is why cops don't get convicted of murder when they kill kids who point fake guns at them.
