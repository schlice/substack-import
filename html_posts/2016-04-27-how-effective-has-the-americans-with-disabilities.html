Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} ADA has done some really great things for the disabled community. Not only has it equalized the workforce and how employers are allowed to hire people and support individuals with disabilities, but it has empowered the disabled community to be open and honest about their abilities, and to seek accommodations when they are needed so that they can function right alongside everyone else in today\'s society. Additionally, ADA is flexible; it does not limit what conditions qualify and what conditions do not. It simply states that you are a person of disability if you are \"a person who has a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities, a person who has a history or record of such an impairment, or a person who is perceived by others as having such an impairment.\" So the law includes both people who self-identify as having a physical or mental impairment, **and** people who have been identified in society as a person with such an impairment, either through history or records, or perception. At the same time, ADA has shifted the ***entire*** burden away from the disabled person, and fully towards providers of public accommodation and employers, since ADA does not require the disabled to *prove* that they have a disability. The disabled can demand accommodations, and providers of public accommodation must comply, whether or not that person actually has a disability. Consequently, the disabled person does not share in the responsibility to ensure the system is not abused. The theory behind the legislation says that the disabled person should not have to \"prove\" that they have a disability. I agree; employers and providers of public accommodation are not doctors; it\'s not their job to determine whether or not someone has a disability. However, the \"balance of power,\" being shifted so in favor of the disabled person, with so little accountability, means that the system is *rife* with abuse. Many companies and providers of public accommodation struggle with how to best offer both services to fully-abled customers and accommodations to disabled customers. Just look at all the issues [[Disney has had](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=f64573&url=https://www.disabilityscoop.com/2016/04/08/disney-again-faulted/22152/){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="disabilityscoop.com"}]{.qlink_container} with its Disability Access System. In trying to balance the need to accommodate persons with disabilities, and at the same time preventing abuse of the system by the fully-abled (but less endowed with conscience) is proving to be a major headache for the proprietor of the most popular theme park in the world. So what can be done to correct this? One thing would be like they have in the UK, a system of national identification to identify a person who is entitled to receive benefits under ADA. It is administered by the DMV, since it would be added to your state identification or license, but it would be a nationally-recognized identifier. Similar to handicapped parking spaces and their administration, handicapped hang tags and handicapped license plates are accepted in all states, even though they are administered state-by-state. Similarly, a simple identifier on a driver\'s license would signify that the person is able to receive accommodations under ADA. Of course, such a system produces more questions than answers. What about children with disabilities who need assistance? What if I am poor and can\'t get a state ID? What if my doctor won\'t sign the form to get the identifier added to my license? Do I not have a disability? And the biggest question: is the abuse of the system worth the trouble of identifying persons with disability in an official capacity?
