**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} **Americans who think universal healthcare is not feasible...Why?** 1. Healthcare is not a right, nor is it a privilege. Healthcare is a **business transaction.** People train to become practitioners of healthcare so that they can run businesses to make money. Yes, they are also noble and want to help people, but primarily they are service providers, providing a service. You simply cannot turn a service into a right by changing who pays and how. All you're doing is taking from the people who can pay and paying for the healthcare of the people who don't. Plus, you're adding additional patients to the existing healthcare recipients, and demanding the same amount of money? No, you'll have to demand more money to cover the new people, which means that overall, it will cost more. Since all you've done (initially) is to change who pays and how, the cost will necessarily go up. You can't force doctors to provide their services for free, since that's slavery (which is pretty much illegal everywhere in the United States). Therefore, healthcare will never be a right until you change how you pay doctors for their services and how those doctors pay for medical school.\ \ [[Why can\'t healthcare be free in America?](https://www.quora.com/Why-cant-healthcare-be-free-in-America)]{.qlink_container} 2. The government is not the agency to do it. Our federal government already runs three single-payer healthcare systems: Medicare, the Veterans Health Administration, and the Indian Health Service. Each one of these programs is an unmitigated disaster, rife with fraud, waste, and abuse. Veterans *literally die* waiting for healthcare. Why would we think that entrusting *more* responsibility to the government, which has already shown that it can't handle the responsibilities it has, make anything better? 3. The history of "health insurance" in the United States started off innocuously enough, but scope creep turned it into an agency which does all of the payments for all health-related procedures, unlike insurance at all. It's really nothing more than an overpriced prepayment plan where no one cares about how much anything costs, and the people making up the prices know this, and don't mind tacking on a few hundred dollars here and there since no one cares. Healthcare consumers not only don't care how much healthcare procedures cost, but they don't know, since doctors and hospitals are not required to show you the actual cost of procedures. Changing who pays and how won't correct this little hiccup in the system. If you're interested in learning the history of our health insurance system in the United States, and how we got where we are, [[this speech](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=e120f9&url=https://imprimis.hillsdale.edu/short-history-american-medical-insurance/){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="hillsdale.edu"}]{.qlink_container} by John Steele Gordon, the author of *An Empire of Wealth: The Epic History of American Economic Power*, among other great reads, illustrates how we got here and why. I think we're just not yet in a place where we can spend 2/3 of our GDP on Medicare for all when we can barely pay for the obligations we currently have. By 2021, the *interest on our national debt* will cost us more than what we spend on the entire military. By 2023, that interest will cost us more than ***all Federal spending combined***. I think we have bigger problems right now than who pays for whose healthcare.
