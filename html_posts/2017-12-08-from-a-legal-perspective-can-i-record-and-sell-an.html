--- title: "From a legal perspective, can I record and sell an accompaniment track of a copyrighted work? In what cases can I not legally do so?" date: "2017-12-08" tags: ["law"] layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} The short answer is **not without securing permission from the original copyright owner.** The longer answer is slightly more complicated, and differs around the world. We'll stick with US copyright law. An accompaniment track would be considered a *derivative work* under US copyright law. This quote from [[Wikipedia](https://www.quora.com/_/redirect?sig=0095ee&url=https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative_work%23Originality_requirement){.external_link rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank" onclick="return Q.openUrl(this);" qt-tooltip="wikipedia.org"}]{.qlink_container} was perfect for this answer: > For copyright protection to attach to a later, allegedly derivative > work, it must display some originality of its own. It cannot be a > rote, uncreative variation on the earlier, underlying work. The latter > work must contain sufficient new expression, over and above that > embodied in the earlier work for the latter work to satisfy copyright > law's requirement of originality. Therefore, since an accompaniment track is actually the original work *minus vocals*, it would not meet the originality standard required to protect it as a derivative work. Therefore, in order to produce and sell an accompaniment track, you would need to secure permission (as well as arrange for royalties) from the original copyright holder. ***Of course, it goes without saying that this is not legal advice, and if you're actually going to record and sell an accompaniment track, you need to seek the advice of a licensed copyright attorney in your area.***
