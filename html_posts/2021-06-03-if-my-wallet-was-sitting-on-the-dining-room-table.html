Be advised that parole rules vary from state to state, so everything in this answer may not necessarily apply to you)* Maybe your husband's parole officer didn't make this clear to you, so let me see if I can help. Parole is not "release from prison." It is "conditional release." > Parole is designed as an opportunity for a prisoner to transition back > into society. The restrictions on parolees are supposed to encourage > good behavior after incarceration. In fact, even before prisoners get > out of custody, the possibility of parole gives them an incentive to > avoid trouble. > > Parole also reduces prison overcrowding, granting offenders who are > considered unlikely to harm others the benefit of supervised life in > society. Parole helps the government try to cut down on the high costs > of maintaining large prison populations while keeping the population > at large safe.[\[1\]](#VluDV){#cite-VluDV .citation_link} In order to ensure that parolees stay out of trouble, they must meet with their parole officer (PO). You probably already knew that. You probably also knew that POs do random site visits to ensure the parolee is reintegrating into society appropriately, and that there are no violations that could send him back to prison. As I initially mentioned, parole is a "conditional release." Some of the conditions that may apply to your husband as a condition of his release: - He may have to get a job and keep it for a certain period of time - He may have to refrain from drug and alcohol use. If part of his sentence was for either, he may have to attend AA or NA meetings regularly. - He may have to stay within a certain geographic area unless his PO gives him permission - He may not contact his victims or former associates, and he may not participate in any criminal activity The PO usually can conduct random, unannounced searches of the parolee's residence, and usually has the authority to search the areas which the parolee has reasonable access to. This includes looking in a wallet on the dining room table for evidence of drug or alcohol use, or for evidence of criminal activity. POs usually have pretty broad discretion in this regard, as your home is essentially considered an extension of the jail, and that PO is responsible for that parolee. If you really believe your rights were violated or that the PO did something he was not allowed to do, you should not ask questions on Quora. You should instead call a criminal defense attorney in your area. You might want to contact your husband's original defense attorney, or you may wish to find another.
