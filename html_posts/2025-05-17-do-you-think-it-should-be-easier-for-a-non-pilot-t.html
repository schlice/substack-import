--- title: '"Do you think it should be easier for a non-pilot to land a plane in a medical or other emergency?"' date: '2025-05-17' tags: - uncategorized layout: post ---

Answer

{.rendered_qtext}
**Content:** []{.rendered_qtext} Herein lies the problem. You\'re never going to know that there is a medical emergency, because the flight deck is locked from the inside. Nobody is ever going to know that there is a medical emergency on the flight deck, if both the pilot and the co-pilot are incapacitated. By the time the rest of the flight crew realizes that there is a medical emergency on the flight deck, the plane has already crashed. This is why we have both a pilot and a co-pilot. Bear in mind, the co-pilot is fully certified and capable of flying the aircraft all by himself. The only thing he lacks is the hours needed to be considered a pilot. Take, for example, the Lufthansa plane which recently had a mid-air medical issue on the flight deck. The pilot stepped out of the flight deck to go to the washroom, and while he was there, the co-pilot passed out. The pilot tried to regain access to the flight deck by calling the co-pilot, and when that failed, the pilot entered the emergency override code on the keypad outside of the flight deck door. At that time, the copilot regained consciousness. The co-pilot was diagnosed with a neurological condition and lost his pilot\'s license. But the likelihood of both the pilot and the co-pilot suffering medical emergencies at exactly the same time? Not very likely. So do we really want to make it easier for average passengers to land a commercial jumbo jet? No, that\'s an expenditure that really doesn\'t pay dividends. We already have sufficient "two in the box\" redundancy on the flight deck. This is a solution in search of a problem.
